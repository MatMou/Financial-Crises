The dot-com bubble of the late 1990s and its aftermath occupy a pivotal position in the narrative of this course. The bubble itself was a classic speculative mania, fitting the Kindleberger-Minsky template with remarkable precision: a displacement (the commercialization of the internet), a credit mechanism (venture capital, IPOs, and margin lending), a euphoria phase (in which established valuation metrics were discarded), and a crash (in which trillions of dollars of market value were destroyed). But the dot-com episode is important not only in its own right. It is equally important for what came after: the policy response to the crash, and the broader economic environment of the early 2000s, created the conditions that led directly to the global financial crisis of 2007-2009.

The Federal Reserve's aggressive interest rate cuts in response to the dot-com crash and the 2001 recession, combined with the global savings glut, produced a prolonged period of unusually low interest rates that fueled a credit boom in housing and structured finance. The corporate governance scandals at Enron, WorldCom, and other firms exposed the limitations of market discipline and the conflicts of interest embedded in the financial system. The regulatory response, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, addressed corporate fraud but left the rapidly growing shadow banking system and the proliferation of complex financial instruments largely untouched.

This session connects the emerging market crises of the 1990s (Session 7) to the global financial crisis (Sessions 9-11) by tracing the economic, financial, and regulatory developments of the 1995-2006 period.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Analyze the dot-com bubble using the Kindleberger-Minsky framework, identifying the displacement, the credit mechanism, the euphoria phase, and the crash.
2. Explain the "new economy" narrative and evaluate the extent to which the technology boom reflected genuine productivity gains versus speculative excess.
3. Describe the corporate governance failures at Enron and WorldCom, explain the conflicts of interest that enabled them, and assess the effectiveness of the Sarbanes-Oxley regulatory response.
4. Explain the concept of the "Greenspan put" and analyze how the Federal Reserve's asymmetric response to asset prices contributed to moral hazard and risk-taking.
5. Describe the global savings glut hypothesis and explain how the combination of low interest rates, financial innovation, and regulatory gaps created the conditions for the housing boom.
6. Trace the connections between the dot-com crash, the policy response, and the buildup of the vulnerabilities that produced the global financial crisis.

## Session Outline

**8.1 The Technology Boom and the New Economy Narrative.** The commercialization of the internet, the productivity acceleration of the late 1990s, and the debate over whether the "new economy" justified new valuation metrics.

**8.2 Anatomy of the Dot-Com Bubble.** The mechanics of the bubble: venture capital, IPOs, retail speculation, analyst conflicts of interest, and the role of financial innovation in fueling the mania.

**8.3 The Crash of 2000-2002.** The collapse of the NASDAQ, the destruction of wealth, the recession of 2001, and the comparison with earlier speculative episodes.

**8.4 Corporate Governance Failures.** Enron, WorldCom, Arthur Andersen, and the systemic conflicts of interest in auditing, equity research, and investment banking. The Sarbanes-Oxley Act and its limitations.

**8.5 The Greenspan Put and the Low-Rate Environment.** The Federal Reserve's response to the dot-com crash, the concept of asymmetric monetary policy, and the debate over whether the Fed's low interest rates contributed to the subsequent housing bubble.

**8.6 The Seeds of the Housing Boom.** The global savings glut, the search for yield, the expansion of subprime mortgage lending, and the growth of securitization and the shadow banking system that would be at the center of the global financial crisis.
