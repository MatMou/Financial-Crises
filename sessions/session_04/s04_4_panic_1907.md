title: "4.4 The Panic of 1907 and the Birth of the Federal Reserve"
subtitle: The crisis that created America's central bank
---

The Panic of 1907 was the last major banking panic in the United States before the creation of the Federal Reserve System in 1913. It was not the most severe crisis of the era (the depression of the 1890s was deeper), but it was the most consequential in institutional terms: it demonstrated beyond doubt that the United States needed a central bank, and it set in motion the political process that created one. The panic also provides a remarkable case study of crisis dynamics, because it unfolded rapidly, in a concentrated geographic area (New York City), and left an unusually detailed documentary record.

## The structural weaknesses of the U.S. financial system

To understand why the Panic of 1907 was so severe, it is necessary to understand the structural weaknesses of the American financial system at the time.

The United States was the only major industrial economy without a central bank. The Federal government had experimented with central banking twice (the First Bank of the United States, 1791-1811, and the Second Bank of the United States, 1816-1836), but both institutions had been eliminated for political reasons, primarily the Jacksonian suspicion of concentrated financial power. Since 1836, the U.S. had relied on a decentralized system of state-chartered banks and (after the National Banking Act of 1863) nationally chartered banks, with no institution responsible for providing liquidity to the system as a whole.

The National Banking System had several design flaws that made it prone to panics. The money supply was "inelastic": it could not expand in response to seasonal fluctuations in the demand for cash (such as the crop-moving season in autumn) or to sudden increases in demand during panics. National banks were required to hold reserves, but the reserves were concentrated in a few "central reserve cities" (primarily New York), which created the same kind of hierarchical reserve structure that Bagehot had criticized in Britain. When rural and regional banks needed cash, they drew down their deposits in New York, which put pressure on the New York banks, which in turn put pressure on the entire system.

The absence of a lender of last resort meant that the system depended on private mechanisms for crisis management. The most important of these was the **clearinghouse association**, a cooperative institution through which banks settled their mutual claims. During panics, the clearinghouse could issue **clearinghouse loan certificates**, which were a form of emergency currency that banks could use to settle claims with each other, conserving their cash reserves for depositor withdrawals. The clearinghouse system was ingenious but limited: it could provide liquidity among member banks, but it could not create new money, it could not lend to institutions outside the clearinghouse, and it depended on the voluntary cooperation of its members, which could break down under stress.

## The panic unfolds

The Panic of 1907 began in October with the failure of a speculative scheme to corner the stock of the United Copper Company. The scheme was organized by F. Augustus Heinze, a copper magnate, and Charles Morse, a financier, who used a chain of banks and trust companies that they controlled to finance the corner. When the corner collapsed, the banks associated with Heinze and Morse came under suspicion, and depositors began to withdraw their funds.

The critical moment came when the contagion spread to the **trust companies**. Trust companies were financial institutions that, unlike national banks, were not subject to reserve requirements and were not members of the clearinghouse. They had grown rapidly in the preceding years by offering higher deposit rates (made possible by lower reserve requirements) and by engaging in riskier activities (including loans to stock market speculators). The largest of the trust companies, the Knickerbocker Trust Company, was rumored to be connected to the Heinze-Morse group. On October 22, the National Bank of Commerce announced that it would no longer clear checks for the Knickerbocker, which amounted to a withdrawal of its financial support. The next day, depositors formed lines outside the Knickerbocker's offices, and the trust company suspended payments after disbursing approximately $8 million in a few hours.

The Knickerbocker's suspension set off a general run on the trust companies. The Trust Company of America and the Lincoln Trust Company came under intense pressure. The contagion spread to the stock market, where the withdrawal of trust company funds from the call money market (which financed stock purchases on margin) caused interest rates to spike to 100% per annum and the stock market to plummet. The crisis threatened to bring down the entire New York financial system.

## J.P. Morgan as the private lender of last resort

In the absence of a central bank, the task of crisis management fell to John Pierpont Morgan, the most powerful private banker in America. Morgan was 70 years old in October 1907 and had been involved in crisis management before (he had organized the gold syndicate that rescued the Treasury's gold reserves during the 1895 crisis). But the scale of the 1907 panic required an unprecedented private effort.

Morgan assembled a group of leading bankers in his private library on Madison Avenue and organized a series of emergency measures. He directed the clearing of the trusts' positions, arranged for loans to the most important trust companies to prevent their failure, and persuaded the Treasury Secretary, George Cortelyou, to deposit $25 million of government funds in New York banks to increase their liquidity. When the stock exchange was on the verge of closing because brokers could not obtain funds to settle their trades, Morgan raised $25 million from a group of bank presidents in approximately 15 minutes and directed it into the call money market.

Morgan's intervention was effective but revealed the fundamental inadequacy of private crisis management. The response depended entirely on the judgment, resources, and willingness of a single individual. Morgan had to make decisions in real time about which institutions to save and which to let fail, with incomplete information about their solvency. He extracted concessions from the institutions he rescued (including the acquisition of the Tennessee Coal, Iron, and Railroad Company by U.S. Steel, which was later criticized as anticompetitive). And his ability to mobilize resources depended on his personal authority and on the cooperation of other bankers, which was not guaranteed and could not be institutionalized.

The crisis lasted approximately three weeks before the most acute phase subsided, though the economic contraction continued into 1908. The episode left a strong impression on the American public and on the political establishment: the world's largest economy had been saved from financial collapse by one elderly man in his library, and the vulnerability of the system to future crises was glaringly obvious.

## From the Aldrich Plan to the Federal Reserve Act

The Panic of 1907 generated immediate demands for reform. In 1908, Congress passed the Aldrich-Vreeland Act, which created the National Monetary Commission to study the banking systems of other countries and to recommend reforms. The commission, chaired by Senator Nelson Aldrich, produced a massive study of foreign banking systems and proposed the Aldrich Plan, which called for the creation of a central institution (the National Reserve Association) with the power to issue currency, hold bank reserves, and lend to banks in times of stress.

The Aldrich Plan was drafted in secret at a meeting on Jekyll Island, Georgia, in November 1910, attended by Aldrich and a small group of prominent bankers. The plan was widely criticized as a creation of the banking establishment (which it was) and was opposed by progressives who feared the concentration of financial power. After Woodrow Wilson's election in 1912, the plan was reworked into the Federal Reserve Act, which was signed into law on December 23, 1913.

The Federal Reserve Act established a system of 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks, coordinated by a Federal Reserve Board in Washington. The system was a political compromise between those who wanted a single, powerful central bank (the bankers) and those who wanted a decentralized system that would be responsive to the needs of different regions (the populists and progressives). Each Reserve Bank could issue Federal Reserve notes (a new elastic currency), hold the reserves of member banks, and lend to member banks through the discount window.

The creation of the Federal Reserve was a direct response to the deficiencies exposed by the Panic of 1907: the inelastic currency, the pyramiding of reserves, and the dependence on private individuals for crisis management. The new system was designed to provide an elastic currency that could expand during panics and contract during normal times, to hold reserves centrally rather than in a pyramid, and to serve as a permanent lender of last resort. Whether the Federal Reserve would fulfill these functions effectively would be tested severely during the Great Depression, the subject of Session 5.
