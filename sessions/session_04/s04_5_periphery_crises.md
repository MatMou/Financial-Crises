The experience of the gold standard era was profoundly different for countries at the core of the system (Britain, France, Germany, the United States) and countries at the periphery (Latin America, Southern and Eastern Europe, parts of Asia and the Middle East). Core countries generally maintained gold convertibility, experienced moderate business cycle fluctuations, and gradually developed more effective institutions for financial crisis management. Peripheral countries experienced far more volatile capital flows, more frequent financial crises, and repeated episodes of sovereign default. Their experience under the gold standard foreshadowed the emerging market crises of the 1990s discussed in Session 7, and many of the mechanisms are identical.

## Capital flow cycles to the periphery

The classical gold standard era coincided with the first great wave of financial globalization. Between 1870 and 1914, international capital flows, measured as current account imbalances relative to GDP, reached levels that were not seen again until the late 20th century. Britain was by far the largest capital exporter, investing approximately 4-5% of its GDP abroad in most years, rising to nearly 9% in the years immediately before World War I. The capital flowed primarily to the "regions of recent settlement" (the Americas, Australasia, Southern Africa) and to other areas with natural resources and growth potential.

Capital flows to the periphery were strongly procyclical and exhibited a boom-bust pattern that {cite:t}`Reinhart2009` have documented across centuries and continents. During booms, capital flowed abundantly to peripheral countries, financing railroad construction, infrastructure investment, government spending, and (inevitably) speculative ventures. Interest rate spreads on peripheral government bonds narrowed, reflecting optimism about the borrowers' prospects. During busts, capital flows reversed sharply, interest rate spreads widened, and peripheral countries were forced into painful adjustment, often including default on their external debt.

The boom-bust cycle in capital flows to the periphery was driven by a combination of "push" factors (conditions in the capital-exporting countries) and "pull" factors (conditions in the capital-importing countries). The most important push factor was the level of interest rates in London: when British interest rates were low, capital flowed outward to the periphery in search of higher returns; when rates rose, capital was attracted back to London. The most important pull factors were the real and perceived profitability of investment in the peripheral economies, which depended on commodity prices, political stability, and the institutional framework for property rights and contract enforcement.

## The Barings Crisis of 1890

The most important financial crisis in the periphery during the classical gold standard era was the Barings Crisis of 1890, which centered on Argentina but had global repercussions, including a near-fatal threat to Baring Brothers, one of the oldest and most prestigious merchant banks in London.

The background was a massive speculative boom in Argentina during the 1880s. Argentina was one of the fastest-growing economies in the world, with enormous agricultural potential in the Pampas and rapidly expanding railroad, port, and urban infrastructure. Foreign capital (primarily British) poured in to finance the boom. The Argentine government, the provincial governments, and the private sector all borrowed heavily, primarily in sterling-denominated bonds issued in London. The national government also issued paper currency (inconvertible pesos) to finance spending, which contributed to inflation and currency depreciation.

By 1889, the bubble was showing signs of strain. The weight of debt service was becoming unsustainable, the fiscal deficit was widening, and the depreciation of the peso was increasing the real burden of the sterling-denominated debt (the currency mismatch problem discussed in subsection 3.3). A political crisis in 1890 (the "Revolución del Parque") further undermined confidence, and capital flows from London dried up.

Baring Brothers had been the principal underwriter and distributor of Argentine government bonds in London. When the Argentine government was unable to service its debt, Baring Brothers found itself holding a large inventory of unsold Argentine securities that it could not offload. The firm faced imminent failure, which would have had catastrophic consequences for the London money market and potentially for the entire international financial system.

The Bank of England organized a rescue. It assembled a guarantee fund of approximately £17 million from the major London banks and financial houses, sufficient to cover Baring Brothers' liabilities and allow it to wind down its Argentine exposure in an orderly fashion. The Bank of England also obtained a loan from the Banque de France and the Russian State Bank to bolster its gold reserves, demonstrating that international cooperation among central banks, though informal and ad hoc, was possible during emergencies.

The Barings Crisis is significant for several reasons. First, it demonstrates the vulnerability of peripheral countries to sudden stops in capital flows, a vulnerability that persisted long after the gold standard era ended. Argentina's crisis was driven by the same dynamics that would produce the Latin American debt crisis of the 1980s and the Argentine crisis of 2001: a capital inflow boom, external borrowing in foreign currency, rising leverage, and an abrupt reversal when confidence turned.

Second, the crisis demonstrates the potential for problems in the periphery to threaten the stability of the core. Baring Brothers was not a peripheral institution; it was one of the pillars of the London financial system. Its failure would have damaged the Bank of England's credibility, disrupted the London money market, and potentially triggered a global financial crisis. The Bank of England's decision to organize a rescue, despite the risks and costs, reflected its understanding that the stability of the core depended on preventing disorderly failures, even when those failures were caused by imprudent lending to the periphery.

Third, the rescue of Baring Brothers illustrates both the effectiveness and the limitations of the pre-1914 approach to crisis management. The rescue was organized quickly and effectively, and it prevented a systemic crisis. But it was organized informally, without a clear legal framework, and it depended on the cooperation of private institutions that had to be persuaded (in some cases pressured) to participate. The rescue also created moral hazard: by protecting Baring Brothers from the consequences of its overexposure to Argentina, the Bank of England may have encouraged future risk-taking by other financial institutions.

## Serial default in the periphery

The Barings Crisis was part of a broader pattern of sovereign default in the periphery that {cite:t}`Reinhart2009` have documented in detail. During the 19th century, virtually every country in Latin America defaulted on its external debt at least once, and many defaulted multiple times. Spain defaulted eight times between 1557 and 1882. The Ottoman Empire defaulted in 1876. Egypt's default in 1876 led to foreign financial control and eventually to British occupation.

The pattern of serial default is one of the most important findings from the Reinhart-Rogoff dataset. Countries that have defaulted once are more likely to default again, not because default is contagious in a biological sense, but because the underlying conditions that produce default (weak fiscal institutions, dependence on volatile commodity revenues, political instability, external borrowing in foreign currency) tend to persist. Default is a symptom of structural vulnerability, and structural vulnerabilities are slow to change.

The response to default followed a predictable sequence. When a peripheral government defaulted, its access to international capital markets was cut off. After a period of negotiation (often lasting years), the government would reach a restructuring agreement with its creditors, typically involving a reduction in the face value of the debt, a reduction in the interest rate, and an extension of the maturity. The government would then gradually regain access to capital markets, initially at high interest rates, and the cycle would begin again.

The costs of default were significant but not catastrophic. Defaulting governments lost access to capital markets for a period (Reinhart and Rogoff estimate an average exclusion of approximately 5-6 years), and they typically had to accept some degree of external supervision or conditionality as part of the restructuring. But the costs of continued debt service, in terms of fiscal austerity and economic stagnation, were often judged to be higher, which is why default recurred.

## The periphery and the gold standard constraint

The gold standard imposed particularly severe constraints on peripheral countries. Because their currencies were less trusted than those of the core countries, peripheral countries had to hold larger gold reserves relative to their money supply to maintain convertibility. They also had to pay higher interest rates to attract and retain capital, which increased their debt service burden. And they were more vulnerable to capital flow reversals, because the foreign investors who held their bonds were quick to sell when conditions deteriorated.

Many peripheral countries attempted to join the gold standard in order to gain the credibility benefits of the gold commitment, hoping that adherence to the gold standard would signal fiscal and monetary discipline and thereby reduce their borrowing costs. Some succeeded (Argentina briefly, Japan after 1897, Russia after 1897), but the commitment was often fragile and was abandoned under stress. The gold standard was, in practice, a club with strict membership criteria, and countries that could not meet those criteria, or that met them only under favorable conditions, experienced the costs of the standard (procyclical monetary policy, deflationary pressure, vulnerability to capital flow reversals) without fully enjoying its benefits (low interest rates, stable capital flows, international credibility).

This experience has direct parallels with the experience of peripheral Eurozone countries before and during the European debt crisis. Just as peripheral gold standard countries borrowed in a currency they could not control (gold-backed sterling) and were vulnerable to sudden stops when creditor confidence turned, peripheral Eurozone countries borrowed in a currency they could not devalue (the euro) and experienced devastating capital flow reversals when the crisis hit. The parallel is not exact, but it is close enough to be instructive, and we will return to it in Session 12.
