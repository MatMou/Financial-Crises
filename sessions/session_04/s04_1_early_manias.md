subtitle: Tulips, Mississippi shares, and the South Sea Company

The earliest well-documented speculative episodes in financial history occurred in the 17th and 18th centuries, a period during which European financial markets were undergoing rapid institutional development. The Amsterdam stock exchange, the Bank of England, the Banque Royale, joint-stock companies, government debt markets, and various forms of derivative contracts were all new or newly prominent. The speculative manias of this era are not merely colorful anecdotes; they illustrate the fundamental dynamics of financial instability in their purest form, before the complications of modern banking, central banking, and financial regulation.

This subsection examines three canonical episodes: the Dutch Tulip Mania of 1636-1637, the Mississippi Bubble of 1719-1720, and the South Sea Bubble of 1720. Each is analyzed through the Kindleberger-Minsky framework introduced in subsection 1.3, with attention to the specific institutional features that enabled the speculation.

## The Dutch Tulip Mania (1636-1637)

The Tulip Mania is the most famous speculative episode in history, and also one of the most misunderstood. The popular narrative, derived largely from Charles Mackay's *Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds* (1841), portrays a frenzy of irrational speculation in which tulip bulbs traded for the price of houses and the entire Dutch economy was brought to its knees when prices collapsed. Recent scholarship, particularly by Peter Garber and Anne Goldgar, has significantly revised this account ({cite:t}`Garber2000`).

The **displacement** in the Kindleberger-Minsky framework was the introduction of exotic tulip varieties into the Netherlands from the Ottoman Empire in the late 16th century. Tulips with unusual color patterns, caused by a mosaic virus that produced "broken" or "flamed" petals, were prized as luxury goods by the Dutch merchant class. The rarest varieties were genuinely scarce: a single bulb could take years to propagate, and the virus that produced the most valued patterns was unpredictable and uncontrollable. There was thus a legitimate basis for high prices on the rarest bulbs.

The **boom** developed in 1634-1636 as speculation spread from connoisseurs and professional growers to a broader public. Trading in tulip bulbs moved from the formal exchanges to informal markets in taverns, where groups of traders (known as "colleges") established their own rules for contracts and settlement. Crucially, the trading increasingly involved **futures contracts**: buyers agreed to purchase bulbs at a specified price for delivery at a future date (typically when the bulbs could be lifted from the ground in spring). These futures contracts allowed speculation without requiring the physical possession of bulbs, which massively increased the volume of trading and the degree of leverage in the market.

The **euphoria** phase, concentrated in the winter of 1636-1637, saw prices for common tulip varieties (not just the rare broken tulips) increase by factors of 10 to 20 within a few weeks. Garber argues that much of the price increase was concentrated in the common bulb market, where speculation was most intense, and that prices for the rarest varieties had already been high for years.

The **crash** came in February 1637, when prices collapsed almost overnight at an auction in Haarlem. Buyers simply stopped bidding, and the market seized up. Attempts to enforce the futures contracts failed because the sellers could not compel payment and the legal status of the contracts was uncertain. The Dutch government eventually allowed contracts to be settled at 3.5% of the contracted price, imposing most of the losses on the sellers.

The Tulip Mania has important features that distinguish it from later speculative episodes. First, the financial system was not significantly damaged. The speculation was conducted primarily by individuals using their own funds or informal credit arrangements, not by banks or other financial intermediaries. There was no banking crisis, no credit contraction, and no noticeable effect on the broader Dutch economy. Second, the episode was geographically concentrated (primarily in the Holland and Utrecht provinces) and socially concentrated (primarily among the middle and upper-middle classes). Third, there is significant scholarly debate about whether the Tulip Mania constituted a true "bubble" in the sense of prices exceeding fundamental values, or whether the price dynamics can be explained by rational responses to the genuine scarcity of rare bulbs and the peculiar contract structure of the futures market.

Nevertheless, the Tulip Mania established several themes that recur in subsequent episodes: the role of financial innovation (futures contracts) in enabling speculation, the extension of speculation from professionals to the general public, and the difficulty of enforcing contracts when prices collapse.

## The Mississippi Bubble (1719-1720)

The Mississippi Bubble, centered on the Compagnie du Mississippi and the Banque Royale in France, was far larger and more consequential than the Tulip Mania. It involved a systematic attempt to restructure the French public finances through the creation of a combined trading company, central bank, and tax collection agency, all under the direction of John Law, a Scottish financier who became the most powerful man in France after the Regent, Philippe d'Orléans.

The **displacement** was France's fiscal crisis following the death of Louis XIV in 1715. The French crown was burdened with enormous debts accumulated during decades of war, and the traditional methods of deficit finance (tax farming, debasement, forced conversion of debt) had exhausted their capacity. John Law proposed a radical solution: the creation of a national bank (the Banque Générale, later the Banque Royale) that would issue banknotes backed by the crown's credit, and a trading company (the Compagnie d'Occident, later the Compagnie du Mississippi, later the Compagnie des Indes) that would develop France's colonial territories and, crucially, would absorb the national debt by allowing holders of government bonds to exchange their bonds for shares in the company.

The **boom** was driven by the interplay between the company's share price and the bank's note issuance. Law's scheme worked as follows: the company issued shares, which were purchased with government bonds or with banknotes issued by the Banque Royale. The Banque Royale, in turn, issued notes to finance the purchase of company shares. The rising share price increased the value of the collateral backing the bank's note issuance, which allowed the bank to issue more notes, which provided more liquidity for purchasing shares, which pushed the share price higher. This was a self-reinforcing feedback loop of exactly the kind that Minsky described, but operating at the level of the state rather than the private sector.

The company's shares rose from approximately 500 livres in early 1719 to a peak of approximately 10,000 livres in January 1720, an increase of roughly 20-fold. The note issuance of the Banque Royale expanded enormously, and a wave of consumer spending and speculative investment swept through Paris. The word "millionaire" was coined during this period to describe the newly wealthy shareholders.

The **collapse** began in early 1720 when some large holders began converting their shares back to gold and silver. Law attempted to support the share price through various administrative measures, including restrictions on gold and silver holdings and forced reductions in the nominal value of shares and banknotes. These measures backfired, destroying confidence in both the company and the bank. By the end of 1720, the company's shares had fallen back to approximately 500 livres, the Banque Royale's notes were worthless, and Law had fled France.

The Mississippi Bubble was far more consequential than the Tulip Mania. The destruction of the Banque Royale's notes wiped out the savings of a large segment of the French public and discredited the concept of paper money and central banking in France for nearly a century. France did not establish a permanent central bank until the creation of the Banque de France by Napoleon in 1800. The experience left a deep cultural aversion to paper money and financial innovation that arguably contributed to France's slower financial development compared to Britain in the 18th and 19th centuries.

The Mississippi Bubble also illustrates a theme that recurs in later crises: the danger of intertwining the financial system with the state's fiscal needs. Law's scheme used the banking system as a device for monetizing the national debt, and when the scheme collapsed, it brought down both the financial system and the state's fiscal credibility simultaneously. This is an early example of the sovereign-bank nexus discussed in subsection 3.4.

## The South Sea Bubble (1720)

The South Sea Bubble, which unfolded in England contemporaneously with the Mississippi Bubble in France, involved a similar scheme to convert government debt into equity, though with different institutional details and different consequences.

The South Sea Company was a joint-stock company that had been granted a monopoly on British trade with South America and the Pacific. The trade itself was of limited value (Spain controlled most of the relevant territory), but the company's real business became the management of the national debt. In 1720, the company proposed to absorb a large portion of the British national debt by allowing government creditors to exchange their bonds for South Sea Company shares. The company would pay the government a large sum for the privilege, funded by the expected appreciation of its own shares.

The scheme depended on a rising share price. If the share price was high enough, the company could issue fewer shares to acquire a given amount of government debt, leaving more shares available to sell to the public at a profit. The company's directors therefore had a strong incentive to promote the share price, which they did through various devices: installment plans that allowed investors to buy shares on credit (paying only a fraction of the price upfront), loans against the company's own shares as collateral, and aggressive marketing.

The share price rose from approximately £130 in January 1720 to a peak of approximately £1,000 in June 1720. The boom attracted a wave of speculative company promotions (the "Bubble companies"), many of which were transparently fraudulent. Parliament passed the Bubble Act in June 1720, ostensibly to protect investors by requiring that joint-stock companies obtain a royal charter, but in practice intended to suppress competition with the South Sea Company by shutting down rival promotions.

The collapse was swift. By September 1720, the share price had fallen back to approximately £150. Many investors who had bought on credit (including members of the aristocracy and the political establishment) were ruined. The directors of the company were investigated by Parliament, and several were found to have engaged in corrupt practices, including bribing government ministers with shares.

The South Sea Bubble had lasting institutional consequences. The Bubble Act remained in force until 1825 and restricted the development of the joint-stock company form in England for over a century. The crisis also reinforced the position of the Bank of England, which had been the South Sea Company's rival and which emerged from the episode with enhanced credibility as the more conservative and reliable institution. The Bank of England's increasing role as the manager of the government's debt and the anchor of the financial system set the stage for its eventual evolution into a central bank with lender-of-last-resort responsibilities.

## Common patterns

The three episodes share several features that anticipate the dynamics of later crises.

All three involved **financial innovation** that enabled speculation on a scale that would not otherwise have been possible. Tulip futures allowed speculation without physical possession of bulbs. The Banque Royale's note issuance provided the liquidity for Mississippi share purchases. South Sea installment plans and share-backed loans allowed leveraged speculation. In each case, the innovation was not inherently harmful, but it amplified the speculative dynamics by increasing leverage and broadening participation.

All three involved a **feedback loop** between asset prices and credit conditions. Rising prices made it easier to borrow (because the assets serving as collateral were more valuable), which provided more purchasing power, which pushed prices higher. The loop worked in reverse during the crash: falling prices eroded collateral values, triggering forced sales, which depressed prices further.

All three episodes were followed by **regulatory responses** that reflected the lessons of the crisis but that also imposed costs of their own. France's aversion to paper money slowed its financial development. The Bubble Act restricted corporate formation in England for a century. These examples illustrate a pattern that recurs throughout the course: post-crisis regulation tends to address the specific vulnerabilities revealed by the most recent crisis, sometimes at the cost of creating new rigidities or vulnerabilities.

Finally, and perhaps most importantly, all three episodes demonstrate that **speculative manias are not modern phenomena**. They do not require sophisticated financial instruments, complex institutional structures, or global capital markets. They require only the combination of a plausible narrative about future profits, a mechanism for leveraged speculation, and the behavioral dynamics of optimism, imitation, and fear that are inherent in human psychology. The Kindleberger-Minsky framework, developed to explain 20th-century crises, maps remarkably well onto episodes that occurred three centuries earlier.
