---
title: "4.3 Banking Panics in the 19th Century"
subtitle: The recurring crises of 1825, 1857, 1873, and 1893
---

The 19th century was punctuated by a series of banking panics that struck with remarkable regularity, approximately once per decade. Each panic followed a recognizable pattern: a period of credit expansion and speculative investment, a triggering event that shattered confidence, a rush for liquidity that overwhelmed the banking system, and a sharp contraction of credit and economic activity. The panics differed in their specific causes and in the institutional context in which they occurred, but the underlying dynamics were strikingly consistent, lending strong support to the Kindleberger-Minsky view that financial instability is a recurring structural feature of market economies rather than a series of unrelated accidents.

This subsection examines four major panics of the 19th century, tracing the evolution of the banking system, the development of the lender-of-last-resort function, and the gradually improving (though never fully adequate) capacity of institutional arrangements to manage financial crises.

## The Panic of 1825

The Panic of 1825 was the first major financial crisis of the industrial era and has been described by {cite:t}`Kindleberger2011` as the first modern financial crisis that fits the full boom-bust template.

The displacement was the opening of Latin American markets to British trade and investment following the independence movements that swept the continent in the early 1820s. British investors, attracted by the prospect of enormous profits from newly independent countries rich in natural resources, poured capital into Latin American government bonds and mining company shares. Between 1822 and 1825, approximately £17 million in Latin American government bonds were issued in London, a very large sum for the time.

The boom was fueled by easy credit conditions in Britain. The Bank of England had lowered its discount rate, and the country banking system (the network of small, lightly regulated provincial banks) expanded rapidly, issuing notes and extending credit. A speculative mania developed not only in Latin American securities but also in domestic shares, canals, and other ventures.

The collapse was triggered by the failure of several Latin American government loans (the borrowers had never had the fiscal capacity to service the debt) and by the tightening of monetary conditions as gold flowed out of the Bank of England. When confidence cracked in December 1825, there was a general rush for liquidity. Country banks, which held few reserves and depended on the London money market for funding, were particularly vulnerable. Approximately 70 banks failed in the weeks following the crisis.

The Bank of England's response was initially inadequate. It had allowed its gold reserves to run dangerously low during the boom and was slow to recognize the severity of the panic. Eventually, it expanded its lending dramatically, providing liquidity to the money market on an unprecedented scale. The episode taught the Bank an important lesson about its responsibility to the financial system, though the full articulation of the lender-of-last-resort doctrine would not come until Bagehot's *Lombard Street* nearly fifty years later.

The Panic of 1825 led to two important reforms. First, the Bank of England began to manage its discount rate more actively as a tool for controlling credit conditions. Second, legislation was passed allowing the formation of joint-stock banks with more than six partners (previously prohibited in England, though not in Scotland), which eventually produced a more consolidated and more resilient banking system.

## The Panic of 1857

The Panic of 1857 was the first genuinely global financial crisis, spreading from the United States to Britain, France, Germany, and Scandinavia. It demonstrated the power of international financial linkages to transmit crises across borders, a theme that would recur in every subsequent international crisis.

The boom of the early 1850s was driven by several factors: the California gold rush (which increased the global money supply), the Crimean War (which stimulated demand for commodities and manufacturing), the expansion of the American railroad network, and a general environment of economic optimism and easy credit. In the United States, state-chartered banks expanded credit aggressively, particularly in the West, where land speculation was intense. In Britain, discount houses (institutions that intermediated between banks and the money market) expanded rapidly and took on increasingly risky positions.

The trigger was the failure of the Ohio Life Insurance and Trust Company in August 1857, which revealed that the company had been speculating heavily in railroad securities. The failure set off a chain reaction of bank failures and credit contraction in the United States. The contagion spread to Britain through the mercantile credit system: British firms that had extended trade credit to American importers found themselves unable to collect, and their distress was transmitted through the chain of endorsements and acceptances that connected the London money market to the rest of the world.

In Britain, the crisis reached its peak in November 1857, when the Bank of England's reserves fell to dangerously low levels and the discount rate was raised to 10%, the highest in living memory. The government authorized the Bank to exceed its statutory note-issuance limit under the Bank Charter Act of 1844, just as it had done in 1847. In Germany and Scandinavia, the crisis produced bank failures and sharp recessions.

The Panic of 1857 is notable for its international transmission mechanism. The crisis spread not through gold flows (the Humean mechanism) but through the credit system: the failure of American borrowers to repay British creditors transmitted the crisis across the Atlantic. This pattern of contagion through credit linkages would be repeated in virtually every subsequent international financial crisis.

## The Long Depression of 1873

The Panic of 1873 and the prolonged depression that followed it (known in the 19th century as the "Great Depression," a label later transferred to the even more severe contraction of the 1930s) was one of the most severe and geographically widespread crises of the 19th century.

The boom that preceded the panic was driven by railroad construction, both in the United States and in Central Europe. In the United States, the transcontinental railroad and its many branches required enormous amounts of capital, much of which was raised through bond issuance in New York and London. In the Austro-Hungarian Empire and Germany, the period following the Franco-Prussian War (1870-1871) and the French indemnity payment saw a speculative boom in railroad construction, industrial investment, and real estate, particularly in Vienna and Berlin.

The panic began in Vienna in May 1873 with the collapse of the Vienna Stock Exchange (the "Krach"), which wiped out a large number of speculative firms and banks. The contagion reached the United States in September 1873 with the failure of Jay Cooke and Company, one of the most prominent banking firms in America and the primary financier of the Northern Pacific Railroad. The failure triggered a panic on Wall Street, and the New York Stock Exchange was closed for ten days, an unprecedented action. Bank failures spread across the country, and a severe credit contraction followed.

The depression that followed was unusually prolonged. In the United States, the contraction lasted from 1873 to 1879 (the longest in U.S. history until the Great Depression), with falling prices, widespread business failures, labor unrest, and high unemployment. In Europe, the depression was less severe in terms of output decline but lasted even longer, with deflation and stagnation persisting through much of the 1880s. The gold standard played a role in prolonging the depression by preventing countries from pursuing expansionary monetary policies: the commitment to convertibility required that the money supply grow no faster than the gold stock, which was insufficient to accommodate the growing demand for money in a rapidly expanding global economy.

The Long Depression of the 1870s had important political consequences. It fueled the rise of protectionism (as governments sought to shield domestic industries from foreign competition during the downturn), the growth of the labor movement and socialist parties, and the debate over bimetallism (whether silver should be remonetized alongside gold to increase the money supply). These political responses to the economic crisis are an early example of a pattern that recurs throughout the course: financial crises generate political movements that challenge the prevailing economic order.

## The Panic of 1893

The Panic of 1893 was the most severe financial crisis in the United States between the Civil War and the Great Depression. It combined a banking panic with a currency crisis, as doubts about the United States' commitment to the gold standard caused capital to flee the country.

The background was the silver question. Under the Sherman Silver Purchase Act of 1890, the U.S. Treasury was required to purchase 4.5 million ounces of silver per month and to issue Treasury notes that were redeemable in gold. The policy was a compromise between the gold standard advocates (primarily Eastern bankers and industrialists) and the silver advocates (primarily Western miners and farmers who wanted an expansion of the money supply to raise agricultural prices). The result was a steady drain on the Treasury's gold reserves, as holders of silver-backed notes presented them for redemption in gold.

By early 1893, the Treasury's gold reserves had fallen below the $100 million level that was considered the minimum necessary to maintain confidence in convertibility. The declining reserves triggered capital flight, as foreign investors sold American securities and repatriated their funds. The National Cordage Company failed in May 1893, setting off a banking panic. Over 500 banks failed during the course of the year, mostly in the South and West. The economy fell into a deep depression, with unemployment estimated at 12-18% and a wave of business failures, foreclosures, and labor unrest.

The crisis was eventually resolved by the repeal of the Sherman Silver Purchase Act (which stopped the drain on gold reserves) and by a series of bond issues to replenish the Treasury's gold stock, arranged by a syndicate led by J.P. Morgan. The episode demonstrated both the fragility of the U.S. financial system (which lacked a central bank and depended on the Treasury and private bankers for liquidity provision) and the political tensions surrounding the gold standard, which pitted the interests of creditors (who benefited from deflation and the gold standard) against those of debtors (who favored silver and inflation). William Jennings Bryan's famous "Cross of Gold" speech at the 1896 Democratic convention, in which he denounced the gold standard as an instrument of oppression against ordinary Americans, was a direct political consequence of the crisis.

## The evolution of crisis management

Across these four episodes, one can trace a gradual improvement in the institutional capacity to manage financial crises, though the progress was uneven and incomplete.

The Bank of England evolved from a reluctant and often clumsy lender of last resort in 1825 to a more confident and effective one by the 1860s, partly in response to Bagehot's public criticism of its earlier failures. The development of the Bank's discount rate as a policy instrument gave it a powerful tool for managing credit conditions and attracting gold. But the Bank remained a private institution with private shareholders, and its willingness to accept losses in the course of providing emergency liquidity was always limited.

In the United States, the absence of a central bank meant that crisis management depended on ad hoc arrangements: clearinghouse loan certificates (issued by private clearinghouse associations to substitute for cash during panics), Treasury deposits in banks, and the private intervention of powerful financiers like J.P. Morgan. These arrangements were resourceful but inadequate: they were unreliable, politically problematic, and incapable of providing liquidity on the scale that a large, complex financial system required. The repeated failures of the U.S. system to prevent or contain banking panics would eventually lead to the creation of the Federal Reserve, the subject of subsection 4.4.
