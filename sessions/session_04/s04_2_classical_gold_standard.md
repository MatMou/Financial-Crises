subtitle: How the pre-1914 international monetary system worked

The classical gold standard, which operated from roughly 1870 to 1914, was the first truly global monetary system. Under the gold standard, the major economies defined their currencies in terms of a fixed weight of gold, maintained convertibility (anyone could exchange banknotes for gold at the central bank at the fixed rate), and allowed gold to flow freely across borders. The system provided a framework of fixed exchange rates, an automatic mechanism for balance-of-payments adjustment, and a degree of monetary discipline that its proponents regarded as essential for economic stability.

The gold standard era also coincided with the first great wave of financial globalization, in which international capital flows reached levels (relative to GDP) that would not be seen again until the late 20th century. Understanding how the gold standard worked, and why it eventually failed, is essential background for the crises discussed in this session and for the analysis of later monetary arrangements, including the Bretton Woods system (Session 6) and the Eurozone (Session 12).

## The mechanics of the gold standard

The gold standard rested on three commitments by participating countries. First, the monetary authority defined the national currency in terms of a fixed weight of gold (for example, the British pound was defined as 113.0016 grains of fine gold, and the U.S. dollar as 23.22 grains). Second, the monetary authority maintained **convertibility**: it stood ready to buy and sell gold at the official price in unlimited quantities. Third, there were no restrictions on the import or export of gold across borders.

These three commitments automatically fixed the exchange rates between participating currencies. If the pound was worth 113 grains of gold and the dollar was worth 23.22 grains, the exchange rate was approximately 113/23.22 = $4.87 per pound. The actual market exchange rate could fluctuate slightly around this parity (within the "gold points," determined by the cost of shipping gold between countries), but it could not deviate significantly, because any deviation created a profitable arbitrage opportunity: if the dollar price of the pound fell below $4.87 minus the shipping cost, it would be profitable to ship gold from London to New York and exchange it for dollars.

## The price-specie-flow mechanism

The classical theory of balance-of-payments adjustment under the gold standard was the **price-specie-flow mechanism**, first articulated by David Hume in 1752. The mechanism works as follows.

Suppose Country A runs a trade surplus with Country B. Country B pays for the excess imports with gold, which flows from Country B to Country A. The inflow of gold increases the money supply in Country A, which raises prices in Country A. The outflow of gold reduces the money supply in Country B, which lowers prices in Country B. The resulting change in relative prices makes Country A's exports more expensive and Country B's exports cheaper, which reduces Country A's trade surplus and Country B's trade deficit. The process continues until the trade balance is restored and gold flows cease.

The price-specie-flow mechanism is elegant in theory, but it describes an idealized version of how the gold standard worked. In practice, the adjustment mechanism was both more complex and more asymmetric than Hume's model suggests.

## How the gold standard actually worked

The historical evidence suggests that the gold standard operated quite differently from the textbook description, in at least three important respects ({cite:t}`Eichengreen1992`).

First, the adjustment to balance-of-payments imbalances did not rely primarily on gold flows and price level changes. Instead, the principal adjustment mechanism was **capital flows** and **interest rate policy**. When a country experienced a balance-of-payments deficit and began losing gold, its central bank raised the discount rate (the interest rate at which it lent to commercial banks). The higher interest rate attracted short-term capital from abroad, which financed the deficit and stemmed the gold outflow. The adjustment was achieved through capital account movements rather than through changes in the price level. This was faster and less disruptive than the Humean mechanism, but it meant that the system depended on the willingness and ability of central banks to manage interest rates actively.

Second, the system was **asymmetric** between surplus and deficit countries. Deficit countries were forced to adjust (because they were losing gold reserves and faced the prospect of being unable to maintain convertibility), while surplus countries were under no corresponding pressure to adjust (because they were accumulating gold, which strengthened rather than threatened their monetary position). The burden of adjustment fell disproportionately on deficit countries, which had to raise interest rates and contract their economies, while surplus countries could sterilize their gold inflows and avoid the expansionary effects that the price-specie-flow mechanism predicted.

Third, the system depended heavily on the **credibility** of the gold commitment. When market participants believed that a country would maintain convertibility, capital flowed in during periods of stress (because they expected the exchange rate to return to parity), which stabilized the system. When credibility was questioned, capital flowed out (because investors feared a devaluation), which destabilized the system. Credibility, in turn, depended on a complex set of factors: the country's gold reserves, its fiscal position, its political institutions, and its track record of maintaining convertibility. The advanced economies of the gold standard core (Britain, France, Germany, the United States) generally maintained credibility, while countries in the periphery (Latin America, Southern Europe, parts of Asia) often did not, which meant that they experienced more volatile capital flows and more frequent crises.

## The role of the Bank of England

The classical gold standard was not a system of equals. It was centered on London, which was by far the world's largest financial center, and on the Bank of England, which functioned as the conductor of the international monetary system.

The Bank of England's pivotal role reflected several factors. Britain was the world's largest creditor nation, the largest trading nation, and the issuer of the currency in which the majority of international trade and finance was denominated. London's financial markets were the deepest and most liquid in the world, and the Bank of England's discount rate was the reference rate for the global financial system.

When the Bank of England raised its discount rate, it attracted capital from around the world, as investors moved funds to London to earn the higher return. This capital inflow strengthened the Bank's gold reserves and supported sterling's convertibility. But it also drained capital from other countries, particularly from the periphery, which could be destabilizing. When the Bank lowered its rate, capital flowed outward, easing conditions abroad but potentially putting pressure on the Bank's own reserves.

The Bank of England thus had a dual role that contained an inherent tension. As the manager of the British monetary system, it was responsible for maintaining the convertibility of sterling and the stability of the British banking system. As the de facto conductor of the international monetary system, its policy decisions had global consequences that it did not fully control and did not always intend. This tension between national responsibility and international influence is a recurring theme in the history of international monetary systems and remains relevant today in the context of the Federal Reserve's role in the global financial system.

## The gold standard as a constraint

The gold standard imposed significant constraints on domestic macroeconomic policy. Because the central bank was committed to maintaining convertibility at a fixed gold price, it could not freely expand the money supply to respond to recessions or banking panics. An expansion of the money supply that was not backed by a corresponding increase in gold reserves would threaten convertibility by making it likely that holders of banknotes would present them for conversion to gold, depleting the central bank's reserves.

This constraint was particularly painful during financial crises. When a banking panic occurred, the demand for cash increased sharply as depositors rushed to withdraw their funds. The central bank faced a dilemma: it could lend freely to distressed banks (as Bagehot recommended), which would increase the money supply and potentially threaten convertibility; or it could defend convertibility by restricting lending, which would allow the panic to deepen and banks to fail. The gold standard effectively forced central banks to choose between financial stability and exchange rate stability, a choice that no central bank wanted to make.

In practice, the tension was managed through a combination of creative improvisation and temporary suspension of the rules. During severe crises, some countries temporarily suspended convertibility (as Britain did during the Napoleonic Wars) or allowed the central bank to exceed its statutory note-issuance limits (as the Bank of England did in the crises of 1847, 1857, and 1866 under letters of indemnity from the Chancellor of the Exchequer). These suspensions were understood to be temporary and were accompanied by commitments to restore convertibility as soon as the crisis passed. The ability to suspend the rules in extremis provided a safety valve that made the gold standard more durable than a purely rigid system would have been.

Nevertheless, the constraint was real and consequential. The gold standard limited the ability of governments to respond to economic downturns, and it transmitted deflationary pressures across borders by requiring deficit countries to contract their money supplies. These features were tolerated during the pre-1914 era, when governments were less responsive to domestic public opinion and when the political costs of deflation and unemployment were lower (or at least less politically salient). After World War I, when mass democracy and organized labor gave voters more political power, the constraints of the gold standard became increasingly intolerable, as we shall see in Session 5.
