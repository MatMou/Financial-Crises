---
title: "13.2 The Financial Market Panic and the Dash for Cash"
subtitle: When even Treasuries stopped being safe
---

The financial market reaction to the pandemic was the fastest and most violent in modern history. Between February 19 and March 23, 2020, the S&P 500 fell by approximately 34%, the fastest decline of that magnitude ever recorded. But the equity market decline, while dramatic, was less important than the disruption in the bond and credit markets, which threatened the core infrastructure of the global financial system and forced the Federal Reserve to intervene on a scale that dwarfed even its GFC-era response.

## The dash for cash

The most alarming feature of the March 2020 panic was the **dash for cash**: a simultaneous rush by investors, corporations, and financial institutions to raise cash by selling assets. The dash for cash reflected the extreme uncertainty generated by the pandemic: no one knew how long the lockdowns would last, how severe the economic damage would be, or which institutions would be able to survive. In this environment, every economic actor sought to build a cash buffer by selling whatever they could.

The dash for cash produced the paradoxical result that assets that normally rise in value during a crisis, most importantly U.S. Treasury bonds, instead fell in value. Treasury bonds are the canonical "safe haven" asset: when risk aversion increases, investors typically sell risky assets (stocks, corporate bonds) and buy Treasuries, pushing Treasury prices up and yields down. In March 2020, the opposite occurred: Treasury yields rose sharply (prices fell) as investors sold Treasuries to raise cash. The 10-year Treasury yield swung from approximately 0.5% to approximately 1.2% in a matter of days, an enormous move for the world's largest and most liquid bond market.

The Treasury market disruption was particularly alarming because it threatened the functioning of the plumbing of the global financial system. Treasury bonds are the collateral that underpins the repo market, the benchmark against which all other bonds are priced, and the safe asset in which central banks, sovereign wealth funds, and institutional investors around the world hold their reserves. A disruption in the Treasury market is not merely a financial market event; it is a disruption of the infrastructure through which global finance operates.

## The corporate credit freeze

The corporate bond market and the commercial paper market froze simultaneously. Investment-grade corporate bond spreads widened from approximately 100 basis points to over 300 basis points in a matter of weeks. High-yield (junk) bond spreads widened from approximately 350 basis points to over 1,000 basis points. The commercial paper market, on which corporations relied for short-term working capital, seized up as money market funds and other buyers withdrew, reprising the dynamics of September 2008 (subsection 10.4).

The credit freeze threatened to transform the pandemic recession into a financial crisis. Corporations that depended on capital markets for financing, including airlines, hotels, retailers, and energy companies, were suddenly unable to roll over their debt or issue new bonds. Even fundamentally solvent companies faced the prospect of liquidity crises if the credit markets remained frozen.

## The run on money market funds (again)

The March 2020 panic included a replay of the money market fund run that had occurred in September 2008. Investors withdrew approximately $120 billion from prime money market funds (which invest in commercial paper and certificates of deposit) in the two weeks ending March 20, shifting the funds to government money market funds (which invest exclusively in Treasuries and agency securities). The run on prime money market funds transmitted the panic directly to the commercial paper market, as the funds stopped purchasing new commercial paper.

The money market fund run demonstrated that the post-2008 reforms to money market fund regulation (which had introduced floating NAVs for institutional prime funds and allowed gates and fees on redemptions) had not eliminated the run risk. The structural vulnerability remained: prime money market funds held illiquid assets funded by instantly redeemable shares, and when confidence was lost, the run equilibrium dominated.

## The speed of the crisis

The most distinctive feature of the March 2020 panic was its speed. The GFC had unfolded over approximately eighteen months, from the first subprime tremors in early 2007 to the Lehman bankruptcy in September 2008. The COVID financial panic compressed an equivalent degree of market disruption into approximately four weeks. The acceleration reflected the speed of the pandemic itself (which went from a Chinese regional health emergency to a global pandemic in approximately two months), the speed of electronic markets (which transmitted price movements and margin calls instantaneously), and the interconnection of the modern financial system (which ensured that stress in any market was immediately felt in all markets).

The speed of the crisis placed enormous demands on the policy response. Policymakers had to diagnose the problem, design interventions, secure legal authority, and implement programs on a timeline measured in days rather than months. The ability of the Federal Reserve and the Treasury to respond as quickly as they did reflected the institutional learning from the GFC: the tools, the legal frameworks, and the institutional muscle memory from 2008-2009 allowed a much faster response in 2020.
