---
title: "13.6 Lessons: How COVID Changed Crisis Economics"
subtitle: What worked, what went too far, and what we learned
---

The COVID economic crisis and its aftermath contain lessons that both confirm and complicate the crisis management framework that had developed over the preceding century. Some lessons reinforced what had been learned from the GFC; others challenged the assumptions that had guided the GFC response; and several are genuinely new, reflecting the unique nature of the pandemic shock.

## What worked

**Speed of intervention.** The most important lesson of the COVID response was that speed matters enormously. The GFC response had been criticized as too slow: it took months for the TARP to be enacted and for the full suite of Fed facilities to be deployed. The COVID response compressed equivalent interventions into weeks. The speed was possible because the institutional frameworks, legal authorities, and operational capacities developed during the GFC were still in place and could be reactivated quickly. The lesson is that crisis infrastructure, the legal authorities, institutional arrangements, and operational procedures for emergency intervention, should be maintained and updated even during calm periods, so that it is available when needed.

**Pre-positioned lending facilities.** The Fed's ability to reactivate the CPFF, PDCF, TALF, and swap lines within days of the onset of the panic was critical. The facilities had been designed, tested, and refined during the GFC, and their reactivation required only a determination of "unusual and exigent circumstances" under Section 13(3) (which was subsequently modified by the Dodd-Frank Act to require Treasury approval). The pre-existing institutional capacity was the single most important factor in the speed of the response.

**Income replacement as crisis management.** The fiscal response demonstrated that direct income replacement (stimulus checks, enhanced unemployment insurance, PPP loans) could effectively prevent a liquidity crisis in the household sector from producing a cascade of defaults on mortgages, consumer loans, and rent payments. The absence of a household debt crisis during the pandemic, in contrast to the devastating household balance sheet recession that followed the GFC, reflected the effectiveness of the income replacement programs.

**European furlough schemes.** The European approach to labor market support, paying firms to retain workers rather than allowing mass layoffs and compensating workers through unemployment insurance, proved effective in preserving employment relationships and facilitating a faster labor market recovery. The furlough model is likely to become a standard element of the crisis policy toolkit in future recessions.

## What went too far

**The cumulative scale of fiscal support** appears, in retrospect, to have been excessive, particularly the American Rescue Plan of March 2021. The combination of approximately $5.3 trillion in fiscal support and approximately $4.6 trillion in Fed balance sheet expansion created monetary and fiscal conditions that were appropriate for a depression but were applied to an economy that was already recovering strongly. The excess demand that resulted was a significant contributor to the inflation surge.

The lesson is that the appropriate scale of crisis intervention depends not only on the severity of the initial shock but also on the economy's trajectory at the time the intervention is implemented. Programs designed when the outlook was extremely uncertain may be excessive by the time they are enacted, particularly if the economy is recovering faster than expected. The difficulty is that political and legislative constraints make it much easier to enact stimulus than to withdraw it.

**The "transitory" inflation misjudgment** reflected an analytical framework that was better suited to demand-driven business cycles than to supply-constrained pandemic recoveries. The Fed's models, which had been calibrated on decades of experience in which inflation was persistently below target, were slow to recognize that the combination of massive demand stimulus and persistent supply constraints could produce sustained inflation. The delay in beginning the tightening cycle may have allowed inflation expectations to drift upward, which made the eventual tightening more aggressive and more costly than it would have been with an earlier start.

## New lessons

**Exogenous shocks can produce financial crises even in a well-regulated financial system.** The post-GFC regulatory reforms had made the banking system more resilient, but the financial market panic of March 2020 demonstrated that extreme uncertainty can overwhelm even well-capitalized institutions. The dash for cash, the Treasury market disruption, and the money market fund run all occurred despite the higher capital requirements, liquidity requirements, and stress testing that had been implemented after the GFC. The lesson is that financial regulation can reduce the probability and severity of crises but cannot eliminate them.

**The boundary between monetary and fiscal policy has blurred.** The Fed's purchase of corporate bonds, its support for the municipal bond market, and its role in channeling fiscal funds through lending facilities (the CARES Act allocated $454 billion for the Fed to leverage into lending capacity) placed the Fed in a quasi-fiscal role that goes beyond traditional monetary policy. The blurring of boundaries raises questions about central bank independence, democratic accountability, and the appropriate division of labor between monetary and fiscal authorities.

**The trade-off between crisis prevention and inflation risk is real.** The GFC experience had led many policymakers to conclude that the risk of doing too little was greater than the risk of doing too much. The COVID experience demonstrated that doing too much also has costs: inflation that erodes real incomes, disproportionately harms lower-income households, and requires a painful tightening cycle to correct. The optimal crisis response must balance the risk of insufficient intervention (a prolonged depression) against the risk of excessive intervention (inflation and its consequences). The COVID experience suggests that the post-GFC consensus had swung too far toward the "do more" end of the spectrum.

**The pandemic exposed the fragility of global supply chains** and demonstrated that supply-side disruptions can produce macroeconomic outcomes (inflation, shortages, relative price volatility) that the post-GFC policy framework, which was focused on demand management, was not designed to address. The lesson is that crisis economics must incorporate supply-side considerations more systematically, particularly in a world where pandemics, climate events, geopolitical conflicts, and deglobalization can produce supply shocks of significant magnitude.
