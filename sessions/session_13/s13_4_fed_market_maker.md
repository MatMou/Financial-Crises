---
title: "13.4 Monetary Policy: The Fed as Market Maker of Last Resort"
subtitle: From lender of last resort to buyer of last resort
---

The Federal Reserve's response to the COVID crisis was even faster and broader than its response to the GFC. Within three weeks of the onset of the financial market panic, the Fed had cut the federal funds rate to zero, restarted quantitative easing on an open-ended basis, revived most of the emergency lending facilities from 2008-2009, and created several entirely new facilities. The speed and scope of the response reflected both the severity of the crisis and the institutional learning from the GFC: the Fed had the playbook, the legal frameworks, and the operational capacity to act immediately.

## The initial response: March 2020

The Fed's response unfolded with extraordinary speed. On March 3, 2020, the Fed cut the federal funds rate by 50 basis points (an emergency inter-meeting cut, the first since 2008). On March 15, the Fed cut rates to zero (0-0.25%), announced the purchase of at least $500 billion in Treasury securities and $200 billion in agency MBS, and reactivated the central bank liquidity swap lines with 14 foreign central banks. On March 17, the Fed revived the Commercial Paper Funding Facility (CPFF) and the Primary Dealer Credit Facility (PDCF), both of which had been created during the GFC.

Over the following two weeks, the Fed announced a series of additional facilities that went significantly beyond the GFC playbook. The **Primary Market Corporate Credit Facility** (PMCCF) and the **Secondary Market Corporate Credit Facility** (SMCCF) authorized the Fed to purchase corporate bonds, including newly issued bonds, directly from corporations and in the secondary market. This was a historic step: the Fed had never previously purchased corporate bonds, and the facilities represented a significant expansion of the Fed's role from lender of last resort to **market maker of last resort**.

The **Municipal Liquidity Facility** (MLF) authorized the Fed to purchase short-term municipal bonds, providing liquidity to state and local governments. The **Main Street Lending Program** (MSLP) authorized the Fed to make loans to medium-sized businesses. The **Term Asset-Backed Securities Loan Facility** (TALF) was revived to support the securitization market. And the **Money Market Mutual Fund Liquidity Facility** (MMLF) was created to provide liquidity to money market funds facing redemptions.

## The scale of intervention

The Fed's balance sheet expanded from approximately $4.2 trillion in early March 2020 to approximately $7.2 trillion by June 2020, an increase of $3 trillion in three months. (During the GFC, the equivalent expansion had taken approximately two years.) At the peak of its intervention in late March and early April, the Fed was purchasing approximately $75 billion per day in Treasury securities, an extraordinary rate that reflected the depth of the Treasury market disruption.

The corporate credit facilities were particularly significant in their market impact relative to their actual usage. The SMCCF ultimately purchased only approximately $14 billion in corporate bonds and bond ETFs, a small amount relative to the approximately $10 trillion corporate bond market. But the announcement of the facility was sufficient to stabilize corporate bond markets: investment-grade spreads narrowed from over 300 basis points to approximately 150 basis points within weeks of the announcement, and the high-yield market also recovered. The facilities worked primarily through the announcement effect: the knowledge that the Fed stood ready to purchase corporate bonds was sufficient to restore confidence, without the Fed needing to purchase very much.

## International dimension: the swap lines

The Fed's international swap lines, which had been established during the GFC and maintained on a standing basis with five major central banks (the ECB, the Bank of England, the Bank of Japan, the Bank of Canada, and the Swiss National Bank), were activated immediately and expanded to include nine additional central banks. The swap lines peaked at approximately $450 billion in outstanding drawings, somewhat below the GFC peak of $580 billion, partly because the post-GFC regulatory reforms had reduced the foreign banking system's reliance on short-term dollar funding.

The swap lines addressed the same dollar funding problem that had afflicted European and Asian banks during the GFC (subsection 10.5): non-U.S. banks that had funded dollar-denominated assets with short-term dollar borrowing faced a dollar liquidity squeeze when the wholesale funding markets froze. The swap lines allowed foreign central banks to provide dollar liquidity to their domestic banking systems, preventing the dollar funding squeeze from amplifying the crisis.

## Assessment

The Fed's COVID response was, by most assessments, highly effective in preventing the financial market panic from producing a financial crisis. The credit markets were stabilized within weeks. The Treasury market disruption was resolved. The money market fund run was halted. And the corporate sector was able to resume borrowing at manageable rates, preventing the liquidity crises that had destroyed firms during the GFC.

The response also raised important questions about the Fed's expanding role. The purchase of corporate bonds represented a significant step toward credit allocation, a function that is traditionally the province of the fiscal authority (Congress and the Treasury) rather than the central bank. The Fed's willingness to support specific credit markets created expectations that it would do so again in future crises, which could influence risk-taking in those markets (the moral hazard concern). And the massive expansion of the balance sheet, combined with the fiscal stimulus, created the monetary conditions that contributed to the subsequent inflation.
