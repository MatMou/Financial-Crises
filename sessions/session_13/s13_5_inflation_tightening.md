---
title: "13.5 Supply Chains, Inflation, and the Tightening Cycle"
subtitle: The hangover from the policy response
---

The most consequential aftereffect of the COVID crisis was the surge in inflation that began in 2021 and reached levels not seen since the early 1980s. U.S. CPI inflation peaked at approximately 9.1% in June 2022, the highest reading in over four decades. Euro-area inflation reached similar levels. The inflation surge forced central banks into the most aggressive tightening cycle since the Volcker era, which itself produced financial stress, including the failure of several U.S. banks in 2023. The inflation episode transformed the legacy of the COVID policy response from a success story into a cautionary tale about the risks of excessive stimulus.

## The supply chain disruptions

The pandemic disrupted global supply chains in ways that were unprecedented in the era of globalization. Factory closures in China and other Asian manufacturing centers interrupted the production of semiconductors, auto parts, electronics, and other intermediate goods. Shipping was disrupted by port closures, container shortages, and labor shortages. The cost of shipping a standard container from Shanghai to Los Angeles increased from approximately $2,000 before the pandemic to over $20,000 at the peak in late 2021.

The disruptions were amplified by the shift in consumer spending from services (which were constrained by social distancing) to goods (which consumers purchased in large quantities while homebound). The demand for goods surged at precisely the moment when the supply of goods was constrained, producing shortages and price increases across a wide range of products: automobiles, furniture, appliances, building materials, and electronics.

## The inflation debate: supply versus demand

The causes of the inflation surge became one of the most contested debates in macroeconomics. Three views emerged.

The **supply-side view** attributed the inflation primarily to supply disruptions: the pandemic, the supply chain bottlenecks, the energy price spike (amplified by Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022), and the labor supply constraints (as workers were slow to return to the labor force). In this view, the inflation was a transitory consequence of an unprecedented supply shock and would resolve as supply chains normalized and the pandemic receded. The policy implication was that aggressive monetary tightening was unnecessary and potentially harmful.

The **demand-side view**, associated with Summers and other critics of the American Rescue Plan, attributed the inflation primarily to excessive fiscal stimulus. The $5.3 trillion in fiscal support had pushed aggregate demand well above the economy's productive capacity, particularly given the supply constraints. The approximately $2.3 trillion in excess savings accumulated during the pandemic fueled a spending boom that the economy could not accommodate without inflation. In this view, the inflation was the predictable consequence of too much stimulus, and aggressive monetary tightening was necessary to restore price stability.

The **integrated view** argued that both supply and demand factors contributed, and that the interaction between them was more important than either alone. Supply disruptions raised prices in specific sectors (energy, used cars, food), but the broad-based fiscal support allowed those price increases to pass through to wages and to spread across the economy, producing a generalized inflation. Without the demand stimulus, the supply disruptions would have produced relative price changes (some prices rising, others falling) rather than a general increase in the price level. Without the supply disruptions, the demand stimulus might have produced higher output rather than higher prices.

The integrated view has gained the most support among economists, though the relative weights assigned to supply and demand factors vary across analyses and time periods. The early phase of the inflation (mid-2021) was predominantly supply-driven; the later phase (late 2021 through 2022) reflected the interaction of supply constraints with strong demand.

## The tightening cycle

The Federal Reserve was slow to recognize the persistence of the inflation, initially characterizing it as "transitory." The Fed began tapering its asset purchases in November 2021 and raised the federal funds rate for the first time in March 2022, from the zero lower bound to 0.25-0.50%. The subsequent tightening was the most aggressive since the Volcker era: the Fed raised rates at every meeting from March 2022 through July 2023, bringing the federal funds rate to 5.25-5.50%, an increase of approximately 525 basis points in sixteen months.

The ECB, the Bank of England, and most other major central banks implemented similarly aggressive tightening cycles, though with varying timing and magnitudes.

## The banking stress of 2023

The rapid increase in interest rates exposed vulnerabilities in institutions that had accumulated long-duration assets during the low-rate era. In March 2023, Silicon Valley Bank (SVB), the sixteenth-largest U.S. bank by assets, failed after suffering a run driven by the decline in the market value of its long-duration bond portfolio. SVB had invested heavily in Treasury bonds and agency MBS during 2020-2021 (when rates were near zero), and the subsequent rate increases had reduced the market value of those holdings by approximately $17 billion, exceeding the bank's total equity capital. When SVB attempted to raise capital to cover the losses, the announcement triggered a panic among its depositors (primarily technology companies with large uninsured deposits), and the bank experienced a classic Diamond-Dybvig run, with approximately $42 billion withdrawn in a single day.

SVB's failure was followed within days by the failure of Signature Bank and, in May 2023, by the failure of First Republic Bank. The FDIC, the Federal Reserve, and the Treasury intervened to guarantee all deposits (including uninsured deposits) at the failed banks and to create the **Bank Term Funding Program** (BTFP), which allowed banks to borrow against their bond holdings at par value rather than market value, preventing fire sales.

The 2023 banking stress was a direct consequence of the interaction between the COVID-era monetary policy (which had kept rates near zero and encouraged banks to invest in long-duration bonds) and the subsequent tightening (which reduced the value of those bonds). It illustrated that even "safe" assets (Treasury bonds) can create losses when interest rates change, and that the maturity mismatch vulnerability that had plagued the S&L industry in the 1980s (subsection 6.6) had not been eliminated.
