---
title: "13.1 The Pandemic as an Exogenous Shock"
subtitle: When the economy was switched off
---

The COVID-19 pandemic, caused by the novel coronavirus SARS-CoV-2, was first identified in Wuhan, China, in December 2019 and was declared a global pandemic by the World Health Organization on March 11, 2020. Within weeks, governments around the world implemented lockdowns, travel bans, and social distancing measures that shut down large portions of their economies. The economic impact was instantaneous and unprecedented: U.S. real GDP fell at an annualized rate of approximately 31% in the second quarter of 2020, the largest single-quarter decline since records began. Approximately 22 million American jobs were lost in March and April 2020.

## The nature of the shock

The COVID crisis was fundamentally different from every other crisis studied in this course. Previous crises had endogenous origins within the economic and financial system: speculative bubbles (the dot-com bubble, the housing bubble), financial fragility (the shadow banking system, the sovereign-bank nexus), or policy errors (the interwar gold standard, the Volcker shock). The COVID crisis was caused by a virus, an event entirely external to the economic system.

The distinction matters for several reasons. First, the crisis was not preceded by the buildup of financial imbalances that characterizes Minsky-type crises. The financial system entering the pandemic was, by most measures, better capitalized and less leveraged than it had been in 2007, thanks to the post-GFC regulatory reforms (subsection 11.4). The banking system was not the source of the crisis, though it would be affected by it. Second, the crisis did not require a loss of confidence or a panic to produce an economic collapse: the governments themselves ordered the shutdown of economic activity. The collapse was a deliberate policy choice, made to save lives, rather than an uncontrolled market process. Third, the nature of the shock implied that the economy could, in principle, recover rapidly once the virus was controlled: the productive capacity of the economy had not been destroyed, only temporarily suspended.

## Simultaneous supply and demand shock

The pandemic produced both a supply shock and a demand shock simultaneously, a combination that is unusual in macroeconomic experience and that created difficult analytical and policy challenges.

The **supply shock** came from the lockdowns themselves. When governments ordered businesses to close, workers to stay home, and borders to shut, the economy's ability to produce goods and services was directly curtailed. Manufacturing, construction, hospitality, retail, transportation, entertainment, and personal services were all severely affected. Global supply chains, which had been optimized for efficiency rather than resilience, were disrupted as factories in one country shut down, depriving factories in other countries of essential components.

The **demand shock** came from several sources. Consumer spending on services (restaurants, travel, entertainment) collapsed because consumers could not or would not engage in activities that required physical proximity. Consumer spending on goods initially fell but then recovered and surged (as homebound consumers shifted spending from services to goods, purchasing electronics, home improvement products, and exercise equipment). Business investment fell sharply as firms postponed capital spending in the face of extreme uncertainty. And the fall in income from job losses and reduced hours further depressed demand.

The combination of supply and demand shocks made the appropriate policy response ambiguous. A pure demand shock calls for fiscal and monetary stimulus to restore spending. A pure supply shock calls for patience (allowing the supply constraint to resolve) and possibly for monetary tightening (to prevent the supply disruption from generating inflation). The COVID shock was both, which meant that stimulus was needed to support incomes and prevent a financial crisis, but the supply constraints limited the economy's ability to respond to that stimulus with higher output rather than higher prices. This tension, which was not fully appreciated in 2020, would become acute in 2021-2022 when inflation surged.

## The initial economic impact

The speed of the economic collapse was without precedent. In the United States, initial unemployment claims surged from approximately 200,000 per week in early March 2020 to nearly 6.9 million per week by late March, a thirty-fold increase in two weeks. The unemployment rate rose from 3.5% in February 2020 to 14.7% in April 2020, the highest level since the Great Depression. The labor force participation rate fell sharply as millions of workers dropped out of the labor market entirely.

The impact was highly uneven across sectors and demographics. "Essential" workers in healthcare, food production, logistics, and utilities continued to work (often at great personal risk). Knowledge workers in finance, technology, and professional services shifted to remote work with relatively modest disruption. But workers in hospitality, retail, entertainment, and personal services, who were disproportionately lower-income, female, and from minority backgrounds, suffered massive job losses. The pandemic exacerbated pre-existing inequalities in ways that would have lasting political and social consequences.

Internationally, the impact was similarly devastating. Euro-area GDP fell by approximately 12% in the second quarter of 2020. India's GDP fell by approximately 24%. Only China, which had imposed the most aggressive lockdowns earliest, experienced a relatively rapid recovery, returning to positive GDP growth by the second quarter of 2020.
