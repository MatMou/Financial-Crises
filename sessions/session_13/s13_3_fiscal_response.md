---
title: "13.3 The Fiscal Response: Unprecedented Scale and Speed"
subtitle: Trillions in weeks
---

The fiscal response to the COVID crisis was the largest and fastest in history. In the United States, four major fiscal packages enacted between March 2020 and March 2021 provided approximately $5.3 trillion in support, roughly 25% of annual GDP. European governments implemented similarly large programs, though with different designs that reflected different institutional structures and policy philosophies. The scale of the response reflected the lessons of the GFC (subsection 11.3), where the stimulus had been widely regarded as too small, and the determination to prevent the pandemic from producing a prolonged depression.

## The CARES Act and subsequent U.S. legislation

The **Coronavirus Aid, Relief, and Economic Security Act** (CARES Act), signed on March 27, 2020, was the largest single fiscal stimulus in American history at approximately $2.2 trillion. Its major components included direct payments to individuals ($1,200 per adult, $500 per child), supplemental unemployment insurance ($600 per week on top of state benefits), the Paycheck Protection Program (PPP, forgivable loans to small businesses that maintained their payroll), aid to state and local governments, and $500 billion in loans and guarantees for large corporations and the Federal Reserve's lending facilities.

The CARES Act was followed by several additional packages: the Consolidated Appropriations Act of December 2020 ($900 billion, including a second round of stimulus checks and extended unemployment benefits), and the American Rescue Plan of March 2021 ($1.9 trillion, including a third round of stimulus checks, extended unemployment benefits, the expanded child tax credit, and aid to state and local governments).

The combined effect was to replace a large share of the income lost during the pandemic. Total personal income in the United States actually increased during 2020 despite the massive job losses, because the government transfer payments more than offset the decline in wages and salaries. The personal saving rate surged from approximately 8% before the pandemic to over 30% in April 2020, as households received transfer payments but had limited opportunities to spend (because of the lockdowns). The accumulated savings, estimated at approximately $2.3 trillion in "excess savings" above the pre-pandemic trend, would fuel the spending boom that contributed to the inflation surge of 2021-2022.

## European approaches: furlough and short-time work

European governments adopted a fundamentally different approach to labor market support. Rather than allowing mass layoffs and compensating workers through unemployment insurance (the American approach), most European countries implemented **furlough schemes** (also known as short-time work programs, building on Germany's Kurzarbeit model from the GFC) that paid firms to retain their workers on reduced hours or no hours. The government covered a large share of the workers' wages (typically 60-80%), and the employment relationship was maintained.

The furlough approach had several advantages. It preserved the match between workers and firms, which avoided the costly process of separation and rehiring. It maintained workers' attachment to the labor force, preventing the discouragement and skill atrophy that characterize long-term unemployment. And it facilitated a faster recovery: when the lockdowns were lifted, firms could simply increase their workers' hours rather than going through the process of recruiting and training new employees.

The European approach resulted in much smaller increases in measured unemployment. The euro-area unemployment rate increased by approximately 1.5 percentage points during the pandemic, compared to approximately 11 percentage points in the United States. The U.S. labor market recovered rapidly (unemployment fell from 14.7% to 6.7% by the end of 2020), but the European approach avoided the social disruption of mass layoffs and the administrative burden of processing millions of unemployment claims.

## The fiscal policy debate

The COVID fiscal response generated a sharp debate that anticipated the inflation that followed. The debate centered on two questions: was the stimulus too large, and was it appropriately targeted?

The **"too large" critique**, most prominently articulated by Lawrence Summers and Olivier Blanchard in early 2021, argued that the American Rescue Plan of March 2021 was excessive relative to the remaining output gap. By early 2021, the economy was already recovering strongly, vaccines were being deployed, and the output gap was narrowing. The $1.9 trillion American Rescue Plan, on top of the $3.1 trillion already enacted, would push total demand above the economy's productive capacity, particularly given the supply constraints created by the pandemic. The result, Summers warned, would be inflation.

The **"insurance" defense** argued that the risks were asymmetric: doing too little would risk a prolonged recession (as had happened after the GFC), while doing too much would produce temporary inflation that could be managed by monetary policy. Given the extreme uncertainty about the pandemic's trajectory in early 2021, erring on the side of too much stimulus was the prudent choice.

The subsequent inflation surge (subsection 13.5) vindicated the "too large" critique to a significant degree, though the relative contributions of demand stimulus and supply disruptions to the inflation remain debated.
