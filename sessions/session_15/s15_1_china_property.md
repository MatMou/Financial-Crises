---
title: "15.1 China's Property Crisis and the Evergrande Moment"
subtitle: The largest asset class in the world under stress
---

China's property sector crisis, which became acute in 2021 with the default of Evergrande and continued to deepen through 2024, is the most significant financial stability event of the post-pandemic period outside the United States. Chinese residential real estate is, by some estimates, the largest asset class in the world, with a total value of approximately $50-60 trillion, exceeding the entire U.S. stock market. The crisis in this sector has the potential to produce a balance sheet recession in China, to disrupt global commodity markets and trade flows, and to test the Chinese government's capacity for crisis management.

## The buildup

China's property boom was one of the most sustained in economic history. Housing construction grew at double-digit rates for most of the period from the late 1990s to the late 2010s. By the peak, property and related sectors (construction, steel, cement, furniture, appliances) accounted for approximately 25-30% of Chinese GDP, a level of dependence on real estate that far exceeded any comparable economy. Housing prices in major cities rose to levels that made Chinese urban real estate among the most expensive in the world relative to incomes: price-to-income ratios in Beijing, Shanghai, and Shenzhen exceeded 40, compared to approximately 5-10 in major U.S. and European cities.

The boom was driven by several reinforcing factors. Rapid urbanization (approximately 300 million people moved from rural to urban areas between 2000 and 2020) created genuine demand for housing. Limited alternative investment options for Chinese households (capital controls restricted overseas investment, bank deposit rates were low, the stock market was volatile) channeled savings disproportionately into real estate. Local government finances depended heavily on land sales (which accounted for approximately 30-40% of local government revenue), creating a powerful incentive for local officials to sustain the property boom. And the banking system, both formal and shadow, provided abundant credit to developers and homebuyers.

Chinese property developers operated on a distinctive **pre-sale model**: they sold apartments to buyers before construction was complete (sometimes before it had begun), using the pre-sale receipts to finance ongoing construction and new land purchases. The model created a form of leverage: developers were building with their customers' money, and the customers were exposed to the risk that the developer would fail before completing the project. The pre-sale model worked as long as prices were rising and developers could continue to sell new projects. When sales slowed, the model became a trap.

## The Evergrande default

Evergrande Group, China's second-largest property developer with approximately $300 billion in liabilities, became the symbol of the crisis. Evergrande had grown aggressively during the boom, accumulating enormous debt to finance land acquisition and construction across hundreds of projects in dozens of cities. By 2021, the company was unable to service its debts.

The immediate trigger was the Chinese government's introduction of the **"three red lines" policy** in August 2020, which imposed limits on developer leverage (measured by three financial ratios). The policy was designed to reduce the property sector's dependence on debt financing, but its timing, coming after decades of debt accumulation, forced highly leveraged developers to curtail new borrowing precisely when they needed it most. Evergrande, which violated all three red lines, found itself unable to refinance its maturing debt, unable to complete its pre-sold projects, and unable to pay its suppliers and bondholders.

Evergrande formally defaulted on its offshore bonds in December 2021. The default did not trigger an immediate systemic crisis (the Chinese government managed the process carefully, avoiding a disorderly collapse), but it revealed the depth of the problems in the property sector. Other major developers, including Country Garden, Sunac, and Kaisa, subsequently defaulted or restructured their debts. Housing sales plummeted: by 2023, new home sales were approximately 40-50% below their 2021 peak.

## The Minsky parallel and the balance sheet recession risk

The Chinese property crisis fits the Minsky framework (subsection 2.2) with precision. The long boom had moved the sector from hedge finance (developers whose cash flows comfortably covered their debt service) through speculative finance (developers who could cover interest but needed to refinance principal) to Ponzi finance (developers who needed new borrowing or asset sales to meet current obligations). The three red lines policy was the Minsky moment: the policy tightening that exposed the fragility that had accumulated during the boom.

The risk going forward is that China enters a **balance sheet recession** of the type analyzed in subsection 3.2. If household wealth is concentrated in property (estimates suggest that property accounts for approximately 60-70% of Chinese household wealth), and if property prices decline substantially, the wealth destruction could cause households to shift from consumption to saving, producing a prolonged demand deficiency. The parallel to Japan's lost decade, where a property and stock market crash in 1990 produced two decades of stagnation, is frequently drawn and is analytically apt, though the Chinese government's control over the banking system and its fiscal capacity give it tools for managing the crisis that the Japanese government lacked.

## Global implications

China's property crisis has global implications through several channels. China is the world's largest consumer of industrial commodities (copper, iron ore, aluminum, cement), and a prolonged construction downturn would depress global commodity markets, affecting commodity-exporting countries (Australia, Brazil, Chile, many African economies). China's demand for imports more broadly would decline, reducing export revenues for its trading partners. And if the crisis produced financial stress in China's banking system, the spillovers could affect global financial markets through confidence effects and through the direct exposures of foreign banks and investors to Chinese assets.

The Chinese government's management of the crisis has, so far, prioritized stability over speed: allowing a gradual deflation of the property sector rather than a sharp collapse, providing liquidity to the banking system, and implementing modest fiscal and monetary stimulus. Whether this gradualist approach will succeed in producing a soft landing, or whether the imbalances are too large to be managed without a more severe adjustment, remains the most important open question for the global economy.
