---
title: "15.6 The Next Crisis: What We Can and Cannot Predict"
subtitle: The only certainty is that there will be another one
---

The history of financial crises, from the Tulip Mania to the crypto crash of 2022, teaches a fundamental lesson: financial crises are a recurrent feature of capitalist economies, and they cannot be permanently prevented. The specific form of the next crisis is unpredictable (it will involve instruments, institutions, or mechanisms that are not currently at the center of attention, because if they were, the risk would be priced and the crisis would not occur). But the general conditions that make crises more likely, the structural vulnerabilities that theory and history have identified, can be monitored and, to some extent, mitigated.

## What we cannot predict

The specific trigger and form of the next crisis cannot be predicted. Every major crisis has been a surprise, not in the sense that no one saw it coming (there are always Cassandras), but in the sense that the timing, the mechanism, and the severity were not anticipated by the consensus. The subprime crisis was building in plain sight for years, but the consensus view until 2007 was that the risks were contained. The COVID pandemic was a possibility that epidemiologists had warned about for decades, but the timing and the specific virus were not predicted. The Eurozone crisis was anticipated by some economists (who had pointed out the design flaws of the monetary union), but the specific sequence of events was not foreseen.

The fundamental reason that crises are unpredictable is that financial systems are complex adaptive systems: the behavior of the system depends on the expectations and actions of millions of interacting agents, and the agents' expectations are influenced by the system's behavior, creating feedback loops that can produce abrupt, nonlinear transitions. Small perturbations can be amplified into system-wide crises through the mechanisms of leverage, interconnection, and self-fulfilling panic studied throughout this course. The timing and magnitude of these transitions depend on the precise configuration of conditions at the moment the perturbation occurs, which is inherently unknowable in advance.

## What we can predict

While the specific form of the next crisis cannot be predicted, the general conditions that make crises more likely can be identified. The history and theory examined in this course point to several recurring indicators.

**Rapid credit growth** is the single most reliable predictor of financial crises. The Jordà-Schularick-Taylor finding (subsection 3.1) that credit booms are the best single predictor of financial crises has been confirmed across multiple countries and historical periods. The BIS credit-to-GDP gap, which measures the deviation of the credit-to-GDP ratio from its long-term trend, has proven to be a useful early warning indicator.

**Asset price booms** that are disconnected from fundamental values (subsection 2.6) are associated with increased crisis risk, particularly when the boom is financed by credit (the combination of asset price appreciation and credit growth is more dangerous than either alone).

**Maturity and currency mismatches** in the financial system (subsections 2.4, 3.3, 9.5) create vulnerability to runs and sudden stops. The shadow banking system's reliance on short-term wholesale funding was the specific vulnerability that the GFC exploited.

**High leverage** amplifies shocks and reduces the margin for error. Leverage in the financial system (measured by bank capital ratios, hedge fund leverage, or the aggregate debt-to-GDP ratio) is a useful indicator of the system's vulnerability to adverse shocks.

**Complacency** is perhaps the most reliable indicator of all. Minsky's fundamental insight (subsection 2.2) is that stability breeds instability: prolonged periods of calm reduce risk aversion, encourage leverage, and lead to the dismantling of the regulatory safeguards that had been put in place after the previous crisis. When market participants, regulators, and policymakers believe that the risks are well-managed and that a crisis is unlikely, the conditions for the next crisis are ripening.

## Resilience over prediction

The impossibility of predicting the specific form of the next crisis implies that the most effective approach to crisis preparedness is to build **resilience** into the financial system: the capacity to absorb shocks and maintain essential functions regardless of the specific nature of the shock.

The elements of resilience include adequate capital buffers in the banking system (so that losses can be absorbed without threatening solvency), liquidity buffers (so that institutions can survive a temporary loss of market access), diversification of funding sources (so that the failure of any single funding market does not produce a systemic crisis), effective resolution mechanisms (so that failing institutions can be wound down without destabilizing the system), and macroeconomic policy space (so that governments and central banks can respond aggressively when a crisis occurs).

The history of financial crises suggests that these elements of resilience are most needed precisely when they appear least necessary: during the calm periods when the memory of the last crisis has faded and the pressure to reduce buffers, relax regulation, and pursue higher returns is strongest. The discipline to maintain resilience during good times is the most important, and the most difficult, lesson of financial history.
