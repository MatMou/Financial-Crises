title: "15.2 Climate-Related Financial Risk"
subtitle: The slow-moving crisis
---

Climate change presents a financial stability risk that is qualitatively different from the risks examined in earlier sessions. Most financial crises are relatively sudden events, unfolding over months or years, that arise from imbalances within the financial system. Climate-related financial risk is a slow-moving, long-horizon threat arising from changes in the physical environment and in the policy environment. The challenge for financial stability analysis is that the tools developed for analyzing traditional crises, which operate on short-to-medium time horizons and involve well-defined financial instruments, must be adapted to address risks that operate on multi-decade horizons and involve deep uncertainty about both the physical and policy trajectories.

## Three categories of climate-related financial risk

The standard taxonomy, developed by Mark Carney during his tenure as Governor of the Bank of England and subsequently adopted by the Task Force on Climate-Related Financial Disclosures (TCFD), distinguishes three categories.

**Physical risk** refers to the financial losses caused by the physical effects of climate change: extreme weather events (hurricanes, floods, wildfires, heatwaves), sea-level rise, droughts, and other environmental changes. Physical risk can cause direct damage to physical assets (real estate, infrastructure, agricultural land), disrupt supply chains and business operations, and reduce the productivity of agriculture and outdoor work. The insurance industry is the first point of contact for physical risk, and the increasing frequency and severity of insured losses from climate-related disasters (insured losses from natural catastrophes exceeded $100 billion per year in 2022 and 2023) is already affecting insurance availability and pricing.

**Transition risk** refers to the financial losses that could arise from the transition to a low-carbon economy. If governments implement ambitious climate policies (carbon pricing, emissions regulations, fossil fuel phase-outs), the value of carbon-intensive assets, fossil fuel reserves, power plants, industrial facilities, and the companies that own them, could decline sharply. The concept of **stranded assets** captures this risk: fossil fuel reserves that are currently booked as assets on corporate and sovereign balance sheets may become economically unburnable if climate policies tighten sufficiently, producing losses for the companies that own them and for the financial institutions that have lent to them.

**Liability risk** refers to the financial losses that could arise from climate-related litigation. If courts hold fossil fuel companies, or other entities, liable for the costs of climate change (analogous to the tobacco litigation of the 1990s), the resulting damages could be enormous. Climate litigation is already expanding rapidly, with cases filed against governments, fossil fuel companies, and financial institutions in multiple jurisdictions.

## The financial stability dimension

Climate-related financial risk becomes a financial stability concern if the losses are concentrated in the financial system, if they materialize abruptly, or if they interact with existing financial vulnerabilities to produce amplification effects.

A **disorderly transition** scenario illustrates the risk most clearly. If climate policies are implemented gradually and predictably, the financial system can adjust over time: carbon-intensive assets are repriced gradually, investment is redirected toward clean energy, and the losses are manageable. But if the transition is delayed and then implemented abruptly (because a climate tipping point is crossed, or because a political shock produces sudden policy change), the repricing could be sharp and concentrated, producing losses in the banking system and the asset management industry that could trigger the fire-sale and contagion dynamics familiar from earlier crises.

Central banks and financial regulators have increasingly incorporated climate risk into their supervisory frameworks. The Network for Greening the Financial System (NGFS), a coalition of central banks and supervisors established in 2017, has developed climate scenarios for stress testing and has advocated for climate-related disclosure requirements. The ECB, the Bank of England, and other central banks have conducted climate stress tests on their banking systems. The results have generally shown that the banking system's direct exposures to climate-sensitive sectors are manageable under orderly transition scenarios but could produce significant losses under disorderly scenarios.

## Analytical challenges

Climate-related financial risk poses analytical challenges that distinguish it from the risks in earlier sessions. The **time horizon** is long: the most severe physical effects of climate change will materialize over decades, while financial markets and financial regulation operate on much shorter horizons. The **uncertainty** is deep: the physical science of climate change involves genuine uncertainty about the sensitivity of the climate system to greenhouse gas concentrations, and the policy response involves uncertainty about the political trajectories of dozens of countries. And the **nonlinearity** of the climate system means that the relationship between current emissions and future risk is not smooth: tipping points (collapse of ice sheets, permafrost thawing, Amazon dieback) could produce abrupt and irreversible changes that would transform the risk landscape.

The integration of climate risk into financial stability analysis is one of the most important and most challenging frontiers in the field. The frameworks developed in this course, stress testing, scenario analysis, the identification of amplification mechanisms, the analysis of interconnection and concentration, are all relevant, but they must be adapted to address risks that are qualitatively different from the credit cycles, speculative bubbles, and institutional failures that have dominated the history of financial crises.
