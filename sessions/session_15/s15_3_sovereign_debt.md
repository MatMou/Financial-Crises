---
title: "15.3 Sovereign Debt Sustainability in the Post-Pandemic World"
subtitle: When r rises and g falls
---

The fiscal expansions of the COVID pandemic (subsection 13.3), coming on top of the debt accumulated during and after the GFC, have pushed government debt ratios in many countries to levels not seen since World War II. U.S. federal debt held by the public exceeded 95% of GDP by 2024. Japanese government debt exceeded 250% of GDP. European government debt ratios, which had been declining slowly since the Eurozone crisis, surged again during the pandemic. And emerging market debt, both in domestic and foreign currency, reached record levels.

## The arithmetic of debt sustainability

The sustainability of government debt depends on the relationship between the interest rate the government pays on its debt (r) and the growth rate of the economy (g). When r is less than g, the government can stabilize or reduce its debt ratio even while running a primary deficit (a deficit excluding interest payments), because the economy is growing faster than the debt is accumulating interest. When r exceeds g, the debt ratio will increase unless the government runs a primary surplus sufficient to offset the difference.

During the decade following the GFC, the r < g condition was satisfied in virtually all advanced economies: interest rates were at or near zero (thanks to central bank policy), while growth, though modest, was positive. The combination made the unprecedented debt ratios appear manageable. Blanchard's 2019 AEA presidential address (subsection 3.6) argued that if r < g were a persistent feature of the economic environment, the fiscal costs of debt were lower than traditionally assumed, and the case for deficit reduction was weaker.

The post-pandemic inflation and the aggressive monetary tightening that followed have changed the calculus. Policy interest rates in the United States rose from near zero to over 5% between 2022 and 2023. Long-term Treasury yields rose from approximately 1% to over 4%. The U.S. government's net interest payments, which had been approximately $350 billion per year in 2020, are projected to exceed $900 billion per year by 2025, surpassing defense spending. If growth slows (because of demographics, productivity stagnation, or the contractionary effects of fiscal consolidation), the r > g condition could persist, putting debt dynamics on an unsustainable trajectory.

## Advanced economy risks

The advanced economies face sovereign debt risks that differ in severity but share common features.

**The United States** benefits from the dollar's reserve currency status, which ensures continued demand for Treasury securities and allows the government to borrow in its own currency. But the fiscal trajectory is deteriorating: the Congressional Budget Office projects that deficits will average approximately 5-7% of GDP over the coming decade (driven by the aging of the population, rising healthcare costs, and debt service), pushing the debt ratio to approximately 120% of GDP by the mid-2030s. The political system has been unable to agree on either spending cuts or tax increases sufficient to stabilize the debt.

**Japan** has the highest debt ratio among the advanced economies but has been insulated by the fact that the vast majority of its debt is held domestically (by Japanese banks, insurance companies, and the Bank of Japan) and denominated in yen. The Bank of Japan's massive bond purchases (it holds approximately 50% of outstanding JGBs) have kept yields near zero. But Japan's demographic trajectory (a shrinking and aging population) poses a long-term threat: as the population ages, domestic savings will decline, reducing the domestic demand for government bonds and potentially requiring Japan to rely on foreign investors, who may demand higher yields.

**The Eurozone** faces a heterogeneous debt landscape. Germany and the Netherlands have relatively low debt ratios and fiscal space. France, Spain, and Belgium have elevated debt ratios and limited fiscal room. Italy, with a debt ratio of approximately 140% of GDP, chronic low growth, and a banking system that remains fragile, is the sovereign debt risk that most concerns European policymakers. The ECB's Transmission Protection Instrument (TPI), the successor to the OMT, provides a backstop against self-fulfilling spread crises (subsection 12.5), but it does not address the underlying fiscal sustainability question.

## Emerging market debt risks

The emerging market economies face sovereign debt risks that are, in many cases, more acute. Total emerging market government debt has approximately doubled since 2010, and the composition has shifted toward more risky forms: more foreign currency debt, more variable-rate debt, and more debt owed to a diverse set of creditors (including China, private bondholders, and multilateral institutions) that complicates restructuring.

The tightening of monetary policy in the advanced economies has increased the debt service burden for emerging market borrowers (through higher global interest rates), reduced capital flows to emerging markets (through the "risk-off" effect), and appreciated the dollar (increasing the domestic currency cost of dollar-denominated debt). Several low-income countries (Sri Lanka, Ghana, Zambia, Ethiopia) have already defaulted or entered debt restructuring.

The absence of an orderly sovereign debt restructuring mechanism, the same gap identified in subsection 6.5 and 12.1, continues to complicate the resolution of sovereign debt crises. The G-20 Common Framework for Debt Treatment, established in 2020 to facilitate restructuring for low-income countries, has proven slow and cumbersome, partly because of the difficulty of coordinating among the diverse set of creditors (including China, which has become the largest bilateral creditor to many developing countries).
