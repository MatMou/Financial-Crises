---
title: "15.5 Geopolitical Fragmentation and Financial Stability"
subtitle: When the plumbing of global finance becomes a weapon
---

The international financial system that has operated since the end of the Cold War is built on the assumption of open capital markets, integrated payment systems, and a dominant reserve currency (the dollar) that provides the infrastructure for global trade and finance. Geopolitical fragmentation, the process by which this integrated system is splitting along geopolitical fault lines, represents a structural risk to financial stability that has no close precedent in the post-Bretton Woods era.

## The weaponization of finance

The most dramatic manifestation of geopolitical fragmentation was the sanctions imposed on Russia following its invasion of Ukraine in February 2022. The sanctions included the freezing of approximately $300 billion in Russian central bank reserves held in Western financial institutions, the exclusion of major Russian banks from the SWIFT messaging system, and comprehensive restrictions on trade and investment with Russia. The sanctions were unprecedented in their scope (targeting a G-20 economy with deep integration into the global financial system) and in their speed (implemented within days of the invasion).

The sanctions demonstrated that the infrastructure of global finance, SWIFT, correspondent banking relationships, central bank reserve holdings, could be used as geopolitical weapons. This demonstration had profound implications for financial stability, because it altered the calculations of every country that might find itself on the wrong side of a future geopolitical conflict. If central bank reserves held in Western institutions could be frozen, then those reserves were not truly "safe." If access to SWIFT could be revoked, then payment systems that depended on SWIFT were not truly reliable.

## De-dollarization and reserve diversification

The weaponization of the dollar-based financial system has accelerated efforts by China, Russia, and other countries to reduce their dependence on the dollar. These efforts include the development of alternative payment systems (China's CIPS, Russia's SPFS), the promotion of trade settlement in non-dollar currencies (particularly the renminbi), the accumulation of gold reserves (central bank gold purchases reached record levels in 2022 and 2023), and bilateral currency swap agreements that bypass the dollar.

The de-dollarization process remains in its early stages. The dollar's share of global foreign exchange reserves has declined from approximately 70% in 2000 to approximately 58% in 2024, but the dollar remains overwhelmingly dominant in international trade invoicing (approximately 40% of global trade), in cross-border bank lending (approximately 50%), and in foreign exchange market turnover (approximately 88% of all transactions involve the dollar on one side). The depth, liquidity, and institutional infrastructure of dollar-denominated markets have no near-term substitute.

Nevertheless, the trend toward fragmentation has financial stability implications. If the international monetary system fragments into competing blocs (a dollar bloc, a renminbi bloc, and perhaps others), the resulting system would be less efficient (because of reduced economies of scale and network effects) and potentially less stable (because the transition between systems would involve uncertainty, adjustment costs, and the possibility of disruptive capital flows as countries reallocate their reserves).

## Financial decoupling

Beyond the monetary system, geopolitical fragmentation is producing a broader financial decoupling between the Western economies and China. Restrictions on technology transfer, investment screening, export controls on semiconductors and other strategic technologies, and the delisting of Chinese companies from U.S. stock exchanges are reducing the financial integration between the world's two largest economies.

Financial decoupling creates stability risks on both sides. For Western financial institutions, the loss of access to Chinese markets reduces diversification opportunities and could produce concentrated losses for institutions with large China exposures. For China, the reduction in foreign capital inflows constrains the financing available for investment and makes the domestic financial system more dependent on domestic savings. And for the global economy, the reduction in financial integration reduces the ability of capital to flow to its most productive uses, potentially reducing global growth.

## The historical parallel

The closest historical parallel to the current geopolitical fragmentation is the interwar period, when the integrated global economy of the pre-1914 era was replaced by a fragmented system of competing blocs, protectionist trade policies, and rival monetary arrangements. The fragmentation of the 1930s contributed to the severity of the Great Depression (subsection 5.3) and to the political instability that led to World War II. The architects of the Bretton Woods system designed it explicitly to prevent a repetition of this fragmentation (subsection 6.1).

The parallel should not be overstated: the current level of global economic integration is far greater than in the 1930s, and the institutional frameworks for managing international economic relations (the IMF, the WTO, the G-20) are more developed. But the direction of change, toward fragmentation and away from integration, is unmistakable, and the risks to financial stability are real.
