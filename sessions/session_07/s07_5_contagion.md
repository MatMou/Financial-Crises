title: "7.5 Contagion and the Architecture of Crisis Propagation"
subtitle: How crises spread across borders
---

One of the most striking features of the 1990s crisis sequence was the speed and breadth of contagion: crises that began in one country spread rapidly to countries that had little direct economic connection to the original crisis country. The Thai devaluation triggered crises in Indonesia, South Korea, Malaysia, the Philippines, and eventually Russia and Brazil. The Russian default triggered the LTCM collapse and a near-panic in global credit markets. Understanding the mechanisms of contagion is essential for assessing the systemic risks of the modern financial system and for designing policy responses that can contain crises before they spread.

## Defining contagion

The term "contagion" is used loosely in the crisis literature, and it is important to distinguish between different types. A useful classification, based on the work of several authors, identifies three categories.

**Fundamental contagion** (sometimes called "interdependence") occurs when a crisis in one country affects another through real economic linkages. If Country A and Country B are major trading partners, a recession in Country A reduces demand for Country B's exports, which depresses Country B's economy and may trigger a crisis. Similarly, if Country A devalues its currency, Country B's exports become less competitive, which puts pressure on Country B's economy and may force Country B to devalue as well (the "competitive devaluation" channel). Fundamental contagion is predictable and proportionate: the countries most affected are those with the strongest trade and financial linkages to the crisis country.

**Information contagion** (the "wake-up call" hypothesis) occurs when a crisis in one country causes investors to reassess the risks of other countries with similar characteristics. The Thai devaluation, for example, may have caused investors to "wake up" to the fact that other Asian countries shared Thailand's vulnerabilities (currency mismatches, maturity mismatches, weak financial supervision), leading them to reduce their exposure to the entire region. Information contagion is rational in the sense that the crisis in the original country provides genuine new information about the risks facing similar countries, but it can also be excessive if investors withdraw from countries that share superficial similarities but do not share the fundamental vulnerabilities.

**Pure contagion** (sometimes called "panic" or "irrational" contagion) occurs when a crisis in one country causes investors to withdraw from other countries for reasons that are unrelated to fundamentals or information. This can happen because of herding behavior (investors follow each other's actions without independent assessment), because of liquidity constraints (investors who suffer losses in one market are forced to sell assets in other markets to meet margin calls, a mechanism similar to the fire-sale dynamics of subsection 2.4), or because of changes in risk appetite (a crisis in one market causes investors to become more risk-averse across all markets). Pure contagion is the most destabilizing type because it can spread crises to countries with sound fundamentals.

## The common creditor channel

One of the most important contagion mechanisms in the 1990s crises was the **common creditor channel**, identified by Kaminsky and Reinhart (2000). This mechanism operates when two or more countries share a common set of creditors (typically international banks). When a crisis in one country causes losses for the common creditors, they reduce their exposure to other countries in the same asset class to reduce their overall risk or to meet capital requirements. The withdrawal of credit from the second set of countries can trigger a crisis there, even if those countries' fundamentals are sound.

The common creditor channel was particularly important in the Asian crisis. Japanese banks were major lenders to all of the Asian crisis countries, and their losses on Thai loans caused them to reduce lending to Indonesia, South Korea, and other countries in the region. European banks were major lenders to both Asian and Latin American countries, and their losses on Asian exposures caused them to reduce lending to Latin America, transmitting the crisis across continents. The common creditor channel explains why the Asian crisis spread to countries that had limited direct trade linkages with Thailand but shared a common set of international bank creditors.

## The role of leverage and portfolio rebalancing

The contagion from the Asian crisis to Russia and from Russia to LTCM and global credit markets illustrates the role of leverage and portfolio rebalancing in propagating crises.

When leveraged investors suffer losses in one market, they may be forced to sell assets in other markets to meet margin calls, raise cash, or reduce their overall risk exposure. These forced sales depress prices in markets that may have no direct connection to the original crisis, transmitting the shock across borders and across asset classes. The mechanism is particularly powerful when many leveraged investors hold similar positions (a phenomenon known as "crowded trades"), because the forced selling by one investor depresses prices, which triggers margin calls for other investors holding similar positions, which forces further selling, in a self-reinforcing downward spiral.

This mechanism explains the seemingly paradoxical transmission of the Russian default to U.S. and European credit markets. Russia's GDP was less than 2% of global GDP, and the direct financial exposure of Western institutions to Russia was relatively small. But the Russian default triggered a global reassessment of risk, which caused leveraged investors (including LTCM) to suffer losses, which forced them to sell assets across all markets, which depressed prices globally, which caused further losses, in a chain reaction that threatened the stability of the global financial system.

## Contagion and the IMF

The contagion dynamics of the 1990s crises had important implications for the role of the IMF. If crises spread primarily through fundamental channels (trade and financial linkages), then the appropriate response is to help the crisis country adjust, and the spillovers to other countries will be limited and manageable. But if crises spread through information or pure contagion channels, then the spillovers can be large and disproportionate, and the appropriate response may include measures to prevent the spread (such as providing precautionary credit lines to countries that are at risk of contagion, even if they have not yet experienced a crisis).

The IMF's response to the 1990s crises was primarily reactive: it provided lending to countries after they entered crisis, rather than preventively. The experience led to proposals for a more proactive approach, including the creation of precautionary lending facilities (the Contingent Credit Line, introduced in 1999, and later the Flexible Credit Line, introduced in 2009) that would provide pre-approved access to IMF resources for countries with sound fundamentals that were at risk of contagion. These facilities reflected the recognition that contagion could make innocent bystanders into crisis victims, and that the international community had a collective interest in preventing the spread of crises.
