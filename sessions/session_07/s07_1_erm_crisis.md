---
title: "7.1 The ERM Crisis of 1992"
subtitle: When Soros broke the Bank of England
---

The crisis of the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM) in September 1992 was a dramatic demonstration that fixed exchange rate regimes are vulnerable to speculative attack when capital is mobile and macroeconomic conditions diverge among participating countries. The crisis forced the United Kingdom and Italy out of the ERM, compelled devaluations by several other countries, and raised fundamental questions about the feasibility of European monetary integration. It also made George Soros famous and provided the clearest real-world illustration of the "impossible trinity" since the collapse of Bretton Woods.

## The background: European monetary integration

The ERM was established in 1979 as part of the European Monetary System (EMS), with the goal of creating a zone of exchange rate stability in Europe. Participating countries pegged their currencies to each other within narrow bands (initially plus or minus 2.25% for most currencies), with the Deutsche Mark serving as the de facto anchor. When a currency reached the edge of its band, the central banks of both countries were obligated to intervene in the foreign exchange market to maintain the peg.

During the 1980s, the ERM functioned reasonably well. The participating countries gradually converged on low inflation rates, anchored by Germany's anti-inflationary credibility. Capital controls, which were still in place during the early years of the ERM, provided a buffer against speculative pressure. The ERM was widely regarded as a success and as a stepping stone toward full monetary union, the eventual creation of the euro.

The critical change came in the late 1980s, when the European Community embarked on a program of financial liberalization that removed capital controls among member states. The Single European Act of 1986 and the subsequent Capital Movements Directive of 1988 required the complete liberalization of capital flows by July 1990. The removal of capital controls eliminated the buffer that had protected the ERM from speculative attack and made the system vulnerable to precisely the dynamics that the impossible trinity predicts: with free capital mobility and fixed exchange rates, participating countries lost the ability to pursue independent monetary policies.

## The trigger: German reunification

The shock that destabilized the ERM was German reunification. When East and West Germany were reunited in October 1990, the West German government undertook a massive fiscal expansion to finance the reconstruction of the East. The transfer payments to East Germany, the conversion of East German marks to Deutsche Marks at an overvalued rate, and the infrastructure investment required to modernize the East German economy produced a large fiscal deficit and a surge in domestic demand.

The Bundesbank, Germany's central bank, responded to the inflationary pressure from the fiscal expansion by raising interest rates. The German discount rate rose from 6% in late 1990 to 8.75% by mid-1992. This tightening was appropriate for Germany, which was experiencing above-trend growth and rising inflation. But it was catastrophically inappropriate for the other ERM members, particularly the United Kingdom and Italy, which were in recession and needed lower, not higher, interest rates.

Under the ERM, countries that pegged to the Deutsche Mark were forced to match German interest rates to maintain their exchange rate pegs. If the United Kingdom had lowered interest rates to stimulate its economy, capital would have flowed from London to Frankfurt (attracted by the higher German rates), the pound would have depreciated, and the UK would have been forced to intervene to defend the peg. The ERM thus forced the United Kingdom to maintain interest rates that were far too high for its domestic economic conditions, deepening the recession and increasing unemployment.

## Black Wednesday: September 16, 1992

By the summer of 1992, currency speculators, led most prominently by George Soros's Quantum Fund, had concluded that the United Kingdom would be unable to maintain its ERM peg. The logic was straightforward: the UK was in a deep recession, unemployment was rising, and the political cost of maintaining high interest rates to defend an exchange rate that was widely regarded as overvalued was becoming unsustainable. The rational expectation was that the UK government would eventually devalue or withdraw from the ERM, and this expectation made it profitable to bet against the pound.

The attack on sterling reached its climax on September 16, 1992, which became known as "Black Wednesday." The Bank of England spent approximately £27 billion of foreign exchange reserves buying pounds in a futile attempt to defend the peg. The government raised interest rates twice in a single day, from 10% to 12% and then to 15%, in a desperate signal of commitment to the peg. Neither the intervention nor the rate increases were sufficient. By the evening, the UK government announced its withdrawal from the ERM, and the pound depreciated by approximately 15% over the following weeks.

Italy was forced out of the ERM on the same day. Spain, Portugal, and Ireland devalued their currencies within the ERM. The French franc came under severe pressure but survived, partly because of joint Franco-German intervention and partly because France's macroeconomic fundamentals were stronger than Britain's. The ERM bands were widened to plus or minus 15% in August 1993, effectively turning the system into a managed float.

Soros's Quantum Fund reportedly earned approximately $1 billion from its short position against the pound, making it one of the most profitable currency trades in history. The episode demonstrated that private capital, when mobilized against a fixed exchange rate, could overwhelm even the resources of a major central bank.

## Lessons from the ERM crisis

The ERM crisis provided several lessons that would prove relevant to subsequent crises.

First, the crisis was a textbook illustration of the **impossible trinity**. The ERM attempted to combine fixed exchange rates and free capital mobility, which required the sacrifice of monetary policy independence. When German reunification created a divergence in monetary policy needs between Germany and the other members, the system could not accommodate the divergence, and the weakest pegs broke.

Second, the crisis demonstrated the power of **self-fulfilling speculative attacks** on fixed exchange rate regimes. The models of Obstfeld (1996) showed that a currency peg that would be sustainable in the absence of a speculative attack can be broken by a sufficiently large attack, because the government's defense of the peg (raising interest rates, spending reserves) imposes costs that the government may rationally decide are not worth bearing ({cite:t}`Obstfeld1996`). The attack itself creates the conditions under which abandoning the peg becomes the optimal policy, even if maintaining the peg would have been optimal in the absence of the attack. This self-fulfilling dynamic makes fixed exchange rates inherently vulnerable under free capital mobility.

Third, and paradoxically, the **aftermath of devaluation was benign**. The UK economy recovered strongly after leaving the ERM: interest rates fell, the depreciation stimulated exports, and GDP growth resumed. The experience suggested that the exchange rate peg had been a straitjacket that was constraining the economy, and that leaving it was beneficial. This lesson, that abandoning an unsustainable peg is better than defending it at enormous cost, would be relevant to the Asian crisis five years later, though it would be learned slowly and painfully.
