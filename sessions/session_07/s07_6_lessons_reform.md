---
title: "7.6 Lessons and the Reform Agenda"
subtitle: What the 1990s crises changed
---

The wave of crises that swept through the emerging markets during the 1990s produced a substantial body of lessons and a reform agenda that reshaped the international financial architecture. Some of the lessons were genuinely learned and led to durable changes in policy and institutional design. Others were acknowledged in principle but implemented only partially, and some were forgotten as the crisis receded and new priorities emerged. This subsection summarizes the principal lessons and assesses the extent to which they were absorbed.

## Exchange rate regimes: the bipolar view

The most immediate lesson from the 1990s crises was that intermediate exchange rate regimes, soft pegs, crawling bands, and managed floats that tried to combine the stability of fixed rates with the flexibility of floating rates, were inherently vulnerable to speculative attack in a world of free capital mobility. The ERM crisis (subsection 7.1), the Mexican crisis (subsection 7.2), and the Asian crisis (subsection 7.3) all involved the collapse of intermediate regimes.

This observation led to the "bipolar" or "two-corner" view of exchange rate regimes: countries should choose either a hard peg (a currency board or monetary union, in which the commitment to the fixed rate is credible because it is institutionally embedded) or a free float (in which the exchange rate adjusts continuously to market conditions). Intermediate regimes, which lacked the credibility of a hard peg and the flexibility of a float, were deemed unsustainable.

The bipolar view was influential in the late 1990s and early 2000s, and many emerging market countries did move toward greater exchange rate flexibility. However, the practical reality was more nuanced than the bipolar prescription suggested. Few countries adopted pure free floats; most adopted managed floats in which the central bank intervened periodically to smooth excessive volatility. And some countries that adopted hard pegs (Argentina's currency board, which pegged the peso one-to-one to the dollar) discovered that the hard peg created its own vulnerabilities when the exchange rate became misaligned, as Argentina's devastating crisis of 2001-2002 would demonstrate.

## Reserve accumulation as self-insurance

The most dramatic behavioral change among emerging market countries after the 1990s crises was the massive accumulation of foreign exchange reserves. Countries that had experienced the humiliation of depleted reserves and IMF conditionality resolved never to be in that position again. China, South Korea, Taiwan, India, and virtually all of the emerging Asian economies built enormous reserve buffers during the 2000s. Global foreign exchange reserves increased from approximately $1.5 trillion in 1995 to over $10 trillion by 2012.

The reserve accumulation served as a form of self-insurance against sudden stops. By holding large reserves, central banks could intervene in foreign exchange markets to support their currencies during periods of capital outflow, providing time for orderly adjustment rather than being forced into the crisis-devaluation-IMF-austerity sequence that the Asian countries had experienced. The accumulation was effective in this regard: the emerging market economies weathered the 2007-2009 global financial crisis much better than the 1997-1998 crisis, and the reserves were widely credited with providing the buffer that made this possible.

However, reserve accumulation also had costs. Holding reserves meant investing national savings in low-yielding U.S. Treasury bonds rather than in higher-yielding domestic investment, which represented an implicit transfer from emerging market economies to the United States. The accumulation also contributed to the global savings glut that, in the analysis of Ben Bernanke and others, helped push down long-term interest rates in the advanced economies and contributed to the credit boom that preceded the 2007-2009 crisis.

## Capital account management

The 1990s crises reinvigorated the debate over capital account liberalization that had been largely settled (in favor of liberalization) during the 1980s and early 1990s. The Asian crisis demonstrated that the benefits of capital account openness (access to foreign savings, diversification, technology transfer) could be overwhelmed by the costs (vulnerability to sudden stops, boom-bust cycles, loss of monetary policy autonomy).

The debate evolved slowly. The IMF, which had been a strong advocate of capital account liberalization in the early 1990s, gradually moved toward a more nuanced position. The 2012 Institutional View on Capital Flows acknowledged that capital flows carry risks as well as benefits, that countries may use capital flow management measures as part of their policy toolkit, and that the appropriate degree of openness depends on the country's level of institutional and financial development. This was a significant shift from the pre-crisis consensus, though it fell short of endorsing the systematic use of capital controls.

## IMF reform

The experience of the 1990s crises led to significant reforms within the IMF. The most important changes included the development of new lending instruments (including precautionary facilities for countries at risk of contagion), greater flexibility in conditionality (moving away from the one-size-fits-all structural adjustment programs that had been criticized during the Asian crisis), increased transparency (the publication of Article IV reports and other surveillance documents), and a greater emphasis on financial sector surveillance (including the Financial Sector Assessment Program, FSAP, jointly with the World Bank).

The IMF also increased the resources available for crisis lending, most dramatically through the quota increases and New Arrangements to Borrow that were expanded during the 2007-2009 crisis. The creation of the Flexible Credit Line in 2009 and the Precautionary and Liquidity Line in 2011 represented an attempt to provide pre-approved access to IMF resources for countries with sound policies, reducing the stigma associated with IMF borrowing and making the Fund more effective as a crisis-prevention (rather than just crisis-response) institution.

## What was not learned

Despite the reforms, several important lessons from the 1990s crises were not fully absorbed. The danger of excessive leverage in the financial system, dramatically illustrated by LTCM, was acknowledged but not effectively addressed through regulation. The interconnection risks created by the concentration of derivatives trading among a small number of large institutions were identified but not mitigated. The tendency of risk models to underestimate tail risks was documented but not corrected.

These unlearned lessons would prove enormously costly during the global financial crisis of 2007-2009, when the same dynamics that had characterized the LTCM crisis, excessive leverage, model failure, interconnection risk, and fire-sale contagion, reappeared on a vastly larger scale. The LTCM episode, which could have served as an early warning of the fragility of the modern financial system, was instead treated as an isolated incident that had been successfully managed, rather than as a harbinger of the systemic risks that were accumulating in the global financial system.

The 1990s crises thus occupy a pivotal position in the history of financial crises. They generated important insights and reforms, many of which made the emerging market economies more resilient. But they also generated complacency in the advanced economies, where the crises were perceived as emerging market problems that could not happen "here." The global financial crisis of 2007-2009 would shatter that complacency.
