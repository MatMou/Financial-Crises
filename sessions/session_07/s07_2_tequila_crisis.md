---
title: "7.2 The Mexican Tequila Crisis (1994-1995)"
subtitle: Tesobonos, political shocks, and the first post-Cold War bailout
---

The Mexican crisis of December 1994, known as the "Tequila crisis," was the first major emerging market crisis of the post-Cold War era. It demonstrated the speed and violence with which capital flows could reverse in an economy that had recently liberalized its financial markets, and it established many of the patterns that would recur in the Asian crisis three years later: a fixed exchange rate, large capital inflows, the accumulation of short-term foreign-currency-denominated debt, and a sudden loss of confidence that triggered a devastating crash.

## The Mexican miracle and its vulnerabilities

Mexico had undergone a dramatic economic transformation during the late 1980s and early 1990s under Presidents Miguel de la Madrid and Carlos Salinas de Gortari. The reforms included trade liberalization (culminating in the North American Free Trade Agreement, NAFTA, which took effect on January 1, 1994), privatization of state-owned enterprises, fiscal consolidation, and the opening of the capital account. Mexico was celebrated as the poster child of the "Washington Consensus": a developing country that was following the prescribed recipe of market-oriented reform and was being rewarded with rapid growth and abundant foreign capital.

Capital flowed into Mexico in enormous quantities. Between 1990 and 1993, Mexico received approximately $90 billion in net capital inflows, much of it in the form of portfolio investment (purchases of Mexican stocks and bonds by foreign investors) rather than foreign direct investment. The inflows financed a widening current account deficit, which reached approximately 7% of GDP in 1994, and fueled a credit boom and a real estate bubble.

The vulnerabilities accumulated beneath the surface of apparent prosperity. The peso was pegged to the dollar within a narrow band (a crawling peg that allowed gradual depreciation), but the real exchange rate had appreciated significantly as Mexican inflation exceeded U.S. inflation, eroding export competitiveness. The current account deficit was large and growing. And, most dangerously, the Mexican government had been financing its deficit by issuing **tesobonos**, short-term government bonds denominated in pesos but indexed to the dollar. By December 1994, approximately $29 billion in tesobonos were outstanding, far exceeding Mexico's foreign exchange reserves of approximately $6 billion.

The tesobonos were a particularly dangerous instrument. They appeared to protect investors from exchange rate risk (because the payout was indexed to the dollar), which made them attractive to foreign investors and allowed the government to borrow at lower interest rates than it would have paid on ordinary peso-denominated debt. But from the government's perspective, tesobonos were effectively dollar-denominated obligations: if the peso were devalued, the peso cost of servicing the tesobonos would increase proportionally. The tesobonos thus concentrated the exchange rate risk in the government's balance sheet, a classic case of the currency mismatch problem described in subsection 3.3.

## The political shocks of 1994

A series of political shocks eroded investor confidence during 1994. In January, the Zapatista uprising in Chiapas raised concerns about political stability. In March, Luis Donaldo Colosio, the ruling party's presidential candidate and presumptive successor to Salinas, was assassinated. In September, Jose Francisco Ruiz Massieu, the secretary-general of the ruling party, was also assassinated. Each shock triggered capital outflows, which the Bank of Mexico attempted to offset by selling foreign exchange reserves and issuing more tesobonos.

The Bank of Mexico's strategy was to **sterilize** the capital outflows: it sold reserves to defend the exchange rate while simultaneously expanding domestic credit to prevent the reserve loss from tightening monetary conditions. This sterilization maintained the appearance of stability but was unsustainable, because it steadily depleted reserves while the stock of short-term dollar-linked obligations continued to grow. By the time the crisis broke, Mexico's reserves were a fraction of its short-term obligations, and the government was in the position of a bank facing a run with insufficient liquid assets.

## The devaluation and its aftermath

On December 20, 1994, just three weeks after the inauguration of President Ernesto Zedillo, the government widened the peso band by 15%, effectively devaluing. The devaluation was intended to be modest and controlled, but it destroyed confidence: investors who had been told repeatedly that the exchange rate was sustainable concluded that the government had been deceiving them, and the rush to exit accelerated. Within days, the government was forced to float the peso, which depreciated by approximately 50% by March 1995. The tesobonos, which were due for rollover, could not be refinanced, and Mexico faced the prospect of sovereign default.

The crisis was resolved through a massive international rescue package. The United States, under President Clinton, provided approximately $20 billion from the Exchange Stabilization Fund (bypassing Congress, which had refused to approve the rescue). The IMF contributed $17.8 billion, the largest loan in its history at the time. The Bank for International Settlements and Canada provided additional support. The total package was approximately $50 billion.

The rescue prevented a sovereign default but did not prevent a severe recession. Mexican GDP fell by approximately 6.2% in 1995, inflation surged to over 50%, and interest rates reached 80%. The banking system, which had expanded rapidly during the boom, suffered a wave of non-performing loans that required a government-funded rescue estimated at approximately 20% of GDP. Real wages fell sharply, and the social costs were enormous: poverty rates increased significantly, and the middle class was devastated.

The recovery, when it came, was surprisingly rapid. Mexico's proximity to the booming U.S. economy, the competitive exchange rate, and the structural reforms of the preceding years all contributed to a strong export-led recovery beginning in 1996. Mexico repaid its emergency loans ahead of schedule, and GDP surpassed its pre-crisis level by 1997.

## Lessons

The Tequila crisis established several lessons that would prove relevant to the Asian crisis. The danger of currency mismatches (tesobonos indexing effectively created dollar-denominated debt), the vulnerability of fixed exchange rates to capital flow reversals, the futility of sterilized intervention when reserves are declining, and the speed with which confidence can collapse in an economy dependent on short-term foreign capital were all demonstrated clearly in Mexico.

The crisis also raised important questions about the role of the IMF and the United States in rescuing countries from financial crises. Critics argued that the rescue created moral hazard: if investors believed that the United States and the IMF would bail out countries (and their creditors) in the event of a crisis, they would take greater risks, making future crises more likely. This moral hazard debate would intensify after the Asian crisis and remains unresolved.
