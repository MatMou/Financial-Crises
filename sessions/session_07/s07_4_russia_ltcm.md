---
title: "7.4 Russia and LTCM (1998)"
subtitle: When the periphery struck the core
---

The Russian default of August 1998 and the near-collapse of Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) in September 1998 represented the climax of the 1990s crisis sequence. The Russian crisis was in some respects a conventional emerging market crisis: a country with weak institutions, an unsustainable fiscal position, and a fixed exchange rate was overwhelmed by the reversal of capital flows that followed the Asian crisis. But its consequences reached far beyond Russia, because the default triggered a global flight to safety that exposed the enormous leverage embedded in the balance sheets of hedge funds and investment banks in the advanced economies, most dramatically in the case of LTCM.

## The Russian crisis

Russia's transition from a planned to a market economy during the 1990s was chaotic and painful. GDP had fallen by approximately 40% between 1991 and 1996. Privatization had transferred state assets to a small group of "oligarchs" through opaque and often corrupt processes. Tax collection was weak, and the government ran persistent fiscal deficits financed by issuing short-term ruble-denominated government bonds (GKOs) at very high interest rates.

The ruble was managed through a crawling band against the dollar, and the Central Bank of Russia maintained the band by selling foreign exchange reserves and keeping interest rates high. The high GKO yields attracted foreign investors, who purchased the bonds in large quantities. By early 1998, approximately one-third of outstanding GKOs were held by foreign investors, creating a vulnerability similar to Mexico's tesobonos: a large stock of short-term government debt held by foreign investors who could exit quickly.

The Asian crisis raised doubts about emerging markets generally and caused a widening of risk spreads across all emerging market assets. Oil prices, which had been declining since late 1997 (partly because of reduced Asian demand), fell to approximately $10 per barrel in late 1998, devastating Russia's fiscal position (oil and gas revenues accounted for a large share of government income). Capital outflows accelerated, and the Central Bank spent heavily to defend the ruble.

On August 17, 1998, Russia simultaneously devalued the ruble, declared a moratorium on servicing its GKO debt, and imposed a 90-day moratorium on foreign debt payments by Russian banks. The announcement was shocking because it combined a currency devaluation with a sovereign default, and because the default was on domestic-currency debt (GKOs), which a government with a printing press should, in theory, always be able to service by printing money. The decision to default rather than inflate reflected the government's judgment that hyperinflation would be even more destructive than default, a judgment that was probably correct given Russia's hyperinflationary experience in the early 1990s.

## The LTCM crisis

Long-Term Capital Management was a hedge fund founded in 1994 by John Meriwether, a legendary bond trader from Salomon Brothers, with a team that included Myron Scholes and Robert Merton, who had won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1997 for their work on options pricing theory. LTCM's strategy was "convergence trading": identifying pairs of securities that were mispriced relative to each other and taking leveraged positions that would profit when the prices converged. The fund bet that the spreads between risky and safe assets, between on-the-run and off-the-run Treasury bonds, between sovereign bonds of different countries, and between corporate and government bonds, would narrow over time.

The strategy required enormous leverage because the mispricings were small. LTCM's capital was approximately $4.7 billion, but its total assets were approximately $125 billion (a leverage ratio of approximately 25:1), and its notional derivatives exposure was approximately $1.25 trillion. The fund's risk models, based on historical correlations, indicated that the portfolio was well-diversified and that the probability of large losses was extremely small.

The Russian default destroyed these calculations. The default triggered a global flight to safety: investors sold risky assets and bought safe assets (primarily U.S. Treasury bonds), causing the spreads that LTCM was betting would narrow to widen dramatically instead. The fund lost approximately $4.6 billion in a few weeks, effectively wiping out its entire capital. Because of its enormous leverage, the fund's losses threatened to cascade through the financial system: LTCM's counterparties (the major investment banks and commercial banks that had lent to LTCM and traded derivatives with it) faced the prospect of being unable to unwind their positions without causing a fire sale that would depress prices further and impose losses on other institutions.

## The rescue

The Federal Reserve Bank of New York, under President William McDonough, organized a rescue of LTCM in September 1998. The rescue was not a government bailout in the conventional sense: no public funds were used. Instead, the Fed convened a meeting of LTCM's 14 major creditors and counterparties (including Goldman Sachs, Merrill Lynch, J.P. Morgan, Morgan Stanley, and others) and persuaded them to inject $3.6 billion of new capital into the fund in exchange for a 90% ownership stake. The fund was then wound down in an orderly fashion over the following year.

The Fed's intervention was justified on the grounds that an uncontrolled collapse of LTCM could trigger a systemic crisis. The fund's positions were so large relative to the markets in which it traded that forced liquidation would have depressed prices, imposing losses on other leveraged institutions, which would have been forced to sell their own positions, depressing prices further, in a self-reinforcing downward spiral. The fire-sale externality (subsection 2.4) was the key mechanism: the social cost of LTCM's failure would have far exceeded the private cost, because the failure would have destabilized the broader financial system.

The LTCM episode demonstrated several important points. First, extreme leverage in the financial core could amplify shocks originating in the periphery to a degree that threatened global financial stability. The Russian default, which involved a relatively small economy, produced losses that nearly brought down a major financial institution in the world's most advanced economy. Second, the risk models that LTCM relied on were fundamentally flawed: they assumed that historical correlations would persist in the future, but the Russian default was a "fat tail" event that the models assigned near-zero probability. Third, the episode highlighted the systemic risks created by the interconnections among large financial institutions: LTCM's counterparties were the same banks that were expected to manage the fallout from LTCM's failure, creating a circular dependency that could only be resolved by coordination.

The LTCM crisis was a warning of the dangers of excessive leverage in the financial system, a warning that was largely ignored in the years that followed. The leverage ratios, risk management failures, and interconnection risks that characterized LTCM would reappear on a vastly larger scale in the global financial crisis of 2007-2009.
