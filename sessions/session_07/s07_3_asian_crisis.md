---
title: "7.3 The Asian Financial Crisis (1997-1998)"
subtitle: The end of the Asian miracle
---

The Asian financial crisis of 1997-1998 was the most severe emerging market crisis since the Latin American debt crisis of the 1980s, and it remains the defining case study for understanding capital account crises in developing countries. The crisis struck economies that had been among the fastest-growing in the world, the "Asian tigers" and "miracle economies" whose success had been widely attributed to sound economic management. The speed and severity of the crisis, and the fact that it hit countries that had been held up as models of development, shattered many assumptions about the relationship between economic fundamentals and financial stability.

## The Asian miracle and its hidden fragilities

The countries most severely affected by the crisis, Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea, Malaysia, and the Philippines, had experienced decades of rapid growth driven by high savings and investment rates, export-oriented industrialization, and macroeconomic policies that combined fiscal discipline with state-directed credit allocation. GDP growth rates of 7-10% per year were common during the 1980s and early 1990s. Inflation was generally low, fiscal positions were balanced or in surplus, and external debt was moderate relative to exports.

These favorable headline indicators masked several structural vulnerabilities. First, the financial systems of the crisis countries were underdeveloped relative to the capital flows they were intermediating. Banks were poorly supervised, connected lending (loans to companies affiliated with the bank's owners) was widespread, and risk management was rudimentary. Second, the exchange rates were pegged or closely managed against the U.S. dollar, which had appreciated significantly against the yen during 1995-1997. The dollar peg reduced the perceived exchange rate risk for borrowers, encouraging them to borrow in foreign currencies (primarily dollars and yen) without hedging. Third, the liberalization of capital accounts during the 1990s had allowed a surge of short-term foreign borrowing by banks and corporations, much of it unhedged and of short maturity.

The result was a classic "twin mismatch" problem: the private sector had borrowed short-term in foreign currency to fund long-term investments in domestic currency. This maturity mismatch and currency mismatch made the crisis countries vulnerable to any shock that raised doubts about their ability to maintain their exchange rate pegs.

## The Thai devaluation: July 1997

The crisis began in Thailand, where the vulnerabilities were most acute. Thailand's current account deficit had reached approximately 8% of GDP in 1996, financed by short-term capital inflows. The Thai baht was pegged to a basket dominated by the U.S. dollar. A property bubble had developed in Bangkok, fueled by easy credit from finance companies (non-bank financial institutions that were poorly regulated). When the property market began to collapse in 1996, the finance companies suffered enormous losses, and their difficulties spread to the banking system.

Speculative pressure on the baht intensified through the first half of 1997. The Bank of Thailand spent heavily to defend the peg, depleting its foreign exchange reserves. Critically, the Bank also sold forward contracts (promises to sell dollars for baht at a fixed rate in the future), which further depleted its effective reserves without appearing on the official reserve figures. By June 1997, the Bank's usable reserves were essentially exhausted, though this was not publicly known.

On July 2, 1997, Thailand was forced to float the baht. The currency depreciated rapidly, falling from approximately 25 baht per dollar to 56 baht per dollar by January 1998, a depreciation of more than 50%. The devaluation triggered the currency mismatch effects described in subsection 3.3: the domestic currency value of foreign-denominated debt roughly doubled, devastating the balance sheets of Thai banks and corporations that had borrowed in dollars.

## The spread of the crisis

The Thai devaluation set off a chain of crises across East Asia. The transmission was rapid and devastating.

**Indonesia** was hit hardest. The Indonesian rupiah, which had been trading at approximately 2,400 per dollar before the crisis, collapsed to approximately 17,000 per dollar by January 1998, a depreciation of approximately 85%. The currency collapse was amplified by a bank run (as depositors withdrew funds from private banks and moved them to state-owned banks or abroad), by capital flight, and by the political crisis that engulfed the Suharto regime. GDP contracted by approximately 13% in 1998, the banking system was effectively destroyed, and the social consequences were catastrophic: poverty rates roughly doubled, and the country experienced food shortages and ethnic violence.

**South Korea** experienced a different variant of the crisis. Korean banks and corporations (the chaebol conglomerates) had borrowed heavily in short-term dollar-denominated loans from international banks. When international banks refused to roll over their loans in late 1997, Korea faced a classic sudden stop. The won depreciated from approximately 900 per dollar to approximately 1,700 per dollar. Several chaebol went bankrupt, the banking system required massive recapitalization, and GDP contracted by approximately 5.8% in 1998.

**Malaysia** was also severely affected, but its response was distinctive. Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad blamed the crisis on speculative attacks by Western hedge funds (singling out Soros by name) and, in September 1998, imposed capital controls, including a fixed exchange rate and restrictions on the repatriation of portfolio investment. The capital controls were widely condemned by the international financial community at the time, but Malaysia's recovery was comparable to those of other crisis countries, and the controls were gradually relaxed without triggering a renewed crisis. The Malaysian experience reinvigorated the debate over whether capital controls could be a legitimate policy tool, a debate that continued until the IMF's 2012 Institutional View effectively endorsed their use in certain circumstances.

## The IMF response and the controversy

The IMF played a central role in the crisis response, providing large loan packages to Thailand ($17.2 billion), Indonesia ($43 billion), and South Korea ($57 billion). The loans were conditional on the implementation of structural reforms, including fiscal austerity, high interest rates to defend the currency, closure of insolvent financial institutions, and structural reforms (including the liberalization of foreign ownership restrictions and the reform of corporate governance).

The IMF's conditionality was fiercely criticized. The fiscal austerity was criticized as inappropriate for countries that had been running budget surpluses before the crisis: the fiscal position was not the problem, and fiscal tightening during a capital account crisis deepened the recession without addressing the underlying cause. The high interest rate policy was criticized as counterproductive: high rates were intended to defend the currency and attract capital, but they also bankrupted domestic firms and banks, which worsened the crisis. The structural reforms were criticized as overreaching: they went far beyond what was necessary to address the immediate crisis and were seen as an attempt to impose a particular model of capitalism (the Anglo-American model of open markets and liberal corporate governance) on countries with very different institutional traditions.

The criticism had a lasting effect on the IMF's approach to crisis management. In subsequent crises, the IMF adopted a more flexible approach to conditionality, was more willing to accept capital controls as a legitimate policy tool, and was more cautious about imposing structural reforms that were not directly related to the crisis. The Asian crisis was, in this sense, a learning experience for the international financial institutions as much as for the crisis countries themselves.

## Two views of the crisis

The academic debate over the causes of the Asian crisis crystallized into two competing views.

The **fundamentals view** argued that the crisis was caused by structural weaknesses in the crisis countries: poorly supervised financial systems, connected lending, crony capitalism, weak corporate governance, and implicit government guarantees that encouraged excessive risk-taking. In this view, the crisis was a necessary correction of deep-seated distortions, and the appropriate response was structural reform to address those distortions.

The **panic view**, associated with Radelet and Sachs (1998), argued that the crisis was primarily a self-fulfilling capital account crisis, analogous to a bank run. In this view, the crisis countries had vulnerabilities (particularly the twin mismatches), but the vulnerabilities were not severe enough to produce a crisis in the absence of a panic. The panic, once it started, was self-fulfilling: the rush to exit depressed exchange rates and asset prices, which worsened balance sheets, which justified the initial panic. The appropriate response, in this view, was liquidity provision and capital flow management, not austerity and structural reform.

The truth almost certainly lies between the two views. The crisis countries had genuine structural weaknesses that made them vulnerable, but the severity and speed of the crisis went far beyond what those weaknesses alone could explain. The self-fulfilling dynamic, in which the crisis created the conditions that justified the crisis, was clearly at work, particularly in Indonesia and South Korea. The lesson is that financial crises in emerging markets are typically the product of both fundamental vulnerabilities and self-fulfilling panic, and that policy responses must address both.
