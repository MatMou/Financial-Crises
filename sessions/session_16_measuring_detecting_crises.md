The preceding sessions have examined the causes, dynamics, and consequences of financial crises through the lens of history and theory. This session turns to the empirical toolkit: the quantitative methods that economists, central banks, and financial regulators use to measure systemic risk, detect the buildup of vulnerabilities, and provide early warning of impending crises.

The development of early warning systems and systemic risk measures has been one of the most active areas of research in financial economics since the GFC. The failure to detect the buildup of risk before 2007, despite the availability of data that, in retrospect, clearly signaled danger, demonstrated that the analytical toolkit was inadequate and needed to be substantially upgraded. The post-crisis research has produced a rich set of indicators and models, some of which have proven useful in identifying vulnerabilities in real time.

This session surveys the most important of these tools, explains the evidence on their predictive performance, and, critically, examines their limitations. The tools can identify conditions that make crises more likely, but they cannot predict the timing or the specific form of the next crisis with the precision that policymakers would need to act preemptively. Understanding both the power and the limits of the toolkit is essential for its responsible use.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Explain the logic of early warning systems for financial crises, including the signal extraction approach and the regression-based approach, and assess the trade-off between false alarms and missed crises.
2. Construct and interpret the BIS credit-to-GDP gap and explain why credit growth is the most reliable single predictor of banking crises.
3. Describe the major market-based systemic risk measures (CoVaR, SRISK, MES) and explain what each captures about the distribution of systemic risk.
4. Interpret financial conditions indices and financial stress indices and explain their role in real-time crisis monitoring.
5. Evaluate the predictive content of yield curve inversions, credit spreads, and other market-based signals.
6. Articulate the fundamental limits of early warning systems and explain why crisis prediction is inherently imperfect.

## Session Outline

**16.1 Early Warning Systems.** The signal extraction approach (Kaminsky-Lizondo-Reinhart), the regression-based approach, the choice of crisis definition, and the trade-off between Type I and Type II errors.

**16.2 Credit-Based Indicators.** The BIS credit-to-GDP gap, the Jordà-Schularick-Taylor evidence, the decomposition of credit booms, and the use of credit indicators in the Basel III countercyclical capital buffer framework.

**16.3 Market-Based Systemic Risk Measures.** CoVaR (Adrian and Brunnermeier), SRISK (Acharya, Engle, and Richardson), Marginal Expected Shortfall (Acharya et al.), and the Absorption Ratio.

**16.4 Financial Stress Indices and Conditions Indices.** The construction and interpretation of composite stress and conditions indices, including the St. Louis Fed Financial Stress Index, the Chicago Fed National Financial Conditions Index, and the ECB's Composite Indicator of Systemic Stress.

**16.5 Yield Curves, Spreads, and Other Market Signals.** The predictive content of yield curve inversions, credit spreads, the TED spread, the LIBOR-OIS spread, and volatility measures.

**16.6 The Limits of Early Warning.** Why early warning systems produce false alarms, why they miss crises, the Goodhart's Law problem, and the political economy of acting on warnings.
