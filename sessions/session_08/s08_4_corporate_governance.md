---
title: "8.4 Corporate Governance Failures"
subtitle: Enron, WorldCom, and the limits of market discipline
---

The collapse of the dot-com bubble was followed by a wave of corporate governance scandals that revealed systematic fraud, manipulation, and conflicts of interest at the heart of the American corporate and financial system. The Enron and WorldCom scandals were the most prominent, but they were not isolated incidents: they reflected structural weaknesses in the system of corporate governance, auditing, and financial regulation that had developed during the boom years.

## Enron

Enron Corporation, based in Houston, Texas, was one of the most celebrated companies of the 1990s. Originally a natural gas pipeline company, Enron reinvented itself as an energy trading firm and then as a conglomerate that traded in everything from electricity and natural gas to broadband internet capacity and weather derivatives. It was ranked as the seventh-largest company in the United States by revenue, and its stock was widely held by institutional and retail investors. Enron was named "America's Most Innovative Company" by Fortune magazine for six consecutive years.

The innovation, it turned out, was primarily in accounting. Enron used a complex web of **special purpose entities** (SPEs), off-balance-sheet vehicles that were nominally independent of Enron but were in practice controlled by Enron executives. The SPEs served several purposes: they allowed Enron to move debt off its balance sheet (making the company appear less leveraged than it was), to book profits on transactions with the SPEs (even though the transactions were essentially with itself), and to hide losses on failing investments. The arrangements were structured to comply with the technical requirements of accounting rules while violating their spirit, a practice known as "structured finance" that would reappear in more sophisticated form during the subprime mortgage crisis.

Enron's fraud was enabled by a series of failures in the system of checks and balances that was supposed to prevent such abuses. Arthur Andersen, Enron's external auditor, approved the accounting treatments that hid the SPE transactions, earning millions in consulting fees in addition to its audit fees, a conflict of interest that undermined its independence. Enron's board of directors approved the SPE transactions, including the arrangements that allowed Enron's Chief Financial Officer, Andrew Fastow, to personally profit from the entities. The equity analysts at the major investment banks maintained "buy" recommendations on Enron's stock throughout the period, even as the company's disclosures became increasingly opaque.

Enron filed for bankruptcy on December 2, 2001, in what was then the largest bankruptcy in American history. The stock, which had traded above $90 in 2000, fell below $1. Thousands of Enron employees lost their jobs and their retirement savings (many had been encouraged to hold Enron stock in their 401(k) accounts). Fastow pleaded guilty to fraud and was sentenced to prison. Enron's CEO, Jeffrey Skilling, was convicted of multiple counts of fraud and conspiracy. Arthur Andersen, which was convicted of obstruction of justice for shredding documents related to the Enron audit, collapsed and ceased to exist as a going concern, reducing the "Big Five" accounting firms to the "Big Four."

## WorldCom

WorldCom, a telecommunications company that had grown through a series of aggressive acquisitions (most notably the $37 billion purchase of MCI Communications in 1998), collapsed in 2002 in what was, at the time, the largest accounting fraud in American history. WorldCom's fraud was more straightforward than Enron's: the company simply capitalized operating expenses (recording them as capital investments rather than current expenses), which inflated reported earnings by approximately $11 billion over several years. The CEO, Bernard Ebbers, was convicted of fraud and sentenced to 25 years in prison.

## The Global Analyst Research Settlement

The corporate governance scandals extended beyond individual companies to the institutions that were supposed to provide independent analysis and oversight. An investigation led by New York Attorney General Eliot Spitzer revealed that equity research at the major investment banks was systematically corrupted by conflicts of interest. Analysts were compensated based partly on their contribution to investment banking revenue, creating a direct financial incentive to issue optimistic research that would support their banks' underwriting and advisory businesses.

The investigation produced the Global Analyst Research Settlement of 2003, in which ten major investment banks paid approximately $1.4 billion in fines and agreed to structural reforms designed to separate equity research from investment banking. The settlement also required the banks to fund independent research and to disclose conflicts of interest in their research reports.

## The Sarbanes-Oxley Act

The regulatory response to the corporate governance scandals was the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 (SOX), one of the most significant pieces of securities legislation since the New Deal. The Act's major provisions included the creation of the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) to regulate the auditing profession, requirements for CEO and CFO certification of financial statements, restrictions on auditing firms providing consulting services to their audit clients (addressing the Arthur Andersen conflict), enhanced disclosure requirements, whistleblower protections, and increased criminal penalties for corporate fraud.

Sarbanes-Oxley was a significant reform, and it addressed many of the specific failures revealed by Enron and WorldCom. The separation of auditing and consulting reduced the conflict of interest that had compromised auditor independence. CEO certification increased personal accountability for the accuracy of financial reporting. The PCAOB created an independent regulator for the auditing profession that had previously been self-regulated.

However, Sarbanes-Oxley had significant limitations. It focused on corporate governance and accounting fraud, which were the most visible problems of the early 2000s, but it did not address the deeper structural issues in the financial system: the growth of the shadow banking system, the proliferation of complex derivatives, the buildup of systemic risk through interconnection and leverage, and the regulatory gaps that allowed enormous risks to accumulate outside the perimeter of bank regulation. These issues, which would be at the center of the global financial crisis, were not on the regulatory agenda in 2002 because they had not yet produced a visible failure. The pattern of crisis-specific, backward-looking regulation (subsection 4.6) was repeating itself.
