---
title: "8.2 Anatomy of the Dot-Com Bubble"
subtitle: Venture capital, IPOs, and the mechanics of speculative excess
---

The dot-com bubble was not merely an episode of irrational enthusiasm; it was enabled and amplified by a specific set of financial mechanisms that channeled capital into speculative investments on a massive scale. Understanding these mechanisms is important because many of them, venture capital cycles, IPO underpricing, analyst conflicts, retail speculation on margin, recur in subsequent episodes, including the cryptocurrency mania of the 2010s and 2020s.

## The venture capital pipeline

The dot-com boom was fueled by an unprecedented surge in venture capital (VC) investment. VC investment in the United States rose from approximately $8 billion in 1995 to approximately $105 billion in 2000, a thirteen-fold increase. The capital came from institutional investors (pension funds, university endowments, foundations) that were attracted by the extraordinary returns that early VC investors had earned on investments in companies like Netscape, Amazon, and Yahoo.

The VC model created a self-reinforcing cycle. Venture capitalists invested in start-up companies, which used the funds to develop their products and acquire customers. The start-ups then went public through IPOs, at which point the VC investors could sell their stakes at enormous profits. The profits attracted more capital into VC funds, which invested in more start-ups, which went public, generating more profits, and so on. The cycle was sustainable only as long as the IPO market remained receptive, which depended on the continued willingness of public market investors to buy shares in companies that had little or no revenue and no prospect of near-term profitability.

## The IPO frenzy

The IPO market was the critical link in the chain. Between 1999 and 2000, approximately 800 technology companies went public in the United States. Many of them had no revenue, no clear business model, and no path to profitability. The average first-day return on technology IPOs in 1999 was approximately 70%, meaning that investors who bought shares at the IPO price saw their investment increase by 70% in a single day. This "IPO pop" attracted enormous demand for IPO shares and created a lottery-like dynamic in which investors clamored to participate in every new offering, regardless of the company's fundamentals.

The IPO process was rife with conflicts of interest. Investment banks earned enormous fees from underwriting IPOs (typically 7% of the amount raised). The banks' equity analysts were expected to provide favorable research coverage of the companies their banks brought public, creating a structural conflict between the analysts' obligation to provide honest assessments and their banks' interest in promoting the stocks. The most prominent technology analysts, such as Henry Blodget at Merrill Lynch and Jack Grubman at Salomon Smith Barney, became celebrities whose recommendations could move stock prices by billions of dollars. Internal emails later revealed that some analysts privately described stocks they were publicly recommending as worthless, a revelation that would contribute to the regulatory response discussed in subsection 8.4.

## Retail speculation

The dot-com bubble was distinctive in the breadth of retail investor participation. The rise of online brokerage platforms (E*Trade, Ameritrade, Charles Schwab's online platform) dramatically reduced the cost of trading and made the stock market accessible to millions of individual investors who had previously been excluded by high commissions and minimum account requirements. The number of online brokerage accounts in the United States grew from approximately 3 million in 1997 to approximately 20 million in 2000.

Many retail investors were drawn into speculation by the stories of ordinary people who had become millionaires through stock trading. Day trading, the practice of buying and selling stocks within a single trading day to profit from short-term price movements, became a cultural phenomenon. Day trading firms sprang up across the country, offering traders access to real-time market data, direct-access trading platforms, and (critically) margin lending that allowed them to trade with borrowed money. The combination of inexperienced investors, leverage, and a rising market created the conditions for enormous losses when the market turned.

## Valuation metrics: the abandonment of fundamentals

Perhaps the most distinctive feature of the dot-com bubble was the systematic abandonment of traditional valuation metrics. As stock prices rose to levels that could not be justified by any reasonable estimate of future earnings, analysts and investors developed new metrics that appeared to justify the higher prices. Price-to-eyeballs (valuing a company based on the number of website visitors rather than revenue), price-to-revenue (used when there were no earnings to base a price-to-earnings ratio on), and even more creative constructions were deployed to rationalize valuations that made no sense by traditional standards.

The NASDAQ Composite, which was heavily weighted toward technology stocks, rose from approximately 1,000 in early 1996 to a peak of 5,049 on March 10, 2000. At the peak, the price-to-earnings ratio of the NASDAQ was approximately 175 (compared to a long-run average of approximately 15-20 for the broader market), and many of the index's largest components had no earnings at all. Amazon, which was one of the most successful dot-com companies and would eventually become enormously profitable, traded at more than 60 times its annual revenue at the peak. Pets.com, which would become one of the most notorious symbols of the bubble, went public in February 2000 at a valuation of approximately $300 million despite revenues of approximately $5 million and losses of approximately $60 million.

The willingness of sophisticated investors, not just retail speculators, to pay these prices reflected several factors: the genuine excitement about the transformative potential of the internet, the momentum effect (investors bought because prices were rising), the fear of missing out (investors who stayed on the sidelines watched their peers earn extraordinary returns), and the structural conflicts of interest that prevented the analysts and investment bankers who understood the risks from communicating them honestly to the public.
