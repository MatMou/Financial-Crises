---
title: "8.6 The Seeds of the Housing Boom"
subtitle: The global savings glut, the search for yield, and the growth of shadow banking
---

The conditions that produced the global financial crisis of 2007-2009 did not emerge suddenly. They accumulated gradually during the early 2000s, driven by the interaction of macroeconomic forces (the global savings glut, low interest rates), financial innovation (securitization, subprime lending, credit derivatives), and regulatory gaps (the growth of the shadow banking system outside the perimeter of bank regulation). This subsection traces the origins of these forces and explains how they combined to create the most dangerous financial environment since the 1920s.

## The global savings glut

In a speech in March 2005, Federal Reserve Governor Ben Bernanke introduced the concept of the **global savings glut** to explain the puzzle of low long-term interest rates. Despite the Fed's rate increases beginning in June 2004, long-term Treasury yields remained stubbornly low, a phenomenon Greenspan called the "bond market conundrum." Bernanke argued that the explanation lay not in U.S. monetary policy but in global capital flows: a glut of savings from emerging Asia, the oil exporters, and Germany was flowing into the United States, pushing down long-term interest rates regardless of what the Fed did with short-term rates.

The savings glut had several sources. The Asian crisis countries, scarred by the experience of the 1990s (subsection 7.6), were accumulating enormous foreign exchange reserves (invested primarily in U.S. Treasuries) as self-insurance against future crises. China was running large and growing trade surpluses, partly as a result of its undervalued currency and its high savings rate, and investing the surpluses in U.S. financial assets. The oil exporters, benefiting from rising oil prices, were recycling their surpluses through the international financial system, as they had done in the 1970s (subsection 6.4). And Germany, where domestic demand was weak and the savings rate was high, was exporting capital to the rest of the Eurozone (a flow that would contribute to the European debt crisis, Session 12).

The savings glut pushed down long-term interest rates, which had two consequences. First, it reduced the yield on safe assets (government bonds), which forced investors who needed higher returns (pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds) to search for yield in riskier assets. This "search for yield" created enormous demand for higher-yielding securities, particularly mortgage-backed securities and other structured credit products. Second, low long-term rates stimulated the housing market directly, by reducing mortgage rates and increasing the affordability of home purchases.

## The expansion of subprime lending

The demand for mortgage-backed securities could only be satisfied if the supply of mortgages increased. Since the pool of creditworthy borrowers was finite, the mortgage industry expanded by lending to borrowers who would not have qualified under traditional underwriting standards: borrowers with low credit scores, high debt-to-income ratios, limited documentation of income, and little or no down payment. These loans were classified as **subprime** (or, in a euphemism that would become infamous, "non-prime").

Subprime lending was not new. It had existed as a small niche market for decades. What was new was its explosive growth in the 2000s and its transformation from a local, portfolio-based business (in which the lender held the loan and bore the risk) to a global, securitization-based business (in which the loan was originated, sold to a securitizer, packaged into a mortgage-backed security, and distributed to investors worldwide). The securitization model severed the link between the originator and the risk of the loan, creating the "originate-to-distribute" model in which the originator had no incentive to ensure that the borrower could repay.

The deterioration of underwriting standards was dramatic. The share of mortgages with no documentation ("no-doc" or "liar's loans") increased from approximately 17% of securitized subprime mortgages in 2001 to approximately 44% in 2006. The share with combined loan-to-value ratios above 90% (meaning the borrower had less than 10% equity in the home) increased sharply. Adjustable-rate mortgages with low initial "teaser" rates that would reset to much higher rates after two or three years proliferated, creating a ticking time bomb of future payment shocks.

## The growth of shadow banking

The financial intermediation that supported the housing boom occurred largely outside the regulated banking system, in what came to be called the **shadow banking system**. The shadow banking system performed the same economic function as the traditional banking system (maturity transformation: borrowing short and lending long) but without the regulatory safeguards (capital requirements, deposit insurance, access to the lender of last resort) that had been imposed on traditional banks after the Great Depression.

The key institutions and instruments of the shadow banking system included **structured investment vehicles** (SIVs) and **asset-backed commercial paper** (ABCP) conduits, which funded long-term mortgage-backed securities with short-term commercial paper; **money market mutual funds**, which promised depositors a stable value (the "$1 net asset value") while investing in short-term instruments including ABCP; **repo markets**, in which investment banks funded their holdings of mortgage-backed securities through overnight repurchase agreements; and **credit default swaps** (CDS), which allowed investors to take synthetic exposure to mortgage credit risk without actually holding mortgage-backed securities.

The shadow banking system grew to enormous proportions during the 2000s. By 2007, the total liabilities of the shadow banking system in the United States were estimated at approximately $20 trillion, roughly equal to the total liabilities of the traditional banking system. The shadow system was performing the same functions as the traditional system (collecting savings, transforming maturities, allocating credit) but without the safety net. When the crisis hit, the shadow banking system would prove just as vulnerable to runs as the uninsured banks of the 1930s, and the resulting panic would be transmitted to the traditional banking system through the dense web of interconnections that linked the two.

## The convergence of vulnerabilities

By 2006, the vulnerabilities had converged. Low interest rates (driven by both Fed policy and the global savings glut) had fueled a housing boom that had pushed house prices far above their historical relationship to rents and incomes. The boom was financed by subprime mortgages with deteriorating underwriting standards, originated by lenders who had no stake in the loans' performance. The mortgages were securitized and distributed through a shadow banking system that was performing maturity transformation on a massive scale without the regulatory safeguards that protected the traditional banking system. And the entire structure was interconnected through a web of derivatives, repo transactions, and counterparty relationships that made it impossible to determine where the risks ultimately resided.

The stage was set for the global financial crisis. Sessions 9 through 11 will trace the crisis from the initial cracks in the subprime market in 2007 through the panic of September-October 2008 and the policy response that prevented a second Great Depression.
