---
title: "8.3 The Crash of 2000-2002"
subtitle: The destruction of the NASDAQ and the recession of 2001
---

The dot-com bubble burst in March 2000, initiating a collapse that would destroy approximately $5 trillion in market value, bankrupt hundreds of companies, and contribute to the recession of 2001. The crash followed the classic Kindleberger-Minsky pattern: a gradual loss of momentum, a triggering event, a rush for the exits, and a prolonged decline that took asset prices far below their fundamental values.

## The turning point

The NASDAQ Composite peaked at 5,049 on March 10, 2000. The initial decline was triggered by a confluence of factors rather than a single dramatic event. The Federal Reserve had been raising interest rates since June 1999 (from 4.75% to 6.5% by May 2000), which increased the discount rate applied to future earnings and raised the cost of margin lending. A cover story in Barron's published in March 2000 highlighted the "burn rates" (the rate at which companies were consuming their cash reserves) of prominent dot-com companies and calculated that many would run out of cash within months. And the Japanese economy, which had been expected to provide a source of demand for technology products, slipped back into recession.

The decline was initially gradual, punctuated by sharp sell-offs and partial recoveries that gave investors hope that the correction was temporary. This pattern of declining peaks and declining troughs is characteristic of the deflation phase of speculative bubbles: each rally attracts buyers who believe the bottom has been reached, but the rally fails to hold, and the subsequent decline takes prices to new lows.

## The scale of destruction

The decline was devastating. The NASDAQ Composite fell from 5,049 in March 2000 to 1,114 in October 2002, a decline of approximately 78%. Individual stocks fared far worse. Amazon fell from approximately $107 to $7 (a decline of 93%). Cisco Systems fell from approximately $80 to $11. Qualcomm fell from approximately $100 to $13. Hundreds of dot-com companies, including many that had gone public at billion-dollar valuations, went bankrupt. Pets.com, Webvan, Kozmo.com, Boo.com, and eToys, among many others, ceased to exist.

The total destruction of market value in U.S. equities between March 2000 and October 2002 was approximately $5 trillion on the NASDAQ alone, and considerably more when including the broader market decline (the S&P 500 fell by approximately 49% from its peak). The wealth destruction reduced household spending through the wealth effect and contributed to the recession that began in March 2001.

## The recession of 2001

The recession of 2001 was mild by historical standards: real GDP declined by only 0.3% at its worst, and the recession was officially dated as lasting only eight months (March to November 2001). But the recession was accompanied by a prolonged and painful adjustment in the technology sector: employment in the information technology sector fell by approximately 17% between 2001 and 2003, and the Silicon Valley economy was depressed for several years.

The September 11, 2001 terrorist attacks deepened the recession and added a dimension of uncertainty that went beyond the normal economic cycle. The attacks disrupted financial markets (the New York Stock Exchange was closed for four trading days, the longest closure since the Great Depression), depressed consumer and business confidence, and prompted a massive government response (military spending, homeland security, airline bailouts) that shifted fiscal policy sharply toward deficit.

The recession was also distinctive in that it was not accompanied by a banking crisis. The dot-com bubble had been concentrated in the equity markets, not in the banking system. Banks had relatively limited direct exposure to technology stocks (unlike their massive exposure to real estate during the subsequent housing boom), and the banking system remained well-capitalized throughout the downturn. This distinction is important: it helps explain why the 2001 recession was mild (no credit channel amplification of the kind described by Bernanke in subsection 5.4) and why the policy response was effective in producing a recovery.

## Comparison with earlier speculative episodes

The dot-com bubble bears striking resemblances to earlier speculative manias, particularly the South Sea Bubble (subsection 4.1) and the stock market boom of the 1920s (subsection 5.1). In each case, a genuine economic transformation (colonial trade, industrialization, the internet) provided the displacement that initiated the speculative process. In each case, financial innovation (joint-stock company shares, margin lending, venture capital and IPOs) provided the mechanism for leveraged speculation. In each case, the abandonment of traditional valuation metrics during the euphoria phase created prices that were unsustainable. And in each case, the crash destroyed enormous wealth and was followed by recriminations, investigations, and regulatory reforms.

The dot-com bubble also differed from these earlier episodes in important ways. The crash did not produce a banking crisis or a depression (as the 1929 crash did) because the speculation was concentrated in equity markets rather than in the banking system. The regulatory response (Sarbanes-Oxley, discussed in subsection 8.4) addressed corporate governance but did not fundamentally reshape the financial system (as the New Deal had done). And the monetary policy response to the crash (aggressive interest rate cuts, discussed in subsection 8.5) would itself become a source of vulnerability by fueling the credit boom that led to the global financial crisis.

The dot-com episode thus illustrates a pattern that Minsky would have recognized: the successful management of one crisis plants the seeds of the next. The relatively mild recession of 2001 and the relatively painless resolution of the dot-com crash may have contributed to overconfidence among policymakers and market participants, reinforcing the belief that the financial system was resilient and that the Fed could always clean up after a bubble. This overconfidence was itself a form of the "this time is different" syndrome, and it would prove costly.
