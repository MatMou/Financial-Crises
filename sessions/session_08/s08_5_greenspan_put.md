---
title: "8.5 The Greenspan Put and the Low-Rate Environment"
subtitle: Asymmetric monetary policy and the buildup of moral hazard
---

The Federal Reserve's response to the dot-com crash and the 2001 recession was to cut interest rates aggressively: the federal funds rate was reduced from 6.5% in January 2001 to 1.75% by December 2001 and eventually to 1.0% by June 2003, where it remained for a full year. This response was effective in the short run: it cushioned the recession, supported the recovery, and prevented the dot-com crash from producing a more severe economic downturn. But it also contributed to the conditions that produced the global financial crisis, by fueling a credit boom in housing and structured finance that dwarfed the dot-com bubble in both scale and systemic consequences.

## The concept of the Greenspan put

The term "Greenspan put" refers to the perception, widely held in financial markets, that the Federal Reserve under Chairman Alan Greenspan would cut interest rates aggressively in response to declining asset prices but would not raise rates to restrain rising asset prices. A "put" in options terminology is the right to sell an asset at a guaranteed minimum price, which provides downside protection. The Greenspan put thus referred to the implicit promise that the Fed would protect investors from large losses by easing monetary policy whenever markets declined.

The Greenspan put was not an explicit policy; it was a market inference drawn from the Fed's behavior during a series of episodes. The Fed eased aggressively after the 1987 stock market crash, after the LTCM crisis in 1998, and after the dot-com crash in 2000-2001. In each case, the Fed's rate cuts prevented the financial market disruption from causing a severe economic downturn. But the pattern created an asymmetry: the Fed appeared to respond forcefully to downside risks (asset price declines, financial stress) while being much more cautious about upside risks (asset price inflation, excessive risk-taking). This asymmetry created a one-sided bet for investors: the upside of risk-taking was unlimited, while the downside was cushioned by the expectation of Fed support.

## The moral hazard problem

The Greenspan put created moral hazard at the systemic level. If market participants believed that the Fed would rescue them from the consequences of excessive risk-taking, they had an incentive to take more risk. The more risk they took, the more vulnerable the financial system became. And the more vulnerable the financial system became, the more certain it was that the Fed would have to intervene when things went wrong, which validated the initial belief and encouraged even more risk-taking. This is a classic moral hazard feedback loop, operating at the level of the entire financial system rather than at the level of individual institutions.

The moral hazard was compounded by the "too big to fail" problem. The LTCM episode (subsection 7.4) had demonstrated that the failure of a large, interconnected financial institution could threaten the stability of the entire system, and that the authorities would intervene to prevent such failures. The lesson that market participants drew was that large institutions would be rescued, which reduced the perceived risk of lending to or investing in those institutions, which allowed them to grow larger and take more risk, which made them even more systemically important and even more certain to be rescued.

The interaction between the Greenspan put and too-big-to-fail created a powerful dynamic of risk accumulation. Financial institutions could borrow cheaply (because their creditors expected government protection), invest in risky assets (because the Greenspan put provided downside protection), and grow to enormous size (because too-big-to-fail guaranteed their survival). The combination produced the explosive growth of the financial sector in the 2000s and the concentration of risk in a small number of systemically important institutions that would be at the center of the global financial crisis.

## The interest rate environment

The low interest rates of 2001-2004 had direct consequences for the housing market and the financial system. With the federal funds rate at 1%, the real (inflation-adjusted) federal funds rate was approximately minus 1%, meaning that the Fed was effectively paying borrowers to borrow. Long-term interest rates, while higher than short-term rates, were also historically low, pushed down by the Fed's rate cuts and by the global savings glut (subsection 8.6).

Low interest rates stimulated housing demand through several channels. Mortgage rates fell, which reduced the cost of home purchases and allowed buyers to qualify for larger loans. Refinancing activity surged, as homeowners replaced old mortgages with new ones at lower rates, extracting equity from their homes in the process. And the low returns available on safe assets (government bonds, bank deposits) pushed investors toward riskier alternatives, including mortgage-backed securities, which offered higher yields.

The debate over whether the Fed's low interest rates caused the housing bubble is one of the most contested questions in recent economic history. John Taylor argued that the Fed kept rates too low for too long, using the "Taylor rule" (a formula that relates the appropriate interest rate to inflation and the output gap) to show that the federal funds rate between 2002 and 2005 was substantially below the level prescribed by the rule ({cite:t}`Taylor2009`). Ben Bernanke, who succeeded Greenspan as Fed chairman in 2006, argued that the housing boom was driven primarily by the global savings glut and by regulatory failures, not by monetary policy, and that the Taylor rule analysis was sensitive to the specification used.

The truth likely involves both factors. Low interest rates made credit cheap and stimulated housing demand. But the transformation of that demand into a speculative bubble required the financial innovations (subprime mortgages, securitization, credit default swaps) and regulatory failures discussed in subsection 8.6 and in Sessions 9-10. Monetary policy created the fuel; financial innovation and regulatory failure provided the spark and the kindling.
