---
title: "8.1 The Technology Boom and the New Economy Narrative"
subtitle: Was this time really different?
---

The technology boom of the late 1990s was accompanied by a powerful narrative: the "new economy." According to this narrative, the commercialization of the internet and related information technologies was producing a fundamental transformation of the American economy, one that would deliver permanently higher productivity growth, render business cycles obsolete, and justify stock market valuations that would have seemed absurd by historical standards. The new economy narrative was the displacement in the Kindleberger-Minsky framework, the exogenous event that changed expectations about future profitability and set the speculative process in motion.

## The productivity acceleration

The new economy narrative was not pure fantasy. It was grounded in a genuine acceleration of productivity growth that began in the mid-1990s and continued through the early 2000s. Labor productivity growth in the U.S. nonfarm business sector, which had averaged approximately 1.4% per year between 1973 and 1995 (the "productivity slowdown" that had puzzled economists for two decades), accelerated to approximately 2.5% per year between 1995 and 2004.

The acceleration was concentrated in the information technology sector and in industries that were heavy users of information technology (finance, wholesale trade, retail trade). The research by Dale Jorgenson, Kevin Stiroh, and others decomposed the productivity acceleration into two components: the production of IT goods (as the semiconductor industry continued to deliver exponential improvements in processing power at declining cost, following Moore's Law) and the use of IT goods by other industries (as firms invested in computers, software, and telecommunications equipment and reorganized their business processes to take advantage of the new technologies).

The productivity acceleration was real and significant. It raised living standards, increased corporate profitability, and contributed to the budget surplus that the U.S. government achieved in the late 1990s. But the key question, which was fiercely debated at the time, was whether the acceleration was permanent or temporary, and whether it justified the extraordinary stock market valuations that accompanied it.

## The narrative and its amplification

The genuine productivity gains were amplified into a narrative of permanent transformation that went far beyond what the data could support. In its most extreme form, the new economy narrative held that the internet would eliminate the business cycle (because information technology would allow firms to manage inventories and supply chains so efficiently that the fluctuations that had caused recessions would disappear), that profits would grow at historically unprecedented rates indefinitely, and that traditional valuation metrics (price-to-earnings ratios, dividend yields, book values) were irrelevant because the new economy operated under different rules.

The narrative was promoted by an ecosystem of technology entrepreneurs, venture capitalists, investment bankers, equity analysts, and financial journalists who had powerful incentives to propagate optimism. Technology entrepreneurs needed the narrative to raise capital at favorable valuations. Venture capitalists needed it to justify their investments and to find buyers for their portfolio companies through IPOs. Investment bankers needed it to generate fees from IPO underwriting and mergers and acquisitions. Equity analysts, who were employed by the same investment banks that were underwriting the IPOs, had a structural conflict of interest that incentivized them to issue optimistic research to support their banks' underwriting business. And financial journalists found that stories about technology visionaries and overnight millionaires attracted more readers than stories about valuation discipline and risk management.

The result was a feedback loop between the narrative and the market. The narrative justified higher stock prices; the higher stock prices seemed to confirm the narrative; the confirmation attracted more investors, which pushed prices higher, which further confirmed the narrative. This is precisely the self-reinforcing dynamic that Shiller describes as "naturally occurring Ponzi process" (subsection 2.6), in which rising prices generate the optimism that produces further price increases.

## Greenspan's "irrational exuberance"

The most famous public acknowledgment that the stock market might be overvalued came from Federal Reserve Chairman Alan Greenspan, who asked in a speech on December 5, 1996, "How do we know when irrational exuberance has unduly escalated asset values, which then become subject to unexpected and prolonged contractions?" The stock market dipped briefly in response to the speech but quickly resumed its upward trajectory. Greenspan did not follow up with any policy action, and indeed he became increasingly sympathetic to the new economy narrative, arguing that the productivity acceleration might justify higher valuations and that the Fed should not attempt to second-guess the market's assessment.

Greenspan's evolution from skeptic to (partial) believer illustrates the difficulty of maintaining contrarian positions during a bubble. The new economy narrative had a kernel of truth (the productivity acceleration was real), which made it difficult to dismiss entirely. The stock market kept rising, which seemed to vindicate the optimists and discredit the skeptics. And the political environment made it dangerous for the Fed chairman to publicly question the boom: any action to deflate the bubble would have been politically unpopular and would have been blamed for the resulting economic downturn.

The "lean versus clean" debate (subsection 2.6) was being played out in real time. Should the Fed raise interest rates to deflate the bubble ("lean against the wind"), or should it wait for the bubble to burst and then clean up the aftermath? Greenspan chose the latter approach, which became known as the "Greenspan put" (subsection 8.5). The consequences of that choice would shape monetary policy for the next two decades.
