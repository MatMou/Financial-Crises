---
title: "10.5 Global Contagion: How the Crisis Went Worldwide"
subtitle: From Wall Street to the world
---

The global financial crisis originated in the United States, but it spread to virtually every country in the world with a speed and severity that surprised policymakers and market participants. The crisis was the most synchronized global downturn since the Great Depression, and the mechanisms of contagion, while related to those that had operated in the 1990s emerging market crises (subsection 7.5), were amplified by the greater size and interconnection of the 21st-century financial system.

## The financial channel

The most direct channel of international transmission was the financial channel: European and Asian financial institutions held large quantities of U.S. mortgage-related securities and were directly exposed to the losses.

European banks were particularly exposed. In the years leading up to the crisis, European banks had been major purchasers of AAA-rated structured credit products, attracted by the higher yields relative to European government bonds. German Landesbanken, Swiss banks (particularly UBS), Dutch banks (ABN AMRO, ING), UK banks (HBOS, Royal Bank of Scotland, Barclays), and French banks (BNP Paribas, Societe Generale) all held significant portfolios of U.S. MBS and CDOs. When the value of these securities collapsed, the European banks suffered losses comparable to those of their American counterparts.

The European banking crisis was compounded by the fact that many European banks had funded their dollar-denominated asset purchases with short-term dollar borrowing in the wholesale markets. When the dollar funding markets froze in September 2008, European banks found themselves unable to refinance their dollar liabilities and faced acute dollar liquidity shortages. The Federal Reserve responded by establishing **central bank liquidity swap lines** with fourteen foreign central banks (including the ECB, the Bank of England, the Bank of Japan, and the Swiss National Bank), allowing these central banks to borrow dollars from the Fed and lend them to their domestic banks. The swap lines, which reached a peak of approximately $580 billion in December 2008, were one of the most important crisis interventions and illustrated the global nature of the dollar-based financial system.

## The trade channel

The financial crisis transmitted itself to the real economy partly through the **trade channel**. The synchronized recession in the advanced economies produced a collapse of international trade that was even sharper than the collapse during the Great Depression. World trade fell by approximately 12% in 2009, with the decline concentrated in the final quarter of 2008 and the first quarter of 2009. The speed of the decline was unprecedented: trade fell at an annualized rate of approximately 30% during the worst months.

The trade collapse reflected several factors. The credit crunch reduced the availability of trade finance (letters of credit, export insurance), which disrupted international supply chains. The synchronized nature of the recession meant that demand fell simultaneously in all major markets, so that no country could export its way out of the downturn (unlike in previous recessions, where growth in some regions offset recession in others). And the rise of global supply chains meant that a decline in final demand produced multiplied effects along the chain, as each link reduced its orders to the previous link.

The trade channel was particularly devastating for export-dependent emerging markets. Countries like China, South Korea, Taiwan, and Germany, whose economies were heavily oriented toward exports, experienced sharp declines in output. China's exports fell by approximately 25% in the first quarter of 2009 relative to a year earlier. The trade channel thus transmitted the crisis from the financial centers to the real economies of countries that had little direct exposure to U.S. subprime mortgages.

## Emerging market stress

The emerging markets experienced the crisis primarily through capital flow reversals and the trade channel. Capital flows to emerging markets reversed sharply in late 2008: portfolio investment flows turned sharply negative, and bank lending contracted. The reversal was particularly severe for emerging market countries in Central and Eastern Europe (Hungary, Latvia, Ukraine, Romania), which had experienced large capital inflow booms during the pre-crisis period and had accumulated significant foreign currency debt, making them vulnerable to the same sudden-stop dynamics that had devastated Asia in 1997 (subsection 7.3).

The countries that weathered the crisis best were those that had accumulated large foreign exchange reserves during the pre-crisis period (subsection 7.6). China, with reserves of approximately $2 trillion, was able to implement a massive fiscal stimulus without external financing constraints. South Korea, which had built substantial reserves after its 1997 crisis, drew on those reserves to stabilize the won and supplement domestic liquidity. The contrast between the relatively mild impact on reserve-rich Asian emerging markets and the severe impact on reserve-poor European emerging markets provided strong evidence that the post-1997 reserve accumulation strategy had been effective.

## The confidence channel

Beyond the direct financial and trade linkages, the crisis was transmitted through a generalized loss of confidence that affected economic behavior globally. Business investment fell sharply as firms postponed spending in the face of extreme uncertainty about the economic outlook. Consumer spending fell as households reduced consumption in response to declining wealth (falling house and stock prices), rising unemployment, and fear of further deterioration. The collapse of confidence was self-reinforcing: the decline in spending reduced output, which increased unemployment, which further reduced confidence and spending.

The confidence channel was amplified by the media coverage of the crisis, which was unprecedented in its intensity and global reach. Images of Lehman Brothers employees carrying boxes out of their offices, of protesters on Wall Street, and of empty factories became iconic representations of the crisis and contributed to a sense of impending catastrophe that depressed economic activity even in countries and sectors that were not directly affected by the financial turmoil.
