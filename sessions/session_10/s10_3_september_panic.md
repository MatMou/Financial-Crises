---
title: "10.3 The September 2008 Panic"
subtitle: Fannie, Freddie, Lehman, and AIG — the week that changed everything
---

September 2008 was the most dramatic month in the history of American finance since October 1929. In a span of two weeks, the government placed the two largest mortgage companies in conservatorship, the fourth-largest investment bank filed for bankruptcy, the largest insurance company was rescued by the government, the two remaining major investment banks converted to bank holding companies, and the Treasury Secretary proposed the largest financial rescue package in history. The events of September 2008 transformed the crisis from a slow-motion deterioration into an acute panic that threatened the collapse of the global financial system.

## The conservatorship of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac

On September 7, 2008, the Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) placed Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac into conservatorship, effectively nationalizing the two government-sponsored enterprises. The GSEs' combined mortgage-related obligations exceeded $5 trillion, and their capital had been eroded by losses on their mortgage portfolios. The Treasury committed up to $200 billion (later increased to $400 billion) to ensure that the GSEs could meet their obligations.

The conservatorship was necessary to prevent a catastrophic disruption of the mortgage market. Fannie and Freddie guaranteed approximately half of all outstanding U.S. mortgages, and their securities were held by central banks, sovereign wealth funds, and financial institutions around the world. A default by the GSEs would have inflicted losses on investors globally and would have frozen the U.S. mortgage market, making it impossible for any American to obtain a home loan.

The conservatorship was executed smoothly and was generally regarded as an appropriate action. But it had an unintended consequence: it demonstrated that the U.S. government would rescue systemically important financial institutions, which reinforced the expectation that other institutions, including Lehman Brothers, would also be rescued if they got into trouble. This expectation would prove fatally wrong.

## The bankruptcy of Lehman Brothers

Lehman Brothers, the fourth-largest U.S. investment bank, had been under severe stress since the spring of 2008. Like Bear Stearns, Lehman was heavily exposed to mortgage-related assets and was dependent on short-term funding. CEO Richard Fuld had resisted selling the firm or raising additional capital, believing that the assets were fundamentally sound and that the market was mispricing them.

By early September 2008, Lehman's position had become critical. Its stock price had fallen from approximately $65 at the beginning of the year to approximately $4. Counterparties were pulling back, and short-term funding was becoming increasingly difficult to obtain. The Fed and the Treasury attempted to arrange a private-sector solution over the weekend of September 13-14, encouraging potential acquirers (Bank of America and Barclays were the leading candidates) to purchase Lehman. Bank of America withdrew and instead agreed to acquire Merrill Lynch. Barclays' bid required a guarantee from the U.S. or UK government, which neither was willing to provide.

On Monday, September 15, 2008, Lehman Brothers filed for bankruptcy, the largest bankruptcy in American history (with approximately $639 billion in assets). The decision to allow Lehman to fail, after rescuing Bear Stearns six months earlier and the GSEs one week earlier, was the single most consequential decision of the crisis.

The reasons for the decision remain debated. Treasury Secretary Henry Paulson and Fed Chairman Bernanke stated that they lacked the legal authority to rescue Lehman (the Fed could lend against collateral, but Lehman's collateral was insufficient to support a loan large enough to cover its liabilities). Critics argued that the legal authority existed but was not exercised, that the decision reflected a political judgment that the public would not tolerate another bailout, and that the authorities underestimated the consequences of allowing Lehman to fail.

Whatever the reasons, the consequences were devastating. Lehman's bankruptcy triggered a cascade of losses and panic that engulfed the global financial system. Counterparties who had traded derivatives with Lehman found their positions frozen in bankruptcy. Investors who held Lehman's commercial paper and bonds suffered immediate losses. Repo counterparties who had lent against Lehman's collateral scrambled to sell the collateral, depressing prices. And the signal effect was enormous: if Lehman, a major global financial institution, could be allowed to fail, then no institution was safe. The assumption that the government would rescue systemically important firms, which had been reinforced by the Bear Stearns rescue and the GSE conservatorship, was shattered.

## The rescue of AIG

The day after Lehman's bankruptcy, AIG was on the brink of collapse. The downgrade of AIG's credit rating on September 15 had triggered massive collateral calls on its CDS contracts (subsection 9.4), and AIG was unable to meet them. Unlike Lehman, AIG was rescued. On September 16, the Federal Reserve extended an $85 billion emergency credit line to AIG (later expanded to approximately $182 billion) in exchange for a 79.9% equity stake, effectively nationalizing the company.

The contrast between the treatment of Lehman and AIG was striking and controversial. The justification for rescuing AIG was that its failure would have been even more catastrophic than Lehman's: AIG's CDS counterparties included virtually every major global bank, and AIG's failure would have imposed immediate losses of hundreds of billions of dollars on those institutions, potentially triggering a cascade of failures across the global banking system. The justification for not rescuing Lehman was that the Fed lacked the legal authority to do so. The inconsistency fed public anger about the crisis response and contributed to the political backlash against the bailouts.

## The consequences

The combination of Lehman's bankruptcy and the AIG rescue, coming on top of the GSE conservatorship, produced a panic of a severity that had not been seen since the 1930s. Every financial institution was now viewed as potentially vulnerable. Credit markets froze. Stock markets plunged (the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell approximately 500 points on September 15 and continued to fall over the following weeks). The VIX index, a measure of stock market volatility often called the "fear index," spiked to levels never previously recorded.

The panic was most intense in the wholesale funding markets that supported the shadow banking system, which is the subject of the next subsection. But the effects extended far beyond the financial sector: the collapse of confidence and the freezing of credit markets would transmit the financial crisis to the real economy with devastating speed and force.
