---
title: "10.6 Transmission to the Real Economy"
subtitle: How the financial crisis produced the Great Recession
---

The global financial crisis produced the deepest and most widespread recession since the Great Depression. U.S. real GDP fell by approximately 4.3% from peak to trough, and the declines in some other advanced economies were even larger (the UK fell by 6.3%, Japan by 8.4%, Germany by 6.8%). Global GDP contracted for the first time since World War II. Approximately 8.7 million jobs were lost in the United States alone between 2008 and 2010, and the unemployment rate rose from 4.7% to 10%. The mechanisms through which the financial crisis was transmitted to the real economy are the subject of this subsection.

## The credit crunch

The most direct transmission mechanism was the **credit crunch**: the sharp reduction in the availability of credit to households and businesses. As banks suffered losses on mortgage-related assets and faced the prospect of further losses, they tightened lending standards, reduced credit lines, and curtailed new lending. The Federal Reserve's Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey showed the sharpest tightening of lending standards in the survey's history during the fourth quarter of 2008.

The credit crunch operated through the financial accelerator mechanism described in subsection 2.5. As asset prices fell, the net worth of borrowers declined, which reduced the value of the collateral they could offer against loans, which increased the external finance premium, which reduced the amount they could borrow, which forced them to cut spending, which reduced economic activity, which further depressed asset prices, in a self-reinforcing downward spiral. The financial accelerator amplified the initial financial shock into a much larger decline in real economic activity.

The credit crunch was particularly severe for small and medium-sized businesses, which depended on bank credit for their day-to-day operations and did not have access to the bond market or other alternative sources of financing. Large corporations could issue bonds or commercial paper (once the Fed intervened to support those markets), but small businesses had no alternative to bank credit. The disproportionate impact on small businesses contributed to the severity of the employment decline, because small businesses account for a large share of total employment.

## The wealth effect

The collapse of house prices and stock prices destroyed trillions of dollars of household wealth. U.S. household net worth fell from approximately $65 trillion at the peak in 2007 to approximately $48 trillion at the trough in early 2009, a decline of approximately $17 trillion (roughly equal to the entire annual GDP of the United States). The decline in wealth reduced consumer spending through the **wealth effect**: the econometric relationship between household wealth and consumption implies that each dollar of wealth loss reduces consumption by approximately 3-5 cents, which implies that the $17 trillion wealth decline reduced annual consumption by approximately $500-850 billion.

The wealth effect was amplified by the balance sheet recession dynamics described in subsection 3.2. Households that had accumulated large mortgage debts during the boom, and whose homes were now worth less than their mortgages ("underwater" households), were forced to reduce consumption and increase saving to rebuild their balance sheets. The shift from consumption to saving, which was rational at the individual level, reduced aggregate demand and deepened the recession, exactly as Koo's balance sheet recession framework predicts.

The regional variation in the housing bust produced corresponding regional variation in the severity of the recession. The states that had experienced the largest house price declines (Nevada, Arizona, Florida, California) experienced the deepest recessions and the highest unemployment rates. {cite:t}`MianSufi2014` documented that counties with higher pre-crisis household debt ratios experienced larger declines in consumption, larger increases in unemployment, and slower recoveries, providing micro-level evidence for the balance sheet channel.

## The uncertainty channel

The financial crisis generated extreme uncertainty about the economic outlook, which depressed investment and hiring decisions. The VIX index, a measure of stock market uncertainty, reached unprecedented levels in October 2008. Survey measures of business confidence and consumer confidence fell to record lows. The uncertainty was compounded by the unprecedented nature of the crisis: policymakers, market participants, and businesses had no recent experience of a financial crisis of this magnitude in the advanced economies, which made it difficult to form expectations about the future path of the economy.

The uncertainty channel operated primarily through its effect on investment. Business investment in the United States fell by approximately 23% between the third quarter of 2008 and the second quarter of 2009, the sharpest decline since the Great Depression. Firms postponed investment projects, delayed hiring, and reduced inventories in response to the extreme uncertainty about future demand. The collapse of investment was a major contributor to the severity of the recession and to the slowness of the subsequent recovery.

## The feedback loop

The transmission mechanisms described above, the credit crunch, the wealth effect, and the uncertainty channel, did not operate independently; they reinforced each other in a feedback loop that amplified the initial financial shock.

The credit crunch reduced spending, which reduced output and employment, which reduced income, which increased loan defaults, which imposed further losses on banks, which tightened credit further. The wealth effect reduced spending, which reduced economic activity, which depressed asset prices further, which reduced wealth further. The uncertainty depressed investment, which reduced economic activity, which increased uncertainty. And the financial accelerator linked all of these channels together: the decline in asset prices reduced the net worth of all economic agents (households, businesses, and financial institutions), which tightened financial conditions, which reduced spending, which depressed asset prices further.

The feedback loop is the reason why the recession was so severe. The initial shock, the subprime mortgage losses, was large but not catastrophically so: total subprime mortgage losses were estimated at approximately $500 billion, which was a significant but manageable amount in the context of a global financial system with trillions of dollars of capital. The amplification through the financial system and the real economy, via the mechanisms described in this subsection, transformed a $500 billion loss into a global recession that destroyed over $17 trillion of household wealth, eliminated millions of jobs, and reduced global GDP by several percent. The ratio of the final damage to the initial shock, a factor of approximately 30:1, illustrates the power of the amplification mechanisms that theory identifies and that the crisis confirmed.
