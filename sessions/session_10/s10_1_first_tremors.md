---
title: "10.1 The First Tremors: Subprime Cracks and the August 2007 Freeze"
subtitle: When the ABX started falling and the music stopped
---

The crisis did not begin with a dramatic collapse. It began with a slow deterioration in the performance of subprime mortgages that became visible in early 2007, gradually eroded confidence in the structured credit products built on those mortgages, and culminated in a sudden and devastating freeze in the short-term funding markets in August 2007. The period from February to August 2007 was the incubation phase of the crisis: the phase in which the losses were recognized but their systemic implications were not yet understood.

## The ABX decline

The first clear market signal that subprime mortgages were deteriorating came from the ABX index, the family of credit default swap indices that tracked the performance of subprime MBS tranches (subsection 9.4). The ABX index for the 2006-2 vintage BBB-rated tranche began to decline in late 2006 and fell sharply in February 2007, from approximately 97 (near par) to approximately 60. The decline indicated that the market was pricing in substantial losses on subprime mortgages originated in 2006, the vintage with the worst underwriting standards.

The ABX decline was initially treated as a problem confined to a single segment of the mortgage market. Federal Reserve Chairman Bernanke testified to Congress in March 2007 that the impact of subprime problems on the broader economy and financial markets appeared likely to be contained. This assessment reflected the widespread view that the subprime market, while troubled, was too small (subprime mortgages were approximately 15% of total mortgage originations) to pose a systemic threat.

The "contained" view was wrong, for reasons that the structure of the financial system made inevitable. The subprime mortgages had been securitized into MBS, the mezzanine tranches of those MBS had been pooled into CDOs, the CDOs had been distributed to investors around the world, and the entire structure was interconnected through CDS contracts and funded through the short-term wholesale markets of the shadow banking system (Session 9). The losses on the underlying mortgages would be amplified and transmitted through this structure in ways that the "contained" view failed to anticipate.

## The Bear Stearns hedge funds

The first institutional casualties of the subprime decline were two hedge funds managed by Bear Stearns Asset Management: the High-Grade Structured Credit Strategies Fund and the High-Grade Structured Credit Strategies Enhanced Leverage Fund. The funds had invested heavily in CDOs backed by subprime MBS, using significant leverage (the Enhanced Leverage Fund was leveraged approximately 20:1).

As the ABX index fell and the value of their CDO holdings declined, the funds' prime brokers (the banks that provided them with leverage through repo and margin lending) demanded additional collateral. The funds were unable to meet the collateral calls and attempted to sell their CDO holdings, but found no buyers: the market for CDOs had become illiquid. In June 2007, Bear Stearns injected $3.2 billion of its own capital into the funds to prevent a forced liquidation, but the effort failed. Both funds collapsed in July 2007, inflicting losses on their investors and on Bear Stearns itself.

The failure of the Bear Stearns hedge funds was a wake-up call. It demonstrated that CDOs that had been rated AAA could lose substantial value, that the market for structured credit products could become illiquid overnight, and that losses on subprime mortgages could threaten the solvency of major financial institutions. The incident foreshadowed Bear Stearns' own demise nine months later.

## The August 2007 freeze

The crisis escalated dramatically in August 2007, when the short-term funding markets that supported the shadow banking system froze.

The trigger was a series of announcements by European financial institutions that they had suffered losses on U.S. subprime-related investments. On August 9, 2007, BNP Paribas, one of France's largest banks, suspended redemptions from three investment funds that held U.S. subprime MBS, announcing that it was unable to value the funds' holdings because the market for those securities had ceased to function. The announcement sent a shock through the global financial system, because it revealed that subprime exposure was not confined to U.S. institutions but was embedded in the balance sheets of European banks and investment vehicles.

The BNP Paribas announcement triggered a freeze in the **interbank lending market**. Banks became unwilling to lend to each other, because they could not determine which of their counterparties held subprime-related assets and might therefore be insolvent. The LIBOR-OIS spread, which measures the difference between the rate at which banks lend to each other (LIBOR) and the risk-free rate (the overnight index swap rate), spiked from approximately 10 basis points (its normal level) to approximately 90 basis points in August 2007, the largest increase since the measure had been created. The LIBOR-OIS spread would become the barometer of the crisis, measuring the degree of fear and distrust in the interbank market.

The **ABCP market** froze simultaneously. Investors in asset-backed commercial paper, who had been rolling over their holdings every 30-90 days, refused to roll over when the paper matured. The outstanding stock of ABCP fell from approximately $1.2 trillion in July 2007 to approximately $800 billion by the end of 2007. The ABCP conduits and SIVs that had funded themselves with commercial paper were unable to refinance and were forced to draw on backup credit lines from their sponsoring banks, bringing the risk back onto the banks' balance sheets.

Central banks responded to the freeze with emergency liquidity injections. The ECB injected €95 billion into the euro-area money market on August 9, the largest single-day injection in its history. The Federal Reserve followed with its own liquidity operations. But the central bank interventions, while preventing an immediate collapse, did not address the underlying problem: the uncertainty about the location and magnitude of subprime losses that was destroying confidence in the financial system.

The August 2007 freeze was the beginning of the crisis proper. It demonstrated that the subprime problem was not contained; it was systemic. The short-term funding markets on which the shadow banking system depended had become fragile, and the fragility would intensify over the following year as the losses mounted and confidence continued to erode.
