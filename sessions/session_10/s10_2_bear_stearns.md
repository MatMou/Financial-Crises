---
title: "10.2 Bear Stearns and the March 2008 Rescue"
subtitle: The first domino
---

Bear Stearns, the fifth-largest U.S. investment bank, was the first major financial institution to be brought down by the crisis. Its near-failure in March 2008, and the Fed-arranged rescue that prevented its collapse, marked a critical escalation: the crisis had moved from the periphery of the financial system (hedge funds, ABCP conduits) to the core (the major investment banks). The Bear Stearns episode also set precedents, both for rescue and for the terms on which rescue would be offered, that would shape the decisions of the following September.

## Bear Stearns' vulnerabilities

Bear Stearns was particularly vulnerable to the crisis for several reasons. It was the most heavily leveraged of the major investment banks, with a leverage ratio of approximately 33:1 at the end of 2007. It had significant exposure to mortgage-related assets, including subprime MBS and CDOs, both through its own proprietary positions and through its role as a major prime broker for hedge funds that held similar positions. And it was heavily dependent on short-term repo funding to finance its balance sheet: approximately $50-75 billion of Bear Stearns' assets were financed through overnight or short-term repo agreements that had to be rolled over daily.

The dependence on repo funding made Bear Stearns acutely vulnerable to a loss of confidence. If repo counterparties refused to roll over their lending, Bear Stearns would have to find alternative funding within hours or face a liquidity crisis. The situation was precisely the maturity mismatch vulnerability that the Diamond-Dybvig model (subsection 2.4) describes: Bear Stearns had funded long-term, illiquid assets with short-term, runnable liabilities.

## The run on Bear Stearns

In early March 2008, rumors spread that Bear Stearns was experiencing liquidity difficulties. The rumors were partly self-fulfilling: as counterparties heard the rumors, they became reluctant to extend short-term credit to Bear Stearns, which worsened the firm's liquidity position, which confirmed the rumors. Hedge fund clients began withdrawing their assets from Bear Stearns' prime brokerage division. Repo counterparties increased their haircuts or refused to roll over their lending. Derivatives counterparties demanded additional collateral.

The run accelerated with extraordinary speed. On Monday, March 10, Bear Stearns' liquidity pool was approximately $18 billion, which appeared adequate. By Thursday, March 13, the liquidity pool had fallen to approximately $2 billion, and the firm was facing the prospect of being unable to meet its obligations when the markets opened on Friday. Bear Stearns' CEO, Alan Schwartz, called the Federal Reserve Bank of New York on Thursday evening to report that the firm would be unable to open for business the next day without emergency assistance.

## The rescue

The Fed acted with unprecedented speed. On Friday, March 14, the Fed extended an emergency loan to Bear Stearns through JPMorgan Chase (the Fed lent to JPMorgan, which lent to Bear Stearns, because the Fed did not have the legal authority to lend directly to an investment bank). The emergency loan was intended as a bridge to allow time for a more permanent solution.

Over the weekend of March 15-16, the Fed brokered the acquisition of Bear Stearns by JPMorgan Chase. JPMorgan initially offered $2 per share (Bear Stearns stock had traded above $170 a year earlier and had closed at $30 on Friday), a price that was later raised to $10 per share. The Fed facilitated the deal by providing $30 billion in non-recourse financing to JPMorgan to absorb potential losses on Bear Stearns' mortgage-related assets. The $30 billion was placed in a special purpose vehicle called Maiden Lane, and the Fed bore the risk of loss on the portfolio.

The Bear Stearns rescue was extraordinary in several respects. It was the first time since the Great Depression that the Federal Reserve had extended emergency credit to a non-bank financial institution. The legal authority for the lending was Section 13(3) of the Federal Reserve Act, which authorized the Fed to lend to non-banks "in unusual and exigent circumstances." The provision had not been invoked since the 1930s. The rescue established the principle that systemically important financial institutions, even those outside the traditional banking system, would not be allowed to fail if their failure threatened the stability of the financial system.

## The aftermath and the new facilities

The Bear Stearns rescue was accompanied by the creation of new Federal Reserve lending facilities designed to address the funding vulnerabilities that the crisis had revealed. The **Term Securities Lending Facility** (TSLF) allowed primary dealers (including investment banks) to borrow Treasury securities from the Fed in exchange for less liquid collateral, including mortgage-backed securities. The **Primary Dealer Credit Facility** (PDCF) provided overnight loans to primary dealers, effectively extending the Fed's discount window to investment banks for the first time.

These facilities addressed the immediate liquidity problem, but they did not address the solvency problem. The investment banks continued to hold enormous portfolios of mortgage-related assets whose values were declining. The market remained aware that several major institutions, particularly Lehman Brothers and Merrill Lynch, were in fragile condition. The Bear Stearns rescue had provided a temporary reprieve, but it had not resolved the underlying crisis. The reprieve would last six months, until the far more devastating events of September 2008.
