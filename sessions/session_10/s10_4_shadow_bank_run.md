title: "10.4 The Run on the Shadow Banking System"
subtitle: The 21st-century bank panic
---

The panic that followed Lehman's bankruptcy was most intense in the shadow banking system described in subsection 9.5. The run on the shadow banking system in September-October 2008 was the functional equivalent of the banking panics of 1930-1933 (subsection 5.2): a massive, self-reinforcing withdrawal of short-term funding that destroyed the ability of the financial system to intermediate credit and transmit payments. Understanding this run is essential for understanding both why the crisis was so severe and why the policy response took the form it did.

## The Reserve Primary Fund and the run on money market funds

On September 16, 2008, the Reserve Primary Fund, one of the oldest and largest money market funds in the United States with approximately $65 billion in assets, announced that its net asset value (NAV) had fallen below $1 per share, the event known as "breaking the buck." The fund held approximately $785 million in Lehman Brothers commercial paper, which had become worthless when Lehman filed for bankruptcy. The loss was sufficient to push the fund's NAV to $0.97 per share.

The Reserve Primary Fund was only the second money market fund in history to break the buck (the first was a small fund in 1994), and the announcement triggered a panic among money market fund investors. Over the following days, investors withdrew approximately $300 billion from prime money market funds (those that invested in commercial paper and other private-sector instruments), representing approximately 15% of the total assets of the prime money market fund industry. The withdrawals were concentrated in the first few days after the announcement and had the characteristics of a classic bank run: investors withdrew not because they had suffered losses (most had not) but because they feared that they would suffer losses if they did not withdraw before others.

The money market fund run transmitted the panic directly to the commercial paper market. Money market funds were the largest buyers of commercial paper, and their withdrawal of funds forced them to stop purchasing new commercial paper as it matured. The commercial paper market froze: issuers that had relied on rolling over their commercial paper (including many corporations that used commercial paper to finance their day-to-day operations) found themselves unable to refinance. The outstanding stock of commercial paper fell by approximately $300 billion in the three weeks following Lehman's bankruptcy.

## The repo run

The repo market experienced a similar run. Repo lenders (typically money market funds, securities lenders, and other institutional investors) became unwilling to lend against collateral that they feared might lose value. Repo haircuts, the discount applied to the collateral's market value, increased sharply. For structured credit products (MBS, CDOs), haircuts increased from approximately 5% to 50% or more, effectively making these securities unfinanceable in the repo market. Even for agency MBS (securities guaranteed by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, which the government had just committed to supporting), haircuts increased.

The increase in repo haircuts was equivalent to a massive margin call on the entire shadow banking system. Institutions that had been financing $100 of assets with $95 of repo borrowing and $5 of their own capital suddenly needed to finance those same assets with $50 of repo borrowing and $50 of their own capital. Since they did not have the additional capital, they were forced to sell assets to repay the repo borrowing. The forced asset sales depressed prices, which further increased haircuts, which forced further sales, in the self-reinforcing fire-sale spiral described in subsection 2.4.

{cite:t}`Gorton2010` described the repo run as a "panic" in the classic sense: a self-fulfilling run driven by the loss of confidence in the collateral that backed the repo transactions, analogous to the loss of confidence in bank solvency that drives traditional bank runs. The crucial difference was that the repo run occurred in the wholesale market, between financial institutions, rather than at the teller window, between depositors and their banks. But the dynamics were identical.

## The interbank market freeze

The interbank lending market, in which banks lend to each other on an unsecured basis, also froze. The LIBOR-OIS spread, which had spiked to 90 basis points in August 2007, surged to over 350 basis points in October 2008. The TED spread (the difference between three-month LIBOR and three-month Treasury bills) reached 450 basis points, the highest level since the measure had been created. Banks hoarded cash, refusing to lend to each other because they could not assess the solvency of their counterparties and because they feared they might need the cash to meet their own obligations.

The freeze in interbank lending was a manifestation of the **asymmetric information problem** that theory predicts will arise during financial crises (subsection 2.3). The complexity and opacity of the structured credit products on banks' balance sheets made it impossible for any institution to determine the true financial condition of its counterparties. The Akerlof "lemons" problem operated: because lenders could not distinguish between sound and unsound borrowers, they refused to lend to anyone, and the market collapsed.

## The scale of the run

The combined run on money market funds, commercial paper, repo, and interbank lending represented a withdrawal of short-term funding from the financial system on a scale comparable to the deposit runs of the 1930s, but operating at much greater speed. In the 1930s, the banking panics unfolded over months and years; in 2008, the shadow banking run reached its peak intensity within days of Lehman's bankruptcy. The acceleration reflected the instantaneous nature of electronic markets and the interconnection of the modern financial system.

The run destroyed the ability of the financial system to perform its core functions: maturity transformation, credit intermediation, and payment processing. Creditworthy borrowers, both financial and non-financial, found themselves unable to obtain short-term financing. The commercial paper market, which corporations relied on for working capital, was frozen. The repo market, which investment banks relied on to finance their balance sheets, was frozen. The interbank market, through which banks managed their daily liquidity needs, was frozen.

The run on the shadow banking system was the mechanism through which the subprime mortgage problem, which had originated in a specific segment of the U.S. housing market, was transformed into a global financial crisis. The securitization machine had dispersed the underlying mortgage risk throughout the financial system, and the shadow banking system's dependence on short-term funding meant that any loss of confidence, however localized its origin, could trigger a systemic run. The parallel with the banking panics of the 1930s was recognized at the time and informed the policy response, which is the subject of Session 11.
