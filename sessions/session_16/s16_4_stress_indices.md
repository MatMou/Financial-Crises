---
title: "16.4 Financial Stress Indices and Conditions Indices"
subtitle: Summarizing the state of the financial system in a single number
---

Financial stress indices (FSIs) and financial conditions indices (FCIs) are composite indicators that aggregate information from multiple financial market variables into a single summary measure of the state of the financial system. FSIs measure the degree of stress or dysfunction in financial markets; FCIs measure the degree of tightness or looseness of financial conditions. Both are used by central banks and market participants for real-time monitoring and for assessing the stance of financial and monetary policy.

## Financial stress indices

Financial stress indices are designed to measure the degree of dysfunction, illiquidity, or panic in financial markets. They typically combine indicators from several market segments: equity markets (volatility, equity risk premium), bond markets (credit spreads, term spreads), money markets (LIBOR-OIS spread, TED spread), and foreign exchange markets (exchange rate volatility). The indicators are standardized, weighted, and aggregated into a single composite measure.

The most widely used FSIs include the **St. Louis Fed Financial Stress Index** (STLFSI), the **Kansas City Fed Financial Stress Index** (KCFSI), the **ECB's Composite Indicator of Systemic Stress** (CISS), and the **Cleveland Fed Financial Stress Index**. These indices differ in their construction details (the specific indicators included, the weighting scheme, the aggregation method), but they produce broadly similar readings: they are low during calm periods, spike sharply during crises, and return to low levels as the crisis abates.

The CISS, developed by the ECB, incorporates an innovation that distinguishes it from simpler aggregation methods: it weights the individual stress indicators by the correlation between them. The logic is that systemic stress is not simply a function of stress in individual market segments; it is a function of the degree to which stress is correlated across segments. When stress is confined to a single market (for example, elevated volatility in equities while credit markets remain calm), the systemic implications are limited. When stress is simultaneously elevated across equities, credit, money markets, and foreign exchange, the systemic implications are far more severe. The CISS captures this correlation structure, making it a more informative measure of systemic stress than simple averages of individual indicators.

## Financial conditions indices

Financial conditions indices measure the overall stance of financial conditions facing the economy. They include variables that capture both the price and quantity of credit: interest rates (policy rates, mortgage rates, corporate bond yields), credit spreads, equity prices, exchange rates, and measures of credit availability (such as lending standards surveys). FCIs are broader than FSIs: they capture the full set of financial variables that affect economic activity, not just the stress component.

The **Chicago Fed National Financial Conditions Index** (NFCI) is the most widely cited U.S. financial conditions index. It is constructed from 105 indicators, grouped into three categories: risk (volatility, credit spreads), credit (lending conditions, loan growth), and leverage (debt ratios, margin requirements). The NFCI is normalized so that zero represents average conditions, positive values represent tighter-than-average conditions, and negative values represent looser-than-average conditions.

The **Goldman Sachs Financial Conditions Index** (GS FCI) is widely used in the private sector and includes the federal funds rate, the 10-year Treasury yield, the investment-grade corporate bond spread, the S&P 500 level, and the trade-weighted dollar. The GS FCI is designed to capture the combined effect of all financial variables on the outlook for GDP growth.

## Uses and limitations

FSIs and FCIs are useful for real-time monitoring: they provide a continuous, high-frequency reading of the state of the financial system that can be tracked in real time and compared to historical episodes. During the GFC, the STLFSI and the CISS both reached levels that were far above any previous reading, providing a clear quantitative signal that the financial system was under extreme stress. During the COVID crisis, the FSIs spiked sharply but returned to normal much faster than during the GFC, reflecting the speed and effectiveness of the policy response.

FCIs are also useful for assessing the stance of monetary policy. When the policy rate is at or near zero (as it was during much of the 2010s), the policy rate alone does not capture the full stance of monetary policy, because unconventional policies (QE, forward guidance) affect financial conditions through channels that are not reflected in the policy rate. FCIs capture these broader effects and provide a more complete picture of the monetary policy stance.

The limitations of FSIs and FCIs are important to acknowledge. They are coincident or slightly lagging indicators: they measure stress that is already happening, rather than predicting stress that is about to happen. They are constructed from market prices, which means they share the limitations of all market-based measures (backward-looking during calm periods, subject to the paradox of volatility). And they provide no information about the structural vulnerabilities that might produce the next crisis; they measure the symptoms of stress, not the underlying causes.
