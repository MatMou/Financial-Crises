---
title: "16.2 Credit-Based Indicators: The BIS Credit-to-GDP Gap"
subtitle: The single best predictor of banking crises
---

The single most reliable predictor of systemic banking crises, across countries and across historical periods, is the deviation of the credit-to-GDP ratio from its long-term trend. This finding, established by Borio and Lowe (2002) and confirmed by the Jordà-Schularick-Taylor macrohistory project (subsection 3.1), is the foundation of the credit-based approach to early warning and has been incorporated into the Basel III macroprudential framework.

## The credit-to-GDP gap

The **credit-to-GDP gap** is defined as the difference between the credit-to-GDP ratio and its long-term trend, typically estimated using a one-sided Hodrick-Prescott filter with a smoothing parameter of 400,000 (as recommended by the BIS). The gap captures the "excess" credit that has accumulated above what would be expected given the long-run trend of financial deepening.

The logic is straightforward. A rising credit-to-GDP ratio that stays close to its trend reflects the normal process of financial development: as economies grow and financial systems mature, the ratio of credit to GDP tends to increase. But when the ratio rises far above its trend, it indicates that credit is growing faster than the economy's capacity to absorb it, which is the hallmark of a credit boom. The credit boom is the condition that, as Minsky's framework (subsection 2.2) and the empirical evidence (subsection 3.1) demonstrate, is the most reliable precursor to a banking crisis.

## Empirical performance

The BIS has published cross-country studies showing that a credit-to-GDP gap exceeding 10 percentage points is a strong predictor of banking crises occurring within one to three years. The indicator correctly predicted the vast majority of the banking crises in the BIS's historical sample, including the crises in Japan (1990), the Nordic countries (1991), the Asian countries (1997), the United States (2007), Ireland (2008), and Spain (2008).

The indicator's performance metrics are strong relative to other early warning indicators. Its noise-to-signal ratio is among the lowest of any single indicator, meaning that it produces relatively few false alarms relative to the number of correct predictions. And its lead time (1-3 years before the crisis) is long enough to provide a potentially useful window for policy action.

The credit-to-GDP gap is not without limitations. It is a slow-moving indicator that captures the accumulation of imbalances over years rather than the acute stress that characterizes the crisis phase. It can produce false alarms in cases where the credit boom is associated with a structural change in the economy (such as financial liberalization) that justifies a permanent increase in the credit-to-GDP ratio. And it is essentially a univariate indicator that does not capture other dimensions of vulnerability (asset price misalignment, maturity mismatches, concentration of exposures).

## The Basel III countercyclical capital buffer

The credit-to-GDP gap has been incorporated into the Basel III macroprudential framework as the **guide** for setting the countercyclical capital buffer (CCyB). The CCyB is an additional capital requirement (of up to 2.5% of risk-weighted assets) that national authorities can impose on banks during periods of excessive credit growth, and that can be released during downturns to support lending.

The Basel Committee recommends that national authorities use the credit-to-GDP gap as the starting point for CCyB decisions, with a gap of 2 percentage points triggering consideration of a buffer buildup and a gap of 10 percentage points indicating that the buffer should be at or near its maximum. The CCyB is the most concrete application of early warning research to policy, and it represents the most significant macroprudential innovation of the post-GFC regulatory framework.

The implementation of the CCyB has been uneven across countries. Some countries (notably the United Kingdom, Sweden, and Switzerland) have activated the buffer; others (including the United States, which has never activated its CCyB) have not, reflecting differences in institutional frameworks, political constraints, and judgments about the state of the credit cycle. The reluctance to activate the buffer during boom periods, when it is politically costly and when the perceived need is lowest, illustrates the political economy challenges of macroprudential policy.

## Beyond the credit-to-GDP gap

Subsequent research has extended the credit-based approach in several directions. Jordà, Schularick, and Taylor (2015) showed that the interaction between credit growth and asset prices is a stronger predictor than either alone: credit booms accompanied by house price booms are particularly dangerous. The BIS has developed additional credit indicators, including the debt service ratio (which captures the burden of debt payments relative to income) and the property price gap (the deviation of real property prices from trend).

The decomposition of credit into household credit and corporate credit has also proven useful. Household credit booms (particularly mortgage credit) have been shown to be more dangerous than corporate credit booms, because household defaults have larger macroeconomic consequences (through the wealth effect and the balance sheet recession mechanisms of subsections 3.2 and 11.5).
