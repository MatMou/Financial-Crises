---
title: "16.6 The Limits of Early Warning: Why We See It Coming but Cannot Stop It"
subtitle: The gap between detection and prevention
---

The toolkit described in this session represents a substantial improvement over the pre-GFC state of the art. Credit-based indicators can identify the buildup of aggregate vulnerabilities with 1-3 years of lead time. Market-based systemic risk measures can identify the institutions that are most systemically important and most vulnerable. Financial stress indices can provide real-time monitoring of the degree of system-wide dysfunction. And market signals (yield curve inversions, credit spreads) provide information about the market's expectations of future conditions.

Yet despite this improved toolkit, the history of crises since 2007 suggests that detection is far easier than prevention. This subsection examines why.

## The false alarm problem

Early warning systems inevitably produce false alarms: they signal a crisis that does not materialize. The credit-to-GDP gap, for example, was elevated in several countries in the early 2000s that did not experience banking crises (because the credit boom was associated with genuine financial deepening rather than speculative excess). The yield curve has inverted on several occasions that were followed by only mild recessions or no recession at all.

False alarms are costly because they undermine the credibility of the warning system. If policymakers act on a warning (by tightening macroprudential policy, raising the CCyB, or issuing public warnings about risk) and the crisis does not materialize, they face political criticism for having imposed unnecessary costs. If policymakers ignore several consecutive false alarms, they may be caught off guard when a genuine crisis occurs. The "cry wolf" dynamic is a serious obstacle to the effective use of early warning systems.

## The Goodhart's Law problem

**Goodhart's Law** states that when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. If policymakers and market participants begin to use the credit-to-GDP gap as a primary indicator of crisis risk, the financial system may adapt in ways that reduce the indicator's predictive power. Credit may migrate from measured to unmeasured channels (from the banking system to the shadow banking system, for example), or risk may accumulate in forms that are not captured by credit data (such as derivatives exposure or contingent liabilities).

The Goodhart's Law problem is not merely theoretical. The pre-GFC risk models focused on observable balance sheet measures, which gave financial institutions an incentive to move risk off-balance-sheet (into SIVs, ABCP conduits, and CDS contracts) where it was not captured by the measures. The post-GFC shift toward credit-based and market-based systemic risk measures may similarly create incentives for risk to migrate to unmeasured channels.

## The political economy of action

The most fundamental limit of early warning systems is not technical but political. Even when the signals are clear and the vulnerabilities are identified, policymakers face enormous political obstacles to taking preemptive action.

During the buildup phase of a crisis, the boom is generating profits, employment, tax revenue, and political satisfaction. The beneficiaries of the boom (banks, property developers, borrowers, investors) are politically powerful and resist any measures that would constrain the boom. The costs of the boom (the vulnerabilities that are accumulating) are diffuse, uncertain, and future; the benefits are concentrated, visible, and present. The political calculus almost always favors inaction.

The parallel to the Greenspan dilemma of the late 1990s (subsection 8.1) is direct. Greenspan recognized that the stock market was overvalued ("irrational exuberance") but chose not to act, partly because the political costs of deflating the bubble (a recession) would have been borne immediately, while the costs of not deflating it (the eventual crash) were uncertain and distant. The same calculus applies to macroprudential policy: raising the CCyB, tightening lending standards, or issuing public warnings about a credit boom imposes immediate political costs, while the benefits (a crisis averted) are invisible because the counterfactual (what would have happened without the action) is unknowable.

## The "this time is different" problem

Even when the early warning signals are flashing, there is always a narrative that explains why the current situation is different from previous crises. The narrative may emphasize structural changes in the economy, financial innovation, improved risk management, or stronger institutions. The narrative is not always wrong: structural changes do occur, and not every credit boom ends in a crisis. But the narrative creates a cognitive bias that makes it psychologically difficult for policymakers and market participants to take the warning signals seriously.

The Reinhart-Rogoff "this time is different" syndrome (subsection 1.3) is the meta-lesson of early warning: the same conditions that produce crises also produce the narratives that explain why a crisis will not occur. The early warning toolkit can identify the conditions, but it cannot overcome the human tendency to believe that the conditions will not produce their historical consequences.

## Implication: resilience over prediction

The limits of early warning reinforce the conclusion of subsection 15.6: the most effective approach to crisis preparedness is to build resilience rather than to rely on prediction. Early warning systems are valuable for calibrating the degree of resilience (higher buffers when the indicators signal elevated risk, lower buffers when they signal calm), but they cannot substitute for a financial system that is designed to withstand shocks regardless of whether the shocks were predicted.

The history of financial crises is, at its core, a history of the failure of prediction. The toolkit described in this session can improve the odds, but it cannot eliminate the fundamental uncertainty that characterizes complex financial systems. The appropriate response to this uncertainty is not despair but humility: the recognition that crises will occur, that their specific form will be a surprise, and that the financial system must be robust enough to survive surprises.
