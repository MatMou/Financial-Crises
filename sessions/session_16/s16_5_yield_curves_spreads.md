---
title: "16.5 Yield Curves, Spreads, and Other Market Signals"
subtitle: What bond markets tell us about the future
---

Financial market prices contain information about investors' expectations of future economic and financial conditions. Several market-based indicators have demonstrated predictive power for recessions and financial crises, and they are widely monitored by central banks, market participants, and financial media. This subsection examines the most important of these indicators: yield curve inversions, credit spreads, and volatility measures.

## Yield curve inversions

The **yield curve** plots the relationship between the maturity of government bonds and their yields. Normally, the yield curve slopes upward: long-term bonds yield more than short-term bonds, because investors demand a premium for bearing the additional interest rate risk and inflation risk of longer-term investments. An **inverted** yield curve, in which short-term yields exceed long-term yields, has historically been one of the most reliable predictors of recessions.

Every U.S. recession since 1955 has been preceded by an inversion of the yield curve (specifically, the spread between the 10-year and 2-year Treasury yields or the 10-year and 3-month yields). The lead time has varied from approximately 6 to 24 months, with an average of approximately 12-18 months. The mechanism through which yield curve inversions predict recessions is debated. The expectations hypothesis of the term structure implies that an inverted curve reflects the market's expectation that the central bank will cut short-term rates in the future (because of a recession), which pushes long-term rates below current short-term rates. Alternatively, the inversion itself may have a causal effect on the economy: by reducing bank profitability (banks borrow short and lend long, so an inversion compresses their net interest margin), it may reduce lending and contribute to the recession.

The yield curve inverted in 2022-2023, with the 10-year minus 2-year spread reaching approximately minus 100 basis points, the deepest inversion since the early 1980s. The subsequent recession did not materialize through 2024-2025, leading some to question whether the yield curve's predictive power had diminished in the era of massive central bank bond purchases (which may have depressed long-term yields for reasons unrelated to the economic outlook).

## Credit spreads

**Credit spreads**, the difference between the yield on corporate bonds and the yield on comparable-maturity government bonds, reflect the market's assessment of default risk and the compensation investors demand for bearing that risk. Wider credit spreads indicate greater perceived risk; narrower spreads indicate lower perceived risk.

Credit spreads have predictive power for both recessions and financial crises. Investment-grade credit spreads typically widen 6-12 months before recessions, as the market anticipates deteriorating corporate creditworthiness. High-yield (junk bond) spreads are more volatile and tend to widen more sharply before severe recessions and financial crises.

The **TED spread** (the difference between three-month LIBOR and three-month Treasury bills) was one of the most closely watched indicators during the GFC. The TED spread captures the credit risk premium in the interbank market: a widening TED spread indicates that banks perceive increased credit risk in lending to other banks. The TED spread spiked to over 450 basis points during the panic of October 2008 (subsection 10.4), its highest level since the measure was created, providing a clear signal of extreme interbank stress.

The **LIBOR-OIS spread** (the difference between LIBOR and the overnight index swap rate) serves a similar function and has become the preferred measure of interbank stress since LIBOR's replacement by risk-free rates (SOFR in the United States). The LIBOR-OIS spread captures the pure credit risk component of interbank lending, because the OIS rate reflects only expected future policy rates and does not include credit risk.

## Volatility measures

The **VIX index**, calculated from S&P 500 option prices, measures the market's expectation of 30-day equity market volatility. The VIX is commonly referred to as the "fear index" because it tends to spike during periods of market stress and to be low during calm periods.

The VIX has limited predictive power for crises (it tends to rise only when the crisis is already underway), but it is useful as a real-time indicator of the degree of market stress. The VIX reached approximately 80 in October 2008 (during the GFC) and approximately 82 in March 2020 (during the COVID panic), both far above its long-run average of approximately 20.

A more subtle use of volatility information involves the **term structure of volatility**: the relationship between implied volatility at different horizons. When short-term volatility exceeds long-term volatility (an inverted volatility term structure), it typically signals that markets are experiencing acute stress but expect the stress to be temporary. When both short-term and long-term volatility are elevated, it signals that markets expect the stress to persist.

## The information content of market signals

Market signals are valuable but imperfect. Their information content depends on the efficiency of the markets that produce them: if market prices accurately reflect all available information, they provide the best available forecast of future conditions. But markets are not always efficient: they can be distorted by central bank intervention (QE has depressed long-term yields and may have reduced the information content of the yield curve), by herding and momentum trading (which can push prices away from fundamental values), and by the structural features of the markets themselves (such as the demand for long-dated bonds by pension funds and insurance companies, which can flatten the yield curve for reasons unrelated to economic expectations).

The most effective approach to early warning combines market signals with the credit-based indicators of subsection 16.2 and the systemic risk measures of subsection 16.3. No single indicator is sufficient; the value lies in the triangulation across multiple indicators, which provides a more robust picture of the evolving risk environment than any individual measure.
