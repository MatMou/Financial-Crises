---
title: "5.1 The Roaring Twenties and the Interwar Gold Standard"
subtitle: The boom that preceded the bust
---

The Great Depression did not begin in October 1929 with the stock market crash. Its roots lay in the structural imbalances that accumulated during the 1920s: the flawed reconstruction of the international gold standard, the unresolved debts from World War I, and the credit and speculative boom in the United States. Understanding these preconditions is essential for understanding why the initial downturn was so severe and why the policy response was so inadequate.

## The aftermath of World War I

World War I shattered the classical gold standard that had operated, with considerable success, from 1870 to 1914 (Session 4). The belligerent countries suspended gold convertibility to finance the war through money creation and borrowing. Price levels roughly doubled in Britain and tripled in France and Germany between 1914 and 1918. The distribution of the world's gold stock shifted dramatically: the United States, which had entered the war late and had been paid in gold for its exports of goods and war materiel, held approximately 40% of the world's monetary gold by the early 1920s, compared to approximately 25% before the war.

The war also created a tangled web of intergovernmental debts that would poison international economic relations throughout the 1920s. The Allied powers owed the United States approximately $10 billion (an enormous sum at the time) in war debts. Germany owed the Allied powers reparations under the Treaty of Versailles, eventually fixed at 132 billion gold marks (approximately $33 billion) by the London Schedule of Payments in 1921. The implicit assumption underlying the settlement was that Germany would pay reparations to France and Britain, which would use the proceeds to repay their war debts to the United States. This triangular flow of payments created a system of mutual dependence that was highly vulnerable to disruption.

Germany's attempt to meet its reparations obligations through money creation contributed to the hyperinflation of 1922-1923, during which the German mark became effectively worthless (the exchange rate reached 4.2 trillion marks per dollar by November 1923). The hyperinflation was eventually stabilized through the introduction of a new currency (the Rentenmark) and the Dawes Plan of 1924, which restructured reparations payments and facilitated large American loans to Germany. The Dawes Plan created a circular flow: American banks lent money to Germany, Germany paid reparations to France and Britain, and France and Britain used the proceeds to repay their war debts to the United States. The system depended entirely on the continued willingness of American investors to lend to Germany, which would prove to be its fatal weakness.

## The return to gold

The dominant economic policy objective of the 1920s was the restoration of the gold standard. Policymakers, particularly in Britain, regarded the prewar gold standard as the natural foundation of international monetary order and believed that its restoration was essential for the recovery of international trade and investment. The question was not whether to return to gold, but at what exchange rate.

Britain's decision, in April 1925, to restore the pound to its prewar gold parity of $4.86 was the most consequential and most controversial of these decisions. The pound had depreciated significantly during the war and the postwar period, and restoring the prewar parity required deflation: British prices had to fall relative to American prices to make the pound's exchange rate sustainable at the prewar level. Winston Churchill, then Chancellor of the Exchequer, made the decision against the advice of John Maynard Keynes, who published a devastating critique in *The Economic Consequences of Mr. Churchill* (1925), arguing that the overvalued pound would depress British exports, increase unemployment, and require a painful and unnecessary deflation.

Keynes was right. The overvalued pound depressed British exports, particularly coal and textiles, and contributed to chronic unemployment and industrial unrest throughout the late 1920s. The Bank of England was forced to maintain high interest rates to defend the gold parity, which further depressed domestic activity. Britain's experience in the late 1920s demonstrates the cost of returning to gold at an overvalued exchange rate, a cost that Keynes had predicted and that contemporary proponents of the gold standard were unwilling to acknowledge.

France took the opposite approach. After a period of inflation and currency depreciation, France stabilized the franc in 1926 at approximately one-fifth of its prewar gold value. The undervalued franc made French exports highly competitive and attracted gold inflows. France accumulated enormous gold reserves during the late 1920s, effectively sterilizing the inflows (failing to expand the money supply in proportion to the gold inflow) and thereby imposing deflationary pressure on the rest of the world. The asymmetry between France's undervalued franc and Britain's overvalued pound was one of the fundamental structural imbalances of the interwar gold standard.

## The American boom

While Europe struggled with the aftermath of the war and the constraints of the restored gold standard, the United States experienced a remarkable economic expansion. The 1920s saw rapid growth in industrial production, rising real wages, the spread of new consumer technologies (automobiles, radios, household appliances), and a construction boom, particularly in Florida and in the growing suburbs of major cities. The expansion was fueled by rising productivity (output per worker grew at approximately 5% per year in manufacturing), by the adoption of new production methods (the assembly line, scientific management), and by the expansion of consumer credit (installment buying).

The stock market boom that accompanied the economic expansion was extraordinary. The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose from approximately 63 in August 1921 to a peak of 381 in September 1929, a six-fold increase. Much of the gain was concentrated in the final two years: the index roughly doubled between early 1928 and September 1929. Stock market speculation was fueled by the widespread availability of margin lending: investors could purchase stocks by putting up as little as 10% of the purchase price, borrowing the rest from brokers, who in turn borrowed from banks. The total volume of brokers' loans rose from approximately $3.5 billion in 1927 to $8.5 billion in October 1929.

The Federal Reserve, created only fifteen years earlier (subsection 4.4), faced a dilemma. The stock market boom was clearly speculative, and Fed officials were concerned that the speculative excess would end badly. But raising interest rates to cool the stock market would also depress the real economy and, because of the gold standard, would attract gold from abroad, putting pressure on other countries' gold reserves and forcing them to raise their own interest rates. The Fed vacillated: it raised the discount rate modestly in 1928-1929 and issued warnings about speculative excess, but it did not take decisive action. The debate within the Fed, between those who favored direct action to restrict credit to speculators and those who favored indirect action through the discount rate, was never resolved.

## The international context

The international monetary system of the late 1920s was fragile in ways that were not fully appreciated at the time. Several structural vulnerabilities had accumulated.

First, the gold exchange standard that replaced the prewar gold standard allowed central banks to hold foreign exchange (primarily dollars and sterling) as reserves alongside gold, which economized on gold but created a pyramid of claims that was vulnerable to a loss of confidence. If central banks that held dollar or sterling reserves decided to convert them into gold, the resulting drain on U.S. and British gold reserves could force those countries to tighten monetary policy, transmitting deflationary pressure to the rest of the world.

Second, the distribution of gold was severely unequal. The United States and France together held the majority of the world's monetary gold. Other countries, particularly Germany and the countries of Central and Eastern Europe, held thin gold reserves and were heavily dependent on short-term foreign capital (primarily from the United States) to maintain their gold standard commitments. Any disruption to the flow of American capital would force these countries into painful adjustment.

Third, the interwar gold standard lacked the informal mechanisms of cooperation and adjustment that had made the prewar system function (subsection 4.2). The Bank of England was no longer the undisputed conductor of the system; the Federal Reserve was powerful but inward-looking; and the Banque de France, flush with gold, was uncooperative. International monetary cooperation was sporadic and ineffective, hampered by political tensions (the reparations dispute), doctrinal disagreements, and the absence of institutional mechanisms for coordination.

These structural vulnerabilities meant that the international monetary system was highly sensitive to a downturn in the United States. When the American boom ended in 1929, the consequences would be transmitted rapidly to the rest of the world through the gold standard's adjustment mechanism, with devastating results.
