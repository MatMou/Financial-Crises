---
title: "5.2 The Crash of 1929 and the Banking Crises of 1930-1933"
subtitle: How a recession became a depression
---

The stock market crash of October 1929 did not, by itself, cause the Great Depression. Stock market crashes are painful but not necessarily catastrophic: the crash of 1987 was comparable in magnitude but produced no recession. What made the 1929 crash different was that it triggered a sequence of banking crises that destroyed the credit system and turned a sharp but potentially manageable recession into the most severe economic contraction in modern history. Understanding this sequence, from stock market crash to banking panics to credit collapse to depression, is essential for understanding both the Great Depression itself and the policy response to the 2007-2009 crisis, which was explicitly designed to prevent a repetition.

## The crash

The stock market peaked on September 3, 1929, when the Dow Jones Industrial Average closed at 381.17. The initial decline was gradual, but panic set in on "Black Thursday," October 24, when the market fell sharply on very heavy volume. A consortium of bankers, organized by Thomas Lamont of J.P. Morgan, intervened to support prices, echoing J.P. Morgan's role in the Panic of 1907 (subsection 4.4). The intervention provided temporary relief, but the selling resumed the following week. On "Black Monday" (October 28), the Dow fell 13%, and on "Black Tuesday" (October 29), it fell a further 12% on record volume of approximately 16.4 million shares. By mid-November, the Dow had fallen to approximately 198, a decline of nearly 50% from the September peak.

The crash destroyed an enormous amount of wealth. Margin investors, who had purchased stocks with borrowed money, were wiped out. The total loss in market value between September and November 1929 was approximately $30 billion, roughly equal to the entire annual GDP of the United States at the time. The collapse of margin lending reduced the availability of credit in the broader economy, and the negative wealth effect depressed consumer spending.

But the crash alone does not explain the Depression. The stock market had experienced comparable declines before (the Panic of 1907 produced a fall of approximately 50%) without producing a depression. The critical question is: what transformed the post-crash recession into a catastrophic depression?

## The first banking crisis: late 1930

The answer lies in the banking system. The United States in 1930 had approximately 24,000 commercial banks, most of them small, single-office institutions serving rural communities and small towns. These banks were poorly diversified, thinly capitalized, and heavily exposed to local economic conditions. They did not have access to the kind of interbank support networks that existed in countries with more concentrated banking systems (such as Britain and Canada), and their access to Federal Reserve lending was often limited (many small banks were not members of the Federal Reserve System).

The first wave of bank failures began in late 1930. The immediate trigger was the failure of Caldwell and Company, a large investment banking firm based in Nashville, Tennessee, in November 1930. Caldwell's failure brought down a network of affiliated banks across the South, and the panic spread to other banks in the region. In December 1930, the Bank of United States, a large New York commercial bank with over 400,000 depositors, failed. Despite its name (which was misleading, as it was a private institution), the Bank of United States was not rescued. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York considered organizing a rescue but was unable to secure the cooperation of the other New York banks, partly because of the social dynamics of the banking community (the Bank of United States served primarily Jewish immigrants and small businesses, and the establishment banks were reluctant to extend themselves on its behalf).

The failure of the Bank of United States was a watershed moment. {cite:t}`FriedmanSchwartz1963` argued that the Fed's failure to prevent the Bank of United States from failing was a critical policy error that transformed a moderate recession into a severe contraction. The failure demonstrated to the public that the Federal Reserve would not necessarily protect depositors, which undermined confidence in the banking system and set the stage for further panics.

## The second and third banking crises: 1931-1933

A second wave of bank failures struck in the spring of 1931, concentrated in the Midwest and West. The failures were driven by the deepening agricultural depression (farm prices had been falling since the mid-1920s, and the general deflation made farm debt increasingly burdensome) and by the general deterioration of economic conditions.

The crisis became international in May 1931 with the failure of the Credit-Anstalt, Austria's largest bank, which held approximately two-thirds of Austrian bank assets. The Credit-Anstalt's failure set off a chain of banking crises that spread from Austria to Germany (where the Darmstädter und Nationalbank failed in July 1931), to Hungary, and eventually to Britain, which was forced to abandon the gold standard in September 1931 (discussed in subsection 5.3). The international dimension of the crisis will be treated more fully below; what matters here is the feedback to the United States. When Britain left the gold standard, fears arose that the United States might do the same, and foreign holders of dollar assets rushed to convert them into gold. The Federal Reserve responded by raising the discount rate sharply (from 1.5% to 3.5% in October 1931) to defend the gold parity, which further tightened credit conditions and deepened the domestic contraction.

A third and final wave of bank failures swept the United States in early 1933. By February 1933, bank runs had become so severe that state governors began declaring "bank holidays," closing banks to prevent further withdrawals. By March 4, 1933, when Franklin Roosevelt was inaugurated as President, virtually every bank in the country was closed. The banking system had effectively ceased to function.

## The scale of destruction

The cumulative damage to the banking system between 1930 and 1933 was staggering. Approximately 9,000 banks failed, representing roughly one-third of all commercial banks in the country. Total bank deposits fell from approximately $50 billion in 1929 to approximately $34 billion in 1933. The money supply (M1) contracted by approximately one-third. The contraction of the money supply and the destruction of the banking system's ability to intermediate between savers and borrowers was, in the view of most economists, the primary mechanism through which the initial recession was transformed into a depression.

The banking collapse operated through both the quantity and the quality of credit. The quantity effect was the direct contraction of bank lending as banks failed, were forced to liquidate assets, or hoarded reserves to meet potential withdrawals. The quality effect, emphasized by {cite:t}`Bernanke1983`, was the destruction of the information capital embodied in bank-borrower relationships. When a bank failed, the specific knowledge it had accumulated about its borrowers, their creditworthiness, their business prospects, and their character, was lost. This knowledge could not be easily transferred to another institution or recreated quickly. The result was that even solvent borrowers found themselves unable to obtain credit, because no lender had the information needed to evaluate their creditworthiness. The destruction of the banking system's information capital was, in Bernanke's analysis, the mechanism through which the financial crisis was transmitted to the real economy with such devastating effect.

## The Federal Reserve's failure

The Federal Reserve's response to the banking crises of 1930-1933 is one of the most studied and most criticized episodes in the history of central banking. The Fed had been created specifically to prevent the kind of banking panics that had plagued the American financial system (subsection 4.4), and yet it stood by while the banking system collapsed.

The reasons for the Fed's inaction are multiple and debated. The institutional structure of the Federal Reserve System, with its 12 semi-autonomous regional Reserve Banks and a weak Federal Reserve Board in Washington, made coordinated action difficult. The dominant figure at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, Benjamin Strong, who had favored aggressive intervention to support the banking system, had died in 1928, and his successors were less decisive and less influential. The prevailing economic doctrine, which held that bank failures were a necessary purging of weak institutions and that monetary expansion during a contraction would be inflationary and would undermine confidence, discouraged intervention. And the gold standard constrained the Fed's ability to expand the money supply: any expansion that was not backed by gold reserves risked triggering a gold outflow that would force further contraction.

The Fed's failure to act as a lender of last resort during the banking crises of 1930-1933 is the central exhibit in the Friedman-Schwartz monetary explanation of the Great Depression, which is discussed in detail in subsection 5.4. Whether the Fed could have prevented the Depression through more aggressive action remains one of the most debated counterfactual questions in economic history. But there is broad agreement that the Fed's inaction made the crisis far worse than it needed to be, and the lesson that central banks must act aggressively during banking crises was the single most important policy lesson drawn from the Depression, a lesson that was applied, with considerable success, during the 2007-2009 crisis.
