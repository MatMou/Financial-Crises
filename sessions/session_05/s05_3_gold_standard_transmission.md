---
title: "5.3 The Gold Standard as Transmission Mechanism"
subtitle: How golden fetters spread the depression worldwide
---

The Great Depression was not merely an American event. It spread to virtually every country in the world, producing the most synchronized and severe global contraction in recorded history. Understanding how the crisis was transmitted from the United States to the rest of the world, and why some countries suffered more than others, requires understanding the role of the interwar gold standard as the primary transmission mechanism.

The definitive analysis of this transmission was provided by Barry Eichengreen in *Golden Fetters: The Gold Standard and the Great Depression, 1919-1939* ({cite:t}`Eichengreen1992`). Eichengreen's central argument is that the gold standard was not merely an innocent bystander during the Depression; it was the principal mechanism through which the deflationary shock originating in the United States was transmitted to the rest of the world, and the principal constraint that prevented governments from adopting the expansionary policies needed to combat the contraction.

## The transmission mechanism

The interwar gold standard transmitted the American contraction to the rest of the world through several reinforcing channels.

The **monetary channel** was the most direct. Under the gold standard, each country's money supply was linked to its gold reserves. When the Federal Reserve raised interest rates in 1928-1929 to cool the stock market boom, and when the post-crash recession reduced American imports, gold flowed from other countries to the United States (attracted by higher American interest rates and the improving U.S. trade balance). Countries losing gold were forced to contract their own money supplies to maintain the gold parity, which raised their interest rates, reduced their credit, and depressed their economies. The deflation that began in the United States was thus exported to the rest of the world through the gold standard mechanism.

The **capital flow channel** amplified the monetary channel. As discussed in subsection 5.1, the late-1920s international monetary system depended on the flow of American capital to debtor countries, particularly Germany and the countries of Central and Eastern Europe. When the stock market boom diverted American capital from foreign lending to domestic speculation, and when the post-crash recession reduced American investors' appetite for foreign assets, the flow of capital reversed. Countries that had been dependent on American capital faced sudden stops (subsection 3.3), forcing them into abrupt and painful adjustment. Germany was the most dramatic case: American lending to Germany virtually ceased in 1929, and the reversal of capital flows forced Germany into a severe fiscal and monetary contraction that deepened the already severe recession.

The **trade channel** operated through the collapse of international trade. As countries contracted their economies and raised tariffs to protect domestic industries, their imports fell, which reduced the export revenues of their trading partners, which forced further contraction abroad. The Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act, signed by President Hoover in June 1930, raised U.S. tariffs to their highest levels in over a century and provoked retaliatory tariffs by other countries. While the direct impact of Smoot-Hawley on U.S. trade was modest (U.S. trade was a relatively small fraction of GDP), the Act's symbolic effect was significant: it signaled that the United States was retreating into protectionism, which undermined international economic cooperation and encouraged other countries to follow suit. International trade fell by approximately two-thirds between 1929 and 1933.

The **confidence channel** operated through the banking system. When the Credit-Anstalt failed in May 1931, it set off a chain of banking crises that spread rapidly across Europe. The failure demonstrated that even large, seemingly stable banks could collapse, and depositors throughout Europe rushed to withdraw their funds. The banking crises forced governments to choose between supporting their banking systems (which required expansionary monetary policy) and defending the gold standard (which required contractionary monetary policy). Most chose the gold standard, at least initially, with devastating consequences for their banking systems and their economies.

## Britain's departure from gold

The most consequential event in the international spread of the Depression was Britain's departure from the gold standard on September 21, 1931. Britain had been struggling to maintain the overvalued pound since 1925 (subsection 5.1), and the European banking crises of 1931 put intolerable pressure on sterling. Foreign holders of sterling assets, alarmed by the banking crises on the Continent and by Britain's growing fiscal difficulties, began converting their sterling into gold. The Bank of England's gold reserves fell rapidly, and despite emergency loans from the Federal Reserve Bank of New York and the Banque de France, the reserves proved insufficient.

The Labour government of Ramsay MacDonald fell over the question of how to respond (the cabinet was split over proposed cuts to unemployment benefits), and a new National Government was formed. The new government attempted to defend the gold standard through austerity measures, but a mutiny by Royal Navy sailors at Invergordon (protesting pay cuts) in September 1931 was interpreted by markets as a sign of social instability, and the run on sterling accelerated. On September 21, the government suspended gold convertibility.

The immediate effect of Britain's departure from gold was a depreciation of sterling by approximately 25% against the dollar. This depreciation improved British competitiveness and, combined with the relaxation of the monetary policy constraint, allowed interest rates to be reduced and the money supply to expand. Britain's recovery from the Depression began almost immediately, and it was one of the first major economies to return to its pre-Depression level of output.

Britain's departure from gold also had a cascading effect on other countries. Sterling's depreciation put competitive pressure on countries that remained on the gold standard, because their exports became less competitive relative to British goods. Many countries, particularly those in the British Commonwealth and Scandinavia, followed Britain off gold within weeks or months. Others, particularly France and the countries of the "gold bloc" (Belgium, the Netherlands, Switzerland, Italy, Poland), attempted to maintain the gold standard, which required severe deflation and produced deeper and more prolonged recessions.

## The evidence: gold standard adherence and economic performance

The strongest evidence for the gold standard's role in the Depression comes from the cross-country comparison of economic performance, which shows a clear relationship between the timing of departure from the gold standard and the timing of economic recovery.

Countries that left the gold standard early (Britain in September 1931, the Scandinavian countries shortly thereafter, Japan in December 1931) experienced shorter and milder recessions and earlier recoveries. Countries that clung to gold the longest (France, Belgium, the Netherlands, which did not devalue until 1935-1936) experienced deeper and more prolonged contractions. The United States, which effectively devalued in 1933-1934 when Roosevelt raised the dollar price of gold from $20.67 to $35 per ounce, began its recovery in 1933.

{cite:t}`Eichengreen1992` and subsequent research by Eichengreen and Jeffrey Sachs documented this pattern systematically, showing that the cross-country variation in the severity and duration of the Depression is strongly correlated with the timing of gold standard departure. Countries that devalued earlier experienced larger increases in their money supplies, lower real interest rates, and faster output recoveries. The relationship is robust to controlling for other factors (initial conditions, trade exposure, political regime) and constitutes the strongest evidence that the gold standard was a central causal factor in the international spread and persistence of the Depression.

## The gold standard and the "golden fetters"

Eichengreen's metaphor of "golden fetters" captures the essence of the gold standard's role in the Depression. The gold standard did not cause the initial downturn (which had its origins in the U.S. stock market boom and the subsequent credit contraction), but it transformed a recession into a global depression by preventing governments from adopting the expansionary monetary policies that would have been appropriate. Central banks that might have cut interest rates, expanded the money supply, and lent freely to distressed banks were instead forced to raise rates, contract credit, and hoard gold to defend their exchange rate parities. The gold standard was a straitjacket that prevented the patient from receiving the treatment it needed.

The interwar gold standard differed from the classical gold standard in ways that made it more fragile. The classical gold standard had benefited from the Bank of England's role as the conductor of the system, from the relative absence of large-scale capital flows driven by speculation or fear, and from the political conditions that allowed governments to impose the costs of adjustment on populations that had limited political voice. The interwar gold standard had none of these advantages: there was no conductor (the Federal Reserve was inward-looking, the Bank of England was weakened, the Banque de France was unhelpful), capital flows were large and volatile, and democratic electorates were increasingly unwilling to tolerate the deflation and unemployment that the gold standard demanded.

The lesson of the interwar gold standard, which Eichengreen's work made explicit, is that fixed exchange rate regimes are dangerous when they prevent governments from responding to macroeconomic shocks. This lesson informed the design of the Bretton Woods system (Session 6), which combined fixed but adjustable exchange rates with capital controls, and it remains relevant to the analysis of the Eurozone crisis (Session 12), where the common currency functioned, for the peripheral countries, as a set of golden fetters remarkably similar to those of the 1930s.
