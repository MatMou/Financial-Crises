---
title: "5.6 Institutional Legacy: The New Deal Financial Architecture"
subtitle: The regulatory innovations that shaped the postwar financial system
---

The Great Depression produced the most comprehensive overhaul of financial regulation in American history. The reforms enacted between 1933 and 1940, collectively known as the New Deal financial architecture, addressed the specific vulnerabilities that the Depression had revealed and established the institutional framework within which the postwar financial system would operate for the next half century. This subsection examines the major regulatory innovations, assesses their effectiveness, and traces their eventual erosion and dismantling in the decades before the global financial crisis of 2007-2009.

## Deposit insurance: the FDIC

The creation of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) in the Banking Act of 1933 was the single most important regulatory innovation of the New Deal era. Deposit insurance addressed the fundamental vulnerability revealed by the banking panics of 1930-1933: the coordination failure among depositors modeled by Diamond and Dybvig (subsection 2.4). If depositors know that their deposits are guaranteed by the government, they have no incentive to run on a bank, even if they fear that other depositors might run. The run equilibrium is eliminated, and only the good equilibrium survives.

The FDIC initially insured deposits up to $2,500 per depositor (later raised repeatedly, to $250,000 after the 2008 crisis). The insurance was funded by premiums paid by member banks, not by taxpayer funds, which gave it a degree of political legitimacy that a taxpayer-funded guarantee might not have had.

The creation of the FDIC was opposed by many in the banking establishment, including President Roosevelt himself, who initially preferred a temporary guarantee. The opposition reflected concerns about moral hazard: if depositors were protected regardless of their bank's riskiness, they would have no incentive to monitor the bank, and banks would have an incentive to take excessive risks. These concerns were legitimate and would prove prophetic in the savings and loan crisis of the 1980s (where deposit insurance contributed to excessive risk-taking by S&L institutions). But in the immediate context of the early 1930s, the benefits of deposit insurance in preventing bank runs far outweighed the moral hazard costs.

The evidence suggests that deposit insurance was extraordinarily effective. The banking panics that had been a regular feature of American economic life for over a century (subsections 4.3 and 4.4) essentially ceased after the creation of the FDIC. Between 1934 and 2007, there was no nationwide banking panic in the United States, a remarkable contrast with the pre-1934 era. Deposit insurance did not prevent individual bank failures (which continued to occur, sometimes in large numbers, as during the S&L crisis), but it prevented the contagious, self-fulfilling runs that had transformed individual bank failures into systemic crises.

## The separation of commercial and investment banking: Glass-Steagall

The Banking Act of 1933 (commonly known as Glass-Steagall after its congressional sponsors) mandated the separation of commercial banking and investment banking. Commercial banks, which accepted deposits and made loans, were prohibited from underwriting or dealing in securities. Investment banks, which underwrote securities and traded for their own account, were prohibited from accepting deposits.

The rationale for the separation was the belief, widely held during the Depression, that the combination of commercial and investment banking had been a source of instability. The argument was that banks that both accepted deposits and traded securities had an incentive to use depositors' funds to finance speculative trading, and that the losses from such trading had contributed to the wave of bank failures. The separation was intended to protect the payments system and the deposit base from the risks of securities trading.

The empirical basis for the Glass-Steagall separation was weaker than its proponents believed. Subsequent research found little evidence that the combination of commercial and investment banking had been a significant source of bank failures during the Depression. Banks that had securities affiliates did not fail at higher rates than banks that did not. Nevertheless, the separation served an important symbolic and practical function: it simplified the banking system, made it easier to supervise, and reduced the scope for conflicts of interest between a bank's lending activities and its securities activities.

Glass-Steagall was gradually eroded beginning in the 1980s, as commercial banks lobbied for permission to engage in a wider range of activities and as financial innovation created new instruments that blurred the boundaries between banking and securities. The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act of 1999 formally repealed the Glass-Steagall separation, allowing the creation of financial holding companies that could engage in both commercial banking and investment banking. Whether the repeal of Glass-Steagall contributed to the 2007-2009 crisis is debated; the crisis was centered on institutions (investment banks, non-bank mortgage lenders, government-sponsored enterprises) that were not covered by Glass-Steagall. But the broader trend of deregulation and de-segmentation that the repeal symbolized is widely viewed as a contributing factor.

## Securities regulation: the SEC

The Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 created the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and established the framework for securities regulation that remains in place today. The Acts required public companies to disclose detailed financial information to investors, prohibited fraud and manipulation in securities markets, and established rules for the issuance and trading of securities.

The rationale was straightforward: the speculative excesses of the 1920s had been facilitated by the absence of reliable information about the companies whose shares were being traded. Investors had made decisions based on rumor, hype, and manipulation rather than on accurate financial data. The Securities Acts addressed this information failure by requiring companies to file regular financial reports with the SEC, by requiring audited financial statements, and by establishing civil and criminal penalties for fraud and manipulation.

The SEC represented a new approach to financial regulation: regulation through disclosure. Rather than attempting to determine which securities were safe for investors to buy (which would have been both impractical and inconsistent with a market economy), the SEC sought to ensure that investors had access to the information needed to make their own informed decisions. The philosophy was captured in Justice Louis Brandeis's famous dictum: "Sunlight is said to be the best of disinfectants."

## Monetary and exchange rate reform

The Gold Reserve Act of 1934 nationalized the gold stock, devalued the dollar, and established the Exchange Stabilization Fund to manage the dollar's exchange rate. The Act effectively ended the domestic gold standard, though gold continued to play a role in international monetary relations.

The Banking Act of 1935 restructured the Federal Reserve System, strengthening the Federal Reserve Board (renamed the Board of Governors) and creating the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) as the principal body for monetary policy decisions. The Act centralized monetary policy authority in Washington, reducing the power of the regional Reserve Banks (particularly the Federal Reserve Bank of New York) that had exercised considerable autonomy under the original Federal Reserve Act. The restructuring reflected the lesson that the decentralized structure of the original Federal Reserve System had contributed to the paralysis during the banking crises: a more centralized system with clearer lines of authority would, it was hoped, be more capable of decisive action in future crises.

## The Bretton Woods system

The most far-reaching institutional legacy of the Depression was the Bretton Woods system, negotiated at a conference in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, in July 1944 and established after the war. The system was designed by John Maynard Keynes (representing Britain) and Harry Dexter White (representing the United States) to create an international monetary order that would avoid the mistakes of the interwar period.

The Bretton Woods system combined three elements: fixed but adjustable exchange rates (currencies were pegged to the dollar, which was pegged to gold at $35 per ounce, but the pegs could be adjusted in the event of a "fundamental disequilibrium"), capital controls (which limited the flow of speculative capital across borders), and international institutions to manage the system (the International Monetary Fund, which provided short-term financing to countries with balance-of-payments difficulties, and the World Bank, which provided long-term development finance).

The design of Bretton Woods reflected the lessons of the Depression in several ways. Fixed exchange rates were retained because the competitive devaluations of the 1930s were seen as destructive and destabilizing. But the pegs were adjustable, unlike the rigid gold standard, which allowed countries to escape the "golden fetters" if their exchange rate became unsustainable. Capital controls were imposed because the experience of the 1930s had shown that speculative capital flows could overwhelm fixed exchange rates and force inappropriate monetary policies. And the IMF was created because the Depression had demonstrated the need for an international institution that could provide liquidity to countries in difficulty, rather than leaving them to choose between painful deflation and disorderly devaluation.

The Bretton Woods system operated from 1945 to 1971 and presided over the most sustained period of economic growth and financial stability in modern history. It is the subject of Session 6.

## The erosion of the New Deal architecture

The New Deal financial architecture was remarkably durable. For approximately half a century, from the 1930s to the 1980s, the regulatory framework established during the Depression constrained the financial system and, by most accounts, contributed to the absence of major systemic financial crises during that period. This era of relative stability, which the Reinhart-Rogoff data (subsection 1.5) confirm as an unusual period of calm in the long history of financial crises, is sometimes called the "quiet period" of finance.

The quiet period ended in the 1980s, as the New Deal regulations were progressively relaxed in response to a combination of political pressure from the financial industry, ideological shifts toward market-oriented economics, and technological changes that made the old regulatory boundaries harder to enforce. The deregulation of savings and loan institutions in the early 1980s, the gradual erosion of Glass-Steagall culminating in its repeal in 1999, the exemption of over-the-counter derivatives from regulation in 2000, and the weakening of capital requirements and leverage limits all contributed to the buildup of the vulnerabilities that produced the global financial crisis of 2007-2009.

The arc from New Deal regulation to deregulation to crisis illustrates Minsky's insight that stability breeds instability. The very success of the New Deal architecture in preventing crises contributed to the belief that the regulations were unnecessary, that financial markets were self-correcting, and that innovation had rendered the old risks obsolete. The "this time is different" syndrome (subsection 1.3) operated not only in financial markets but also in the regulatory sphere: policymakers and regulators came to believe that the vulnerabilities of the 1930s had been permanently overcome, which made them willing to dismantle the protections that had been put in place specifically to address those vulnerabilities.
