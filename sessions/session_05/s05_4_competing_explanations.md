title: "5.4 Competing Explanations"
subtitle: Friedman-Schwartz, Keynes, and Bernanke
---

The Great Depression has generated one of the most sustained and productive intellectual debates in the history of economics. Three main explanations have been offered, each emphasizing a different mechanism and drawing different policy implications. This subsection presents each explanation, identifies its strengths and limitations, and argues that a comprehensive understanding of the Depression requires elements of all three.

## The Friedman-Schwartz monetary explanation

The most influential single work on the Great Depression is Milton Friedman and Anna Schwartz's *A Monetary History of the United States, 1867-1960* (1963), particularly its chapter on "The Great Contraction, 1929-1933" ({cite:t}`FriedmanSchwartz1963`). Friedman and Schwartz argued that the Depression was primarily caused by the Federal Reserve's failure to prevent a catastrophic contraction of the money supply.

The facts they marshaled were striking. Between 1929 and 1933, the U.S. money supply (M2) fell by approximately one-third, the largest monetary contraction in American history. Friedman and Schwartz argued that this contraction was not an inevitable consequence of the Depression; it was a policy failure. The Federal Reserve had the tools and the resources to prevent the contraction, but it chose not to use them. The Fed could have lent freely to distressed banks through the discount window, preventing bank failures and the associated contraction of deposits. It could have conducted open market operations to inject reserves into the banking system and offset the decline in the money supply. It did neither, or rather, it did far too little and far too late.

The mechanism through which the monetary contraction caused the Depression, in the Friedman-Schwartz account, was the standard quantity theory of money: a reduction in the money supply reduces nominal spending, and because prices and wages are sticky (they adjust slowly), the reduction in nominal spending translates into a reduction in real output and employment. The severity of the Depression reflected the severity of the monetary contraction.

The strengths of the Friedman-Schwartz explanation are considerable. The correlation between the money supply and economic activity during the 1929-1933 period is very strong. The timing of the banking panics (late 1930, spring 1931, early 1933) corresponds closely to the timing of the sharpest declines in output. And the explanation provides a clear and testable counterfactual: if the Fed had prevented the money supply from contracting, the Depression would have been much milder.

The limitations are also significant. First, the direction of causation is not entirely clear. Friedman and Schwartz treated the money supply as an exogenous variable controlled by the Fed, but the money supply is also endogenous: it falls when banks fail and deposits are destroyed, which is a consequence of the Depression as well as a cause. Second, the monetary explanation does not fully account for the severity of the contraction. Monetary contractions of comparable magnitude (relative to trend) had occurred before without producing a Depression of anything like the same severity. Something else was at work. Third, the monetary explanation does not adequately explain why the Depression was so prolonged: even after the money supply began to recover in 1934, the recovery was slow and incomplete until the onset of World War II.

## The Keynesian aggregate demand explanation

The Keynesian explanation, rooted in Keynes's *General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936), emphasizes the collapse of aggregate demand, particularly investment demand, as the primary cause of the Depression.

In the Keynesian framework, the stock market crash of 1929 destroyed wealth and confidence, which reduced consumption and investment spending. The decline in spending reduced output and employment, which further reduced income and spending, in a downward multiplier process. The economy fell into a "liquidity trap" in which monetary policy was ineffective: even with interest rates near zero, firms and households were unwilling to borrow and spend because their expectations about the future were so pessimistic. In this situation, only fiscal policy (government spending to replace the missing private demand) could lift the economy out of the Depression.

The Keynesian explanation has several strengths. It accounts for the collapse of investment, which fell by approximately 80% between 1929 and 1932, far more than can be explained by the decline in the money supply alone. It provides a clear rationale for the effectiveness of fiscal stimulus, which is consistent with the fact that the Depression finally ended with the massive fiscal expansion of World War II. And it explains the psychological dimension of the crisis, the collapse of confidence, the retreat to liquidity, and the self-reinforcing nature of the downturn, in a way that the mechanical quantity-theory framework of Friedman and Schwartz does not.

The limitations of the Keynesian explanation parallel those of the monetary explanation, but in the opposite direction. The Keynesian framework underemphasizes the role of money and banking. In the simplest Keynesian models, money is either irrelevant (in a liquidity trap) or passive (adjusting to accommodate changes in demand). The massive destruction of the banking system and its consequences for credit availability are not central to the standard Keynesian account. This is a significant omission, because the banking crises were clearly a major mechanism through which the initial recession was transformed into a depression.

## The Bernanke credit channel explanation

Ben Bernanke's 1983 paper "Nonmonetary Effects of the Financial Crisis in the Propagation of the Great Depression" ({cite:t}`Bernanke1983`) proposed a third explanation that synthesized elements of the first two while adding a crucial new mechanism.

Bernanke accepted the Friedman-Schwartz finding that the monetary contraction was severe and that it contributed to the Depression. But he argued that the monetary contraction alone could not account for the depth and duration of the crisis. Even after correcting for the decline in the money supply, there remained a large "residual" decline in output that could not be explained by standard monetary transmission channels.

Bernanke's explanation for this residual was the destruction of the **credit intermediation** function of the banking system. As described in subsection 5.2, the failure of approximately 9,000 banks between 1930 and 1933 destroyed the information capital embodied in bank-borrower relationships. This information capital, the knowledge that banks had accumulated about their borrowers' creditworthiness, could not be easily replicated by other institutions. The result was a massive increase in the "cost of credit intermediation" (a concept closely related to the external finance premium discussed in subsection 2.5), which choked off credit to borrowers who were fundamentally solvent but could not demonstrate their creditworthiness to new lenders.

The credit channel explanation fills an important gap in both the monetary and Keynesian accounts. It explains why the Depression was so much more severe than other monetary contractions of comparable magnitude: the destruction of the banking system's information capital was a unique feature that amplified the monetary contraction far beyond what the decline in the money supply alone would have produced. It explains why the Depression was so prolonged: rebuilding the information capital of the banking system took years, and until that capital was rebuilt, credit remained scarce even as the money supply recovered. And it provides a clear mechanism through which the financial crisis was transmitted to the real economy, through the disruption of the credit allocation process, which is more specific and more empirically testable than the generic "animal spirits" mechanism of the Keynesian account.

## Synthesis: three explanations, one depression

The three explanations are not mutually exclusive; they are complementary. The Great Depression was caused by a combination of monetary contraction (Friedman-Schwartz), aggregate demand collapse (Keynes), and credit system destruction (Bernanke), operating simultaneously and reinforcing each other.

The monetary contraction reduced the money supply, raised real interest rates, and created deflationary pressure. The deflation increased the real burden of debt, triggering the Fisher debt-deflation mechanism (subsection 2.1) and forcing debtors to cut spending and sell assets. The banking crises destroyed the credit intermediation function, cutting off credit to solvent borrowers and preventing the financial system from allocating resources efficiently. The collapse of investment and consumption reduced aggregate demand, which reduced output and employment, which further reduced income and spending. The gold standard transmitted the deflationary shock to the rest of the world and prevented governments from adopting the expansionary policies needed to break the spiral.

Each explanation captures an essential aspect of the Depression, and no single explanation is sufficient on its own. The Friedman-Schwartz account correctly identifies the monetary contraction as a necessary condition but underemphasizes the role of credit intermediation and aggregate demand. The Keynesian account correctly identifies the collapse of demand but underemphasizes the role of money and banking. The Bernanke account correctly identifies the destruction of the credit system but does not claim to provide a complete explanation on its own.

The synthesis has direct implications for policy. The lesson of Friedman-Schwartz is that the central bank must prevent the money supply from contracting during a crisis. The lesson of Keynes is that fiscal policy must fill the demand gap when monetary policy alone is insufficient. The lesson of Bernanke is that the banking system must be preserved (or rapidly rebuilt) because its destruction has consequences that go far beyond the mechanical reduction in the money supply. All three lessons were applied during the 2007-2009 crisis, when central banks expanded the money supply aggressively (Friedman-Schwartz), governments implemented large fiscal stimulus programs (Keynes), and regulators intervened to prevent the collapse of the banking system (Bernanke). The fact that the 2007-2009 recession, though severe, was far less catastrophic than the Great Depression is due in no small part to the lessons learned from the Depression and applied by policymakers who had studied it carefully.
