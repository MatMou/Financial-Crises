title: "5.5 Policy Responses and Recovery"
subtitle: Roosevelt, the banking holiday, and the path out of the abyss
---

The failure of the initial policy response to the Depression, both in the United States and internationally, is one of the central lessons of the episode. The Hoover administration and the Federal Reserve were slow to act, constrained by the gold standard, by orthodox economic doctrine, and by a political reluctance to intervene in markets. When the Roosevelt administration took office in March 1933, it implemented a dramatic series of policy changes that, while imperfect, marked the beginning of the recovery. Understanding what worked, what did not, and what finally ended the Depression is essential for evaluating the policy responses to subsequent crises.

## The Hoover years: 1929-1933

The initial policy response to the Depression was woefully inadequate. President Hoover was not the do-nothing president of popular mythology; he was more interventionist than any previous president had been during an economic downturn. He organized conferences with business leaders, urged them to maintain wages, expanded federal public works spending, and created the Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC) in January 1932 to provide emergency loans to banks, railroads, and other financial institutions.

But Hoover's interventions were insufficient in scale and constrained by his attachment to orthodox principles. He was committed to balancing the federal budget, which led him to sign the Revenue Act of 1932, a large tax increase that deepened the contraction. He was committed to the gold standard, which prevented the monetary expansion that might have arrested the deflation. And he was reluctant to provide direct relief to the unemployed, believing that such assistance would undermine individual initiative and create permanent dependence on the government.

The Federal Reserve was equally passive. Despite having the legal authority and the gold reserves to expand the money supply, the Fed conducted only modest open market purchases in 1932 (the so-called "open market operations experiment"), and these were quickly reversed when gold flowed out of the country. The dominant view within the Fed was that the banking failures represented a necessary correction that should be allowed to run its course, and that monetary expansion would be inflationary and would undermine confidence in the dollar. This view was catastrophically wrong, but it was sincerely held and was consistent with the prevailing economic orthodoxy.

## The banking holiday and the rescue of the banking system

Franklin Roosevelt was inaugurated on March 4, 1933, at the nadir of the Depression. His first act was to declare a national "bank holiday," closing every bank in the country for four days (eventually extended to eight) to halt the bank runs that had reached epidemic proportions.

During the bank holiday, the Emergency Banking Act was drafted, passed by Congress, and signed into law in a single day (March 9, 1933). The Act gave the President broad powers to regulate banking, authorized the RFC to purchase preferred stock in banks (effectively recapitalizing them with government funds), and empowered the Federal Reserve to issue emergency currency backed by bank assets. The Act also established a process for reopening banks: the Treasury would inspect each bank and certify it as sound before it was allowed to reopen.

The bank holiday and the Emergency Banking Act were remarkably successful. When the first banks were allowed to reopen on March 13, 1933, depositors lined up not to withdraw their money but to deposit it. The panic had broken. Confidence was restored not by any fundamental improvement in the banks' condition (many of the banks that reopened were still in fragile shape) but by the government's credible commitment to stand behind the banking system. Roosevelt's fireside chat on March 12, in which he explained the banking situation in plain language and assured the public that their deposits were safe, played a crucial role in restoring confidence.

The banking holiday illustrates a fundamental principle of crisis management: in a panic, the most important thing the government can do is provide a credible signal that it will support the financial system. The specific mechanism matters less than the credibility of the commitment. Roosevelt's willingness to close the banks, inspect them, and reopen only those that were certified as sound was a dramatic and risky gamble, but it worked because it demonstrated that the government was in control and was willing to take responsibility for the banking system's integrity.

## Departure from gold

The second major policy shift was the abandonment of the gold standard. On April 5, 1933, Roosevelt issued Executive Order 6102, which prohibited the private holding of gold and required individuals and institutions to sell their gold to the Federal Reserve. On June 5, 1933, Congress abrogated the gold clause in contracts, which had required debtors to repay in gold or gold-equivalent currency. And in January 1934, the Gold Reserve Act established a new gold price of $35 per ounce, up from $20.67, effectively devaluing the dollar by approximately 41%.

The devaluation had several important effects. It ended the deflationary pressure that the gold standard had imposed. It raised the dollar price of gold, which increased the value of the gold reserves held by the Federal Reserve, giving the Fed more room to expand the money supply. It depreciated the dollar against currencies that remained on the gold standard, improving U.S. export competitiveness. And it raised the dollar price of commodities, particularly agricultural commodities, which provided relief to the heavily indebted farm sector.

The devaluation was followed by a rapid recovery in output and prices. Industrial production, which had hit its trough in March 1933, recovered quickly: by mid-1933, it had risen by approximately 50% from the trough. The stock market rallied sharply. Wholesale prices, which had been falling since 1929, stabilized and began to rise. The recovery was not smooth or complete (it was interrupted by the sharp "Roosevelt recession" of 1937-1938, discussed below), but the turning point was clear and it coincided precisely with the policy shift.

## Fiscal policy: the New Deal

The New Deal encompassed a vast range of programs, from financial regulation (discussed in subsection 5.6) to labor legislation to public works to agricultural price supports. The fiscal component of the New Deal, government spending to provide employment and stimulate demand, is the aspect most relevant to macroeconomic analysis.

The New Deal's fiscal stimulus was significant by the standards of the time, but modest by the standards of subsequent crises. Federal spending increased from approximately 3.5% of GDP in 1929 to approximately 10% of GDP by 1936. The federal deficit peaked at approximately 5% of GDP in 1934. The programs that provided the most direct stimulus included the Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC), the Works Progress Administration (WPA), and the Public Works Administration (PWA), which together employed millions of workers on public construction projects, parks, roads, and buildings.

The effectiveness of the New Deal's fiscal stimulus is debated. Keynesian economists argue that the stimulus was too small to close the enormous output gap (GDP in 1933 was approximately 30% below its 1929 level) and that a larger stimulus would have produced a faster recovery. The evidence supports this interpretation: the pace of recovery between 1933 and 1937 was historically rapid (GDP grew at approximately 9% per year), but the level of output remained well below its 1929 level. Unemployment, while declining from its 1933 peak of approximately 25%, remained above 14% in 1937.

The "Roosevelt recession" of 1937-1938 provides a natural experiment on the effects of fiscal policy. In 1937, the Roosevelt administration, concerned about the size of the budget deficit and encouraged by the recovery, implemented a significant fiscal contraction: spending was cut and taxes were increased (partly through the new Social Security payroll taxes that took effect in 1937). The Federal Reserve simultaneously doubled reserve requirements for banks, reducing the money supply. The result was a sharp recession: industrial production fell by approximately 30% and unemployment rose from 14% to 19%. The episode is widely interpreted as evidence that the recovery of 1933-1937 had been driven by policy stimulus, and that premature withdrawal of that stimulus was self-defeating, exactly the pattern predicted by the balance sheet recession framework (subsection 3.2).

## What ended the Depression?

The Great Depression in the United States did not truly end until the massive fiscal expansion associated with World War II. Defense spending rose from approximately 2% of GDP in 1940 to approximately 40% of GDP in 1944. The federal deficit peaked at approximately 30% of GDP in 1943. Unemployment, which had still been above 14% in 1940, fell below 2% by 1943.

The wartime fiscal expansion was, in Keynesian terms, the demand stimulus that the New Deal had failed to provide on a sufficient scale. It demonstrated that the economy's productive capacity had not been destroyed by the Depression: when demand was provided, output responded. It also demonstrated, more clearly than any peacetime example could, that fiscal policy can be effective in lifting an economy out of a deep recession, provided it is large enough.

The lesson is sobering. The Great Depression lasted for over a decade in the United States, not because the economy was incapable of recovery, but because the policy response was too timid. The banking system was rescued in 1933, the gold standard was abandoned, and a significant fiscal stimulus was provided, all of which helped. But the stimulus was never large enough to close the output gap, and when it was prematurely withdrawn in 1937, the economy fell back into recession. Only the truly massive fiscal expansion of wartime, which dwarfed anything that would have been politically feasible in peacetime, provided enough demand to end the Depression.
