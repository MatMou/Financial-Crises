---
title: "17.4 Capital Controls and the Management of Capital Flows"
subtitle: A tool rehabilitated by experience
---

Capital controls, restrictions on the flow of financial capital across national borders, were a central feature of the Bretton Woods system (subsection 6.1) and were progressively dismantled during the 1970s-1990s as part of the broader liberalization of international finance. The emerging market crises of the 1990s (Session 7) and the GFC demonstrated that free capital mobility could produce boom-bust cycles of devastating severity, and the intellectual consensus shifted toward a more nuanced view that acknowledged a legitimate role for capital flow management measures under certain conditions.

## The case for capital flow management

The theoretical case for some form of capital flow management rests on several arguments. Free capital flows are procyclical: capital floods into countries during booms (when perceived risk is low and returns are high) and floods out during busts (when risk is reassessed upward), amplifying both phases of the cycle. The sudden stop (subsection 3.3) is the most dangerous manifestation of this procyclicality: a sharp reversal of capital inflows that forces a country into a painful and potentially catastrophic adjustment.

Capital inflow surges can produce domestic credit booms, asset price bubbles, and real exchange rate appreciation that erode export competitiveness, creating the vulnerabilities that make the eventual reversal so destructive. The Asian crisis countries, the European periphery, and many emerging markets have experienced this pattern.

The impossible trinity (subsection 4.6) provides the formal framework: a country cannot simultaneously maintain a fixed exchange rate, free capital mobility, and an independent monetary policy. If a country wants exchange rate stability and monetary policy independence, it must restrict capital mobility. The Bretton Woods system made this choice explicitly. The post-Bretton Woods consensus favored free capital mobility, but the crises of the 1990s and 2000s demonstrated the costs of that choice.

## The IMF's Institutional View

The IMF's evolving position on capital flows reflects the shift in the intellectual consensus. Before the Asian crisis, the IMF was a strong advocate of capital account liberalization and had proposed amending its Articles of Agreement to make capital account liberalization a formal objective. The Asian crisis and the GFC forced a reassessment.

The **IMF Institutional View on Capital Flows**, adopted in 2012, represents the current consensus. The View acknowledges that capital flows carry both benefits (access to foreign savings, diversification, technology transfer) and risks (boom-bust cycles, sudden stops, loss of monetary policy autonomy). It accepts that capital flow management measures (CFMs), including both inflow restrictions and outflow restrictions, can be a legitimate element of the policy toolkit when other measures (macroeconomic adjustment, macroprudential policy, reserve accumulation) are insufficient or inappropriate.

The Institutional View specifies a hierarchy of policy responses. Countries experiencing capital inflow surges should first adjust macroeconomic policies (allowing the exchange rate to appreciate, tightening fiscal policy), then deploy macroprudential measures (to address the domestic financial stability risks of the inflows), and only then consider CFMs as a residual measure. CFMs should be targeted, transparent, temporary, and non-discriminatory.

## The experience of capital controls

Several countries have used capital controls effectively during the post-GFC period. **Brazil** imposed a tax on foreign portfolio inflows (the IOF tax) in 2009-2012 to reduce the appreciation pressure on the real and to slow the buildup of short-term foreign-currency-denominated liabilities. Studies of the IOF tax found that it was partially effective in changing the composition of inflows (reducing the share of short-term portfolio flows) but had limited effect on the total volume.

**Iceland** imposed comprehensive capital controls following the collapse of its banking system in 2008, preventing the outflow of capital that would have produced a devastating depreciation of the krona. The controls were maintained for approximately eight years and were gradually relaxed as the economy stabilized. The Icelandic experience demonstrated that capital controls can be effective in managing the aftermath of a financial crisis, though the long duration of the controls imposed costs on economic efficiency.

**China** has maintained extensive capital controls throughout its development, which insulated its financial system from the volatility that affected other emerging markets during the Asian crisis and the GFC. China's experience is often cited as evidence that capital controls can support financial stability, though the effectiveness of China's controls has diminished over time as the financial system has become more sophisticated and avenues for circumvention have multiplied.

**Malaysia's** imposition of capital controls during the Asian crisis (subsection 7.3), initially condemned by the international financial community, is now generally regarded as having been at least as effective as the IMF-supported programs adopted by Thailand, Indonesia, and South Korea.
