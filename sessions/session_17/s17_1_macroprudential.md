subtitle: Regulating the system, not just the institution
---

The most important conceptual innovation in financial regulation since the GFC is the distinction between **microprudential** and **macroprudential** regulation. Microprudential regulation focuses on the safety and soundness of individual financial institutions: ensuring that each bank has sufficient capital, adequate liquidity, and sound risk management. Macroprudential regulation focuses on the stability of the financial system as a whole: preventing the buildup of systemic risk and ensuring that the system can withstand aggregate shocks.

The distinction matters because the safety of individual institutions does not guarantee the safety of the system. A system composed of individually sound institutions can still be fragile if those institutions are exposed to common risk factors, if their behavior is procyclical (amplifying booms and busts), or if the failure of any one institution would impose losses on the others through interconnection. The GFC demonstrated this forcefully: many institutions that appeared well-capitalized on the eve of the crisis (by the standards of the Basel II framework) became insolvent when the crisis hit, because the risk-weighted capital requirements had systematically underestimated the risks of structured credit products, and because the interconnections among institutions amplified the initial losses.

## The macroprudential toolkit

The macroprudential toolkit includes instruments that operate on different dimensions of systemic risk.

**Time-dimension instruments** address the procyclicality of the financial system: the tendency of credit, leverage, and risk-taking to expand during booms and contract during busts, amplifying the business cycle. The **countercyclical capital buffer** (CCyB), discussed in subsection 16.2, is the primary time-dimension instrument: it requires banks to build up additional capital during booms (when the credit-to-GDP gap is elevated) that can be released during downturns (to support lending). Dynamic provisioning, which requires banks to set aside provisions for expected loan losses during good times (rather than waiting until the losses materialize), serves a similar function. Spain implemented dynamic provisioning before the GFC, and while it did not prevent the crisis, it did provide a buffer that absorbed some of the initial losses.

**Cross-section instruments** address the distribution of systemic risk across institutions. **Systemic risk surcharges** require systemically important financial institutions (SIFIs) to hold additional capital, reflecting the greater damage that their failure would cause to the system. The Basel III framework imposes an additional capital surcharge of 1-3.5% of risk-weighted assets on globally systemically important banks (G-SIBs), calibrated to the institution's size, interconnectedness, complexity, substitutability, and cross-border activity. **Concentration limits** restrict the exposure of individual institutions to specific counterparties, sectors, or asset classes, reducing the risk that a concentrated exposure could bring down a systemically important institution.

**Borrower-based instruments** address the vulnerability of the household sector to credit-driven asset price booms. **Loan-to-value (LTV) limits** restrict the maximum amount that can be borrowed relative to the value of the collateral (typically a house). **Debt-to-income (DTI) limits** restrict the maximum amount that can be borrowed relative to the borrower's income. These instruments directly constrain the buildup of household leverage that, as the GFC demonstrated, can produce devastating balance sheet recessions when asset prices fall.

## Institutional framework

Effective macroprudential policy requires an institutional framework that assigns clear responsibility for macroprudential decisions and provides the authority and independence needed to make politically difficult decisions during boom periods. Most countries have established dedicated macroprudential authorities since the GFC: the Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC) in the United States, the Financial Policy Committee (FPC) at the Bank of England, the European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB) in the EU, and equivalent bodies in most other advanced economies.

The Bank of England's FPC is often cited as the best-designed macroprudential authority. The FPC has a clear statutory mandate (to protect and enhance the stability of the UK financial system), the power to direct the microprudential regulators to implement specific macroprudential measures (such as activating the CCyB or imposing LTV limits), and the independence from political interference that is needed to take countercyclical action during booms.

The effectiveness of the macroprudential framework remains to be tested in a full-scale crisis. The CCyB has been activated and released in several countries, and LTV limits have been shown to constrain housing credit growth. But whether the macroprudential toolkit is sufficient to prevent a major financial crisis, or merely to reduce its severity, is an open question that can only be answered by experience.
