---
title: "17.6 The Political Economy of Crisis Prevention"
subtitle: Why we know what to do but fail to do it
---

The most persistent puzzle of financial crisis policy is not the absence of knowledge about what should be done but the failure to do it. The tools described in this session, macroprudential regulation, adequate capital buffers, prudent lending standards, LOLR facilities, effective resolution frameworks, are well-understood and widely endorsed by economists and policymakers. Yet the historical record demonstrates that these tools are systematically under-deployed during boom periods and are only strengthened after crises have already occurred. Understanding why requires an analysis of the political economy of crisis prevention.

## The Minsky cycle of regulation

The pattern of regulatory evolution described throughout this course follows a cycle that mirrors Minsky's financial instability hypothesis. After a crisis, the memory of the damage produces strong political support for regulatory reform. The reforms are enacted and implemented, and they contribute to a period of stability. The stability reduces the perceived need for regulation, and the regulated industry begins to lobby for relaxation. The relaxation is granted, partly because the stability appears to confirm that the regulations are unnecessary and partly because the political benefits of relaxation (higher growth, more credit, lower costs) are immediate and visible while the costs (increased vulnerability to the next crisis) are distant and uncertain. The relaxation eventually contributes to a new crisis, and the cycle repeats.

The arc from New Deal regulation to deregulation to the S&L crisis (subsections 5.6 and 6.6), and from post-GFC re-regulation to the gradual relaxation of Dodd-Frank provisions in the late 2010s, are the clearest examples. In both cases, the regulations that had been enacted in response to a crisis were gradually weakened as the memory of the crisis faded and the political economy shifted in favor of the regulated industry.

## The asymmetry of political costs

The fundamental political problem of crisis prevention is an asymmetry in the visibility and timing of costs and benefits. The costs of crisis prevention (tighter credit, lower growth, reduced profitability for the financial sector, constraints on homeownership or business expansion) are **immediate, visible, and concentrated** on identifiable groups (banks, borrowers, developers). The benefits (a crisis averted) are **distant, invisible, and diffuse**, because the counterfactual (the crisis that would have occurred without the prevention) is inherently unobservable.

No policymaker has ever been rewarded for preventing a crisis that did not happen, because the prevention is invisible. Policymakers are, however, routinely punished for imposing costs in the name of prevention: tightening lending standards makes housing less affordable, raising capital requirements makes credit more expensive, and activating the CCyB during a boom generates opposition from every stakeholder who benefits from the boom.

This asymmetry creates a systematic bias toward inaction during boom periods. The rational political strategy for a policymaker is to avoid the certain costs of prevention and to gamble that the crisis will not occur on their watch (or, if it does, to claim that it was unforeseeable and to deploy the crisis management tools that are politically easier to use after the damage has been done).

## The role of institutional design

The political economy problem cannot be fully resolved, but it can be mitigated by institutional design. The key principle is to insulate crisis prevention decisions from the political pressures that bias them toward inaction.

**Independent macroprudential authorities**, modeled on the independence of central banks in monetary policy, can make decisions about macroprudential tools (the CCyB, LTV limits, stress testing) without direct political interference. The Bank of England's Financial Policy Committee is designed with this principle in mind.

**Rule-based activation** of macroprudential instruments can reduce the discretionary element and thereby reduce the political cost of activation. If the CCyB is activated automatically when the credit-to-GDP gap exceeds a specified threshold, the political responsibility shifts from the individual policymaker to the rule. The Basel Committee's recommendation to use the credit-to-GDP gap as a guide for CCyB decisions reflects this logic.

**Transparency and accountability** can create political support for prevention by making the case publicly. Regular publication of financial stability reports (which identify vulnerabilities and explain the macroprudential policy stance), stress test results (which reveal the financial system's vulnerability to adverse scenarios), and early warning indicators (which show how current conditions compare to historical pre-crisis periods) can help build public understanding of the need for prevention.

## The ultimate constraint

Even with the best institutional design, crisis prevention faces an irreducible constraint: the future is uncertain, and the case for prevention is inherently probabilistic. Prevention is a bet on the counterfactual: it imposes certain costs today in exchange for a reduction in the probability of costs tomorrow. The probability reduction can be estimated but never observed directly. And the political system, which is designed to respond to observable outcomes rather than to counterfactual probabilities, is poorly suited to supporting policies whose benefits are inherently invisible.

The history of financial crises is, in this sense, a history of this constraint: the repeated failure to maintain the preventive measures that are most needed precisely when they appear least necessary. The Minsky insight that stability breeds instability is not only a statement about financial markets; it is a statement about the political economy of regulation. The stability that good regulation produces undermines the political support for the regulation, creating the conditions for the instability that good regulation was designed to prevent.

The appropriate response is not resignation but determination: determination to build institutional structures that are as resistant as possible to the political pressures that erode them, and determination to learn from each crisis the lessons that will be needed for the next one, even in the knowledge that those lessons will eventually be forgotten.
