---
title: "17.2 The Lender of Last Resort: Bagehot and Beyond"
subtitle: From the Bank of England to the Fed as market maker
---

The lender of last resort (LOLR) is the oldest crisis management tool, tracing its origins to the Bank of England's evolution during the 19th century (subsection 4.3). The concept has been extended, modified, and debated continuously since Walter Bagehot articulated its classic formulation in *Lombard Street* (1873). The GFC and the COVID crisis forced further extensions that carried the LOLR function far beyond anything Bagehot envisioned.

## Bagehot's principles

Bagehot's prescription for a banking panic was clear: the central bank should lend freely, at a penalty rate, against good collateral. Each element of the prescription served a purpose. **Lending freely** meant that the central bank should provide liquidity without limit to any institution that was solvent but illiquid, thereby preventing the panic from spreading. **At a penalty rate** meant that the interest rate should be above the normal market rate, to discourage borrowing except in genuine emergencies and to compensate the central bank for the risk. **Against good collateral** meant that the central bank should lend only against assets that were fundamentally sound (even if temporarily illiquid), to protect the central bank from losses and to ensure that the lending supported solvent institutions rather than insolvent ones.

Bagehot's principles remain the foundation of LOLR policy, but every element has been modified in practice. The distinction between illiquidity and insolvency, which is central to Bagehot's framework, is extremely difficult to make in real time during a crisis, when asset values are uncertain and balance sheets are opaque. The penalty rate principle has been abandoned in most modern LOLR operations: the Fed's emergency lending during the GFC and COVID was typically at rates near or below the market rate, to encourage institutions to use the facilities rather than stigmatizing them. And the collateral requirements have been progressively relaxed: the Fed accepted a much wider range of collateral during the GFC and COVID than Bagehot would have recognized as "good."

## Extension to non-banks

The most significant extension of the LOLR function since Bagehot is the extension to non-bank financial institutions. Bagehot's framework assumed that the LOLR would lend to banks, which were the dominant financial intermediaries of his time. The GFC demonstrated that the shadow banking system (investment banks, money market funds, ABCP conduits, repo markets) could experience runs just as devastating as bank runs, and that the central bank needed to extend its LOLR function to these institutions and markets to prevent the panic from spreading.

The Fed's use of Section 13(3) during the GFC to lend to Bear Stearns and AIG, and to create facilities that supported the commercial paper market, the money market fund industry, and the asset-backed securities market, represented a dramatic expansion of the LOLR perimeter. The COVID response extended the perimeter further, to the corporate bond market and the municipal bond market.

Each extension of the LOLR perimeter raises the moral hazard concern: if institutions know that the central bank will provide liquidity in a crisis, they have less incentive to maintain their own liquidity buffers. The moral hazard is particularly acute for institutions that are outside the regulatory perimeter, because they benefit from the implicit guarantee of central bank support without being subject to the regulations (capital requirements, liquidity requirements, supervisory oversight) that are imposed on regulated banks as a condition of their access to the LOLR.

## The distinction between LOLR and market maker of last resort

The COVID response introduced a new concept: the central bank as **market maker of last resort** (MMLR). The Fed's purchases of corporate bonds and municipal bonds were not loans to institutions (the traditional LOLR function) but purchases of assets in markets that had ceased to function. The Fed was not lending to General Electric or the State of California; it was buying their bonds in the secondary market to ensure that the market continued to function and that these issuers could continue to access capital market financing.

The MMLR function is analytically distinct from the LOLR function. The LOLR supports specific institutions; the MMLR supports specific markets. The LOLR lends against collateral and expects to be repaid; the MMLR purchases assets and bears the market risk. The LOLR operates behind the scenes (through the discount window); the MMLR operates publicly and visibly (through announced purchase programs).

The MMLR function is more powerful than the LOLR function (because it can support any market, not just institutions that have access to the discount window), but it is also more problematic from a governance perspective (because the central bank is making decisions about which markets to support, which is effectively a form of credit allocation that has traditionally been the province of elected officials rather than unelected central bankers).
