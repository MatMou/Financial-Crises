---
title: "17.3 Deposit Insurance and Bank Resolution"
subtitle: Protecting depositors and resolving failures without bailouts
---

Deposit insurance and bank resolution are the two complementary pillars of the safety net that protects the banking system from runs and manages the consequences when individual banks fail. Deposit insurance prevents runs by guaranteeing that depositors will be repaid regardless of the bank's financial condition. Bank resolution provides a mechanism for winding down failing banks in an orderly manner that minimizes systemic disruption. The design of both institutions involves a fundamental trade-off between financial stability (protecting the system from contagion) and moral hazard (reducing the incentive for depositors and creditors to monitor the banks they entrust with their funds).

## Deposit insurance design

The creation of the FDIC in 1933 (subsection 5.6) effectively eliminated the bank runs that had been a recurring feature of American financial life. But deposit insurance creates moral hazard: if depositors are protected regardless of the bank's riskiness, they have no incentive to move their deposits to safer banks, and banks have an incentive to take excessive risks (because they can attract deposits at the insured rate regardless of how they invest those deposits). The S&L crisis (subsection 6.6) was the most dramatic demonstration of this moral hazard.

The design of deposit insurance involves several choices that affect the balance between stability and moral hazard. The **coverage limit** (currently $250,000 per depositor per institution in the United States) determines which depositors are protected. A higher limit protects more depositors and reduces run risk, but it also increases moral hazard by reducing the pool of depositors with an incentive to monitor the bank. The decision to guarantee all deposits (including those above the coverage limit) at SVB and Signature Bank in March 2023 (subsection 13.5) demonstrated that the stated coverage limit may not be the effective limit during a crisis, because the systemic consequences of imposing losses on uninsured depositors may be too severe for the authorities to tolerate.

**Risk-based premiums**, in which banks that take more risk pay higher insurance premiums, are the standard mechanism for addressing the moral hazard of deposit insurance. Risk-based premiums create an incentive for banks to manage risk prudently, because riskier behavior increases their insurance costs. The FDIC has used risk-based premiums since 1993, and the European deposit insurance schemes use similar mechanisms. The effectiveness of risk-based premiums depends on the accuracy with which the insurance fund can assess the riskiness of each institution, which is limited by the same information asymmetries that affect all financial regulation.

## The too-big-to-fail problem

The too-big-to-fail (TBTF) problem arises when a financial institution is so large, so interconnected, or so complex that its failure would impose unacceptable costs on the financial system and the broader economy. If the authorities are expected to rescue such institutions (as they did during the GFC), the implicit government guarantee gives TBTF institutions an unfair competitive advantage (they can borrow at lower rates, because creditors expect to be bailed out) and encourages them to take excessive risk (because the downside is borne by the public).

The TBTF problem has been addressed, but not resolved, by the post-GFC reforms. The Dodd-Frank Act's Orderly Liquidation Authority (subsection 11.4) provides a legal framework for resolving failing SIFIs without a taxpayer bailout. The Financial Stability Board's "Total Loss-Absorbing Capacity" (TLAC) standard requires G-SIBs to hold a minimum amount of bail-in-able debt that can be converted to equity in resolution, ensuring that the institution's shareholders and creditors bear the losses rather than taxpayers. And the living wills requirement forces SIFIs to develop resolution plans that demonstrate how they could be resolved in an orderly manner.

Whether these measures would actually be used in a crisis remains untested. The political and economic pressures to bail out a failing SIFI, rather than subjecting it to the untested resolution framework, would be enormous. The credibility of the resolution framework is the central question for TBTF policy: if market participants believe that the framework would be used, the implicit subsidy is eliminated and TBTF institutions lose their competitive advantage; if they believe that the authorities would revert to bailouts in a crisis, the subsidy persists.

## Bail-in versus bailout

The post-GFC reform agenda has shifted the framework for managing bank failures from **bailout** (using taxpayer funds to recapitalize failing institutions) to **bail-in** (imposing losses on the institution's shareholders and creditors to recapitalize it). The bail-in concept was formalized in the EU's Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive (BRRD) and was applied in practice during the Cypriot banking crisis of 2013 (subsection 12.4), where uninsured depositors were bailed in to recapitalize the Bank of Cyprus.

The bail-in framework establishes a hierarchy of loss absorption: shareholders bear losses first, followed by subordinated debt holders, then senior unsecured creditors, and finally uninsured depositors. Insured depositors are protected. The hierarchy ensures that the losses are borne by those who were compensated for taking the risk (equity holders and creditors who received higher yields) rather than by taxpayers or insured depositors.

The bail-in framework is a significant improvement over the pre-crisis approach of ad hoc bailouts, but it faces challenges. The application of bail-in during a systemic crisis (when many institutions are in distress simultaneously) could produce contagion: if creditors of one institution are bailed in, creditors of other institutions may run, fearing that they will be next. The distinction between bail-in that stabilizes the system and bail-in that destabilizes it depends on the circumstances, and the authorities' ability to make this distinction correctly in real time is limited.
