Financial crises that originate in one country frequently spread to others through the contagion channels analyzed in subsections 7.5 and 10.5. The management of international crises requires coordination among multiple sovereign governments, central banks, and international institutions, each with different interests, institutional constraints, and political pressures. The International Monetary Fund is the central institution in the international crisis management architecture, but it is supplemented by regional arrangements, bilateral swap lines, and ad hoc coalitions that together constitute the **global financial safety net**.

## The IMF's lending toolkit

The IMF's lending toolkit has evolved considerably since the institution's creation at Bretton Woods (subsection 6.1). The current toolkit includes several facilities designed for different circumstances.

**Stand-By Arrangements** (SBAs) are the traditional IMF lending instrument, providing short-to-medium-term financing to countries experiencing balance-of-payments difficulties. SBAs are conditional on the implementation of a policy adjustment program (typically including fiscal consolidation, monetary tightening, and structural reforms), and the disbursement of funds is phased and contingent on compliance with the program's conditions.

**Extended Fund Facility** (EFF) arrangements provide longer-term financing for countries with deep-seated structural problems that require extended adjustment periods. The Greek programs of 2010-2015 (subsection 12.3) were, in part, EFF arrangements.

**Flexible Credit Line** (FCL) and **Precautionary and Liquidity Line** (PLL) are precautionary facilities designed for countries with strong fundamentals that are at risk of contagion. The FCL, created in 2009 in response to the GFC, provides pre-approved access to IMF resources without the traditional conditionality, reflecting the recognition that contagion can affect countries with sound policies. Mexico, Colombia, and Poland have used the FCL.

The IMF's total lending capacity has been increased substantially since the GFC. Quota resources (the subscriptions paid by member countries) were doubled in 2010 and augmented by the New Arrangements to Borrow (NAB). The IMF's total lending capacity is currently approximately $1 trillion, a significant increase from the pre-crisis level but still modest relative to the scale of global capital flows.

## Conditionality and its critics

IMF conditionality, the set of policy conditions attached to IMF lending, has been one of the most debated aspects of international crisis management. The Asian crisis (subsection 7.3) generated fierce criticism of IMF conditionality, particularly the fiscal austerity and structural reform requirements that were imposed on countries with budget surpluses and that deepened the recession without addressing the underlying capital account crisis. The European sovereign debt crisis (Session 12) generated similar criticism, with the Troika programs blamed for the devastating social consequences of austerity in Greece and the other program countries.

The IMF has responded to the criticism by modifying its approach. The institution has adopted a more flexible approach to conditionality, focusing on the macroeconomic conditions most directly relevant to the crisis (rather than imposing broad structural reforms), allowing greater use of fiscal policy for stabilization purposes, and showing greater willingness to accept debt restructuring as part of crisis programs. The creation of the FCL, which provides financing without traditional conditionality, reflects the recognition that conditionality is not always appropriate.

## The global financial safety net

The IMF is the centerpiece but not the entirety of the international crisis management architecture. The **global financial safety net** (GFSN) includes several layers.

**Self-insurance** through foreign exchange reserve accumulation is the first layer. As discussed in subsection 7.6, many emerging market countries responded to the 1990s crises by accumulating large reserve buffers. Global reserves grew from approximately $1.5 trillion in 1995 to over $12 trillion by 2024.

**Bilateral swap lines**, particularly the Fed's central bank liquidity swap lines, provide dollar liquidity to foreign central banks during crises. The swap lines were activated during the GFC and COVID and were critical in preventing the dollar funding squeeze from producing banking crises in Europe and Asia (subsections 10.5 and 13.4).

**Regional financing arrangements** (RFAs) include the European Stability Mechanism (ESM), the Chiang Mai Initiative Multilateralization (CMIM) in Asia, the Arab Monetary Fund, and the BRICS Contingent Reserve Arrangement. These arrangements provide an additional layer of financing that can be deployed more quickly than IMF resources and with conditions that may be better tailored to regional circumstances.

The GFSN is more comprehensive than it was before the GFC, but it remains fragmented and incomplete. The different layers are not well-coordinated: the IMF, the RFAs, and the swap lines operate independently, with different governance structures, different conditions, and different trigger mechanisms. The overall coverage is uneven: countries with access to swap lines (primarily the advanced economies) have a much stronger safety net than countries that rely on IMF resources alone.
