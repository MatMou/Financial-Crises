The European sovereign debt crisis, which erupted in 2010 and continued in various forms through 2015, was the most severe test of the European monetary union since its creation. The crisis exposed fundamental design flaws in the architecture of the Eurozone: the absence of a fiscal union to complement the monetary union, the lack of a common deposit insurance scheme, the prohibition on ECB purchases of government bonds (later circumvented), and the absence of a mechanism for orderly sovereign debt restructuring. These flaws transformed what began as a fiscal crisis in a small peripheral country (Greece) into a systemic crisis that threatened the survival of the euro itself.

The European crisis is the natural successor to the crises studied in earlier sessions. It combined elements of a sovereign debt crisis (Session 6), a banking crisis (Sessions 5 and 10), a balance-of-payments crisis (Sessions 3 and 7), and an exchange rate crisis (Session 7), all operating simultaneously within the unique institutional framework of the monetary union. The sovereign-bank nexus analyzed in subsection 3.4 was the central mechanism: bank losses threatened sovereign solvency, sovereign stress threatened bank solvency, and the feedback between the two created the "doom loop" that was the defining feature of the crisis.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Explain the structural flaws in the architecture of the Eurozone that made it vulnerable to a sovereign debt crisis, with reference to the theory of optimal currency areas and the impossible trinity.
2. Describe how the capital flow boom of the 2000s created the imbalances that would produce the crisis, drawing parallels to the gold standard periphery experience (subsection 4.5).
3. Provide a detailed account of the Greek crisis and distinguish its fiscal origins from the banking-driven crises in Ireland and Spain.
4. Analyze the sovereign-bank doom loop as it operated in Ireland, Portugal, Spain, and Cyprus, and explain the role of bank recapitalization costs in transforming banking crises into sovereign crises.
5. Explain how Draghi's "whatever it takes" speech and the OMT program resolved the self-fulfilling component of the crisis, with reference to the De Grauwe model of subsection 3.4.
6. Evaluate the austerity programs imposed on the crisis countries and assess the debate over internal devaluation as a substitute for exchange rate adjustment.

## Session Outline

**12.1 The Architecture of the Eurozone and Its Structural Flaws.** Optimal currency area theory, the Maastricht criteria, the missing elements (fiscal union, banking union, lender of last resort), and the parallel with the gold standard.

**12.2 The Periphery Boom and the Buildup of Imbalances.** Capital flows from the core to the periphery, the convergence of interest rates, the credit and housing booms in Ireland and Spain, and the fiscal deterioration in Greece.

**12.3 Greece: Fiscal Crisis and the First Bailout.** The revelation of fiscal misreporting, the spread crisis, the first and second bailout programs, the PSI debt restructuring, and the near-exit from the euro.

**12.4 Ireland, Portugal, Spain, and Cyprus.** The banking-driven crises in Ireland and Spain, the mixed fiscal-structural crisis in Portugal, and the banking crisis in Cyprus, each illustrating a different variant of the doom loop.

**12.5 The ECB and "Whatever It Takes."** The ECB's evolving role, the LTRO, the OMT announcement, and the resolution of the self-fulfilling crisis dynamic.

**12.6 Austerity, Internal Devaluation, and Lessons.** The economics and politics of internal devaluation, the comparison with the gold standard adjustment mechanism, and the lessons for monetary union design.
