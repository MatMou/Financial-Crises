The 1990s witnessed a sequence of financial crises that swept through the emerging markets and, on occasion, threatened the stability of the advanced economies. The European Exchange Rate Mechanism crisis of 1992, the Mexican "Tequila" crisis of 1994-1995, the Asian financial crisis of 1997-1998, and the Russian default and LTCM collapse of 1998 followed each other in rapid succession, each sharing common features but also displaying distinct dynamics.

These crises were the product of the new financial landscape that had emerged from the collapse of Bretton Woods and the liberalization of capital markets described in Session 6. Capital was now mobile on an unprecedented scale, flowing freely across borders in search of higher returns. Emerging market economies that opened their capital accounts attracted enormous inflows during good times, which fueled credit booms, asset price inflation, and currency appreciation. When confidence turned, the flows reversed with devastating speed and force, producing the sudden stops analyzed in subsection 3.3.

The 1990s crises generated an extensive academic and policy literature on contagion, the role of the IMF, the design of exchange rate regimes for emerging markets, and the appropriate pace and sequencing of financial liberalization. They also provided the empirical testing ground for many of the theoretical frameworks developed in Sessions 2 and 3. The financial accelerator, the sudden stop model, the sovereign-bank nexus, and the Diamond-Dybvig coordination failure all found vivid illustration in the 1990s episodes.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Explain how the ERM crisis demonstrated the vulnerability of fixed exchange rate regimes to speculative attack when capital is mobile and macroeconomic conditions diverge.
2. Analyze the Mexican crisis of 1994-1995 as a case of currency mismatch, short-term debt rollover risk, and the dangers of sterilized intervention.
3. Provide a detailed account of the Asian financial crisis, distinguishing between the current account view and the capital account view, and explain the roles of currency mismatch, maturity mismatch, and financial sector weakness.
4. Explain how the Russian default and the LTCM collapse demonstrated that emerging market crises could threaten the stability of the global financial system.
5. Analyze the channels of contagion that linked the crises of the 1990s, distinguishing between fundamental contagion, information contagion, and pure contagion.
6. Evaluate the reform proposals that emerged from the 1990s crises, including changes to IMF lending practices, the adoption of flexible exchange rates, and the debate over capital account liberalization.

## Session Outline

**7.1 The ERM Crisis of 1992.** The background of European monetary integration, the tensions created by German reunification, Soros and the attack on sterling, and the broader lessons about fixed exchange rates under capital mobility.

**7.2 The Mexican Tequila Crisis (1994-1995).** The liberalization of the Mexican economy, the buildup of short-term dollar-denominated debt (tesobonos), the political shocks of 1994, the devaluation and its aftermath, and the U.S.-led rescue.

**7.3 The Asian Financial Crisis (1997-1998).** The "Asian miracle" and its vulnerabilities, the Thai devaluation of July 1997, the spread to Indonesia, South Korea, and Malaysia, the role of the IMF, and the debate over the causes of the crisis.

**7.4 Russia and LTCM (1998).** The Russian default of August 1998, the near-collapse of Long-Term Capital Management, the Fed-orchestrated rescue, and the demonstration that leverage in the core could amplify shocks from the periphery.

**7.5 Contagion and the Architecture of Crisis Propagation.** The mechanisms through which crises spread across countries: trade linkages, competitive devaluation, common creditor effects, wake-up calls, and pure panic.

**7.6 Lessons and the Reform Agenda.** What the 1990s crises taught about exchange rate regimes, capital account management, IMF conditionality, and the international financial architecture.
