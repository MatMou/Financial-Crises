---
title: "14.4 FTX: Fraud, Commingling, and the Absence of Regulation"
subtitle: When the exchange was the fraud
---

The collapse of FTX in November 2022 was the final and most dramatic episode of the crypto crisis of 2022. FTX, which had been the second-largest cryptocurrency exchange in the world with a peak valuation of approximately $32 billion, collapsed in a matter of days when it was revealed that the exchange had been commingling customer funds with its affiliated trading firm, Alameda Research, and that Alameda had lost billions of dollars on speculative trades funded with customer deposits. The FTX collapse was not a market failure or a liquidity crisis; it was a fraud, and its parallels to earlier financial frauds (Enron, MF Global, and the Ponzi schemes that recur throughout financial history) are direct.

## The structure of the fraud

FTX was founded in 2019 by Sam Bankman-Fried and quickly grew to become one of the most prominent cryptocurrency exchanges, processing billions of dollars in daily trading volume. Bankman-Fried cultivated a public persona as a responsible leader of the crypto industry, lobbying for regulation, making large political donations, and promoting the philosophy of "effective altruism."

Behind the public image, FTX was commingling customer funds with Alameda Research, a proprietary trading firm also controlled by Bankman-Fried. Alameda used FTX customer deposits to fund its trading positions, its venture capital investments, and its personal expenditures. The commingling was not disclosed to FTX's customers, who believed their deposits were held in segregated accounts. Alameda also had privileged access to FTX's trading platform, including the ability to trade with negative balances and to access customer funds directly.

The fraud was exposed in November 2022, when CoinDesk published a report revealing that Alameda's balance sheet was heavily concentrated in FTT, FTX's own exchange token. Binance, the largest cryptocurrency exchange and a major holder of FTT, announced that it would sell its FTT holdings, which triggered a crash in FTT's price and a run on FTX by customers seeking to withdraw their funds. FTX was unable to meet the withdrawal requests because the customer funds had been lent to Alameda, which had lost them. FTX suspended withdrawals on November 8 and filed for bankruptcy on November 11, 2022.

The bankruptcy revealed a shortfall of approximately $8-10 billion in customer funds. Bankman-Fried was arrested in the Bahamas, extradited to the United States, and convicted of fraud, conspiracy, and money laundering in November 2023.

## The parallels to traditional financial fraud

The FTX fraud was not a novel form of financial crime. It was the oldest form of financial crime: the misappropriation of customer funds by a trusted intermediary. The parallels to earlier episodes are instructive.

The **commingling of customer funds** with proprietary trading operations mirrors the abuses that the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and the subsequent regulation of broker-dealers were designed to prevent. The rule that customer funds must be held in segregated accounts, separate from the firm's own capital, is one of the most basic protections in securities regulation. FTX, operating offshore and outside the jurisdiction of U.S. securities regulators, was not subject to these rules.

The **use of a self-issued token (FTT) as collateral** parallels the practices of Enron (subsection 8.4), which used its own stock to support off-balance-sheet financing. In both cases, the institution's apparent solvency depended on the value of a self-referential asset: as long as the asset's price was high, the institution appeared solvent; when the price fell, the insolvency was revealed. The reflexivity of the arrangement (the token's value depended on confidence in the exchange, which depended on the token's value) created the same death-spiral dynamic as the Terra/Luna mechanism.

The **absence of independent auditing and governance** mirrors the failures revealed by the Enron and WorldCom scandals. FTX did not undergo a standard financial audit (its auditor was a small firm with limited experience), its board of directors consisted of Bankman-Fried and two associates, and its risk management and internal controls were essentially nonexistent. The bankruptcy examiner's report described a corporate environment characterized by a complete absence of the controls that would be standard at any regulated financial institution.

## The regulatory gap

The FTX fraud was enabled by a regulatory environment that had failed to keep pace with the growth of the cryptocurrency industry. FTX was incorporated in the Bahamas, which had adopted a permissive regulatory framework designed to attract crypto businesses. FTX's U.S. subsidiary, FTX.US, was regulated by state money transmitter licenses and was subject to some oversight, but the bulk of FTX's operations (and the bulk of the fraud) occurred offshore, outside the jurisdiction of any major financial regulator.

The FTX collapse intensified the debate over cryptocurrency regulation. The fundamental question was whether crypto exchanges and lending platforms should be regulated like the financial institutions they resemble (broker-dealers, futures exchanges, banks), or whether the unique features of cryptocurrency technology required a different regulatory approach. The FTX experience provided powerful evidence for the former view: the financial functions (custody, exchange, lending, leverage) create the same risks regardless of the underlying technology, and the same regulatory safeguards (segregation of customer funds, capital requirements, disclosure, auditing, governance) are needed.
