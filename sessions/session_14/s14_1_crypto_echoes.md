title: "14.1 Cryptocurrency Markets and the Echoes of Financial History"
subtitle: New technology, old dynamics
---

The cryptocurrency boom of 2020-2021 and the crash of 2022 followed the Kindleberger-Minsky template with a fidelity that would be remarkable if the pattern were not, by this point in the course, entirely familiar. A technological displacement (blockchain and decentralized finance), a credit mechanism (stablecoin issuance, crypto lending platforms, leveraged trading), a euphoria phase (in which the total market capitalization of cryptocurrencies rose from approximately $200 billion in early 2020 to approximately $3 trillion in November 2021), and a crash (in which approximately $2 trillion in value was destroyed in 2022) reproduced the dynamics that have characterized speculative manias for four centuries.

## The displacement: blockchain and "Web3"

The displacement, in the Kindleberger-Minsky sense, was the development of blockchain technology and the vision of decentralized finance that it enabled. Bitcoin, launched in 2009, demonstrated that a peer-to-peer electronic payment system could operate without a central authority. Ethereum, launched in 2015, extended the concept by introducing programmable "smart contracts" that could automate financial transactions. The combination of these technologies gave rise to the vision of "DeFi" (decentralized finance): a financial system that would operate without banks, brokers, or regulators, governed instead by code and run on public blockchains.

Like the new economy narrative of the late 1990s (subsection 8.1), the DeFi vision contained a kernel of genuine innovation surrounded by an enormous amount of speculative excess. The blockchain technology was real and had legitimate applications (settlement, provenance tracking, programmable contracts). But the speculative frenzy that developed around the technology went far beyond any reasonable assessment of its near-term economic value.

## The credit mechanism

The speculative boom was enabled by a set of credit mechanisms that were structurally analogous to those that had fueled previous bubbles, despite operating on novel technological infrastructure.

**Stablecoins** (cryptocurrencies designed to maintain a stable value relative to a fiat currency, typically the U.S. dollar) functioned as the money supply of the crypto ecosystem. The total supply of stablecoins grew from approximately $5 billion in early 2020 to over $180 billion at the peak, providing the liquidity that fueled trading and speculation. The issuance of stablecoins, particularly Tether (USDT) and USD Coin (USDC), was the crypto equivalent of money creation: each new stablecoin issued represented new purchasing power that could be deployed in the crypto markets.

**Crypto lending platforms** (Celsius, BlockFi, Voyager, Genesis, and others) accepted deposits of cryptocurrency from retail and institutional investors, paid high interest rates (often 8-20% per year), and lent the deposited crypto to borrowers (including hedge funds, trading firms, and DeFi protocols). The platforms were performing classic banking functions, maturity transformation and credit intermediation, but without any of the regulatory safeguards (capital requirements, reserve requirements, deposit insurance, disclosure rules) that govern traditional banks. The platforms were, in effect, unregulated banks, and they would prove just as vulnerable to runs as the unregulated banks of the 19th century.

**Leveraged trading** was endemic. Cryptocurrency exchanges offered futures and perpetual swap contracts with leverage of 20:1, 50:1, or even 100:1. DeFi protocols allowed users to borrow against their crypto holdings and reinvest the borrowed funds, creating leveraged positions with effective leverage ratios that rivaled those of LTCM. The combination of high leverage and volatile underlying assets created a system that was extremely sensitive to price declines: even modest falls in crypto prices could trigger margin calls, forced liquidations, and cascading sell-offs.

## The euphoria and its characteristics

The crypto boom of 2020-2021 displayed all the hallmarks of speculative euphoria identified by Kindleberger and Minsky. The abandonment of traditional valuation metrics (most cryptocurrencies had no cash flows, no earnings, and no fundamental value anchor, making valuation by traditional methods impossible). The proliferation of new assets of dubious quality (thousands of new tokens were created, many with no purpose beyond speculation, including "memecoins" whose value derived entirely from social media hype). The entry of retail investors attracted by stories of overnight wealth (crypto social media was dominated by narratives of "financial freedom" through crypto investing). And the creation of increasingly complex and opaque financial structures (yield farming, liquidity mining, staking derivatives, algorithmic stablecoins) that few participants fully understood.

The total market capitalization of cryptocurrencies peaked at approximately $3 trillion in November 2021. Bitcoin reached approximately $69,000, Ethereum approximately $4,800, and the smaller tokens appreciated by even larger multiples. The boom was global, drawing in investors from virtually every country, and it was particularly popular among younger investors who had come of age after the GFC and had limited experience with severe market downturns.

The crash, when it came, would follow the familiar pattern: a triggering event (the Terra/Luna collapse), a cascade of failures among overleveraged and interconnected institutions, a run on the crypto equivalent of bank deposits, and a revelation that much of the apparent innovation had been, at best, the rediscovery of old financial vulnerabilities, and at worst, outright fraud.
