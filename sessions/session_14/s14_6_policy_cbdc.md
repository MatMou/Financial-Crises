---
title: "14.6 Policy Responses: Regulation, CBDCs, and the Future of Digital Finance"
subtitle: Bringing crypto inside the regulatory perimeter
---

The crypto crises of 2022 accelerated a policy debate that had been developing since Bitcoin's creation in 2009: how should governments and regulators respond to the growth of cryptocurrency markets and decentralized finance? The debate involves three broad approaches: regulating the existing crypto ecosystem, creating regulated alternatives (stablecoin frameworks and central bank digital currencies), and managing the interface between crypto and the traditional financial system.

## The regulatory debate

The fundamental regulatory question is whether crypto assets and crypto intermediaries should be regulated under existing financial regulatory frameworks or whether new frameworks are needed. The crypto crises of 2022 shifted the debate decisively toward the former view.

The case for applying existing frameworks is straightforward: the financial functions performed by crypto intermediaries (custody, exchange, lending, borrowing, leveraged trading) are the same functions performed by traditional financial intermediaries, and they create the same risks (fraud, misappropriation, insolvency, contagion). The FTX collapse demonstrated that an unregulated exchange can misappropriate customer funds just as easily as a regulated one, and the Celsius/Voyager/BlockFi failures demonstrated that unregulated lending platforms are just as vulnerable to runs as uninsured banks. The argument that crypto technology is fundamentally different from traditional technology, and therefore requires different regulation, was severely weakened by the demonstration that the same old risks produced the same old outcomes.

The European Union moved most aggressively, adopting the Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) in 2023, which established a comprehensive framework for the regulation of crypto asset issuers and service providers. MiCA requires crypto asset issuers to publish a white paper (analogous to a prospectus), subjects crypto exchanges and custodians to licensing and capital requirements, and imposes reserve and governance requirements on stablecoin issuers.

In the United States, the regulatory response was more fragmented, reflecting the U.S. regulatory structure. The SEC claimed jurisdiction over crypto assets that it classified as securities (invoking the Howey test), while the CFTC claimed jurisdiction over crypto commodities (primarily Bitcoin). The lack of a comprehensive federal framework created regulatory gaps and jurisdictional disputes that remained largely unresolved.

## Stablecoin regulation

Stablecoins attracted particular regulatory attention because of their systemic significance. Stablecoins are the primary medium of exchange within the crypto ecosystem, and the largest stablecoins (Tether, USDC) have market capitalizations in the tens of billions of dollars. A run on a major stablecoin could produce contagion effects not only within the crypto ecosystem but also in the traditional financial system if the stablecoin's reserves included traditional financial assets (Treasury bills, commercial paper, bank deposits).

The debate over stablecoin regulation centered on whether stablecoins should be regulated as bank deposits (requiring the issuer to hold a banking charter and to comply with banking regulation), as money market funds (requiring the issuer to hold high-quality liquid assets and to comply with securities regulation), or under a new bespoke framework. The Terra/Luna collapse demonstrated the danger of algorithmic stablecoins (which have no reserve assets and maintain their peg through market mechanisms), and most regulatory proposals effectively banned or severely restricted algorithmic designs.

## Central bank digital currencies

The growth of stablecoins and the broader crypto ecosystem prompted central banks around the world to explore the creation of **central bank digital currencies** (CBDCs): digital forms of central bank money that would be available to the general public (retail CBDCs) or to financial institutions (wholesale CBDCs).

The motivations for CBDCs are multiple. CBDCs could provide a safe, public alternative to private stablecoins, reducing the risk of runs and the concentration of monetary functions in private, potentially unstable entities. CBDCs could improve the efficiency of payment systems, particularly for cross-border payments. And CBDCs could preserve the role of central bank money in an economy that is increasingly digital.

By 2024, over 130 countries were exploring CBDCs, and several had launched or piloted retail CBDCs (including the Bahamas, Nigeria, Jamaica, and China's e-CNY pilot). The European Central Bank was developing the digital euro, though it had not yet been launched. The Federal Reserve was studying the issue but had not committed to issuing a CBDC, reflecting political opposition and concerns about financial disintermediation (if households could hold central bank money directly, they might withdraw deposits from commercial banks, reducing the banks' funding and their ability to lend).

The CBDC debate connects to the broader themes of this course in several ways. The question of whether the public should have access to central bank money, rather than relying on private intermediaries whose deposits may be unsafe, echoes the debate over deposit insurance that followed the banking panics of the 1930s (subsection 5.6). The question of how to design CBDCs to avoid financial disintermediation echoes the concerns about Regulation Q and financial repression that characterized the Bretton Woods era (subsection 6.2). And the question of how to balance innovation with stability echoes the tension that runs through the entire history of financial crises: the persistent tendency of financial innovation to outpace regulation, with crisis as the eventual result.
