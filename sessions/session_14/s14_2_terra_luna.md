title: "14.2 Stablecoin Fragility: Terra/Luna and the Mechanics of a Death Spiral"
subtitle: An algorithmic peg that broke
---

The collapse of the Terra/Luna ecosystem in May 2022 was the triggering event of the broader crypto crisis of 2022. Terra was an algorithmic stablecoin, UST, that was designed to maintain a 1:1 peg to the U.S. dollar through a mechanism that linked it to a companion token, LUNA. The collapse of the peg destroyed approximately $60 billion in combined market value within a week and set off a chain of failures that would engulf the entire crypto lending industry.

## The design of the Terra/Luna system

UST was an **algorithmic stablecoin**: unlike Tether or USDC, which maintain their peg by holding reserves of dollar-denominated assets (Treasury bills, commercial paper, bank deposits), UST maintained its peg through an arbitrage mechanism linked to LUNA. The mechanism worked as follows: users could always exchange 1 UST for $1 worth of LUNA, and vice versa. If UST's price fell below $1, arbitrageurs could buy cheap UST and redeem it for $1 worth of LUNA, profiting from the difference and reducing the supply of UST (pushing the price back up). If UST's price rose above $1, arbitrageurs could mint new UST by burning $1 worth of LUNA, profiting from the difference and increasing the supply of UST (pushing the price back down).

The mechanism was elegant in theory but contained a fatal flaw: it was **reflexive**. The value of LUNA depended on the demand for UST (because LUNA was burned to create UST), and the stability of UST depended on the value of LUNA (because UST could be redeemed for LUNA). If confidence in UST was lost and holders began to redeem, the mechanism required the minting of new LUNA, which increased LUNA's supply and reduced its price. The falling LUNA price meant that more LUNA had to be minted to honor each UST redemption, which further depressed LUNA's price, which required even more minting, in a self-reinforcing death spiral.

The Terra ecosystem was sustained, in part, by the **Anchor Protocol**, a DeFi lending platform that offered approximately 20% annual yield on UST deposits. The 20% yield attracted enormous deposits (approximately $14 billion at its peak), but it was not generated by productive lending: it was subsidized by the Luna Foundation Guard, the entity that managed the Terra ecosystem. The unsustainable yield functioned as a mechanism for attracting capital into the system, creating demand for UST that supported the peg. When doubts arose about the sustainability of the yield, the capital that had been attracted by the yield began to leave, undermining the peg.

## The collapse: May 2022

The collapse began in early May 2022, when large UST sales (possibly by a single large holder) pushed UST's price slightly below its $1 peg. The initial depeg was modest (UST fell to approximately $0.98), but it triggered the reflexive dynamic. UST holders, fearing a further decline, rushed to redeem their UST for LUNA. The redemptions required the minting of new LUNA, which crashed LUNA's price (from approximately $80 to near zero within days). The falling LUNA price further undermined confidence in UST's peg, because the collateral backing UST (LUNA) was evaporating. UST collapsed to approximately $0.10, and LUNA became effectively worthless.

The Luna Foundation Guard attempted to defend the peg by deploying its reserves (approximately $3.5 billion in Bitcoin and other assets), but the reserves were vastly insufficient relative to the $18 billion in outstanding UST. The intervention depleted the reserves without restoring the peg, adding selling pressure to the Bitcoin market and contributing to the broader decline in crypto prices.

## The parallels to traditional finance

The Terra/Luna collapse was a textbook currency crisis, structurally identical to the emerging market currency collapses studied in Session 7. UST was a currency with a fixed peg (to the dollar). The peg was maintained by an institution (the Terra protocol) that functioned as a central bank, defending the peg by adjusting the supply of the companion token. When confidence in the peg was lost, the defense mechanism was overwhelmed, the reserves were depleted, and the peg collapsed.

The parallels to the ERM crisis (subsection 7.1), the Thai baht collapse (subsection 7.3), and the Argentine peso collapse of 2001-2002 are direct. In each case, a fixed exchange rate was maintained as long as confidence held, but once confidence was lost, the self-fulfilling dynamic took over: the expectation of devaluation created the selling pressure that produced the devaluation. The Obstfeld model of self-fulfilling currency crises (subsection 7.1) applies to Terra/Luna with minimal modification.

The Terra/Luna collapse also had elements of a bank run. The Anchor Protocol was functionally a bank: it accepted deposits (UST), paid interest (20%), and invested the funds. When depositors lost confidence and withdrew, the withdrawal reduced the demand for UST, which undermined the peg, which caused further withdrawals, in a Diamond-Dybvig dynamic operating on a blockchain rather than at a teller window.
