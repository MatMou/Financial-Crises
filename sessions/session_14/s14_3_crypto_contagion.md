---

The Terra/Luna collapse was the trigger for a cascade of failures among crypto lending platforms and hedge funds that unfolded over the summer of 2022 and demonstrated that the contagion mechanisms of traditional finance, the common creditor channel, the leverage channel, and the fire-sale channel (subsection 7.5), operate with equal force in the crypto ecosystem.

## Three Arrows Capital

Three Arrows Capital (3AC) was a Singapore-based cryptocurrency hedge fund that had been one of the largest and most prominent investors in the crypto ecosystem. The fund had accumulated leveraged long positions across a wide range of crypto assets, including a large position in LUNA and in Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC), which traded at a significant discount to its net asset value. When Terra/Luna collapsed and crypto prices fell broadly, 3AC's leveraged positions produced losses that exceeded its capital.

3AC had borrowed from virtually every major crypto lending platform: Genesis, Voyager, BlockFi, Celsius, and others. When 3AC defaulted on its loans in June 2022, the losses were transmitted to all of these lenders. The transmission was a textbook common creditor channel (subsection 7.5): 3AC was the common borrower, and its default imposed losses on multiple lenders simultaneously.

The parallel to LTCM (subsection 7.4) is direct. Like LTCM, 3AC was a leveraged fund that had borrowed from multiple counterparties. Like LTCM, its positions were concentrated and correlated. Like LTCM, its default threatened to cascade through the institutions that had lent to it. The difference was that LTCM was rescued (by a Fed-coordinated private-sector intervention), while 3AC was not: there was no lender of last resort in the crypto ecosystem, no regulator to organize a rescue, and no mechanism for orderly resolution. The fund simply collapsed, and its creditors absorbed the losses.

## The cascade: Celsius, Voyager, BlockFi

The losses from 3AC's default triggered a cascade of failures among the crypto lending platforms.

**Celsius Network** was a crypto lending platform that had approximately $12 billion in assets under management and approximately 1.7 million users. Celsius accepted crypto deposits from retail investors, paid interest rates of 8-18%, and lent the deposited crypto to institutional borrowers (including 3AC) and to DeFi protocols. When crypto prices fell and 3AC defaulted, Celsius suffered losses that exceeded its ability to meet withdrawal requests. On June 13, 2022, Celsius suspended all withdrawals, trapping its depositors' funds. The company filed for bankruptcy in July 2022.

**Voyager Digital** was a publicly listed crypto brokerage and lending platform that had lent approximately $660 million to 3AC. When 3AC defaulted, Voyager was unable to absorb the loss and suspended customer withdrawals on July 1, 2022. The company filed for bankruptcy shortly thereafter.

**BlockFi**, another major crypto lending platform, suffered losses from both the general decline in crypto prices and from its exposure to 3AC. BlockFi was temporarily rescued by a credit facility from FTX (which would itself collapse four months later) but eventually filed for bankruptcy in November 2022 in the wake of the FTX collapse.

The cascade of failures had a common structure. Each platform had accepted deposits from retail investors, promised high returns, and lent the funds to leveraged borrowers and DeFi protocols. When the borrowers defaulted and crypto prices fell, the platforms were unable to meet withdrawal requests, and they suspended withdrawals, effectively confiscating their customers' funds. The dynamics were identical to a traditional bank run (subsection 2.4), except that there was no deposit insurance, no lender of last resort, and no regulatory framework to protect depositors or to resolve the failing institutions in an orderly manner.

## The crypto credit crisis as a miniature financial crisis

The sequence of events from May to November 2022, Terra/Luna, 3AC, Celsius, Voyager, BlockFi, and eventually FTX (subsection 14.4), constituted a miniature financial crisis that replicated, in compressed form, the dynamics of the major financial crises studied in this course. The sequence had all the essential elements: a speculative boom fueled by leverage and new financial instruments; a triggering event that exposed the fragility of the system; a cascade of failures among interconnected institutions; a run on deposits by panicked investors; and a devastating destruction of wealth.

The crypto crisis of 2022 destroyed approximately $2 trillion in market value, bankrupted dozens of companies, and inflicted severe losses on millions of retail investors. The losses were concentrated among younger and less wealthy investors who had been attracted by the promise of high returns and "financial democratization." The crypto ecosystem's lack of regulatory safeguards, which its proponents had celebrated as a feature (freedom from government interference), proved to be the vulnerability that allowed the crisis to inflict maximum damage on those least able to bear it.
