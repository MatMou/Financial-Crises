---
title: "6.4 The Transition to Floating Rates and Petrodollar Recycling"
subtitle: Oil shocks, stagflation, and the seeds of the debt crisis
---

The collapse of Bretton Woods coincided with the most severe commodity price shock of the postwar era. The combination of floating exchange rates, oil price increases, and the recycling of oil revenues through international banks created the conditions for the Latin American debt crisis of the 1980s.

## The first oil shock: 1973

In October 1973, OAPEC imposed an oil embargo on countries supporting Israel in the Yom Kippur War. Combined with OPEC production cuts, the price of oil quadrupled from approximately $3 to $12 per barrel within months.

The shock was simultaneously inflationary (higher energy costs raised all prices) and contractionary (reduced purchasing power for oil importers). The result was "stagflation": high inflation and stagnation that the prevailing Keynesian framework struggled to explain. Central banks faced an agonizing dilemma between fighting inflation (tightening) and fighting recession (loosening). Most chose partial accommodation, producing a prolonged period of high inflation and sluggish growth through the rest of the 1970s.

## Petrodollar recycling

The oil price increase transferred enormous wealth from importers to exporters. Oil exporters deposited surplus revenues, denominated in dollars, in money-center banks in New York and London. The banks, flush with deposits, needed borrowers. Developing country governments, especially in Latin America, were eager to finance development and willing to borrow at the low real interest rates of the inflationary 1970s. The banks were enthusiastic: sovereign lending appeared safe ("countries don't go bankrupt"), and spreads above LIBOR were attractive.

The recycling was celebrated as a triumph of financial intermediation. But it created dangerous vulnerability: developing country borrowers accumulated dollar-denominated, floating-rate debt. Their debt service burden would explode if interest rates rose or the dollar appreciated. Both would happen in the early 1980s.

## The experience of floating rates

The academic case for floating exchange rates had been made by Milton Friedman as early as 1953. Proponents argued that flexible rates would provide automatic balance-of-payments adjustment, eliminate the need for large reserves, and allow countries to pursue independent monetary policies. In practice, the experience was far more turbulent than advocates had predicted.

Exchange rates did not move smoothly to new equilibria; they overshot, undershot, and were buffeted by speculative capital flows that were now unconstrained by the Bretton Woods-era capital controls. The dollar-deutschmark exchange rate, for example, moved through swings of 30-40% during the 1970s, creating enormous uncertainty for international trade and investment. The volatility undermined one of the principal arguments for floating: that exchange rate flexibility would insulate countries from external shocks. In practice, exchange rate movements often amplified shocks rather than absorbing them, particularly for developing countries whose currencies were too thin and too vulnerable to sustain a free float.

The dismantling of capital controls, which accelerated through the 1970s and 1980s, removed one of the key pillars of the Bretton Woods architecture without replacing it with an alternative mechanism for managing the risks of free capital mobility. The combination of floating rates and free capital flows placed enormous demands on domestic macroeconomic policy: central banks had to manage domestic inflation, domestic financial stability, and exchange rate stability simultaneously, with only one instrument (the interest rate). The trilemma (subsection 4.6) was resolved in favor of capital mobility and monetary autonomy, but the resulting exchange rate volatility and vulnerability to capital flow reversals would prove extremely costly, particularly for emerging markets.

## The second oil shock and the Volcker tightening

The second oil shock came in 1979, when the Iranian Revolution disrupted oil supplies and the price roughly doubled again to approximately $30 per barrel. U.S. inflation surged above 13%.

Paul Volcker, appointed Federal Reserve Chairman in August 1979, announced a dramatic shift in monetary policy in October: the Fed would target the money supply rather than the interest rate, allowing rates to rise to whatever level was necessary to break inflation. The federal funds rate rose from approximately 11% in September 1979 to over 20% by June 1981. The prime rate reached 21.5%.

The Volcker tightening was successful in breaking inflation, which fell from over 13% in 1980 to approximately 3% by 1983. But the cost was enormous. The United States experienced two severe recessions in quick succession (1980 and 1981-1982), and unemployment rose to nearly 11%. The strong dollar that resulted from high U.S. interest rates devastated American manufacturing exports and contributed to a massive U.S. current account deficit. And because developing country debts carried floating interest rates linked to U.S. rates, the Volcker shock transmitted the tightening to the entire developing world. Each percentage point increase in LIBOR added billions to the annual interest bill of Latin American debtors.

The Volcker episode demonstrates a fundamental asymmetry in the international monetary system that persists to this day. When the Federal Reserve adjusts monetary policy for domestic reasons, the consequences are transmitted globally through the dollar's role as the dominant international currency. Countries that have borrowed in dollars, or that peg their currencies to the dollar, are forced to adjust to U.S. monetary policy decisions over which they have no influence. This asymmetry, sometimes described as the "exorbitant privilege" of the dollar's reserve currency status, is the modern manifestation of the core-periphery dynamics that operated under the gold standard (subsection 4.5).
