---
title: "6.6 The U.S. Savings and Loan Crisis"
subtitle: Deregulation, moral hazard, and the unraveling of New Deal safeguards
---

While Latin America suffered through its lost decade, the United States experienced a financial crisis of its own: the collapse of the savings and loan industry. The crisis, unfolding between the early 1980s and early 1990s, illustrated with unusual clarity the consequences of dismantling Depression-era regulatory safeguards. It was a product of the perverse interaction between financial deregulation and deposit insurance, providing a textbook demonstration of the moral hazard problem discussed in subsection 2.3.

## The savings and loan industry

Savings and loan associations, also known as "thrifts," were specialized institutions that accepted savings deposits and made residential mortgage loans. They operated under the informal "3-6-3" model: borrow at three percent (the rate paid on deposits), lend at six percent (the mortgage rate), and be on the golf course by three in the afternoon. Regulation Q capped the interest rates thrifts could pay on deposits, and activity restrictions limited their lending to residential mortgages.

The model worked well when interest rates were stable. But it contained a fundamental vulnerability: funding long-term, fixed-rate mortgages with short-term deposits created a **maturity mismatch** that exposed the industry to interest rate risk. If rates rose, deposit costs would increase while yields on existing mortgage portfolios remained fixed. This vulnerability was latent during the low-volatility Bretton Woods era; it became acute with the Volcker tightening.

## The interest rate shock

The Volcker tightening of 1979-1982 was devastating. Deposit costs surged to 12-15 percent while mortgage portfolio yields remained at 6-8 percent. The industry was technically insolvent by 1982, with the market value of assets below liabilities.

At this point, the crisis could have been resolved for approximately $25 billion, a painful but manageable sum. The Federal Savings and Loan Insurance Corporation (FSLIC) would have closed the insolvent thrifts and paid off insured depositors. Instead, Congress and the regulators chose a fateful alternative: deregulation.

## Deregulation as a response

Rather than closing insolvent institutions, policymakers deregulated them. The Depository Institutions Deregulation and Monetary Control Act of 1980 phased out Regulation Q. The Garn-St Germain Act of 1982 went further, permitting thrifts to make commercial real estate loans, consumer loans, and other investments previously restricted to commercial banks. Deposit insurance coverage was increased from $40,000 to $100,000, and accounting rules were relaxed to allow insolvent institutions to continue operating.

The logic was that higher returns from diversified activities would offset losses on old mortgage portfolios and restore solvency. This reasoning was catastrophically flawed because it ignored the moral hazard created by combining deposit insurance with deregulation. Since deposits were insured by the FSLIC, depositors had no reason to monitor institutional risk. Meanwhile, insolvent thrifts with nothing to lose had every incentive to take maximum risk. If the gamble succeeded, solvency would be restored and owners would profit. If it failed, losses fell on the insurance fund and ultimately on taxpayers. This was "gambling for resurrection" on a massive scale.

## The gambling phase

The result was a wave of reckless and often fraudulent speculation, concentrated in Texas, California, and the Southwest. Newly deregulated thrifts poured money into commercial real estate development, junk bonds, and speculative ventures. Some institutions grew their assets by factors of ten or twenty in a few years, funded by "brokered deposits": large deposits placed by brokers seeking the highest rates, indifferent to institutional soundness because the deposits were government-insured.

The most notorious cases became emblematic of the era. Lincoln Savings and Loan, controlled by Charles Keating, used depositors' funds for speculative real estate projects and channeled political contributions to five U.S. senators (the "Keating Five") who pressured regulators to ease supervisory scrutiny. Numerous Texas thrifts financed speculative commercial real estate during the oil boom with inadequate underwriting and pervasive insider dealing. Subsequent investigations found that fraud or insider abuse was a significant factor in approximately sixty percent of thrift failures.

The regulatory response was hampered by political pressure from the industry and its congressional allies, inadequate supervisory resources (the number of examiners was actually reduced during the early 1980s), and a philosophy of forbearance that allowed insolvent institutions to continue operating. The longer insolvent thrifts survived, the larger the eventual losses grew, because the gambling-for-resurrection dynamic meant they systematically increased their risk exposure over time.

## The collapse and resolution

The speculative bubble burst in the mid-to-late 1980s as commercial real estate markets collapsed, oil prices fell (devastating the Texas economy), and junk bond markets froze. The wave of failures overwhelmed the FSLIC, which was itself rendered insolvent by 1987.

Congressional intervention became unavoidable. The Financial Institutions Reform, Recovery, and Enforcement Act (FIRREA) of 1989 abolished the FSLIC, transferred deposit insurance to the FDIC, created the Resolution Trust Corporation (RTC) to manage and dispose of failed thrift assets, and tightened regulatory oversight. The RTC conducted the largest asset disposition in history to that point, selling hundreds of billions of dollars in real estate, mortgages, and other assets over six years.

The total cost is estimated at approximately $160 billion (roughly $350 billion in 2024 dollars), of which approximately $130 billion was borne by taxpayers. Roughly one thousand thrifts failed between 1986 and 1995, representing approximately one-third of the industry. Several hundred executives were convicted of criminal offenses related to fraud and mismanagement.

## Lessons

The crisis illustrates several principles that recur throughout this course with unusual clarity.

First, it demonstrates the danger of **combining deposit insurance with deregulation**. Deposit insurance eliminates the depositor's incentive to monitor the institution; deregulation removes the regulatory constraints that substitute for depositor monitoring. The combination creates a one-way bet: the institution captures the upside from risk-taking and passes the downside to the insurance fund and, ultimately, the taxpayer. This is the moral hazard problem of subsection 2.3 in its purest institutional form.

Second, the crisis demonstrates the **cost of regulatory forbearance**. The problem could have been resolved for approximately $25 billion in 1982. By allowing insolvent institutions to gamble for resurrection for seven more years, the eventual cost multiplied to $160 billion, more than six times higher. The lesson that early intervention is far less costly than forbearance has been confirmed in virtually every subsequent banking crisis, yet the temptation to forbear remains powerful because early intervention imposes concentrated, visible costs while the costs of forbearance are diffuse and deferred.

Third, the crisis demonstrates the **Minsky pattern applied to the regulatory sphere**. The New Deal safeguards (Regulation Q, activity restrictions, specialized supervision) had kept the thrift industry stable for half a century. Stability bred complacency about the risks those safeguards were designed to contain. Complacency bred deregulation. And deregulation, combined with the residual safety net of deposit insurance, bred the very instability the original regulations had been designed to prevent. The arc from regulation to stability to deregulation to crisis mirrors Minsky's insight that stability itself breeds instability, applied not to the financial cycle but to the regulatory cycle.

The crisis was a clear warning that eroding Depression-era safeguards would produce financial instability. The warning was not heeded. The deregulatory trend continued through the 1990s and 2000s, culminating in the repeal of Glass-Steagall in 1999, the exemption of over-the-counter derivatives from regulation in 2000, and the relaxation of leverage limits for investment banks in 2004. The consequences of that continued deregulation are the subject of Sessions 9 through 11.
