---
title: "6.3 The Triffin Dilemma and the Collapse of Bretton Woods"
subtitle: The contradiction at the heart of the dollar-gold system
---

The Bretton Woods system contained an internal contradiction that Robert Triffin identified as early as 1960. This contradiction, the **Triffin Dilemma**, would gradually undermine the system and force its abandonment.

## The Triffin Dilemma

The system required the United States to perform two functions simultaneously. As the anchor, the U.S. maintained dollar-gold convertibility at $35 per ounce, requiring sufficient gold reserves to back foreign-held dollars. Simultaneously, as the sole provider of the reserve currency, the growing world economy demanded an expanding dollar supply, possible only if the U.S. ran balance-of-payments deficits.

These requirements were mutually inconsistent. As the world economy grew, the U.S. had to run larger deficits, increasing foreign-held dollars. But as the ratio of foreign dollars to U.S. gold reserves rose, gold convertibility credibility declined. Eventually foreign central banks held more dollars than U.S. gold could cover, creating a crisis of confidence.

Triffin proposed a new international reserve asset, eventually realized in attenuated form as the IMF's Special Drawing Rights (SDRs) in 1969, but the SDR came too late and was too limited.

## The erosion of confidence

During the 1960s, the U.S. balance-of-payments deficit widened, driven by Vietnam War spending, foreign aid, and capital outflows. By the mid-1960s, foreign central banks held more dollars than the U.S. had gold.

Defensive measures multiplied. The **London Gold Pool** (1961), a consortium of eight central banks, intervened to hold the market gold price at $35. France withdrew in 1967, aggressively converting dollar reserves into gold. The Pool collapsed in March 1968, replaced by a two-tier market: official transactions at $35, market price floating. The U.S. also imposed capital controls (Interest Equalization Tax, 1963; Voluntary Foreign Credit Restraint Program, 1965) and pressured governments not to convert dollars.

## The Nixon shock

By August 1971, the situation was untenable. On August 15, President Nixon suspended dollar-gold convertibility, imposed a 10% import surcharge, and introduced wage and price controls. The Smithsonian Agreement (December 1971) attempted to re-establish fixed rates at new parities, but these proved unsustainable. By March 1973, the major currencies were floating.

## Assessment

The collapse resulted from the system's internal design. The Triffin Dilemma was inherent in any system using a national currency as the international reserve asset. The United States could not simultaneously provide dollar liquidity and maintain gold convertibility. The world that emerged had greater capital mobility, greater exchange rate volatility, and greater vulnerability to financial crises, but also greater monetary policy flexibility.

The transition to floating rates was messy and unplanned. The academic case for floating had been made by Milton Friedman as early as 1953, and many economists welcomed the move, arguing that flexible rates would provide automatic adjustment, eliminate the need for reserves, and allow independent monetary policies. In practice, floating rates proved far more volatile than advocates had predicted. Exchange rates did not move smoothly to new equilibria; they overshot, undershot, and were buffeted by speculative capital flows now unconstrained by capital controls.

The dismantling of capital controls, which accelerated through the 1970s and 1980s, removed one of the key pillars of the Bretton Woods architecture without replacing it with an alternative mechanism for managing the risks of free capital mobility. The "quiet period" of finance that had characterized the Bretton Woods era gave way to a new era of frequent crises: the Latin American debt crisis, the ERM crisis of 1992, the Asian crisis of 1997, and ultimately the global financial crisis. The correlation between the dismantling of capital controls and the return of frequent financial crises is one of the most striking patterns in the Reinhart-Rogoff data.

The Triffin Dilemma, moreover, has not been resolved. The dollar remains the dominant reserve currency, and the global financial system depends on the Federal Reserve to provide dollar liquidity in times of stress. The dilemma has changed form: instead of a formal gold convertibility commitment, there is now an informal dollar-centered system that generates many of the same tensions between U.S. domestic policy objectives and international monetary stability.
