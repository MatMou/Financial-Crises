---
title: "6.2 The Golden Age: Stability and Growth, 1945-1965"
subtitle: Two decades without systemic financial crises
---

The first two decades of the Bretton Woods era produced macroeconomic outcomes that were, by historical standards, extraordinary. Growth was rapid, unemployment was low, inflation was contained, international trade expanded at an unprecedented rate, and systemic financial crises were almost entirely absent.

## The macroeconomic record

Real GDP per capita in the advanced economies grew at approximately 4% per year between 1950 and 1973, roughly double the rate achieved in any comparable period before or since. Unemployment in most Western European countries averaged below 3%, and in the United States it averaged approximately 5%. Inflation was moderate, typically in the range of 2-4% per year. International trade grew even faster than output, at approximately 8% per year, as the progressive reduction of trade barriers through the GATT and the stability of exchange rates encouraged international specialization.

The Reinhart-Rogoff data confirm the exceptional financial stability of this period. Between 1945 and 1970, the BCDI composite crisis index (subsection 1.4) was at historically low levels. Systemic banking crises were virtually absent in the advanced economies. Currency crises were rare. Sovereign defaults were infrequent. The "quiet period" of finance was the longest stretch of financial stability in the modern era.

## Why was the Bretton Woods era so stable?

Several features of the institutional environment contributed to financial stability.

**Capital controls** were the most important. By restricting speculative capital flows across borders, capital controls insulated domestic financial systems from sudden stops and capital flow reversals. They also gave governments more room to pursue independent monetary policies: central banks could set interest rates to stabilize the domestic economy without worrying that interest rate differentials would trigger destabilizing flows.

**Financial regulation** was extensive. The New Deal regulatory architecture (subsection 5.6), including deposit insurance, Glass-Steagall separation, and interest rate ceilings (Regulation Q), limited risk-taking by financial institutions. Banks were heavily supervised, their activities were restricted, and competition was limited. The result was a stable but conservative banking system that earned moderate returns and served primarily as a conduit for channeling household savings to business investment.

**The absence of large-scale international capital flows** reduced vulnerability to contagion. During the Bretton Woods era, the current account was the dominant component of the balance of payments. Countries experiencing difficulties could draw on IMF resources and adjust gradually, rather than being forced into the violent adjustments that characterize capital account crises.

**Active fiscal policy** played a stabilizing role. The postwar Keynesian consensus held that governments should use fiscal policy to manage aggregate demand and maintain full employment. Countercyclical fiscal policy smoothed the business cycle and reduced stress on the financial system.

**The memory of the Depression** acted as a powerful behavioral constraint. Policymakers and market participants who had lived through the 1930s were acutely cautious about risk-taking. This institutional memory, which Minsky's framework predicts will fade as stability persists, was a potent stabilizing force.

## The costs of stability

The stability was not costless. Capital controls reduced the efficiency of international capital allocation by preventing savings from flowing to their most productive uses across borders. Financial regulation constrained competition and innovation in banking, which may have reduced the efficiency of domestic financial intermediation. Interest rate ceilings (Regulation Q) were particularly distortionary: by preventing banks from paying market rates on deposits, they created incentives for disintermediation, as savers moved money out of the regulated banking system and into unregulated alternatives such as money market funds, a process that would eventually undermine the regulatory framework from within.

The fixed exchange rate system imposed constraints on monetary policy that became increasingly burdensome as the postwar period progressed. Countries experiencing higher inflation than the United States found their currencies overvalued and their exports uncompetitive, but the political costs of devaluation (which was seen as a national humiliation and a sign of economic failure) made governments reluctant to adjust. The United Kingdom devalued the pound in 1967 (from $2.80 to $2.40) only after years of chronic balance-of-payments difficulties and deflationary policies that depressed growth. France devalued the franc repeatedly during the 1950s but attempted to avoid devaluation during the 1960s at considerable cost to domestic economic management.

The system also depended on the willingness of the United States to subordinate its domestic policy objectives to the requirements of the international monetary system. As the Vietnam War and the Great Society programs generated inflationary pressures in the 1960s, this willingness diminished, creating the tensions that would eventually bring the system down.

These costs were tolerable when the memory of the 1930s was fresh and when the benefits of the system (rapid growth, full employment, expanding trade) were manifest. But as a new generation of policymakers replaced those who had experienced the Depression, and as the costs of the Bretton Woods constraints became more salient than their benefits, political support for the system eroded. The intellectual climate shifted as well: the rise of monetarism and rational expectations theory in the 1960s and 1970s challenged the Keynesian consensus that had underpinned the Bretton Woods arrangements, and a new generation of economists argued that free markets, including free capital markets, would allocate resources more efficiently than the controlled system that Bretton Woods had established. The process that would eventually dismantle both the architecture and the financial stability it had produced began in the late 1960s and would accelerate dramatically in the decades that followed.
