---
title: "6.1 The Design and Logic of Bretton Woods"
subtitle: Building a new international monetary order
---

The Bretton Woods system was designed at a conference in July 1944, attended by delegates from 44 Allied nations. The system that emerged was a compromise between two competing visions: the ambitious plan proposed by John Maynard Keynes for Britain, and the more conservative plan proposed by Harry Dexter White for the United States. The resulting architecture reflected the overwhelming economic and political dominance of the United States at the end of World War II, but it also incorporated lessons from the interwar catastrophe that were broadly shared.

## The Keynes Plan

Keynes proposed the creation of an International Clearing Union (ICU), a supranational institution that would function as a central bank for central banks. The ICU would issue a new international reserve currency, the "bancor," used to settle balance-of-payments imbalances. Countries with surpluses would accumulate bancor balances, and countries with deficits would borrow bancor from the ICU. Crucially, Keynes proposed that the adjustment burden should fall on both surplus and deficit countries: surplus countries would be charged interest on excessive bancor accumulations, creating an incentive for them to increase imports or lend abroad, thereby helping to reduce the surplus and alleviate the deficit country's adjustment problem.

The Keynes Plan addressed one of the fundamental weaknesses of the interwar gold standard: the asymmetric adjustment burden that fell entirely on deficit countries (subsection 5.3). Under the gold standard, deficit countries were forced to deflate, while surplus countries faced no corresponding pressure to expand. Keynes's bancor system would have created a symmetric adjustment mechanism.

The plan was visionary but politically impractical. The United States, which was running massive trade surpluses, had no interest in a system that would penalize surplus countries. It would also have required the United States to cede significant monetary sovereignty to an international institution. The Keynes Plan was rejected, though its intellectual influence persisted and many of its ideas have resurfaced in subsequent debates about international monetary reform.

## The White Plan and the final design

The White Plan, which formed the basis of the final agreement, was more conservative. It proposed fixed but adjustable exchange rates, anchored by the dollar's convertibility into gold at $35 per ounce. Countries would peg their currencies to the dollar and maintain their pegs through intervention in foreign exchange markets. Exchange rate changes would be permitted only in cases of "fundamental disequilibrium," a term deliberately left undefined.

Two new international institutions would support the system. The **International Monetary Fund** (IMF) would provide short-term financing to countries experiencing balance-of-payments difficulties, giving them time to adjust without resorting to deflationary policies or competitive devaluations. The **International Bank for Reconstruction and Development** (World Bank) would provide long-term financing for postwar reconstruction and economic development.

The final Bretton Woods agreement combined elements of both plans, though it was far closer to White's design. The key features were as follows.

**Fixed but adjustable exchange rates.** Each country declared a par value for its currency in terms of gold or the U.S. dollar, maintaining its market exchange rate within 1% of the par value. Changes in the par value were permitted with IMF approval in cases of fundamental disequilibrium.

**Dollar-gold convertibility.** The United States committed to convert dollars into gold at $35 per ounce for foreign central banks and governments. Other countries pegged to the dollar, and the dollar was pegged to gold. The dollar thus served as the system's reserve currency, and the United States served as the system's anchor.

**Capital controls.** The agreement explicitly permitted countries to maintain controls on capital flows, reflecting the lesson of the 1930s that speculative capital flows could overwhelm fixed exchange rates. The architects of Bretton Woods believed capital controls were necessary to maintain fixed rates and to preserve domestic monetary policy independence.

**IMF lending.** The IMF was endowed with a pool of resources from which it could lend to countries facing balance-of-payments difficulties, subject to conditionality requiring policy adjustments to correct the imbalance.

## The logic of the design

The Bretton Woods design reflected a coherent set of lessons drawn from the interwar experience. Fixed exchange rates addressed the competitive devaluations of the 1930s. The adjustable peg addressed the rigidity of the gold standard that had prevented realignment when rates were clearly misaligned. Capital controls addressed the vulnerability of fixed exchange rates to speculative attack, an early practical recognition of the trilemma (subsection 4.6): Bretton Woods chose fixed exchange rates and monetary policy independence, which required the sacrifice of free capital mobility. And IMF lending addressed the liquidity problem that had plagued deficit countries under the gold standard, functioning as an international lender of last resort for balance-of-payments crises.

The system embodied what the political scientist John Ruggie later called **embedded liberalism**: a commitment to international economic openness (free trade, stable exchange rates) combined with domestic policy autonomy (the ability of governments to pursue full employment and social welfare policies). The interwar experience had demonstrated that unfettered laissez-faire, in which domestic policy was subordinated to the requirements of the gold standard, was politically unsustainable in democracies with universal suffrage and organized labor. But the protectionism and autarky of the 1930s had been economically catastrophic. Bretton Woods sought a middle path: international cooperation to maintain open trade and stable exchange rates, combined with capital controls and policy flexibility to allow governments to manage their domestic economies.

The compromise was made possible by U.S. dominance. The United States held the majority of the world's gold reserves, produced approximately half of the world's industrial output, and was the only major economy to emerge from the war with its productive capacity intact. The dollar's role as the system's anchor was not an arbitrary design choice; it reflected the economic reality that the United States was the only country capable of maintaining gold convertibility and providing the reserve currency that the system required. This dominance gave the system a stability that it might not otherwise have had, but it also meant that the system's viability depended on the United States' willingness and ability to manage its economy in a manner consistent with its international responsibilities, a condition that would eventually be violated.

The system was not perfect in design, and its contradictions would eventually destroy it (subsection 6.3). But it represented the most thoughtful and comprehensive attempt to build an international monetary system based on explicit principles and managed through international cooperation, and its legacy continues to shape debates about international monetary reform.
