---
title: "6.5 The Latin American Debt Crisis"
subtitle: The lost decade
---

The Latin American debt crisis, which erupted in August 1982 when Mexico announced that it could no longer service its external debt, was the first major sovereign debt crisis of the post-Bretton Woods era. It engulfed virtually every country in Latin America, produced a "lost decade" of economic stagnation and social hardship, and threatened the stability of the international banking system. The crisis demonstrated the dangers of petrodollar recycling, the vulnerability of developing countries to interest rate shocks in creditor countries, and the difficulty of resolving sovereign debt crises in the absence of an international bankruptcy mechanism.

## The buildup

During the 1970s, Latin American governments borrowed heavily from international banks, using the petrodollar surpluses flowing through the international banking system (subsection 6.4). Total external debt of Latin American countries rose from approximately $75 billion in 1975 to approximately $315 billion in 1983. The borrowing was concentrated in a few large countries: Mexico, Brazil, Argentina, and Venezuela accounted for the majority of the debt.

Several features of the borrowing made it particularly dangerous. The debt was overwhelmingly denominated in U.S. dollars, exposing borrowers to exchange rate risk. The interest rates were floating, linked to LIBOR, meaning debt service costs would rise if rates increased. Much of the debt was of short maturity, requiring frequent refinancing. And a significant portion had financed current consumption, public sector deficits, and capital flight rather than productive investment, which meant the borrowed funds were not generating the foreign exchange earnings needed to service the debt.

The lending banks bear a large share of the responsibility. The money-center banks competed aggressively for sovereign lending mandates, offering increasingly generous terms and conducting inadequate due diligence. The view that sovereign lending was inherently safe, encapsulated in Citibank chairman Walter Wriston's dictum that "countries don't go bankrupt," reflected a fundamental misunderstanding of sovereign risk. Countries may not go bankrupt in the legal sense, but they can and do become unable or unwilling to service their debts, as the long history of sovereign default documented by {cite:t}`Reinhart2009` demonstrates.

## The trigger: the Volcker shock

The Volcker tightening of 1979-1982 (subsection 6.4) was the proximate cause. The sharp increase in U.S. interest rates raised debt service costs dramatically: each percentage point increase in LIBOR added approximately $2-3 billion to the annual interest bill. Simultaneously, the U.S. recession reduced demand for Latin American exports, and the appreciation of the dollar increased the real burden of dollar-denominated debt.

The combination made it impossible for many countries to service their debts. Mexico was the first to break. On August 12, 1982, Finance Minister Jesus Silva Herzog informed the U.S. Treasury and the IMF that Mexico could not meet its upcoming payments. The announcement sent shockwaves through the system because the major U.S. money-center banks had lent approximately $80 billion to Latin America, an amount exceeding their total capital. A default by Mexico, Brazil, or Argentina could have rendered the largest American banks insolvent.

The dynamics match the sudden stop framework of subsection 3.3 with precision: capital inflow boom during the 1970s, accumulation of foreign-currency debt, an external trigger (the Volcker shock rather than a domestic event), and an abrupt reversal that forced violent current account adjustment. The difference from the classic emerging market sudden stop was that the creditors were not dispersed bondholders but a concentrated group of large banks, which created both a systemic risk to the creditor countries and a bargaining dynamic that shaped the crisis resolution.

## Crisis management: muddling through

The initial response prioritized protecting the international banking system over the recovery of debtor countries. The strategy, led by the U.S. Treasury and the IMF, provided emergency financing to prevent formal default while debtors implemented austerity programs to reduce their fiscal and current account deficits. Commercial banks were persuaded to roll over existing loans and provide modest new lending.

This "muddling through" was based on the assumption that the crisis was one of illiquidity rather than insolvency: debtors had a temporary inability to pay, but their debts were fundamentally sustainable given time to adjust. The assumption was wrong for many countries. The austerity programs required to maintain debt service produced deep recessions, rising unemployment, falling wages, and severe social hardship. Real GDP per capita in Latin America fell by approximately 10% between 1981 and 1983, and in many countries it did not return to its pre-crisis level until the early 1990s. The phrase "lost decade" understates the damage: it was not merely a decade of zero growth but a decade of declining living standards, rising poverty, and the erosion of public services and infrastructure.

The muddling-through strategy also produced perverse incentives. Because the banks continued to lend just enough to cover the interest payments on their existing loans, the debt stock continued to grow even as the debtor countries ran primary surpluses (fiscal surpluses excluding interest payments). The debtors were effectively borrowing to pay interest, a Ponzi financing arrangement in Minsky's terminology, which made the debt increasingly unsustainable over time.

## The Baker and Brady Plans

By the mid-1980s, the muddling-through strategy was visibly failing. The **Baker Plan** (1985), proposed by U.S. Treasury Secretary James Baker, called for increased lending by banks and multilateral institutions to support growth-oriented reforms. The plan represented a philosophical shift from austerity to growth, but it failed because banks were unwilling to increase their exposure to countries already struggling to service existing debt.

The **Brady Plan** (1989), proposed by Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady, represented a more fundamental break. It acknowledged that the debts of many Latin American countries were not fully repayable and that debt reduction was necessary. Under the Brady Plan, commercial bank loans were exchanged for "Brady bonds": new bonds with lower face values, lower interest rates, or both, partially collateralized by U.S. Treasury zero-coupon bonds. The debt reduction gave debtors fiscal breathing room, and the Brady bonds were tradeable, allowing banks to exit their Latin American positions.

The Brady Plan is generally regarded as the turning point. The combination of debt reduction, market-oriented reforms (trade liberalization, privatization, fiscal discipline), and recovering commodity prices allowed most countries to resume growth in the 1990s. But the damage was lasting: a generation experienced declining living standards, inequality widened, and the region's development trajectory was set back by at least a decade.

## Lessons

The Latin American debt crisis established several principles relevant to later episodes. First, sovereign debt crises result from the interaction of domestic vulnerabilities (excessive borrowing, fiscal deficits) and external shocks (interest rate increases, commodity price declines). Domestic failures create vulnerability; external shocks provide the trigger. Second, the illiquidity-versus-insolvency distinction is critical for crisis management: treating an insolvency problem as a liquidity problem prolongs the crisis and increases its cost. Third, the absence of an international sovereign bankruptcy mechanism makes resolution slow and costly for all parties. These lessons would be tested again in the Asian crisis (Session 7), Argentina's 2001 default, and the European debt crisis (Session 12).
