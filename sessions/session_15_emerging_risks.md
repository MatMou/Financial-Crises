The preceding sessions have examined crises that have already occurred. This session turns to the risks that may produce the next crisis, or the one after that. Predicting the specific form of the next financial crisis is impossible (as Reinhart and Rogoff note, the essence of "this time is different" thinking is the belief that the specific configuration of circumstances is unprecedented). But identifying the structural vulnerabilities that make crises more likely, and analyzing the mechanisms through which those vulnerabilities could produce systemic instability, is both possible and essential for policy.

This session examines five categories of emerging risk: China's property crisis and its implications for the global economy, climate-related financial risk, sovereign debt sustainability in the post-pandemic world, the risks created by artificial intelligence and algorithmic trading, and the financial stability implications of geopolitical fragmentation. The session concludes by reflecting on what the history of financial crises can and cannot tell us about the future.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Analyze China's property sector crisis using the frameworks of credit booms, Minsky dynamics, and balance sheet recessions developed in Sessions 2-3.
2. Explain the channels through which climate change could produce financial instability, distinguishing between physical risk, transition risk, and liability risk.
3. Assess the sustainability of sovereign debt in the advanced and emerging economies after the fiscal expansions of the pandemic era.
4. Evaluate the financial stability risks created by AI and algorithmic trading, including model monoculture, speed-of-light contagion, and herding.
5. Explain how geopolitical fragmentation could disrupt the dollar-based international financial system and create new sources of financial instability.
6. Reflect on what the historical study of financial crises reveals about the limits of prediction and the importance of resilience.

## Session Outline

**15.1 China's Property Crisis and the Evergrande Moment.** The buildup of the Chinese property boom, the Evergrande default, the broader crisis in Chinese real estate, and the implications for the Chinese and global economies.

**15.2 Climate-Related Financial Risk.** Physical risk, transition risk, and liability risk. The repricing of carbon-intensive assets, stranded assets, and the role of central banks and financial regulators.

**15.3 Sovereign Debt Sustainability in the Post-Pandemic World.** The fiscal legacy of COVID, the interaction between high debt and rising interest rates, and the risks of a sovereign debt crisis in advanced and emerging economies.

**15.4 AI, Algorithmic Trading, and New Sources of Systemic Risk.** Model monoculture, flash crashes, algorithmic herding, and the systemic implications of AI-driven financial decision-making.

**15.5 Geopolitical Fragmentation and Financial Stability.** Sanctions, de-dollarization, financial decoupling, and the risks of a fragmented international monetary system.

**15.6 The Next Crisis: What We Can and Cannot Predict.** The limits of crisis prediction, the importance of resilience over prediction, and the recurring patterns that history reveals.
