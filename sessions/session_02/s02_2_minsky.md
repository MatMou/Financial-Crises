subtitle: The endogenous cycle of stability and fragility

Subsection 1.3 introduced Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis (FIH) at a narrative level, describing the three financing regimes (hedge, speculative, Ponzi) and the endogenous drift from stability to fragility. This subsection goes deeper. We examine the theoretical structure of the FIH, including its foundations in Keynes's investment theory, Minsky's two-price model, and the cash-flow analysis that gives the hypothesis its operational content. We also discuss the institutional dimension of the FIH, its relationship to mainstream macroeconomics, and the principal criticisms that have been leveled against it.

Minsky's work is unusual in the history of economic thought. He wrote primarily in prose rather than in equations, published mainly in working papers and books rather than in the leading journals, and was largely ignored by the mainstream profession during his lifetime (he died in 1996). The global financial crisis of 2007-2009 transformed his reputation. The crisis fit the Minsky template so closely that the term "Minsky moment" moved from a term of art used by a few financial market practitioners to a standard phrase in central bank speeches, newspaper editorials, and academic papers. Understanding what Minsky actually said, as opposed to the simplified version that circulates in popular discussion, is important for evaluating both the strengths and the limitations of his framework.

## Intellectual foundations: Keynes's investment theory

Minsky was, above all, a Keynesian, but his Keynesianism was of a specific kind. He drew on what he considered the revolutionary core of Keynes's *General Theory* (1936), which he distinguished sharply from the "bastardized" version of Keynesianism that entered textbooks through the IS-LM framework of Hicks and Hansen ({cite:t}`Minsky1986`).

For Minsky, the essential insight of the *General Theory* was not the multiplier, not sticky wages, and not the liquidity trap, but rather the theory of investment under fundamental uncertainty. Keynes argued that investment decisions depend on entrepreneurs' expectations about an inherently uncertain future, expectations that cannot be reduced to probability distributions because the relevant events are unique and non-repeatable. Investment is therefore governed by what Keynes called "animal spirits," meaning the confidence, optimism, and willingness to act that fluctuate for reasons that are not fully captured by rational calculation.

Minsky built on this foundation by asking a question that Keynes did not fully address: how does the financial system interact with investment decisions? Keynes treated the interest rate as the link between the monetary and real sectors, but did not develop a detailed theory of how the structure of financial markets and institutions shapes the investment process. Minsky's contribution was to put finance at the center of the story. The FIH is, in essence, an investment theory of the business cycle embedded in a financial theory of investment.

## The two-price model

The analytical core of the FIH is what Minsky called the "two-price" model. The two prices in question are the price of current output and the price of capital assets.

The **price of current output** is determined by the standard forces of supply and demand in the goods market, reflecting production costs (labor, materials, overhead) plus a markup. This is the price level that appears in conventional macroeconomic models.

The **price of capital assets** is determined by the expected future income stream from owning and operating the asset, discounted at a rate that reflects both the time value of money and the degree of uncertainty about the future. This is an asset price, and it is determined not in the goods market but in the financial markets, where expectations, liquidity preferences, and the availability of credit all play a role.

Investment occurs when the price of capital assets exceeds the price of current output, meaning when the capitalized value of the expected income from a new investment project exceeds the cost of producing the capital goods required. This is Minsky's version of Keynes's marginal efficiency of capital: investment is profitable when the demand price for capital (what investors are willing to pay for an asset, given their expectations about its future returns) exceeds the supply price (what it costs to produce).

The crucial point is that the demand price for capital assets is not a fixed quantity. It depends on expectations, which are formed in an environment of fundamental uncertainty, and on the availability and cost of external finance. When confidence is high and credit is abundant, the demand price rises. When confidence is low and credit is scarce, the demand price falls. The gap between the demand price and the supply price, which determines the level of investment, is therefore inherently volatile and procyclical.

Minsky added a third element to the two-price model: the **lender's and borrower's risk**. Even if the demand price of capital assets exceeds the supply price, investment may not occur if either the borrower or the lender perceives the financing arrangement as too risky. Borrower's risk reflects the entrepreneur's assessment of the probability that the investment will not generate enough income to service the debt. Lender's risk reflects the bank's assessment of the probability that the borrower will default. Both risks are subjective and endogenous: they decline during periods of prosperity (as defaults are rare and cash flows are strong) and increase during periods of stress (as defaults rise and cash flows weaken).

The interaction of these three elements, the demand price for capital, the supply price of current output, and the lender's and borrower's risk, generates the investment cycle. During a boom, rising confidence pushes up the demand price for capital, while the absence of defaults reduces perceived risk, which reduces the margin between the demand price and the price that borrowers are actually willing to pay (net of borrower's risk) and the price that lenders are willing to finance (net of lender's risk). The gap between the two prices widens, investment accelerates, and the expansion feeds on itself. During a bust, the process reverses.

## Cash-flow analysis and the three financing regimes

The operational content of the FIH comes from Minsky's classification of financing positions, which was introduced briefly in subsection 1.3 and is developed more fully here.

Minsky's classification is based on the relationship between an economic unit's expected cash inflows from operations and its contractual cash outflows on debt. The three regimes are defined as follows.

In **hedge finance**, the unit's expected operating cash flow in every period is sufficient to meet all contractual obligations, including both interest and scheduled principal repayments. The unit is financially robust: it can service its debt without relying on the capital market or on asset sales. Its solvency is not contingent on the continued availability of credit or on the behavior of asset prices.

In **speculative finance**, the unit's expected operating cash flow is sufficient to cover interest payments but not principal repayments. The unit must roll over its debt: when a loan matures, it must take out a new loan to repay the old one. This is not necessarily imprudent; maturity transformation is a fundamental feature of banking and of many other financial activities. But speculative finance introduces vulnerability to changes in the cost and availability of credit. If interest rates rise or if lenders refuse to roll over the debt, a speculative unit faces a liquidity crisis even though it may be solvent in the sense that the present value of its assets exceeds the present value of its liabilities.

In **Ponzi finance**, the unit's expected operating cash flow is insufficient to cover even the interest on its debt. The shortfall must be financed by additional borrowing (which increases the outstanding principal) or by selling assets. A Ponzi unit is viable only if the value of its assets is appreciating fast enough to offset the accumulation of debt, or if credit continues to be available on sufficiently generous terms. The label "Ponzi" does not imply fraud; it refers to the structural dependence of the unit's solvency on capital gains or continued access to credit.

The key insight is that these regimes are not fixed characteristics of individual firms or households. They are endogenous outcomes that depend on the macroeconomic environment, particularly on the level of interest rates, the availability of credit, and the behavior of asset prices. A firm that is a hedge borrower at low interest rates may become a speculative borrower if rates rise. A household that is a speculative borrower when house prices are increasing may become a Ponzi borrower if prices stagnate or decline.

More importantly, the distribution of units across the three regimes shifts endogenously over the course of the business cycle, and this shift is the mechanism through which stability generates fragility.

## The endogenous transition: why stability is destabilizing

The central proposition of the FIH is that a prolonged period of stable economic growth and low default rates causes the financial system to evolve, gradually and endogenously, from a regime dominated by hedge finance toward a regime in which speculative and Ponzi finance are increasingly prevalent.

The mechanism operates through several reinforcing channels.

The first channel works through expectations. During a period of sustained prosperity, the subjective probability of adverse outcomes (recession, default, asset price decline) falls. Firms and households revise their expectations upward and become willing to take on more debt relative to their income. Lenders observe low default rates and strong collateral values, and relax their lending standards. Both borrower's risk and lender's risk decline, widening the gap between the demand price and supply price of capital and stimulating investment and asset acquisition.

The second channel works through competitive pressure. Financial institutions compete for market share and profitability. In a stable environment with low default rates, institutions that maintain conservative lending standards earn lower returns than institutions that extend credit more aggressively. The pressure to match competitors' profitability leads to a systematic erosion of lending standards across the industry. This is the mechanism that Charles Prince, then CEO of Citigroup, described in 2007 when he said that "as long as the music is playing, you've got to get up and dance."

The third channel works through financial innovation. Periods of stability and prosperity encourage the development of new financial instruments and practices that allow market participants to take on more risk, often in ways that are not fully understood by regulators or even by the institutions themselves. Securitization, off-balance-sheet vehicles, credit default swaps, and other innovations of the pre-2007 period are examples. These innovations are typically presented as devices for managing and distributing risk, but they often have the effect of increasing the total amount of risk in the system by making it easier to create leveraged positions.

The fourth channel works through the institutional memory of regulation. Financial regulations are typically introduced after a crisis and are designed to prevent a recurrence of the specific problems that caused the crisis. Over time, as the memory of the crisis fades and the costs of regulation become more salient than its benefits, political pressure builds to relax the rules. Regulators themselves may come to believe that the regulations are no longer necessary because the financial system has become more sophisticated. This process of regulatory relaxation, which Minsky saw as a natural and predictable consequence of financial stability, is closely related to the "this time is different" syndrome discussed in subsection 1.3.

Through these four channels, the financial structure of the economy becomes progressively more fragile during a boom, even though no single participant has acted irrationally. Each hedge borrower who takes on additional debt, each lender who relaxes a lending standard, each regulator who approves a new financial instrument, is making a decision that appears justified by the available evidence. But the collective result of these individually rational decisions is a financial system that is increasingly vulnerable to a disturbance.

## The Minsky moment and the crisis dynamics

The transition from boom to bust, the "Minsky moment," occurs when some event reveals the fragility of the financial structure. Minsky did not specify what the triggering event would be; it could be a policy change (such as an increase in interest rates), an external shock (such as a commodity price movement), or simply the exhaustion of profitable opportunities as the boom matures. The trigger is almost always small relative to the magnitude of the ensuing crisis, which is precisely the point: the crisis is caused not by the trigger but by the accumulated fragility of the system.

Once the Minsky moment arrives, the dynamics of the bust mirror the dynamics of the boom but in reverse. Ponzi units, which depend on rising asset prices or continued access to credit, are the first to fail. Their distress forces asset sales, which depresses prices. Falling asset prices push speculative units into Ponzi territory, as the value of their collateral declines and their ability to roll over debt is impaired. The contagion spreads through the financial system as interconnected institutions discover that their counterparties are in distress.

The process is amplified by two mechanisms that Minsky emphasized. The first is the **debt-deflation** mechanism identified by Fisher (subsection 2.1): falling asset prices increase the real burden of nominal debt, which forces further asset sales, which drives prices down further. The second is what might be called the **liquidity preference** mechanism: as uncertainty increases, both borrowers and lenders become reluctant to enter into new financial commitments. Credit contracts not because the central bank has tightened policy but because private agents are unwilling to lend or borrow. The result is a sharp and self-reinforcing decline in investment, employment, and output.

## The institutional dimension

One aspect of Minsky's work that distinguishes it from most formal macroeconomic models is its emphasis on institutional evolution. Minsky argued that the financial system is not a fixed structure that can be described once and for all. It evolves continuously as market participants innovate, as regulations change, and as the macroeconomic environment shifts. The FIH is therefore not just a theory of the business cycle; it is a theory of the co-evolution of financial institutions and macroeconomic dynamics.

This institutional dimension has several implications. First, it means that the specific form of financial fragility changes over time. The fragility that led to 19th-century banking panics (illiquid reserve structures) is different from the fragility that led to the savings and loan crisis of the 1980s (interest rate risk and regulatory forbearance), which is different from the fragility that led to the 2007-2009 crisis (securitized mortgage debt, off-balance-sheet vehicles, and interconnected derivatives markets). The underlying logic is the same in each case, as stability breeds fragility, but the institutional channels through which the logic operates are historically specific.

Second, it means that regulatory interventions, while necessary, are inherently limited. Each regulatory reform addresses the vulnerabilities revealed by the most recent crisis, but the financial system adapts and evolves in ways that create new vulnerabilities. Minsky did not argue that regulation is futile; he argued that it must be adaptive and must anticipate the endogenous tendency of the financial system to innovate around constraints.

Third, it means that the FIH cannot be fully captured in a single mathematical model with a fixed institutional structure. This is one reason why Minsky wrote primarily in prose: he believed that the richness of the institutional dynamics could not be reduced to a set of equations without losing essential features. This methodological choice has been both a strength and a weakness. It has allowed Minsky's ideas to be applied flexibly to a wide range of historical episodes, but it has also made the FIH difficult to test rigorously and has limited its influence within a profession that increasingly values formal modeling.

## Criticisms and limitations

The FIH has been subject to several important criticisms, which should be weighed alongside its considerable explanatory power.

The most common criticism from mainstream economists is the **lack of formal microfoundations**. Minsky's theory describes how the aggregate financial structure evolves over the cycle, but it does not derive the behavior of individual agents from explicit optimization problems. The drift from hedge to speculative to Ponzi finance is described as a tendency, but the mechanism by which individual agents make the decisions that produce this drift is left somewhat vague. Critics argue that without microfoundations, it is impossible to evaluate the robustness of the theory, to distinguish it from alternative explanations, or to derive precise quantitative predictions.

A related criticism concerns **rationality and learning**. If the Minsky cycle is a recurring pattern, why don't rational agents learn to anticipate it? Why do lenders continue to relax their standards during booms if they know (or should know) that a bust will follow? Minsky's answer, that stability itself reduces the perceived probability of a bust, is psychologically plausible but does not constitute a formal model of belief formation. Rational expectations models, by contrast, require agents to be aware of the structure of the economy and to form expectations consistently with that structure. Reconciling the Minsky cycle with rational expectations has been a major challenge for the formal literature.

A third criticism concerns the **role of monetary policy and regulation**. The FIH describes an endogenous cycle, but in practice the timing and severity of financial crises are heavily influenced by policy decisions. The Volcker shock of 1979-1982, which triggered the Latin American debt crisis, was not an endogenous event but a deliberate policy choice. The deregulation of the U.S. financial system in the 1980s and 1990s was the result of specific political decisions, not an automatic consequence of financial stability. Critics argue that Minsky's framework, by emphasizing the endogenous nature of instability, underweights the role of exogenous policy choices.

Fourth, there is the question of **empirical testing**. The FIH makes qualitative predictions (credit will expand during booms, financial structures will become more fragile, crises will follow periods of stability), but these predictions are broad enough to be consistent with many alternative theories. Testing the specific mechanism that Minsky proposed, the endogenous shift from hedge to speculative to Ponzi finance, requires detailed data on the financing structures of individual firms and households, which is difficult to obtain. Some recent work has attempted to construct Minsky indices that track the distribution of financing regimes over time, but this literature is still in its early stages.

Finally, there is the criticism that the FIH is **better as history than as prediction**. The framework is powerful as a retrospective explanatory tool: after a crisis, one can almost always identify the elements of the Minsky cycle in the preceding boom. But it is less useful as a predictive tool, because it does not specify when the transition from boom to bust will occur or what will trigger it. Every boom involves increasing leverage and relaxed lending standards, but not every boom ends in a crisis. The FIH identifies the necessary conditions for a crisis (accumulated financial fragility) but does not provide sufficient conditions.

Despite these limitations, the FIH remains one of the most important theoretical contributions to the study of financial crises. Its core insight, that financial fragility is endogenous and that stability breeds instability, is now widely accepted, even by economists who do not work in the Minskyan tradition. The formal models discussed in the subsequent subsections, including the financial accelerator (subsection 2.5) and the models of bank runs and contagion (subsection 2.4), can be understood in part as attempts to capture specific aspects of Minsky's broader vision within the framework of rigorous, microfounded economic theory.
