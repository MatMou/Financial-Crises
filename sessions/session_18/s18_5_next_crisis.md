title: "18.5 What Will the Next Crisis Look Like?"
subtitle: Applying the patterns to the present
---

Predicting the specific form of the next financial crisis is, as argued in subsection 15.6, impossible. But the patterns identified in this course can be used to assess the current landscape of vulnerabilities and to identify the scenarios that deserve the most attention. This subsection applies the ten lessons of the preceding subsection to the risks analyzed in Session 15, producing a structured assessment of where the next crisis might originate.

## The current vulnerability landscape

Several of the conditions that have historically preceded financial crises are present in the current environment.

**Elevated leverage.** Global debt (government + household + corporate) has reached approximately 330% of GDP in the advanced economies and approximately 250% globally, the highest levels in recorded history. Government debt ratios are at or near World War II levels in many countries. Corporate debt, particularly in the leveraged loan and private credit markets, has grown rapidly. The leverage creates sensitivity to interest rate increases, economic downturns, and shocks of any kind.

**Elevated asset valuations.** U.S. equity valuations, as measured by the cyclically adjusted price-to-earnings ratio (CAPE), are above their historical average and near levels that have historically been followed by below-average returns. Property prices in several countries remain elevated relative to rents and incomes. The search for yield during the long period of low interest rates pushed asset prices higher across the board, and the normalization of rates has only partially reversed this.

**Financial activity outside the regulatory perimeter.** Private credit markets (private debt funds that make loans outside the regulated banking system) have grown rapidly, from approximately $500 billion in 2015 to over $1.5 trillion by 2024. These markets are opaque, illiquid, and largely unregulated, and they resemble in some respects the shadow banking system that was at the center of the GFC. The crypto ecosystem, despite the crash of 2022, continues to grow and innovate outside the regulatory perimeter.

**Geopolitical fragmentation.** The weaponization of financial infrastructure (subsection 15.5), the decoupling of the U.S. and Chinese economies, and the rise of economic nationalism create new sources of uncertainty and potential disruption that have no close precedent in the post-war period.

## Plausible scenarios

Several scenarios for the next crisis emerge from the analysis.

**A sovereign debt crisis in the advanced economies** could be triggered by a fiscal shock (a recession that pushes already-elevated debt ratios above a critical threshold), a loss of market confidence (triggered by political instability or perceived fiscal irresponsibility), or a structural shift in the demand for government bonds (if foreign central banks reduce their holdings of Treasuries as part of the de-dollarization trend). Italy, with its combination of high debt, low growth, and political instability, is often cited as the most vulnerable large economy.

**A private credit crisis** could be triggered by a recession that produces a wave of defaults on leveraged loans and private credit, imposing losses on the private debt funds that hold them and on the institutional investors (pension funds, insurance companies, endowments) that have allocated to those funds. The opacity of the private credit market means that the losses could be concentrated in ways that are not visible until the crisis is already underway.

**A China hard landing** could be triggered by a further deterioration of the property sector, producing a balance sheet recession that depresses Chinese domestic demand, reduces Chinese imports, and transmits deflationary pressure to the global economy through the trade and commodity channels.

**A climate-related financial shock** could be triggered by an extreme weather event that produces insured losses exceeding the capacity of the insurance industry, or by an abrupt policy shift that produces a sharp repricing of carbon-intensive assets.

**A geopolitical shock** could produce a financial crisis if it disrupts the international payment system, triggers a sudden reallocation of reserve assets, or produces a commodity price shock comparable to the oil shocks of the 1970s.

## What we know and what we do not

We know, from the historical record, that the current configuration of vulnerabilities, high leverage, elevated asset prices, financial activity outside the regulatory perimeter, and geopolitical uncertainty, has historically been associated with elevated crisis risk. We know that the specific trigger will be a surprise. And we know that the policy response will be shaped by the institutional frameworks, the political constraints, and the intellectual assumptions that prevail at the time.

What we do not know is when the next crisis will occur, what form it will take, and how severe it will be. The honest answer to "what will the next crisis look like?" is "we don't know, but it will be different from what we expect." The appropriate response is not to try to predict the specific form but to ensure that the financial system is resilient enough to survive whatever form it takes.
