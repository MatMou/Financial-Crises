---
title: "18.3 The Regulation-Innovation Arms Race"
subtitle: Can regulators keep up with the financial system they regulate?
---

One of the most persistent patterns in the history of financial crises is the cycle of financial innovation, crisis, and regulatory response. Financial innovation creates new instruments, institutions, and markets that generate genuine economic benefits but also create new vulnerabilities. The vulnerabilities eventually produce a crisis. The crisis produces a regulatory response that addresses the specific vulnerabilities that caused the crisis. The new regulation constrains the old forms of risk-taking but does not (and cannot) anticipate the new forms that will emerge. And financial innovation continues, finding new ways to take risk that are outside the regulatory perimeter, until the next crisis reveals the next set of vulnerabilities.

## The historical pattern

The pattern is visible in every era of financial history studied in this course.

The South Sea Bubble (subsection 4.1) led to the Bubble Act, which restricted the formation of joint-stock companies. The restriction constrained one form of speculation but did not prevent the speculative manias that occurred in subsequent decades through other channels.

The banking panics of the 19th century (subsection 4.3) led to the creation of central banks and the development of the lender of last resort function. The LOLR reduced the frequency of banking panics but did not prevent the Great Depression, which operated through different mechanisms (the interwar gold standard, the Federal Reserve's policy failures).

The Great Depression (Session 5) led to the New Deal regulatory architecture: deposit insurance, Glass-Steagall, the SEC. These institutions produced approximately half a century of financial stability, but they were gradually eroded as financial innovation (securitization, derivatives, shadow banking) created new forms of intermediation that were outside the regulatory perimeter.

The GFC (Sessions 9-11) led to Dodd-Frank, Basel III, and the macroprudential framework. These reforms addressed the specific vulnerabilities that had caused the GFC (inadequate capital, unregulated derivatives, shadow banking), but financial activity has continued to evolve (fintech, crypto, DeFi, private credit markets) in ways that create new risks outside the regulatory perimeter.

## Why the pattern persists

The regulation-innovation cycle persists for several reasons that are, to some degree, inherent in the structure of financial regulation.

**Regulation is backward-looking.** Regulatory frameworks are designed in response to the most recent crisis and are optimized to prevent a repetition of that specific crisis. They are less well-suited to anticipating the novel forms of risk-taking that will emerge in the future. The New Deal regulations were designed to prevent 1930s-style bank runs and were not equipped to address 2000s-style shadow banking runs. The post-GFC regulations were designed to prevent a repetition of the subprime-securitization-shadow-banking crisis and may not be equipped to address crises that emerge from AI-driven finance, DeFi, or climate-related financial disruption.

**Innovation is driven by powerful incentives.** Financial innovation is motivated by the desire to earn higher returns, to reduce costs, to circumvent regulation, and to serve unmet demand. These incentives are strong and persistent, and they ensure that the financial system will continue to evolve regardless of the regulatory framework. The innovations are often genuinely useful (securitization reduces the cost of credit, derivatives allow risk management, digital payments increase efficiency), which makes it politically difficult to restrict them even when they also create risks.

**Regulatory arbitrage is rational.** When regulation imposes costs on certain types of financial activity (by requiring capital, limiting leverage, or restricting certain transactions), profit-seeking institutions have an incentive to shift the activity to less-regulated channels. The growth of the shadow banking system before the GFC was, in large part, a response to the capital requirements imposed on regulated banks: banks moved activity off-balance-sheet (into SIVs, ABCP conduits, and repo-financed holdings) to avoid the capital charges. Post-GFC, the growth of private credit markets (private debt funds that make loans outside the regulated banking system) is a similar response to the higher capital requirements imposed by Basel III.

## Can the cycle be broken?

The macroprudential framework (subsection 17.1) represents the most sophisticated attempt to break the regulation-innovation cycle. By focusing on systemic risk rather than on specific instruments or institutions, macroprudential policy is, in principle, adaptable to new forms of financial activity. The CCyB can be activated regardless of whether the credit boom is occurring through traditional banks, shadow banks, or DeFi protocols. LTV limits can constrain housing credit regardless of whether the lender is a bank, a fintech company, or a peer-to-peer platform.

In practice, the adaptability of macroprudential policy is limited by the same political economy constraints described in subsection 17.6. Extending macroprudential regulation to new sectors requires legislative action, which is slow, contested, and subject to industry lobbying. The crypto crises of 2022 (Session 14) demonstrated that a large and rapidly growing financial sector can operate entirely outside the regulatory perimeter for years before a crisis forces the regulatory response.

The more realistic conclusion is that the regulation-innovation cycle cannot be broken entirely, but it can be managed. The speed of the regulatory response to new forms of risk can be increased (by empowering macroprudential authorities to extend their perimeter without new legislation). The resilience of the system can be maintained (by ensuring that the regulated core has sufficient capital and liquidity to absorb shocks that originate in the unregulated periphery). And the severity of the inevitable crises can be reduced (by maintaining the crisis management tools described in Session 17).
