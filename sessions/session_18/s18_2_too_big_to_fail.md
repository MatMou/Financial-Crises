---
title: "18.2 Has 'Too Big to Fail' Been Solved?"
subtitle: The question that defines post-crisis reform
---

The too-big-to-fail (TBTF) problem was the central political scandal of the GFC: the perception that governments used taxpayer funds to rescue the institutions that had caused the crisis, while ordinary households bore the consequences of the recession. The post-GFC reform agenda was, in large part, designed to eliminate TBTF by ensuring that failing systemically important institutions could be resolved without taxpayer bailouts. Whether this objective has been achieved is one of the most important open questions in financial regulation.

## The case that TBTF has been solved

The optimistic case rests on several pillars. The Dodd-Frank Orderly Liquidation Authority (subsection 11.4) provides a legal framework for resolving failing SIFIs. The FSB's Total Loss-Absorbing Capacity (TLAC) standard requires G-SIBs to hold substantial bail-in-able debt, ensuring that shareholders and creditors, rather than taxpayers, absorb the losses. Living wills force SIFIs to demonstrate that they can be resolved in an orderly manner. The Basel III capital surcharges make G-SIBs hold more capital, reducing the probability that they will fail in the first place. And the stress testing framework (CCAR) provides ongoing assessment of whether institutions have sufficient capital to survive adverse scenarios.

Academic evidence suggests that the implicit TBTF subsidy has diminished since the GFC. Studies of the funding cost advantage enjoyed by TBTF banks (the difference between their borrowing costs and those of comparable smaller banks) have generally found that the advantage has narrowed since the implementation of the post-GFC reforms, suggesting that the market assigns a lower probability to government bailouts.

## The case that TBTF persists

The skeptical case is also compelling. The largest banks are even larger than before the crisis: JPMorgan Chase's total assets have approximately doubled since 2007. The consolidation that occurred during the crisis (JPMorgan acquiring Bear Stearns and Washington Mutual, Bank of America acquiring Merrill Lynch and Countrywide) increased concentration rather than reducing it. The largest U.S. banks now hold a larger share of total banking system assets than they did before the crisis.

The resolution framework has never been tested in a systemic crisis. The OLA has never been used. The bail-in mechanism has been applied in a few cases (Cypriot banks in 2013, the resolution of Spain's Banco Popular in 2017), but never to a globally systemically important institution during a period of systemic stress. The SVB episode of March 2023 (subsection 13.5) raised uncomfortable questions: the authorities chose to guarantee all deposits (including those above the insurance limit) and to create the Bank Term Funding Program, rather than imposing losses on uninsured depositors, suggesting that the political and economic pressures to avoid imposing losses remain powerful even for institutions that were not designated as G-SIBs.

The cross-border dimension adds further complexity. A globally systemically important bank operates in dozens of countries, each with its own resolution authority, its own legal framework, and its own political interests. Resolving such an institution in an orderly manner requires coordination among these authorities, and the incentive for each national authority is to protect its own depositors and creditors at the expense of foreign claimants. The crisis resolution simulations conducted by the FSB have identified significant operational and legal obstacles to cross-border resolution that have not been fully addressed.

## The honest assessment

The honest assessment is that the TBTF problem has been significantly mitigated but not eliminated. The probability that a major bank will fail has been reduced (by higher capital requirements and better supervision). The loss-absorbing capacity of major banks has been increased (by TLAC and bail-in provisions). And the legal framework for resolution has been established (by OLA and the BRRD). But whether the framework would actually be used in a systemic crisis, when the political and economic pressures to bail out are at their maximum, remains an open and genuinely uncertain question. The credibility of the "no more bailouts" commitment is the central issue, and credibility can only be established by testing, which no one wants to do.
