---
title: "18.1 Are Financial Crises Inherent to Capitalism?"
subtitle: Minsky versus the optimists
---

The deepest question raised by this course is whether financial crises are an eliminable defect of the capitalist system or an inescapable consequence of its fundamental mechanisms. The answer has profound implications for policy: if crises can be prevented through sufficiently good institutions and policies, then the priority is to design those institutions and policies correctly. If crises are inherent, then the priority shifts to building resilience and managing the damage when they occur.

## The Minsky view: instability as an endogenous feature

The Minsky hypothesis, developed in subsection 2.2 and observed operating throughout the historical record of this course, holds that financial instability is generated endogenously by the normal functioning of a capitalist economy. Stability breeds complacency, complacency breeds risk-taking, risk-taking breeds fragility, and fragility breeds crisis. The cycle does not require external shocks, policy errors, or institutional failures to produce crises; it requires only the passage of time and the normal operation of profit-seeking, forward-looking agents operating under uncertainty.

The evidence in favor of the Minsky view is substantial. The Reinhart-Rogoff data (subsection 1.4) document financial crises across centuries, across countries, across different institutional arrangements, and across different levels of financial development. Crises occurred under the gold standard and under fiat money, under fixed exchange rates and floating rates, under regulated financial systems and deregulated ones. The regularity of crises across such diverse conditions is difficult to reconcile with explanations based on specific policy errors or institutional failures, which would predict that crises should be concentrated in specific institutional settings.

The psychological and behavioral mechanisms that drive the Minsky cycle, extrapolative expectations, herding, overconfidence during booms, and panic during busts (subsection 2.6), are deeply rooted in human cognitive architecture and are unlikely to be eliminated by institutional reform. The "this time is different" syndrome (subsection 1.3) is not a failure of intelligence; it is a feature of how human minds process information under uncertainty, systematically overweighting recent experience and underweighting base rates.

## The policy-failure view: crises as avoidable mistakes

The alternative view holds that crises are caused by identifiable policy errors and institutional failures that can, in principle, be corrected. Each crisis studied in this course can be attributed to specific mistakes: the Fed's failure to act as lender of last resort during the Great Depression, the deregulation of S&Ls without addressing the moral hazard of deposit insurance, the failure to regulate the shadow banking system before the GFC, the incomplete architecture of the Eurozone.

Proponents of this view point to the "quiet period" of the Bretton Woods era (subsection 6.2), when systemic banking crises were virtually absent in the advanced economies, as evidence that good institutions can prevent crises. The combination of capital controls, financial regulation, deposit insurance, and active macroeconomic management produced approximately three decades of financial stability, demonstrating that crises are not inevitable but are the product of institutional choices.

The policy-failure view implies an optimistic agenda: identify the institutional and policy failures that caused previous crises, correct them, and crises will be prevented. The post-GFC reform agenda (subsection 11.4) was motivated by this view: higher capital requirements, improved liquidity regulation, macroprudential oversight, and resolution frameworks were designed to correct the specific failures that had produced the GFC.

## A synthesis

The most defensible position lies between the two extremes. Crises are not purely exogenous events imposed on a well-functioning system by bad luck or bad policy. But neither are they iron laws of nature that cannot be mitigated by institutional design. The historical record suggests that good institutions and policies can reduce the frequency and severity of crises (the Bretton Woods quiet period is real evidence), but they cannot eliminate crises entirely, because the Minsky dynamics of endogenous risk accumulation operate within any institutional framework, and because the political economy of regulation (subsection 17.6) ensures that the institutions will eventually be weakened.

The implication is that the appropriate policy stance combines prevention (to reduce the frequency and severity of crises) with preparedness (to manage the damage when crises occur despite prevention). Neither prevention alone nor management alone is sufficient. The history of financial crises is the history of societies oscillating between overconfidence in prevention (which leads to complacency) and fatalism about inevitability (which leads to under-investment in the institutions that can reduce the damage).
