The global financial crisis of 2007-2009 was the most severe financial crisis since the Great Depression. It destroyed trillions of dollars of wealth, produced the deepest recession in the advanced economies since the 1930s, triggered sovereign debt crises in Europe, and led to the most far-reaching reforms of financial regulation since the New Deal. Understanding how the crisis developed requires a detailed examination of the financial instruments, institutions, and regulatory failures that created the conditions for catastrophe.

This session provides that examination. It traces the build-up to the crisis from the U.S. housing bubble through the machinery of subprime lending, securitization, credit derivatives, and shadow banking that transformed mortgage risk into systemic risk. The session draws heavily on the theoretical frameworks developed in Sessions 2 and 3, particularly the Minsky model of endogenous instability, the Diamond-Dybvig model of bank runs, the financial accelerator, and the analysis of information asymmetries.

The session is the first of three that cover the global financial crisis. Session 9 focuses on the build-up; Session 10 covers the crisis itself (from the initial tremors in 2007 through the panic of September-October 2008); and Session 11 examines the policy response and the aftermath.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Describe the U.S. housing bubble, including the evidence that house prices had departed from fundamental values, and explain the role of low interest rates, speculation, and credit availability in driving the boom.
2. Explain the originate-to-distribute model of mortgage lending and analyze how it created agency problems that led to the deterioration of underwriting standards.
3. Describe the securitization process, including the creation of mortgage-backed securities and collateralized debt obligations, and explain how tranching and credit enhancement created the illusion of safety.
4. Explain the role of credit default swaps in amplifying and redistributing mortgage credit risk, with particular attention to the case of AIG.
5. Describe the shadow banking system and explain why it was vulnerable to runs despite performing the same economic functions as the traditional banking system.
6. Analyze the failures of credit rating agencies, financial regulators, and the international monetary system that allowed the vulnerabilities to accumulate unchecked.

## Session Outline

**9.1 The U.S. Housing Bubble.** The evidence for a housing bubble, the role of speculation and credit availability, and the regional variation in the housing boom.

**9.2 Subprime Lending and the Originate-to-Distribute Model.** The expansion of subprime lending, the deterioration of underwriting standards, the agency problems in the originate-to-distribute chain, and the role of government-sponsored enterprises.

**9.3 Securitization: CDOs, Tranching, and the Alchemy of AAA.** The mechanics of mortgage-backed securities and collateralized debt obligations, the role of tranching in creating apparently safe securities from risky underlying assets, and the concept of CDO-squared.

**9.4 Credit Default Swaps and Synthetic Risk Transfer.** The growth of the CDS market, the role of CDS in creating synthetic exposure to mortgage risk, the counterparty risk that CDS created, and the case of AIG Financial Products.

**9.5 The Shadow Banking System.** The institutions and instruments that constituted the shadow banking system, the economic logic of shadow banking, and the vulnerability of the system to runs.

**9.6 Rating Agencies, Regulatory Failure, and Global Imbalances.** The conflicts of interest in the rating agency business model, the regulatory gaps that allowed risks to accumulate, and the role of global imbalances in fueling the credit boom.
