---
title: "12.6 Austerity, Internal Devaluation, and Lessons"
subtitle: The economics and politics of adjustment within a monetary union
---

The European sovereign debt crisis imposed on the peripheral countries a form of adjustment that had not been seen in the advanced economies since the 1930s: **internal devaluation**, the reduction of domestic wages and prices relative to trading partners, as a substitute for the exchange rate devaluation that membership in the monetary union precluded. The experience of internal devaluation, its costs, its effectiveness, and its political consequences, is one of the most important lessons of the European crisis, and it has direct implications for the future of the Eurozone and for other monetary unions.

## The mechanics of internal devaluation

In a country with its own currency, an overvalued exchange rate can be corrected by devaluation: the central bank allows the currency to depreciate, which immediately reduces the cost of the country's exports in foreign currency terms and makes imports more expensive in domestic currency terms. The adjustment is rapid (it occurs as soon as the exchange rate moves), relatively painless (it reduces real wages through higher import prices rather than through nominal wage cuts), and politically manageable (the government can present the devaluation as a necessary response to market forces).

In a monetary union, this option is not available. The country must instead achieve the same real adjustment through a reduction in domestic wages and prices. This requires either explicit wage cuts (which are politically difficult and are resisted by workers and unions) or a prolonged period of high unemployment that gradually forces wages down (which is even more painful). The adjustment is slow (wages are sticky, particularly downward), painful (it requires extended unemployment and recession), and politically destabilizing (the social costs are concentrated on workers and the unemployed).

## The experience of the crisis countries

The peripheral countries underwent internal devaluations of varying degrees during the crisis. Greece experienced the most extreme adjustment: nominal wages fell by approximately 20% and unit labor costs fell by approximately 15% between 2010 and 2014. Ireland achieved a significant cost adjustment, aided by the flexibility of its labor market and the presence of large multinational firms whose output prices were set in global markets. Spain achieved a partial cost adjustment, concentrated in the non-tradable sector. Portugal's adjustment was more modest.

The internal devaluations did eventually restore competitiveness. Current account deficits in the peripheral countries narrowed dramatically and, in several cases, swung to surplus. Exports grew, particularly in Ireland and Spain. But the adjustment was achieved primarily through the compression of imports (which fell because domestic demand collapsed) rather than through the expansion of exports (which grew but not by enough to offset the decline in domestic demand). The distinction matters: import compression reflects a contraction of the economy, while export expansion reflects a genuine improvement in competitiveness. The peripheral countries' current account adjustment was primarily the former, which is the painful and welfare-reducing form of adjustment.

## The austerity debate in the European context

The austerity programs imposed on the peripheral countries by the Troika generated a fierce debate that mirrored the broader fiscal policy debate discussed in subsection 3.6 but with additional intensity because of the political dimension of conditionality imposed by foreign institutions.

The case for austerity rested on the argument that the peripheral countries had been living beyond their means, that their fiscal positions were unsustainable, and that restoring fiscal credibility required demonstrable commitment to spending cuts and tax increases. The creditors (Germany, the Netherlands, Finland, and the other Northern European countries that were providing the bailout funds) insisted that their taxpayers would not finance continued profligacy, and that conditionality was essential to ensure that the borrowed funds were used to address the underlying problems rather than to postpone adjustment.

The case against austerity rested on the argument that fiscal consolidation during a severe recession is self-defeating: the contractionary effects of spending cuts and tax increases reduce GDP, which reduces tax revenue, which increases the deficit, which requires further austerity, in a vicious cycle. The Blanchard-Leigh finding that multipliers were significantly larger than the Troika's models had assumed (subsection 3.6) was directly relevant: the austerity was more contractionary than the programs predicted, which meant that the fiscal targets were missed, which led to demands for additional austerity, which deepened the recession further.

Greece was the most dramatic illustration. Despite implementing the deepest fiscal consolidation in modern history (a primary balance swing of approximately 16 percentage points of GDP between 2009 and 2014), Greece's debt-to-GDP ratio increased, because the GDP collapse outpaced the deficit reduction. The austerity did not achieve debt sustainability; it achieved social devastation.

## Lessons for monetary union design

The European crisis revealed that the Eurozone's architecture was incomplete and that the missing elements, the absence of a fiscal union, common deposit insurance, a sovereign lender of last resort, and a mechanism for orderly debt restructuring, had been critical contributors to the crisis. The reform agenda that emerged from the crisis addressed some of these gaps.

The **European Stability Mechanism** (ESM), established in 2012, provided a permanent bailout fund with lending capacity of €500 billion, replacing the temporary European Financial Stability Facility. The **Banking Union**, launched in 2014, established the Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM, under which the ECB supervises the largest euro-area banks) and the Single Resolution Mechanism (SRM, which provides a framework for resolving failing banks). The OMT provided a de facto lender of last resort for sovereigns, though subject to conditionality.

But the reform agenda remained incomplete. Common deposit insurance (the third pillar of the Banking Union) was blocked by Northern European opposition. A common fiscal capacity for macroeconomic stabilization did not exist (the NextGenerationEU recovery fund, created in response to the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, represented a step in this direction, but it was presented as a one-time measure rather than a permanent facility). Sovereign debt restructuring remained ad hoc, without a standing mechanism.

The deeper lesson of the European crisis is that a monetary union without a fiscal union is inherently fragile. The gold standard analogy (subsection 12.1) is instructive: the classical gold standard worked tolerably well when the costs of adjustment could be imposed on populations with limited political voice, but it became intolerable when democratic electorates demanded that their governments prioritize domestic welfare over external commitments. The Eurozone faces the same tension: the adjustment mechanism available within a monetary union, internal devaluation, imposes costs that democratic societies find increasingly difficult to accept. The long-term survival of the euro depends on whether the member states can build the institutional mechanisms, fiscal transfers, common insurance, political solidarity, that are needed to make the adjustment process less painful and more equitable.
