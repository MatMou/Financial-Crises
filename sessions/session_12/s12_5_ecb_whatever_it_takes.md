---
title: "12.5 The ECB and 'Whatever It Takes'"
subtitle: How three words saved the euro
---

The European Central Bank's role in the crisis evolved dramatically between 2010 and 2012, from a reluctant and constrained institution to the decisive actor that ultimately resolved the self-fulfilling component of the crisis. The transformation culminated in ECB President Mario Draghi's famous speech on July 26, 2012, in which he declared that the ECB was "ready to do whatever it takes to preserve the euro" and added: "and believe me, it will be enough." The speech, and the Outright Monetary Transactions (OMT) program that followed, was the turning point of the European crisis.

## The ECB's initial reluctance

The ECB's initial response to the crisis was constrained by its institutional design, its legal mandate, and the preferences of its most influential member, the German Bundesbank. The ECB's mandate focused narrowly on price stability, and its founding treaty prohibited "monetary financing" of governments (the direct purchase of government bonds in the primary market). The German tradition of central bank independence and anti-inflationary credibility, which had shaped the ECB's institutional culture, made it reluctant to take actions that could be perceived as bailing out fiscally irresponsible governments.

The ECB did take some early actions. It reduced its main refinancing rate from 4.25% in October 2008 to 1.0% by May 2009. In May 2010, it established the **Securities Markets Programme** (SMP), under which it purchased government bonds of peripheral countries in the secondary market. The SMP was controversial within the ECB (the Bundesbank president, Axel Weber, publicly opposed it and later resigned), and its purchases were sterilized (the ECB withdrew an equivalent amount of liquidity through other operations), limiting its macroeconomic impact. The SMP was a temporary and reluctant measure that did not address the fundamental problem.

## The LTRO

In December 2011 and February 2012, the ECB conducted two three-year **Long-Term Refinancing Operations** (LTROs), lending approximately €1 trillion to euro-area banks at an interest rate of 1%. The LTROs addressed the acute funding pressures facing European banks (particularly in the periphery), which had been unable to access wholesale funding markets as the crisis deepened.

The LTROs also had an important indirect effect on sovereign bond markets. Banks that borrowed from the ECB at 1% could invest the funds in government bonds yielding 5-7% (in the case of Italian and Spanish bonds), earning a substantial carry. Many peripheral banks did exactly this, which increased the demand for peripheral government bonds, compressed spreads, and provided temporary relief to the sovereign debt crisis. But the LTRO carry trade also deepened the sovereign-bank nexus: banks that had loaded up on government bonds became even more exposed to sovereign risk, and the fate of the banks and the sovereign became even more tightly intertwined.

## "Whatever it takes" and the OMT

By the summer of 2012, the crisis had reached a critical point. Spanish and Italian government bond yields had risen to levels that threatened those countries' ability to refinance their debt. Spain's 10-year yield exceeded 7%, and Italy's exceeded 6%. A full-scale sovereign debt crisis in Spain or Italy, the third and fourth largest economies in the Eurozone, would have exceeded the capacity of the existing bailout mechanisms and would likely have destroyed the euro.

Draghi's "whatever it takes" speech on July 26, 2012, was delivered at the Global Investment Conference in London. The key passage was brief: "Within our mandate, the ECB is ready to do whatever it takes to preserve the euro. And believe me, it will be enough." The speech was not accompanied by any specific policy announcement, but the market reaction was immediate and dramatic. Spanish 10-year yields fell from approximately 7.5% to below 6% within weeks. Italian yields fell by a comparable amount.

The OMT program, announced on September 6, 2012, provided the institutional framework for Draghi's commitment. Under the OMT, the ECB would purchase unlimited quantities of short-term government bonds (with maturities of one to three years) in the secondary market, for countries that had agreed to an ESM adjustment program. The purchases would not be sterilized, and there would be no ex ante limit on their size.

The OMT has never been activated. No country has requested an ESM program and triggered OMT purchases. Yet the OMT is widely regarded as the single most effective crisis intervention during the entire European sovereign debt crisis. Its effectiveness lay in its credibility: by committing to purchase unlimited quantities of bonds, the ECB eliminated the self-fulfilling crisis equilibrium identified by {cite:t}`DeGrauwe2012`. If markets knew that the ECB would purchase bonds in unlimited quantities, there was no incentive to sell, because the ECB's purchases would support the price. The bad equilibrium, in which expectations of default led to high yields, which led to fiscal stress, which led to default, was eliminated, and only the good equilibrium remained.

The OMT resolved the self-fulfilling component of the crisis but not the fundamental component. Countries with unsustainable fiscal positions (Greece) or unresolved banking problems (Italy) continued to face challenges. But the elimination of the self-fulfilling dynamic reduced sovereign spreads across the periphery, eased funding conditions for banks, and created the space for gradual adjustment. The ECB had, belatedly, assumed the role of lender of last resort to sovereigns that it had previously refused.
