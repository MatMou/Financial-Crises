---
title: "12.4 Ireland, Portugal, Spain, and Cyprus: Variations on the Doom Loop"
subtitle: How banking crises became sovereign crises
---

While Greece's crisis was primarily fiscal in origin, the crises in Ireland, Spain, Portugal, and Cyprus were driven primarily by the banking sector. In each case, the sovereign-bank doom loop (subsection 3.4) was the central mechanism: banking losses threatened sovereign solvency, sovereign stress threatened banking solvency, and the feedback between the two deepened the crisis beyond what either alone would have produced.

## Ireland: the banking crisis that swallowed the sovereign

Ireland's crisis was the purest example of a banking crisis transforming into a sovereign crisis. Before the crisis, Ireland had been a fiscal model: the government ran budget surpluses and had a debt-to-GDP ratio of approximately 25%, well below the Maastricht limit. The crisis was caused entirely by the Irish banking system, which had fueled an enormous property bubble with funds borrowed from European wholesale markets.

When the Irish property bubble burst in 2008, the six main Irish banks faced losses that exceeded their capital many times over. On September 30, 2008, the Irish government issued a blanket guarantee of all bank liabilities, including deposits, bonds, and subordinated debt, with a total value of approximately €440 billion, more than twice Irish GDP. The guarantee was intended to prevent a bank run and was initially celebrated as a decisive intervention. But it proved catastrophic for the sovereign: by guaranteeing the banks' liabilities, the government had effectively transferred the banking losses to the public balance sheet.

The fiscal cost of the bank rescue was enormous. The recapitalization of the banks cost approximately €64 billion (approximately 40% of GDP), the largest bank rescue relative to GDP in history. The fiscal deficit ballooned to approximately 32% of GDP in 2010 (including the bank recapitalization costs), and the debt-to-GDP ratio soared from 25% to over 120%. Ireland was forced to enter a €85 billion bailout program with the Troika in November 2010.

Ireland's experience illustrates the doom loop in its most dramatic form. A banking crisis that would have been severe but manageable if Ireland had had its own central bank (which could have provided liquidity and financed the recapitalization through money creation) became a sovereign crisis because the Irish government had to borrow in a currency it could not create.

## Spain: the savings bank crisis

Spain's crisis shared many features with Ireland's but was larger in scale and more complex in institutional detail. Spain's property bubble was enormous: house prices roughly tripled between 1996 and 2007, construction accounted for approximately 13% of GDP, and the building boom produced approximately 800,000 new housing units per year at the peak.

The crisis was concentrated in the **cajas** (savings banks), which were regional financial institutions governed by boards that included local politicians, trade union representatives, and civic organizations. The cajas had expanded aggressively into real estate lending during the boom, often with inadequate risk management and sometimes with lending decisions influenced by political connections. When the property market collapsed, the cajas' non-performing loan ratios soared, and many became insolvent.

Spain resisted acknowledging the scale of the banking problem for several years, hoping that the economy would recover and that the losses would prove manageable. The delay, a form of regulatory forbearance similar to the S&L episode (subsection 6.6), allowed the problems to worsen. By 2012, the losses had grown too large to conceal, and Spain was forced to request a €100 billion credit line from the European Stability Mechanism (ESM) for bank recapitalization. The ESM funds were used to recapitalize the most troubled institutions, several of which were merged into a "bad bank" (SAREB) that absorbed their toxic real estate assets.

Unlike Ireland, Spain avoided a full sovereign bailout, partly because its economy was much larger (a Spanish bailout would have strained the resources of the ESM and the Eurozone) and partly because the ECB's OMT announcement in September 2012 (subsection 12.5) reduced the pressure on Spanish government bond spreads. But Spain endured a severe recession (GDP fell by approximately 9% from peak to trough) and prolonged unemployment (approximately 26% at the peak, with youth unemployment exceeding 55%).

## Portugal: the quiet crisis

Portugal's crisis was less dramatic than Greece's or Ireland's but equally debilitating. Portugal had been experiencing chronic low growth, declining competitiveness, and mounting public and private debt since before the euro's creation. The adoption of the euro had reduced interest rates, which fueled a modest credit boom, but the underlying structural problems, low productivity growth, a rigid labor market, and an oversized public sector, were not addressed.

Portugal entered a €78 billion bailout program with the Troika in May 2011, after its government bond spreads widened to levels that made market borrowing unsustainable. The program imposed austerity measures and structural reforms similar to those in Greece, and Portugal experienced a significant recession (GDP fell by approximately 7% from peak to trough) and high unemployment (approximately 17% at the peak). Portugal's adjustment was less dramatic than Greece's, partly because its initial imbalances were smaller and partly because it implemented the reform program more effectively. Portugal exited the bailout program in 2014 and returned to growth, though the recovery was slow and the social costs were substantial.

## Cyprus: a banking crisis at the intersection of geopolitics

Cyprus entered a €10 billion bailout program in March 2013 after its two largest banks, Bank of Cyprus and Laiki Bank, suffered devastating losses on their holdings of Greek government bonds (a consequence of the Greek PSI). The Cypriot banking sector was enormous relative to the economy (bank assets were approximately eight times GDP), and the losses were far too large for the Cypriot government to absorb.

The Cypriot bailout was notable for the introduction of **bail-in**: rather than using taxpayer funds to recapitalize the banks (as had been done in Ireland), uninsured depositors (those with deposits exceeding €100,000) were forced to absorb losses. Laiki Bank was closed, and its uninsured depositors lost nearly everything. At Bank of Cyprus, uninsured deposits were converted into equity, with depositors losing approximately 47.5% of their uninsured balances.

The bail-in was controversial, particularly because a large share of the uninsured deposits belonged to Russian nationals and companies. But it established an important precedent: that bank creditors, including uninsured depositors, could be required to share the losses of bank failures, rather than the losses being transferred entirely to the sovereign (as in Ireland) or to the ESM. The bail-in principle would later be formalized in the EU's Bank Recovery and Resolution Directive (BRRD), which established a hierarchy of loss absorption for failing banks.
