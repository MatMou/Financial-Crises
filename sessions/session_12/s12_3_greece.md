---
title: "12.3 Greece: Fiscal Crisis and the First Bailout"
subtitle: The crisis that nearly broke the euro
---

The European sovereign debt crisis began in Greece. In October 2009, the newly elected government of George Papandreou revealed that the previous government had been systematically misreporting Greece's fiscal statistics: the actual deficit for 2009 was approximately 12.7% of GDP, more than double the previously reported figure of 6%. The revelation shattered market confidence in Greek sovereign debt and initiated a crisis that would last for more than five years, produce the deepest peacetime recession in any advanced economy since the Great Depression, and bring the Eurozone to the brink of dissolution.

## The origins of the Greek crisis

Greece's crisis was, at its core, a fiscal crisis. Unlike Ireland and Spain, where the crisis originated in the banking sector, Greece's fundamental problem was that the government had been spending far more than it was collecting in revenue for decades, and that euro membership had allowed it to finance the deficits at artificially low interest rates.

Greek government spending was high by European standards, driven by a large public sector (public employees accounted for approximately 25% of total employment), generous pension commitments (retirement ages were among the lowest in Europe), and extensive subsidies and transfers. Revenue collection was weak, undermined by widespread tax evasion (particularly among the self-employed and professionals), an inefficient tax administration, and a culture of non-compliance that the government had been unable or unwilling to address.

The misreporting of fiscal statistics compounded the problem. Greece had used creative accounting (including off-balance-sheet transactions arranged with the help of Goldman Sachs) to meet the Maastricht criteria for euro membership in 2001, and it continued to underreport its deficits thereafter. The Eurostat statistical agency had repeatedly questioned Greece's fiscal data but lacked the authority to enforce accurate reporting. The revelation in October 2009 was thus not just a fiscal shock but a credibility shock: if Greece had been lying about its deficit, what else might it have been lying about?

## The spread crisis

The market reaction to the Greek fiscal revelation was swift and severe. Greek government bond spreads over German bunds, which had been approximately 30 basis points in early 2009, widened to approximately 300 basis points by January 2010, 600 basis points by April 2010, and eventually exceeded 3,000 basis points in 2012. Greece was effectively shut out of the bond market: no private investor would lend to the Greek government at any affordable rate.

The spread crisis was amplified by the self-fulfilling dynamic identified by {cite:t}`DeGrauwe2012`. Because Greece did not have its own central bank that could act as a lender of last resort (the ECB was prohibited from purchasing Greek bonds in the primary market and was reluctant to purchase them in the secondary market), there was no backstop to prevent a liquidity crisis from developing. If investors believed that Greece might default, they would refuse to roll over their holdings of Greek bonds, which would force Greece to default, validating the initial belief. The self-fulfilling dynamic meant that the crisis was more severe than Greece's underlying fiscal position warranted: a country with its own central bank and the same fiscal position would not have experienced the same degree of market panic.

## The first bailout: May 2010

After months of contentious negotiations, the Eurozone and the IMF agreed on a €110 billion bailout package for Greece in May 2010. The program was conditional on the implementation of severe austerity measures: deep spending cuts, tax increases, pension reform, labor market liberalization, and privatization of state-owned assets. The conditionality was imposed by the "Troika" of the European Commission, the ECB, and the IMF, which would monitor Greece's compliance with the program.

The austerity was devastating. Greek GDP fell by approximately 25% between 2009 and 2013, comparable to the decline experienced by the United States during the Great Depression. Unemployment rose to approximately 27%, with youth unemployment exceeding 60%. Wages fell by approximately 20%. The social consequences were severe: poverty increased dramatically, the healthcare system deteriorated, and emigration of young, skilled workers accelerated.

The austerity also failed to achieve its stated fiscal objective. The deficit was reduced, but the GDP decline was so severe that the debt-to-GDP ratio actually increased, from approximately 120% in 2009 to approximately 175% in 2013. The denominator was shrinking faster than the numerator, precisely the self-defeating dynamic that the Blanchard-Leigh analysis had identified (subsection 3.6): the fiscal multipliers were far larger than the Troika's models had assumed, which meant that the austerity produced a much deeper recession than expected, which undermined the fiscal consolidation.

## The second bailout and the PSI

By 2012, it was clear that Greece's debt was unsustainable and that the first bailout program was failing. A second bailout of €130 billion was agreed in February 2012, accompanied by the largest sovereign debt restructuring in history: the Private Sector Involvement (PSI), in which private holders of Greek government bonds accepted a face-value haircut of approximately 53.5% and an extension of maturities, reducing the nominal value of private-sector claims by approximately €107 billion.

The PSI was a milestone in the European crisis because it broke the taboo against sovereign debt restructuring within the Eurozone. The restructuring was contentious: it imposed losses on Greek banks (which held large portfolios of Greek government bonds and had to be recapitalized with public funds), on Greek pension funds, and on foreign investors (primarily European banks). The ECB's holdings of Greek bonds were exempted from the restructuring, establishing the controversial principle of preferred creditor status for the central bank.

The PSI reduced Greece's debt burden but did not resolve the crisis. Greece continued to struggle with implementation of the reform program, political instability, and a depressed economy. A third bailout of €86 billion was agreed in August 2015, following the dramatic confrontation between the Syriza government and the Eurozone creditors that brought Greece to the brink of euro exit (the "Grexit" crisis). Greece eventually returned to growth in 2017, but its GDP remained approximately 25% below its pre-crisis level, and the scars of the crisis, in terms of lost output, emigration, and social damage, were deep and lasting.
