---
title: "12.2 The Periphery Boom and the Buildup of Imbalances"
subtitle: Capital flows, interest rate convergence, and the illusion of prosperity
---

The creation of the euro in 1999 set in motion a massive reallocation of capital within the Eurozone that would create the imbalances at the heart of the crisis. Capital flowed from the core countries (Germany, the Netherlands, France, Austria) to the periphery (Greece, Ireland, Spain, Portugal, and later Cyprus), fueling credit booms, housing bubbles, and current account deficits that were unsustainable but appeared benign as long as the inflows continued.

## Interest rate convergence

The most dramatic consequence of the euro's creation was the convergence of government bond yields across the Eurozone. Before the euro, peripheral countries had paid significantly higher interest rates than Germany, reflecting the risk of devaluation and inflation. Greek 10-year government bonds had yielded approximately 10% more than German bunds in the early 1990s. With the elimination of exchange rate risk, the spreads narrowed dramatically: by the early 2000s, Greek government bonds yielded only 10-30 basis points more than German bunds, implying that the market treated Greek and German sovereign debt as near-perfect substitutes.

The convergence of yields was rational insofar as the exchange rate risk had been eliminated. But it was irrational insofar as it ignored the credit risk that remained. Greece, Ireland, Spain, and Portugal had very different fiscal positions, economic structures, and institutional capacities from Germany, and these differences should have been reflected in risk premiums. The near-complete elimination of spreads reflected the market's assumption that the Eurozone was effectively a transfer union: that the core countries would rescue the periphery if it got into trouble, or that the ECB would act as a lender of last resort to sovereigns. Neither assumption was correct, as the crisis would reveal.

## The capital flow boom

The convergence of interest rates fueled a massive flow of capital from the core to the periphery. German banks, insurance companies, and institutional investors, seeking higher yields than were available on German government bonds, lent enormous sums to banks, corporations, and governments in the peripheral countries. The gross capital flows were very large: Germany's current account surplus (which was mirrored by peripheral deficits) averaged approximately 6-7% of GDP during the 2000s.

The capital flows took different forms in different countries, which produced different types of crises.

In **Ireland and Spain**, the capital flowed primarily into the banking system, which channeled it into real estate lending. Ireland experienced a housing bubble of extraordinary proportions: house prices roughly tripled between 1996 and 2007, and construction accounted for approximately 13% of GDP at the peak (compared to a normal level of approximately 5-6%). Spain experienced a similar housing and construction boom: approximately 800,000 new housing units were being built per year at the peak, more than in France, Germany, and the UK combined. The Irish and Spanish banking systems grew to enormous size relative to their domestic economies, funded by wholesale borrowing from core European banks.

In **Greece**, the capital flowed primarily into government borrowing. Greece ran persistent fiscal deficits that averaged approximately 6-7% of GDP during the 2000s, financed by borrowing at the low interest rates that euro membership provided. The borrowing funded public sector wages, pensions, and consumption rather than productive investment, and Greece's competitiveness deteriorated as wages rose faster than productivity.

In **Portugal**, the pattern was mixed: both private and public debt accumulated, and the economy suffered from chronic low productivity growth and competitiveness problems that predated the euro.

## The current account dimension

The capital flows produced large and persistent current account deficits in the peripheral countries and corresponding surpluses in the core. By 2007, Greece was running a current account deficit of approximately 15% of GDP, Portugal approximately 10%, Spain approximately 10%, and Ireland approximately 5%. Germany was running a current account surplus of approximately 7% of GDP.

In a monetary union, current account imbalances do not automatically produce the adjustment pressures that they would produce under floating exchange rates (where the deficit country's currency would depreciate) or under the gold standard (where gold outflows would force monetary contraction). The capital account offset the current account through continued capital inflows, and the common monetary policy set by the ECB was calibrated for the Eurozone as a whole (where inflation was close to the 2% target) rather than for the booming peripheral economies (where inflation was well above target and real interest rates were often negative).

The result was that the peripheral economies were operating with monetary policy that was far too loose for their conditions, while the core (particularly Germany) was operating with monetary policy that was somewhat too tight. This asymmetry, the mirror image of the asymmetry that had destabilized the ERM in 1992 (subsection 7.1), fueled the boom in the periphery and suppressed demand in the core, widening the imbalances.

## The illusion of sustainability

The imbalances were widely visible but were not regarded as threatening. The dominant view, shared by the European Commission, the ECB, and most market participants, was that current account deficits within a monetary union were benign: they were simply the natural result of capital flowing from rich, slow-growing countries (the core) to poorer, faster-growing countries (the periphery), where the return on investment was higher. The analogy with capital flows between states within the United States was frequently invoked.

The analogy was misleading. The United States has a common fiscal mechanism, a common deposit insurance system, a common labor market, and a common language, all of which facilitate adjustment to asymmetric shocks. The Eurozone had none of these. When the capital flows reversed in 2010, the peripheral countries would face sudden stops (subsection 3.3) without the adjustment mechanisms that make sudden stops manageable.
