---
title: "12.1 The Architecture of the Eurozone and Its Structural Flaws"
subtitle: A monetary union without a fiscal union
---

The European Economic and Monetary Union (EMU), launched in 1999 with the introduction of the euro, was the most ambitious experiment in monetary integration since the gold standard. Eleven countries (later expanded to twenty) adopted a common currency, surrendering their national monetary policies to the European Central Bank. The design of the Eurozone reflected a political compromise between the ambition of European integration and the reluctance of member states to cede fiscal sovereignty. The resulting architecture contained structural flaws that would be brutally exposed by the crisis.

## Optimal currency area theory

The economic theory relevant to evaluating the Eurozone is the theory of **optimal currency areas** (OCA), developed by Robert Mundell in 1961. Mundell's theory identifies the conditions under which a group of countries would benefit from sharing a common currency: high labor mobility between the member countries (so that workers can move from depressed to booming regions), flexible wages and prices (so that relative costs can adjust without exchange rate changes), a common fiscal mechanism to transfer resources from prosperous to depressed regions, and symmetric business cycles (so that a single monetary policy is appropriate for all members).

The Eurozone satisfied few of these conditions. Labor mobility in Europe was low relative to the United States, constrained by language barriers, cultural differences, and segmented labor markets. Wages and prices, particularly in the peripheral countries, were relatively rigid, especially downward. There was no significant fiscal transfer mechanism: the EU budget was approximately 1% of GDP, far too small to provide meaningful stabilization. And business cycles, while somewhat synchronized during the boom years, diverged sharply when the crisis hit, with the peripheral countries experiencing deep recessions while Germany recovered relatively quickly.

The architects of the Eurozone were aware of these limitations but believed that the creation of the monetary union would itself promote the convergence of economic structures and the development of the institutional mechanisms needed to make it work. This was the "coronation theory" of monetary integration: the common currency would catalyze the political integration needed to support it. The crisis demonstrated that this theory was wrong, or at least premature.

## The Maastricht framework

The Eurozone's fiscal framework was established by the Maastricht Treaty (1992) and the Stability and Growth Pact (1997). The framework set limits on government deficits (3% of GDP) and government debt (60% of GDP), with the intention of preventing fiscal irresponsibility that could threaten the stability of the common currency. The framework reflected the dominant concern of Germany and other Northern European countries that the monetary union might lead to fiscal profligacy by peripheral countries, which would ultimately require bailouts or inflation.

The Maastricht framework had several critical weaknesses. It focused exclusively on fiscal indicators (deficits and debt) and ignored private-sector imbalances (private debt, current account deficits, banking sector risks) that would prove far more important in the crisis. Ireland and Spain, which would suffer the most severe banking crises, were actually running fiscal surpluses before the crisis and had debt ratios well below 60% of GDP. The Maastricht criteria would have given them a clean bill of health, because the framework was blind to the private-sector credit booms that were creating the real vulnerabilities.

The framework also lacked effective enforcement. When France and Germany themselves breached the deficit limits in 2003, the Stability and Growth Pact was effectively suspended, undermining the credibility of the fiscal rules. And Greece, as would later be revealed, had been misreporting its fiscal statistics to qualify for euro membership and to maintain the appearance of compliance.

## The missing elements

The Eurozone's architecture was incomplete in several critical respects that the crisis would expose.

**No lender of last resort for sovereigns.** The ECB was prohibited by its founding treaty from purchasing government bonds in the primary market and was reluctant to purchase them in the secondary market. This meant that Eurozone governments, unlike governments with their own central banks, could face liquidity crises: they could be unable to roll over their debt if markets refused to lend, even if their fiscal positions were fundamentally sustainable. This vulnerability, analyzed by {cite:t}`DeGrauwe2012`, created the possibility of self-fulfilling sovereign debt crises that could not occur in countries with their own central banks.

**No common deposit insurance.** Bank deposits were insured at the national level, which meant that the credibility of deposit insurance depended on the fiscal capacity of the national government. When sovereign creditworthiness was in doubt, deposit insurance was also in doubt, which created an incentive for depositors to move their savings from peripheral banks to core-country banks, accelerating the doom loop.

**No fiscal transfer mechanism.** Unlike the United States, where federal transfers automatically flow from prosperous states to depressed states (through progressive taxation, unemployment insurance, and social spending), the Eurozone had no mechanism for fiscal risk-sharing. Each member state bore the full cost of its own economic downturns, which made the adjustment to asymmetric shocks far more painful than it would have been with a common fiscal backstop.

**No mechanism for orderly sovereign debt restructuring.** When Greece's debt proved unsustainable, there was no established framework for restructuring it. The restructuring that eventually occurred (the 2012 PSI, discussed in subsection 12.3) was improvised under extreme time pressure and was delayed for years by the political difficulty of acknowledging that a Eurozone sovereign's debt was not fully payable.

## The parallel with the gold standard

The structural position of the peripheral Eurozone countries bore a striking resemblance to the position of peripheral countries under the gold standard (subsections 4.5 and 5.3). Like gold-standard countries, Eurozone members had surrendered their ability to adjust the exchange rate, which meant that correction of external imbalances required "internal devaluation": a reduction in domestic wages and prices relative to trading partners. Like gold-standard countries, they had no national lender of last resort that could create unlimited liquidity in the currency of their obligations. And like gold-standard countries, they were subject to sudden stops when capital flows reversed.

Eichengreen's "golden fetters" metaphor (subsection 5.3) applies directly to the Eurozone periphery during the crisis. The common currency was a straitjacket that prevented the peripheral countries from devaluing to restore competitiveness, from inflating to reduce the real burden of debt, or from having their central banks act as lenders of last resort to their governments and banking systems. The adjustment mechanism that remained, internal devaluation through wage and price cuts, was as painful and politically destabilizing as the deflation that the gold standard had imposed in the 1930s.
