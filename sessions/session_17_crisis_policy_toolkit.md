The preceding sessions have examined crises and the specific policy responses they provoked. This session steps back to consider the policy toolkit as a whole: the set of instruments and institutional arrangements that are available for preventing crises and for managing them when prevention fails. The session draws on the entire arc of the course, from the evolution of the lender of last resort in the 19th century (subsection 4.3) through the post-GFC regulatory reforms (subsection 11.4) and the COVID-era innovations (subsection 13.4), to present a unified analysis of what policymakers can do, what they cannot do, and why the gap between the two is so persistent.

The session is organized around the two phases of crisis policy: prevention (reducing the probability that a crisis occurs) and management (reducing the damage when it does). Prevention relies primarily on macroprudential regulation and the management of capital flows. Management relies on the lender of last resort, deposit insurance, bank resolution, and fiscal support. International coordination is essential for both.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Explain the principles of macroprudential policy, distinguish macroprudential from microprudential regulation, and describe the main macroprudential instruments.
2. State Bagehot's principles for the lender of last resort and explain how they have been extended and modified in the modern context.
3. Analyze the design of deposit insurance and bank resolution frameworks, including the trade-off between financial stability and moral hazard.
4. Evaluate the case for and against capital controls as a tool for managing financial stability risks.
5. Assess the role of the IMF in international crisis management and the challenges of coordinating crisis responses across sovereigns.
6. Explain why crisis prevention is systematically underprovided and identify the political economy constraints that limit the effectiveness of the policy toolkit.

## Session Outline

**17.1 Macroprudential Policy.** The distinction between microprudential and macroprudential regulation, the countercyclical capital buffer, loan-to-value limits, sectoral capital requirements, and the institutional framework for macroprudential policy.

**17.2 The Lender of Last Resort.** Bagehot's principles, the extension of LOLR to non-bank institutions and to markets, the distinction between liquidity and solvency, and the moral hazard trade-off.

**17.3 Deposit Insurance and Bank Resolution.** The design of deposit insurance, the too-big-to-fail problem, bail-in versus bailout, and the development of resolution frameworks.

**17.4 Capital Controls and the Management of Capital Flows.** The case for capital flow management measures, the IMF's Institutional View, and the experience of countries that have used capital controls.

**17.5 International Crisis Management and the IMF.** The IMF's lending toolkit, conditionality, the global financial safety net, and the challenges of coordinating crisis responses in a fragmented international system.

**17.6 The Political Economy of Crisis Prevention.** Why prevention is systematically underprovided, the Minsky cycle of regulation, and the institutional design problem.
