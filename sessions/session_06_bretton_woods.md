The institutional architecture born from the Great Depression (subsection 5.6) produced the most sustained period of economic growth and financial stability in modern history. Between the end of World War II and the early 1970s, the advanced economies grew rapidly, unemployment was low, inflation was contained, international trade expanded dramatically, and systemic financial crises were virtually absent. This period, sometimes called the "Golden Age" of capitalism, coincided with the operation of the Bretton Woods international monetary system, which combined fixed but adjustable exchange rates, capital controls, and international institutions designed to manage the system cooperatively.

The Golden Age did not last. The Bretton Woods system contained an internal contradiction, identified by Robert Triffin as early as 1960, that would eventually destroy it. The system collapsed in 1971 when the United States suspended gold convertibility, and the world moved to floating exchange rates. The transition was accompanied by unprecedented macroeconomic turbulence: the oil shocks of the 1970s, stagflation, the Volcker disinflation, and two major financial crises, the Latin American debt crisis and the U.S. savings and loan crisis, that foreshadowed the more severe crises of the following decades.

This session traces the arc from the creation of Bretton Woods through its collapse and the transition to the modern financial landscape. It provides the essential context for understanding the emerging market crises of the 1990s (Session 7), the deregulation that set the stage for the global financial crisis (Sessions 9-11), and the ongoing debates over international monetary reform.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Explain the design of the Bretton Woods system, including the roles of the IMF and the World Bank, the adjustable peg mechanism, and the capital control framework, and relate these design choices to the lessons of the interwar period.
2. Describe the macroeconomic performance of the Bretton Woods era and explain why the system was associated with financial stability and rapid growth.
3. State the Triffin Dilemma and explain how the contradiction between the dollar's role as a reserve currency and the requirement for gold convertibility led to the system's collapse.
4. Analyze the consequences of the transition to floating rates, including the petrodollar recycling mechanism that channeled oil surpluses to developing countries.
5. Explain the causes, dynamics, and resolution of the Latin American debt crisis of the 1980s, including the role of the Volcker shock, the Baker and Brady Plans, and the implications for sovereign debt restructuring.
6. Analyze the U.S. savings and loan crisis as a case study in the consequences of financial deregulation combined with deposit insurance moral hazard.

## Session Outline

This session is divided into the following subsections:

**6.1 The Design and Logic of Bretton Woods.** The Keynes and White plans, the compromise that emerged, and how the system addressed the failures of the interwar gold standard. The roles of the IMF and the World Bank.

**6.2 The Golden Age: Stability and Growth, 1945-1965.** The macroeconomic performance of the Bretton Woods era, the role of capital controls in insulating domestic financial systems, and why the period was characterized by an almost complete absence of systemic banking crises.

**6.3 The Triffin Dilemma and the Collapse of Bretton Woods.** The internal contradiction of the dollar-gold system, the erosion of confidence in the dollar, and the sequence of events from the London Gold Pool to the Nixon shock of August 1971.

**6.4 The Transition to Floating Rates and Petrodollar Recycling.** The move to floating exchange rates, the oil shocks of 1973 and 1979, and the recycling of oil surpluses through the international banking system to developing countries, which set the stage for the debt crisis of the 1980s.

**6.5 The Latin American Debt Crisis.** The buildup of sovereign debt in Latin America during the 1970s, the Volcker shock as the trigger, Mexico's near-default in 1982, the "lost decade" of the 1980s, and the resolution through the Baker and Brady Plans.

**6.6 The U.S. Savings and Loan Crisis.** The deregulation of the S&L industry, the perverse interaction between deposit insurance and deregulation, the wave of S&L failures, and the taxpayer-funded resolution. A case study in how the erosion of New Deal safeguards produced financial instability.
