The Federal Reserve cut the federal funds rate to effectively zero (a target range of 0-0.25%) in December 2008. With the conventional policy instrument exhausted, the Fed and other central banks were forced to develop unconventional tools to provide further stimulus. The unconventional monetary policy response to the GFC was the most extensive experiment in central banking history, and it drew directly on the analysis of the zero lower bound developed in subsection 3.5.

## The Fed's emergency lending facilities

Before turning to quantitative easing, the Fed deployed an extraordinary array of emergency lending facilities designed to address the specific funding market failures described in subsection 10.4. These included the **Term Auction Facility** (TAF), which auctioned term loans to depository institutions; the **Commercial Paper Funding Facility** (CPFF), which purchased commercial paper directly from issuers to restart the frozen commercial paper market; the **Term Asset-Backed Securities Loan Facility** (TALF), which provided financing to investors purchasing newly issued asset-backed securities; the **Money Market Investor Funding Facility** (MMIFF), designed to support money market funds; and the **Primary Dealer Credit Facility** (PDCF) and **Term Securities Lending Facility** (TSLF) that had been established during the Bear Stearns crisis.

These facilities were targeted interventions designed to address specific market failures. The CPFF, for example, addressed the freeze in the commercial paper market that was threatening the ability of non-financial corporations to finance their operations. The TALF addressed the freeze in the securitization market that was reducing the availability of auto loans, student loans, and credit card lending. The facilities were creative applications of the central bank's lender-of-last-resort function, extending it to markets and institutions that had never previously received central bank support.

## Quantitative easing

The Fed's most significant unconventional policy was **quantitative easing** (QE): the large-scale purchase of long-term securities to reduce long-term interest rates and provide stimulus to the economy. The Fed conducted three rounds of QE between 2008 and 2014.

**QE1** (November 2008 to March 2010) focused on mortgage-backed securities and agency debt. The Fed purchased approximately $1.25 trillion in agency MBS, $300 billion in Treasury bonds, and $175 billion in agency debt. The primary objective was to support the housing market by reducing mortgage rates and to signal the Fed's commitment to providing stimulus.

**QE2** (November 2010 to June 2011) involved the purchase of approximately $600 billion in longer-term Treasury securities. QE2 was motivated by concern that the recovery was stalling and that the economy was at risk of deflation.

**QE3** (September 2012 to October 2014) was an open-ended program in which the Fed purchased $40 billion per month in agency MBS (later increased to $85 billion per month including Treasuries). QE3 was notable for its open-ended structure: rather than announcing a fixed amount of purchases, the Fed committed to continue purchasing "until the outlook for the labor market has improved substantially."

The three rounds of QE expanded the Fed's balance sheet from approximately $900 billion before the crisis to approximately $4.5 trillion by 2014. The mechanism through which QE was expected to work was the **portfolio balance channel**: by purchasing long-term securities, the Fed reduced the supply of those securities available to private investors, which pushed down their yields and encouraged investors to rebalance their portfolios toward riskier assets (corporate bonds, equities, real estate), thereby easing financial conditions more broadly.

## Forward guidance

The Fed complemented QE with increasingly explicit **forward guidance** about the future path of interest rates. The evolution of forward guidance during the crisis and its aftermath illustrates the central bank's learning process.

Initially, the Fed used qualitative guidance: the December 2008 statement said that rates were expected to remain low "for some time," which was strengthened to "for an extended period" in March 2009. In August 2011, the Fed moved to calendar-based guidance, specifying that rates were expected to remain near zero "at least through mid-2013" (later extended to "at least through late 2014" and then "at least through mid-2015"). In December 2012, the Fed adopted state-contingent guidance, specifying that rates would remain near zero "at least as long as the unemployment rate remains above 6.5 percent, inflation between one and two years ahead is projected to be no more than a half percentage point above the Committee's 2 percent longer-run goal, and longer-term inflation expectations continue to be well anchored."

The progression from qualitative to calendar-based to state-contingent guidance reflected the Fed's growing understanding of how to communicate its intentions effectively. State-contingent guidance was the most sophisticated form: it tied the Fed's actions to observable economic conditions, which allowed the market to update its expectations automatically as new data arrived.

## Other central banks

The Fed was not alone in deploying unconventional monetary policy. The Bank of England purchased approximately £375 billion in gilts through its Asset Purchase Facility. The Bank of Japan expanded its quantitative easing program dramatically under Governor Haruhiko Kuroda beginning in 2013, and introduced yield curve control in 2016 (subsection 3.5). The European Central Bank, constrained by legal restrictions on purchasing government bonds and by the opposition of Germany and other Northern European countries, was slower to adopt QE but eventually launched its own program in January 2015, purchasing approximately €60 billion per month in government and private-sector bonds.

The ECB also introduced the **Long-Term Refinancing Operations** (LTRO) in December 2011 and February 2012, providing approximately €1 trillion in three-year loans to euro-area banks at an interest rate of 1%. The LTRO was designed to address the banking crisis in the euro area (discussed in Session 12) by ensuring that banks had access to funding, but it also had the effect of encouraging banks to purchase government bonds (which offered higher yields than the LTRO rate), thereby supporting government bond markets.

## Assessment

The effectiveness of unconventional monetary policy remains debated. Event studies suggest that QE reduced long-term yields by approximately 50-100 basis points, equivalent to a conventional rate cut of 100-300 basis points. The emergency lending facilities succeeded in unfreezing specific markets. And forward guidance helped to anchor expectations about the future path of rates.

The critics argue that the effects were modest relative to the scale of the intervention, that the benefits accrued disproportionately to holders of financial assets (increasing wealth inequality), that prolonged low interest rates encouraged reach-for-yield behavior that created new financial stability risks, and that the eventual normalization of monetary policy would be difficult and disruptive. These critiques have merit, but the counterfactual, what would have happened without unconventional monetary policy, is clearly worse: a deflationary spiral, a deeper recession, and a longer recovery.
