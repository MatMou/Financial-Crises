---
title: "11.3 Fiscal Stimulus: The Global Keynesian Moment"
subtitle: ARRA, the G-20 response, and the pivot to austerity
---

The global financial crisis produced a brief but remarkable revival of Keynesian fiscal policy. For the first time since the 1970s, governments around the world implemented large-scale fiscal stimulus programs to offset the collapse of private demand. The fiscal response was coordinated through the G-20, was substantial in scale, and was broadly effective in preventing the recession from deepening into a depression. But the fiscal stimulus was also politically fragile, and the pivot to austerity that began in 2010 may have slowed the recovery and contributed to the prolonged period of weak growth that followed.

## The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act

The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (ARRA) was signed into law by President Obama on February 17, 2009. The Act provided approximately $787 billion (later revised to $831 billion) in fiscal stimulus over ten years, front-loaded into 2009-2011. The stimulus was divided roughly equally among three components: tax cuts and credits (approximately $288 billion), direct government spending on infrastructure, education, and energy (approximately $275 billion), and transfers to state and local governments and increased social spending (approximately $268 billion).

ARRA was the largest fiscal stimulus in American history, but it was widely regarded as too small relative to the output gap. The output gap, the difference between actual and potential GDP, was estimated at approximately $2 trillion in 2009. ARRA's spending of approximately $400 billion in the first year (the peak year) filled only a fraction of this gap. Christina Romer, the chair of Obama's Council of Economic Advisers, had reportedly advocated for a stimulus of $1.2 trillion, but the proposal was scaled back for political reasons (Congress would not have approved a larger package) and because of concerns about the debt.

The debate over the effectiveness of ARRA mirrors the broader debate over fiscal multipliers discussed in subsection 3.6. Estimates of the multiplier effect of ARRA range from approximately 0.5 (critics who argue that government spending crowded out private activity) to approximately 2.5 (proponents who argue that the multiplier was large because the economy was at the zero lower bound). The Congressional Budget Office estimated that ARRA increased GDP by 1.0-4.6% and increased employment by 1.0-4.7 million jobs at its peak effect. Most mainstream estimates suggest that ARRA was effective in reducing the severity of the recession but insufficient in scale to produce a rapid recovery.

## The G-20 coordinated stimulus

The G-20 summit in London in April 2009 produced a coordinated commitment to fiscal stimulus that was unprecedented in scale and scope. The summit communiqué committed the G-20 members to fiscal expansion totaling approximately $5 trillion by the end of 2010, along with $1.1 trillion in additional resources for the IMF, the World Bank, and trade finance.

China's stimulus was the most dramatic: a ¥4 trillion ($586 billion) package announced in November 2008 that was focused on infrastructure investment (railways, roads, airports), housing, and reconstruction following the Sichuan earthquake. The Chinese stimulus was extraordinarily large relative to GDP (approximately 13%) and was implemented rapidly through the state-controlled banking system, which could direct lending without the legislative delays that constrained Western democracies. The Chinese stimulus was effective in sustaining China's growth (GDP growth slowed to approximately 6% in 2009, compared to outright contraction in most other major economies) but also contributed to the buildup of debt and overcapacity that would become sources of concern in the 2010s.

Germany, despite being one of the countries least affected by the direct financial effects of the crisis, implemented a stimulus of approximately €80 billion, including a "cash for clunkers" program that supported the auto industry and the Kurzarbeit (short-time work) program that subsidized reduced working hours to prevent layoffs. Japan implemented multiple stimulus packages totaling approximately ¥25 trillion.

## The pivot to austerity

The global Keynesian consensus that prevailed in 2008-2009 proved remarkably short-lived. By 2010, the policy debate had shifted from stimulus to austerity, driven by several factors.

Rising government debt ratios, which had increased by 20-30 percentage points of GDP in many countries as a result of the recession and the crisis response, raised concerns about fiscal sustainability. The publication of {cite:t}`ReinhartRogoff2010`, which argued that government debt above 90% of GDP was associated with significantly lower growth, provided an influential (though later discredited due to a coding error) academic justification for fiscal consolidation.

The European sovereign debt crisis, which erupted in 2010 (Session 12), demonstrated that sovereign debt could become unsustainable and that fiscal profligacy could trigger market panic. The Greek crisis, in particular, created a powerful political narrative that linked government spending to sovereign default.

The G-20 Toronto summit in June 2010 marked the official pivot: the communiqué committed the advanced economies to halving their fiscal deficits by 2013 and stabilizing their debt-to-GDP ratios by 2016. The pivot to austerity was premature by the standards of the balance sheet recession framework (subsection 3.2): the private sector was still deleveraging, the economy was still well below potential, and the zero lower bound meant that monetary policy could not fully offset the contractionary effect of fiscal tightening. The experience would confirm the Blanchard-Leigh finding that fiscal multipliers during recessions at the zero lower bound are substantially larger than the models assumed (subsection 3.6).
