---
title: "11.6 Did We Prevent a Second Great Depression?"
subtitle: Comparing 2008 to 1929 and assessing the policy response
---

"We prevented a second Great Depression" became the defining claim of the policymakers who managed the crisis response. The claim is important because it provides both a benchmark for evaluating the policy response and a framework for understanding the relationship between the 2007-2009 crisis and the historical episodes studied in earlier sessions. This subsection assesses the claim by comparing the two crises and evaluating what the policy response accomplished.

## The comparison: 2008 versus 1929

The parallels between the two crises are striking. Both were preceded by prolonged credit booms and asset price bubbles. Both involved the collapse of a shadow banking system that was performing maturity transformation without adequate safeguards. Both produced banking crises that destroyed the credit intermediation function of the financial system. Both spread globally through financial linkages and trade channels. And both generated deflationary pressure and a sharp contraction in economic activity.

The initial trajectories were remarkably similar. Research by Barry Eichengreen and Kevin O'Rourke showed that in the first twelve months of the two crises, the decline in global industrial production, the decline in world trade, and the decline in stock markets were of comparable magnitude. Through the first year, the world was on a trajectory that looked very much like the beginning of another Great Depression.

The trajectories diverged sharply beginning in the second year, and the divergence is attributable to the difference in policy responses. During the Great Depression, the Federal Reserve allowed the money supply to contract by one-third, allowed approximately 9,000 banks to fail, raised interest rates to defend the gold standard, and the Hoover administration raised taxes to balance the budget (subsection 5.5). During the GFC, the Federal Reserve expanded the money supply aggressively through quantitative easing, recapitalized the banking system through TARP, cut interest rates to zero and deployed unconventional tools, and the government implemented a large fiscal stimulus.

The results speak for themselves. U.S. GDP fell by approximately 4.3% during the GFC, compared to approximately 30% during the Great Depression. Unemployment peaked at 10% during the GFC, compared to 25% during the Depression. The banking system was stabilized within months during the GFC, compared to the three-year cascade of bank failures during the Depression. And the recovery, while slow, began within approximately 18 months, compared to the decade-long depression of the 1930s.

## What the policy response accomplished

The policy response succeeded on several critical dimensions.

**It stopped the panic.** The combination of TARP, FDIC guarantees, Fed lending facilities, and central bank swap lines halted the run on the shadow banking system and restored basic functioning to the money markets within weeks of the most acute phase of the crisis. This was the single most important accomplishment: the run, if it had continued unchecked, would have produced a complete collapse of the financial system comparable to 1933.

**It prevented a deflationary spiral.** The aggressive monetary expansion prevented the deflation that had devastated the economy in the 1930s. Inflation in the United States remained positive throughout the crisis, and inflation expectations remained anchored near the Fed's 2% target, which prevented the Fisher debt-deflation mechanism (subsection 2.1) from taking hold.

**It preserved the banking system.** The bank recapitalization program, combined with the stress tests and the FDIC guarantee programs, restored confidence in the banking system and prevented the cascade of bank failures that had destroyed the credit intermediation function in the 1930s. The preservation of the banking system meant that the Bernanke credit channel mechanism (subsection 5.4), which had amplified the Depression by an order of magnitude, was short-circuited.

**It provided a fiscal bridge.** The fiscal stimulus, while arguably too small, offset a significant portion of the decline in private demand and prevented the recession from deepening further. The automatic stabilizers (progressive income tax, unemployment insurance) also played a major role, cushioning the income decline for the most affected households.

## What the policy response failed to accomplish

The response also had significant shortcomings.

**The recovery was too slow.** The balance sheet recession dynamics and the premature pivot to austerity produced a recovery that was prolonged and painful, with unemployment remaining above 7% for four years. The slow recovery inflicted lasting damage on the long-term unemployed, on communities dependent on construction and manufacturing, and on young workers entering the labor market during the downturn.

**The distributional consequences were uneven.** The policy response protected the financial system but imposed heavy costs on households. Homeowners who were underwater on their mortgages received relatively little direct assistance (the Home Affordable Modification Program, HAMP, helped far fewer homeowners than intended), while the banks that had originated the toxic mortgages were recapitalized with public funds. The perception that the response had bailed out Wall Street at the expense of Main Street generated a political backlash that shaped the subsequent decade of politics.

**The structural problems were not fully addressed.** The too-big-to-fail problem was mitigated but not eliminated. The shadow banking system was partially regulated but continued to evolve outside the regulatory perimeter. The fundamental tension between financial innovation and financial stability remained unresolved.

## Lessons for future crises

The GFC and its aftermath confirmed several lessons from the Great Depression while adding new ones.

The lesson of Friedman-Schwartz, that the central bank must prevent the money supply from contracting, was applied successfully. The lesson of Keynes, that fiscal policy must fill the demand gap when monetary policy is insufficient, was applied partially and then abandoned prematurely. The lesson of Bernanke, that the banking system must be preserved to maintain credit intermediation, was applied effectively.

The new lessons include the importance of extending the lender of last resort to the shadow banking system, the effectiveness of bank stress tests in restoring confidence, the value of international coordination in a globally integrated financial system, and the danger of pivoting to austerity before the private sector has completed its deleveraging.

The ultimate lesson may be the most sobering: even with the benefit of studying the Great Depression for eight decades, even with a Fed chairman who was the world's leading academic expert on the Depression, even with a coordinated global response of unprecedented scale, the crisis still produced the worst recession since the 1930s, a painfully slow recovery, and lasting damage to millions of lives. Financial crises are inherently difficult to manage, and the best policy response is to prevent them from occurring in the first place, a lesson that history suggests is the hardest to learn and the easiest to forget.
