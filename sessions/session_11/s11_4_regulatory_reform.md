---
title: "11.4 Regulatory Reform: Dodd-Frank and Basel III"
subtitle: Rebuilding the regulatory architecture
---

The crisis exposed catastrophic failures in the regulatory framework that was supposed to prevent the accumulation of systemic risk (subsection 9.6). The regulatory response, centered on the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act in the United States and the Basel III international capital standards, represented the most comprehensive overhaul of financial regulation since the New Deal. Whether these reforms were sufficient to prevent a future crisis remains an open question.

## The Dodd-Frank Act

The Dodd-Frank Act, signed into law on July 21, 2010, was an enormous piece of legislation (approximately 2,300 pages) that addressed virtually every aspect of financial regulation. Its major provisions can be grouped into several categories.

**Systemic risk oversight.** The Act created the Financial Stability Oversight Council (FSOC), chaired by the Treasury Secretary, to identify and monitor systemic risks to the financial system. The FSOC was given the authority to designate non-bank financial institutions as "systemically important financial institutions" (SIFIs), subjecting them to enhanced prudential standards and Federal Reserve supervision. The designation authority was intended to address the regulatory gap that had allowed institutions like AIG and the major investment banks to accumulate systemic risk outside the traditional banking regulatory perimeter.

**Resolution authority.** Title II of the Act created an Orderly Liquidation Authority (OLA) that allowed the FDIC to resolve failing systemically important financial institutions in a manner that imposed losses on shareholders and creditors rather than on taxpayers. The OLA was designed to address the too-big-to-fail problem: by providing a credible mechanism for resolving failing institutions without a taxpayer bailout, it was intended to reduce the moral hazard that the implicit government guarantee had created. Whether the OLA would actually be used in a crisis, rather than a bailout, remains untested.

**The Volcker Rule.** Named after former Fed Chairman Paul Volcker, the Volcker Rule (Section 619) prohibited banks and their affiliates from engaging in proprietary trading (trading for their own account rather than on behalf of clients) and from investing in or sponsoring hedge funds and private equity funds. The Volcker Rule was a partial restoration of the Glass-Steagall separation that had been repealed in 1999 (subsection 5.6), reflecting the view that proprietary trading by banks had contributed to excessive risk-taking and to the losses that had necessitated the bailouts.

**Derivatives regulation.** Title VII required standardized over-the-counter derivatives (including most CDS) to be cleared through central counterparties (CCPs) and traded on regulated exchanges or swap execution facilities. The central clearing requirement addressed the counterparty risk and opacity that had been critical amplifiers of the crisis (subsection 9.4). By interposing a CCP between the buyer and seller of a derivative, the regulation reduced bilateral counterparty exposure and increased transparency.

**Consumer protection.** The Act created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), an independent agency with authority to regulate consumer financial products (mortgages, credit cards, student loans). The CFPB was designed to address the predatory lending practices that had contributed to the subprime crisis, consolidating consumer protection authority that had previously been scattered among multiple regulators.

**Stress testing.** The Act required large financial institutions to undergo annual stress tests conducted by the Federal Reserve (the Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review, CCAR), in which the institutions' capital positions were evaluated under hypothetical adverse economic scenarios. Institutions that failed the stress tests were required to raise additional capital or reduce dividends and share buybacks.

## Basel III

The international regulatory response was centered on Basel III, a set of capital and liquidity standards developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision and agreed upon in 2010-2011.

Basel III addressed several of the weaknesses revealed by the crisis. **Higher capital requirements** increased the minimum common equity Tier 1 capital ratio from 2% to 4.5%, with an additional capital conservation buffer of 2.5% and a countercyclical buffer of up to 2.5%, bringing the effective minimum to as high as 9.5%. The higher capital requirements meant that banks had larger loss-absorbing cushions before the losses reached depositors or required government intervention.

**Leverage ratio.** Basel III introduced a non-risk-weighted leverage ratio of 3%, designed to serve as a backstop to the risk-weighted capital requirements. The leverage ratio addressed the problem that the risk-weighted requirements could be manipulated through internal models that systematically underestimated risk (a critical failure of Basel II).

**Liquidity requirements.** Basel III introduced two new liquidity standards: the Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR), which required banks to hold enough high-quality liquid assets to survive a 30-day stress scenario, and the Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR), which required banks to maintain a stable funding structure relative to their assets. The liquidity requirements addressed the maturity mismatch vulnerability that had been at the heart of the shadow banking run.

## Assessment

The post-crisis regulatory reforms were substantial and addressed many of the specific failures that had contributed to the crisis. Banks hold significantly more capital and liquidity than before the crisis. The derivatives market is more transparent and centrally cleared. Systemic risk oversight is more comprehensive. And the resolution authority provides, in principle, a mechanism for resolving failing institutions without taxpayer bailouts.

However, the reforms have limitations. The regulatory perimeter has been expanded but not closed: new forms of financial intermediation (fintech, crypto) have grown rapidly outside the regulatory framework. The too-big-to-fail problem has been mitigated but not eliminated: the largest banks are even larger than before the crisis, and the credibility of the resolution authority has not been tested. The international coordination of regulation remains incomplete, creating opportunities for regulatory arbitrage. And the political durability of the reforms is uncertain: parts of Dodd-Frank have been weakened or rolled back, and the cycle of regulation, stability, deregulation, and crisis described in subsection 5.6 may well be repeating itself.
