title: "11.1 Emergency Interventions: TARP, Bank Recapitalization, and Guarantees"
subtitle: Stopping the panic
---

The immediate priority in the fall of 2008 was to stop the panic described in Session 10. The emergency interventions of September 2008 through early 2009 constituted the largest government intervention in financial markets in history, involving the commitment of trillions of dollars of public funds across multiple programs. The interventions were improvised under extreme time pressure, were politically controversial, and were imperfect in design, but they succeeded in their primary objective: preventing the collapse of the financial system.

## TARP: the Troubled Asset Relief Program

On September 19, 2008, four days after Lehman's bankruptcy, Treasury Secretary Paulson proposed the Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP), a plan to use $700 billion of public funds to purchase "troubled assets" (primarily mortgage-backed securities) from banks, thereby removing the toxic assets from bank balance sheets and restoring confidence in the banking system.

The original TARP proposal was three pages long and granted the Treasury virtually unlimited discretion in how the funds would be used. Congress initially rejected the proposal on September 29, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 778 points (approximately 7%) in response, the largest single-day point decline in the index's history at that time. A revised version, the Emergency Economic Stabilization Act (EESA), was signed into law on October 3, 2008.

The Treasury quickly abandoned the original plan to purchase toxic assets, concluding that the process of identifying, valuing, and purchasing individual mortgage-backed securities would take too long to address the immediate crisis. Instead, Paulson redirected TARP toward **bank recapitalization**: using the funds to purchase preferred stock in banks, thereby injecting capital directly into the banking system.

On October 13, 2008, Paulson convened the CEOs of the nine largest U.S. banks and informed them that the Treasury would invest $125 billion in their institutions, with another $125 billion available for smaller banks. The nine banks received investments ranging from $25 billion (Citigroup, JPMorgan Chase, Wells Fargo, Bank of America) to $10 billion (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Merrill Lynch, Bank of New York Mellon, State Street). The investments were structured as preferred stock paying a 5% dividend for the first five years and 9% thereafter, with warrants that gave the government an equity upside if the banks recovered.

The recapitalization was controversial but effective. The simultaneous injection into all nine banks was designed to prevent any single bank from being stigmatized as weak (if only some banks received government capital, the market would infer that those banks were in the worst condition, which could trigger a run). The capital injections restored confidence that the banks would survive, which helped to stabilize the interbank lending market and the broader financial system. Most of the TARP bank investments were repaid within two years, and the government ultimately earned a modest profit on the bank recapitalization program.

## The FDIC guarantee programs

The FDIC played a critical role in the crisis response through two guarantee programs. The **Transaction Account Guarantee Program** temporarily provided unlimited deposit insurance for non-interest-bearing transaction accounts (primarily business checking accounts), which prevented a flight of corporate cash from the banking system. The **Temporary Liquidity Guarantee Program** (TLGP) guaranteed newly issued senior unsecured debt of banks and bank holding companies, allowing the banks to issue bonds at rates only slightly above Treasuries and thereby restoring their access to medium-term funding.

The TLGP was particularly important because it addressed the wholesale funding crisis that was at the heart of the panic. Banks that had been unable to issue unsecured debt at any price were suddenly able to borrow at near-Treasury rates, because the FDIC guarantee eliminated the credit risk for bondholders. The guarantee program was a direct application of the Diamond-Dybvig insight: by providing a credible government guarantee, the FDIC eliminated the incentive to run, and the good equilibrium was restored.

## International coordination

The U.S. interventions were mirrored by similar actions in Europe and around the world. The United Kingdom was a leader in the bank recapitalization approach: on October 8, 2008, the UK government announced a £500 billion rescue package that included £50 billion for bank recapitalization (Royal Bank of Scotland, Lloyds Banking Group, and HBOS received the bulk of the funds), £200 billion in short-term lending support, and £250 billion in government guarantees for bank debt. Germany, France, the Netherlands, Belgium, Ireland, and other European countries implemented their own bank rescue packages.

The international coordination was facilitated by the G-7 and G-20 groupings. The G-7 finance ministers issued a five-point plan on October 10, 2008, committing to use "all available tools" to prevent the failure of systemically important institutions, to ensure that institutions could raise capital, to ensure that deposit insurance was robust, to restart the securitization markets, and to maintain macroeconomic stimulus. The G-20 summit in Washington on November 15, 2008, brought together the leaders of the world's largest economies and produced a coordinated commitment to fiscal stimulus and financial reform.

The coordinated response was essential because the crisis was global. Unilateral action by any single country would have been insufficient, because capital would have flowed to countries with more generous guarantees, and the failure of banks in one country would have imposed losses on counterparties in others. The coordination was imperfect (the burden-sharing between countries was contentious, and some countries, particularly Ireland, took on guarantees that would later prove unsustainable), but it was far more effective than the uncoordinated response to the crises of the 1930s.
