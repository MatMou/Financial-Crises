The economic crisis triggered by the COVID-19 pandemic in early 2020 was unlike any crisis studied in this course. Every previous crisis had endogenous origins: financial imbalances, speculative bubbles, policy errors, or institutional failures that built up within the economic and financial system. The COVID crisis was triggered by an exogenous shock, a novel coronavirus, that forced governments to shut down large portions of their economies virtually overnight. The shock was simultaneously a supply shock (production was halted), a demand shock (consumption collapsed), and a financial shock (asset prices plunged and credit markets froze).

The policy response was also unprecedented in its speed, scale, and design. Governments implemented fiscal support programs measured in the trillions of dollars within weeks of the initial lockdowns. Central banks deployed the full toolkit of unconventional monetary policy, and invented new tools, to prevent the financial system from seizing up. The response drew explicitly on the lessons of the 2007-2009 crisis: policymakers who had been criticized for doing "too little, too late" during the GFC were determined not to repeat the mistake.

The COVID crisis also produced consequences that extended well beyond the initial pandemic: supply chain disruptions, a surge in inflation to levels not seen in four decades, and the most aggressive monetary tightening cycle since the Volcker era, which itself produced financial stress (including bank failures in 2023). This session traces the full arc from the initial shock through the inflation and tightening aftermath.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Explain why the COVID-19 pandemic produced an economic crisis despite having no endogenous financial origins, and compare the pandemic shock to previous crisis triggers.
2. Describe the financial market panic of March 2020, including the dash for cash, the Treasury market disruption, and the breakdown of safe-asset correlations.
3. Evaluate the fiscal response to the pandemic, including the CARES Act, furlough schemes, and the debate over the scale and design of transfers.
4. Explain the Federal Reserve's crisis interventions, including the expansion of lending facilities and the role of the Fed as "market maker of last resort."
5. Analyze the origins of the post-pandemic inflation surge and the debate over its causes (supply disruptions versus demand stimulus).
6. Assess the lessons of the COVID crisis for crisis economics, including the effectiveness of pre-positioned policy frameworks and the risks of aggressive intervention.

## Session Outline

**13.1 The Pandemic as an Exogenous Shock.** The nature of the COVID shock, the lockdowns, the initial economic impact, and the comparison with previous crisis triggers.

**13.2 The Financial Market Panic and the Dash for Cash.** The market crash of March 2020, the Treasury market disruption, the breakdown of traditional hedging relationships, and the run on money market funds and corporate credit.

**13.3 The Fiscal Response.** The CARES Act, European furlough schemes, the G-20 response, the unprecedented speed and scale of fiscal support, and the debate over program design.

**13.4 Monetary Policy: The Fed as Market Maker of Last Resort.** The Fed's emergency interventions, the revival and expansion of GFC-era facilities, the purchase of corporate bonds, and the international dollar swap lines.

**13.5 Supply Chains, Inflation, and the Tightening Cycle.** The post-pandemic supply chain disruptions, the demand-supply mismatch, the inflation surge of 2021-2022, the Fed's pivot to tightening, and the banking stress of 2023.

**13.6 Lessons: How COVID Changed Crisis Economics.** What worked, what went too far, how the COVID response differed from the GFC response, and the implications for future crisis management.
