The theoretical frameworks developed in Sessions 2 and 3, Minsky's endogenous cycle, the financial accelerator, debt-deflation, bank runs as coordination failures, were all formulated with modern economies in mind. But the phenomena they describe are not modern. Financial crises are as old as financial markets themselves. Speculative manias, banking panics, and sovereign defaults have occurred in every century for which we have records, across very different institutional settings, monetary regimes, and levels of economic development.

This session covers the long historical arc from the earliest well-documented speculative episodes of the 17th and 18th centuries through the classical gold standard era that ended with the outbreak of World War I in 1914. The purpose is not merely antiquarian. These early crises established many of the patterns that recur throughout the course: the Kindleberger-Minsky sequence of displacement, boom, euphoria, and panic; the role of leverage and maturity mismatch in generating fragility; the tension between the gold standard's promise of stability and its tendency to transmit and amplify shocks across borders; and the recurring political struggle over the design of the lender of last resort.

The gold standard era is also the period during which the institutional infrastructure of modern central banking took shape. Bagehot's principles (subsection 2.1) were formulated in response to crises under the gold standard. The Federal Reserve was created in direct response to the Panic of 1907. The lessons and institutional innovations of this era continue to shape financial regulation today.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Analyze the early speculative manias (Tulip Mania, the South Sea Bubble, the Mississippi Bubble) using the Kindleberger-Minsky framework, identifying the displacement, the credit mechanism, and the role of institutional innovation in each episode.
2. Explain the mechanics of the classical gold standard, including the price-specie-flow mechanism, the "rules of the game," and the role of the Bank of England as the conductor of the international monetary system.
3. Describe the recurring pattern of 19th-century banking panics and explain how the absence of a formal lender of last resort amplified these crises.
4. Analyze the Panic of 1907 as the event that catalyzed the creation of the Federal Reserve, and explain how the crisis revealed the structural weaknesses of the U.S. financial system.
5. Compare the experience of core and peripheral countries under the gold standard, including the role of capital flow reversals and sovereign default in the periphery.
6. Draw connections between the vulnerabilities of the gold standard era and the vulnerabilities of modern international monetary arrangements, particularly the Eurozone.

## Session Outline

This session is divided into the following subsections:

**4.1 Early Speculative Manias.** The Tulip Mania (1637), the Mississippi Bubble (1720), and the South Sea Bubble (1720). Each episode is analyzed through the Kindleberger-Minsky lens, with attention to the specific financial innovations and institutional features that enabled the speculation.

**4.2 The Classical Gold Standard: Architecture and Mechanisms.** How the gold standard worked in theory and in practice. The price-specie-flow mechanism, the asymmetric adjustment between surplus and deficit countries, the role of London as the financial center, and the credibility of the gold commitment.

**4.3 Banking Panics in the 19th Century.** The recurring pattern of boom, panic, and contraction in the British, American, and Continental European banking systems. The crises of 1825, 1857, 1873, and 1893, and the evolution of the lender-of-last-resort function.

**4.4 The Panic of 1907 and the Birth of the Federal Reserve.** A detailed case study of the crisis that led to the creation of the U.S. central bank. J.P. Morgan as the private lender of last resort, the structural weaknesses of the National Banking System, and the political economy of central bank creation.

**4.5 Crises in the Periphery.** The experience of emerging market economies under the gold standard, including the Barings Crisis of 1890, capital flow cycles to Latin America, and the pattern of sovereign default and recovery.

**4.6 Lessons from the Gold Standard Era.** What the pre-1914 experience teaches us about the relationship between monetary regimes, capital mobility, and financial stability, and why many of these lessons were forgotten and had to be relearned.
