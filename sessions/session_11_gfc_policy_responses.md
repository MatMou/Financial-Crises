The policy response to the global financial crisis of 2007-2009 was the most aggressive and coordinated intervention in the history of economic policymaking. Central banks cut interest rates to zero and then invented new tools when conventional monetary policy was exhausted. Governments injected hundreds of billions of dollars into failing banks, guaranteed trillions of dollars of bank liabilities, and implemented the largest fiscal stimulus programs since World War II. Regulators overhauled the rules governing the financial system in an effort to prevent a repetition.

The response was explicitly informed by the lessons of the Great Depression. Policymakers who had studied the Depression, most notably Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke, whose academic career had been devoted to understanding the monetary and financial causes of the Depression (subsection 5.4), were determined not to repeat the mistakes of the 1930s: the passive monetary policy, the inadequate fiscal response, and the failure to rescue the banking system. The fact that the 2007-2009 recession, while severe, was far less catastrophic than the Great Depression is due in significant measure to the application of those lessons.

This session examines the major components of the policy response, assesses their effectiveness, and considers the longer-term consequences, including the slow recovery, the regulatory reforms, and the debate over whether the response was adequate.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Describe the emergency interventions of 2008-2009, including TARP, the bank recapitalization programs, and the government guarantees, and assess their effectiveness in stabilizing the financial system.
2. Explain the unconventional monetary policies adopted by the Federal Reserve and other central banks, including quantitative easing, forward guidance, and the zero lower bound problem.
3. Evaluate the fiscal stimulus programs implemented in the United States and globally, and assess the debate over their size, composition, and effectiveness.
4. Describe the major regulatory reforms enacted after the crisis, including the Dodd-Frank Act and Basel III, and assess their strengths and limitations.
5. Explain why the recovery from the crisis was so slow and evaluate the secular stagnation hypothesis.
6. Assess the overall policy response: what worked, what did not, and what lessons were drawn.

## Session Outline

**11.1 Emergency Interventions.** TARP, bank recapitalization, the FDIC's guarantee programs, the auto industry bailout, and the international coordination of rescue efforts.

**11.2 Unconventional Monetary Policy.** Quantitative easing (QE1, QE2, QE3), forward guidance, Operation Twist, and the zero lower bound. The Fed's emergency lending facilities and the expansion of the central bank balance sheet.

**11.3 Fiscal Stimulus.** The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, the G-20 coordinated stimulus, and the debate over multipliers, composition, and the pivot to austerity.

**11.4 Regulatory Reform.** The Dodd-Frank Act, the Volcker Rule, the creation of the FSOC and the CFPB, Basel III capital and liquidity requirements, and the debate over too-big-to-fail.

**11.5 The Slow Recovery and Secular Stagnation.** Why the recovery was the slowest since World War II, the balance sheet recession interpretation, and the secular stagnation hypothesis.

**11.6 Did We Prevent a Second Great Depression?** Comparing the GFC to the Great Depression, assessing what the policy response accomplished, and identifying the lessons for future crises.
