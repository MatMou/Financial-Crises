---
title: "9.6 Rating Agencies, Regulatory Failure, and Global Imbalances"
subtitle: The gatekeepers that failed
---

The vulnerabilities described in the preceding subsections, the housing bubble, subprime lending, securitization, CDS, and the shadow banking system, did not develop in a vacuum. They developed in a regulatory environment that was supposed to prevent the accumulation of excessive risk in the financial system but that failed to do so. The failures were multiple and mutually reinforcing: failures of credit rating agencies, failures of financial regulators, and failures of the international monetary system. This subsection examines each in turn.

## The failure of credit rating agencies

The credit rating agencies, primarily Moody's, Standard & Poor's, and Fitch, occupied a pivotal position in the securitization machine. Their ratings determined which institutional investors could hold structured credit products (many investors were restricted by regulation or by their own investment mandates to holding only investment-grade or AAA-rated securities), how much capital banks had to hold against them, and what haircut repo lenders applied to them. The rating agencies were, in effect, the gatekeepers of the structured credit market: a AAA rating was the passport that allowed a security to be held by the widest possible universe of investors.

The rating agencies' failure to accurately assess the risk of structured credit products was one of the most consequential failures in the history of financial regulation. The agencies assigned AAA ratings to trillions of dollars of securities that would ultimately suffer catastrophic losses. Of the approximately $14,000 CDO tranches rated AAA by Moody's between 2005 and 2007, roughly 75% were eventually downgraded, and a substantial fraction defaulted.

The failure had several causes. **The issuer-pays model** created a fundamental conflict of interest: the rating agencies were paid by the same institutions (the investment banks) whose securities they were rating. An agency that was too strict in its ratings would lose business to a competitor that was more lenient, creating a "race to the bottom" in rating standards. The conflict was compounded by the concentration of the structured credit rating business among a small number of agencies and a small number of investment bank clients, which created intense competitive pressure.

**Model limitations** were equally important. The rating agencies' models relied on historical data on mortgage default rates and house price movements, which did not include a scenario of nationwide house price decline because no such decline had occurred in the data sample. The models also assumed low default correlations among mortgages in different regions, which was critical to the mathematics of tranching but which proved catastrophically wrong. The Gaussian copula model, developed by David Li and widely adopted by the rating agencies and investment banks, provided an elegant mathematical framework for calculating default correlations but was sensitive to the correlation assumptions, and the assumptions used in practice were far too low.

**Inadequate resources** compounded the model failures. The rating agencies' analytical staff was small relative to the volume and complexity of the securities they were rating. Analysts were expected to rate dozens of complex structured deals per month, with limited time for independent analysis. Many structured finance analysts were relatively junior and were outmatched by the highly compensated financial engineers at the investment banks who designed the structures.

## Regulatory failure

The regulatory failure that allowed the vulnerabilities to accumulate was not a failure of a single institution but of the entire regulatory architecture.

**Fragmented regulation** was a central problem. The U.S. financial regulatory system was built for the financial system of the 1930s, with separate regulators for commercial banks (the OCC, FDIC, and Fed), investment banks (the SEC), insurance companies (state regulators), and thrifts (the OTS). The shadow banking system that grew in the 2000s did not fit neatly into any of these categories, and no single regulator had a comprehensive view of the risks building up in the system. The ABCP conduits, SIVs, repo markets, and CDS market all fell into regulatory gaps.

**Deregulatory ideology** contributed to the failure. The dominant intellectual framework among financial regulators in the 2000s, particularly at the Federal Reserve under Greenspan, held that financial markets were largely self-correcting, that sophisticated financial institutions were better at managing their own risks than regulators were at prescribing rules, and that financial innovation was generally beneficial and should not be constrained by regulation. This framework discouraged aggressive supervision and predisposed regulators toward a light-touch approach.

The **Commodity Futures Modernization Act of 2000** explicitly exempted over-the-counter derivatives (including CDS) from regulation by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC), based on the argument that the counterparties to these contracts were sophisticated institutions that did not require the protection of regulation. The exemption removed any possibility of regulatory oversight of the CDS market during the critical period of its growth.

**Basel II capital regulation**, which was being adopted internationally during the 2000s, allowed banks to use their own internal risk models to calculate the capital they needed to hold against their assets. The models systematically underestimated the risk of structured credit products (for the same reasons that the rating agency models did), which meant that banks held far less capital than the true risk of their portfolios warranted. The leverage embedded in the system was far greater than the regulatory framework recognized.

## Global imbalances

The macroeconomic context of the crisis was shaped by the global imbalances discussed in subsection 8.6. The large and persistent current account surpluses of China, the Asian tigers, the oil exporters, and Germany were mirrored by the large and persistent current account deficit of the United States. The surplus countries' savings flowed into the U.S. financial system, primarily through purchases of Treasury bonds and agency MBS, pushing down long-term interest rates and increasing the supply of credit.

The global imbalances contributed to the crisis in two ways. First, by pushing down long-term interest rates, they fueled the housing boom and the search for yield that drove demand for structured credit products. Second, by channeling enormous flows of capital into the U.S. financial system, they increased the quantity of credit available for subprime lending and securitization. The global imbalances were, in effect, the macro-level fuel for the micro-level financial engineering that created the crisis.

The imbalances reflected failures of the international monetary system. The absence of an effective mechanism for adjusting global imbalances (the original Bretton Woods system had been designed to address exactly this problem, through the adjustable peg and IMF surveillance) meant that the surpluses and deficits persisted for years, growing larger over time. The IMF identified the global imbalances as a risk in its Global Financial Stability Reports and World Economic Outlooks, but it had no tools to compel adjustment.

## The convergence

By 2006, these failures had converged to create a financial system of extraordinary fragility. An enormous housing bubble, financed by subprime mortgages with deteriorating underwriting standards, had been securitized into structured credit products that were rated AAA by conflicted and model-dependent rating agencies, distributed through a shadow banking system that was performing maturity transformation without a safety net, interconnected through a CDS market that no one could map, supervised by a fragmented regulatory system guided by a deregulatory ideology, and fueled by global capital flows that the international monetary system was unable to manage.

The system needed only a trigger. When house prices began to fall in 2006, the trigger was pulled.
