---
title: "9.1 The U.S. Housing Bubble"
subtitle: The biggest asset price bubble in American history
---

The U.S. housing bubble of 2002-2006 was the largest asset price bubble in American history, measured by the total value of the assets involved. At the peak in 2006, the total value of U.S. residential real estate was approximately $23 trillion, and the bubble component, the amount by which prices exceeded fundamental values, was estimated at $4-8 trillion depending on the methodology used. The bubble was not merely a stock market phenomenon affecting a narrow class of investors; it involved the most widely held asset in the economy, the family home, and its collapse would devastate the balance sheets of households, banks, and the financial system as a whole.

## The evidence for a bubble

The evidence that U.S. house prices had departed from fundamental values by 2005-2006 was, in retrospect, overwhelming.

The most widely cited indicator was the **price-to-rent ratio**, the ratio of house prices to the rental value of equivalent housing. The logic of the price-to-rent ratio is analogous to the price-to-earnings ratio for stocks: the fundamental value of a house is the present discounted value of the stream of housing services it provides, which is proxied by the rental value. Between 1997 and 2006, the national price-to-rent ratio increased by approximately 50%, meaning that house prices had risen 50% more than could be explained by changes in rents. In the most inflated markets (Las Vegas, Phoenix, Miami, parts of California), the price-to-rent ratio had roughly doubled.

The **price-to-income ratio** told a similar story. House prices had historically tracked household income, because the ability to pay for housing ultimately depends on income. Between 2000 and 2006, the national price-to-income ratio increased by approximately 30%, and in the most inflated markets the increase was much larger. The disconnect between house prices and incomes meant that home purchases were increasingly financed by mortgage debt rather than by income, which made the housing market vulnerable to any tightening of credit conditions.

The Case-Shiller national home price index, which Robert Shiller had developed to track house prices over long periods, showed that real (inflation-adjusted) house prices had been approximately stable for a century before the boom, fluctuating within a relatively narrow band. The 2002-2006 increase was by far the largest deviation from the historical norm, exceeding even the run-ups that had preceded the Great Depression and the postwar housing booms.

## Why did the bubble develop?

Several factors contributed to the housing bubble, and they reinforced each other in a self-amplifying process.

**Low interest rates**, driven by both Federal Reserve policy and the global savings glut (subsection 8.5 and 8.6), reduced mortgage rates to historically low levels. The 30-year fixed mortgage rate fell from approximately 8% in 2000 to approximately 5.5% in 2003. The lower rates increased the size of the mortgage that a given income could support, which directly pushed up house prices. Adjustable-rate mortgages with low initial teaser rates made housing even more affordable in the short term, although the eventual rate reset would create enormous payment shocks.

**Credit availability** expanded dramatically. The expansion of subprime lending (subsection 9.2) and the relaxation of underwriting standards meant that borrowers who would previously have been excluded from the mortgage market were now able to buy homes. The increased demand from these new buyers pushed prices higher, particularly in markets where supply was constrained.

**Speculation** played an increasingly important role as the boom progressed. In the most inflated markets, a significant share of purchases were made by investors who intended to flip the properties for a quick profit rather than to live in them. In Miami, approximately 70% of condominium purchases in 2005-2006 were made by investors. In Las Vegas and Phoenix, the investor share was similarly high. The speculative activity amplified the price increases and made the market more vulnerable to a reversal, because speculators were more likely than owner-occupiers to sell quickly when prices began to fall.

**Extrapolative expectations** reinforced the boom. Surveys conducted by Shiller showed that homebuyers in the most inflated markets expected house prices to continue rising at rates of 10% per year or more for the next decade. These expectations were based on the recent past (house prices had been rising rapidly for several years) rather than on any analysis of fundamentals. The expectation of continued price appreciation made even overpriced homes appear to be good investments, because buyers expected to earn capital gains that would more than compensate for the high purchase price.

**The belief that house prices could not fall nationally** was perhaps the most dangerous assumption. The U.S. had not experienced a nationwide decline in nominal house prices since the Great Depression. Regional declines had occurred (Texas in the 1980s, California in the early 1990s), but they had been offset by price increases in other regions. The absence of a national decline in living memory led many market participants, including the rating agencies whose models were critical to the securitization process (subsection 9.3), to assign near-zero probability to a scenario in which house prices fell simultaneously across the entire country. This assumption was embedded in the risk models that priced and rated mortgage-backed securities, and its failure would be a central mechanism of the crisis.

## Regional variation

The housing bubble was not uniform across the country. It was concentrated in a group of states and metropolitan areas that shared certain characteristics: rapid population growth, elastic or inelastic housing supply, heavy reliance on subprime and non-traditional mortgages, and high levels of speculative activity.

The "sand states" (California, Arizona, Nevada, and Florida) experienced the most extreme price increases and would suffer the most extreme declines. In Las Vegas, the Case-Shiller index approximately tripled between 2000 and 2006 before falling by approximately 62% from peak to trough. In Miami, the pattern was similar. Other metropolitan areas with large bubbles included Phoenix, San Diego, Los Angeles, Washington D.C., and Tampa.

Importantly, many parts of the country did not experience a significant bubble. House prices in much of the Midwest, the South (outside Florida), and the interior West remained close to fundamental values throughout the boom. This regional variation would prove important for understanding the crisis, because the mortgage-backed securities that were at the center of the financial system pooled mortgages from across the country, and their performance depended on the correlation of house price movements across regions, precisely the correlation that the models assumed would be low but that the crisis revealed to be high.
