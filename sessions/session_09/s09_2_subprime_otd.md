---
title: "9.2 Subprime Lending and the Originate-to-Distribute Model"
subtitle: How the mortgage market was transformed into a machine for manufacturing risk
---

The U.S. housing bubble was financed by a revolution in mortgage lending that transformed the mortgage market from a relationship-based, portfolio-lending business into an industrialized, securitization-driven business. The originate-to-distribute (OTD) model, in which mortgages were originated by one institution, sold to another, securitized by a third, and ultimately held by investors around the world, created an extraordinarily efficient machine for channeling capital into housing. But it also created a chain of agency problems that systematically degraded the quality of the underlying loans, producing what would prove to be the most dangerous collection of financial claims in history.

## The originate-to-distribute chain

Under the traditional "originate-to-hold" model, a bank originated a mortgage, held it on its balance sheet, and bore the risk of default. The bank had a strong incentive to screen borrowers carefully, because it would suffer the loss if the borrower defaulted. The bank's relationship with the borrower, and its knowledge of the local housing market, constituted the information capital that Bernanke identified as central to the credit intermediation process (subsection 5.4).

The originate-to-distribute model broke this chain into pieces. The mortgage was originated by a **mortgage broker** (who found the borrower and matched them with a lender) or a **mortgage company** (which funded the loan using short-term warehouse lines of credit). The originator immediately sold the mortgage to an **aggregator** (typically a large investment bank or a government-sponsored enterprise such as Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac). The aggregator pooled thousands of mortgages into a **mortgage-backed security** (MBS), which was then sold to investors. At each step, the institution that held the risk for the briefest period had the weakest incentive to ensure the quality of the underlying loan.

The agency problems were pervasive. Mortgage brokers were compensated by volume (the number and size of loans originated) rather than by quality (the likelihood that the loans would be repaid). They had a direct incentive to originate as many loans as possible, regardless of the borrower's ability to repay, because they earned their commission at origination and bore none of the default risk. Mortgage companies had similarly weak incentives: they held the loans for only a few weeks before selling them, and their compensation depended on volume. The aggregators who purchased the loans performed some due diligence, but the due diligence was insufficient because the aggregators also planned to sell the loans quickly (into MBS) and because the competitive pressure to maintain volume made them reluctant to reject loans from their origination partners.

## The deterioration of underwriting standards

The consequences of these agency problems were visible in the dramatic deterioration of mortgage underwriting standards between 2003 and 2007.

**NINJA loans** (No Income, No Job, no Assets) became common. These were loans made to borrowers who provided no documentation of their ability to repay. The share of "low-doc" or "no-doc" loans in subprime securitizations increased from approximately 28% in 2001 to approximately 51% in 2006. Stated-income loans, in which the borrower simply declared their income without verification, were widespread. Industry insiders called them "liar's loans" because the incentive to overstate income was obvious and the verification was nonexistent.

**Loan-to-value ratios** increased, meaning borrowers were putting up less equity and taking on more debt relative to the value of the property. Combined loan-to-value ratios (including second mortgages and home equity lines of credit) of 95% or even 100% became common, meaning the borrower had little or no equity at stake. The absence of borrower equity removed a critical incentive for the borrower to continue making payments: if house prices fell even slightly, the borrower would be "underwater" (owing more than the property was worth) and would have a financial incentive to default.

**Exotic mortgage products** proliferated. The 2/28 adjustable-rate mortgage, which offered a low teaser rate for the first two years and then reset to a much higher rate, was the most common subprime product. The assumption underlying these loans was that the borrower would refinance before the rate reset, which was only possible if house prices continued to rise (providing the equity needed to qualify for a new loan). Option-ARMs, which allowed borrowers to make payments that were less than the interest owed (with the unpaid interest added to the principal), resulted in "negative amortization," in which the loan balance grew over time rather than shrinking. These products were designed to minimize the initial payment, making houses appear affordable, while deferring the true cost to the future.

## The role of government-sponsored enterprises

Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs) that had traditionally dominated the secondary mortgage market, played a complex role in the crisis. The GSEs did not originate subprime mortgages directly, but they purchased and guaranteed large quantities of mortgage-backed securities, including some backed by subprime and Alt-A (near-prime) loans. Their purchases were motivated partly by their affordable housing mandates (which required them to support lending to low- and moderate-income borrowers) and partly by competitive pressure (as the private-label securitization market grew, the GSEs' market share declined, and they expanded into riskier segments to maintain volume and profitability).

The GSEs' implicit government guarantee (the market's assumption that the government would not allow them to fail, which proved correct when they were placed into conservatorship in September 2008) allowed them to borrow at rates only slightly above Treasuries, which gave them a cost-of-funds advantage that they used to build enormous portfolios of mortgage-backed securities. By 2008, the combined mortgage-related obligations of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac exceeded $5 trillion, making them among the largest financial institutions in the world and among the most important sources of systemic risk.

The role of the GSEs in the crisis remains politically contested. Conservative critics argue that the GSEs' affordable housing mandates drove the expansion of subprime lending and that the crisis was primarily caused by government intervention in the housing market. The evidence does not support this interpretation: the GSEs were followers rather than leaders in the subprime market, and the worst-performing loans were originated by private-sector lenders and securitized by private-sector investment banks. But the GSEs' enormous portfolios and their implicit guarantee did amplify the crisis and increased the fiscal cost of the resolution.
