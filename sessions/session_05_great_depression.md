The Great Depression of the 1930s remains the most severe economic catastrophe in modern history. Between 1929 and 1933, real GDP fell by approximately 30% in the United States and by comparable magnitudes in many other countries. Unemployment reached 25% in the United States, 30% in Germany, and similarly devastating levels across the industrialized world. International trade collapsed by approximately two-thirds. The political consequences were profound: the Depression contributed to the rise of fascism in Europe, the election of Franklin Roosevelt and the New Deal in the United States, and the eventual destruction of the interwar international order.

The Great Depression is also the single most studied event in macroeconomics and financial history. It has shaped, and continues to shape, the way economists think about financial crises, monetary policy, fiscal policy, banking regulation, and international monetary arrangements. Every major school of macroeconomic thought, Keynesian, monetarist, Austrian, New Classical, has developed its interpretation of what went wrong and what should have been done differently. The crisis was explicitly on the minds of policymakers during the 2007-2009 global financial crisis, and the policy response to the GFC was designed, in large part, to avoid repeating the mistakes of the 1930s.

This session provides a detailed analysis of the Great Depression, from the credit expansion of the 1920s through the crash, the banking crises, the international transmission of the crisis, and the eventual recovery. It draws on the theoretical frameworks developed in Sessions 2 and 3 and builds on the gold standard analysis of Session 4.

## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, you should be able to:

1. Describe the macroeconomic and financial conditions of the 1920s, including the return to the gold standard, the U.S. credit and stock market boom, and the structural imbalances of the interwar international monetary system.
2. Analyze the sequence of events from the stock market crash of 1929 through the banking panics of 1930-1933, and explain why the initial recession deepened into a depression.
3. Explain the role of the interwar gold standard in transmitting and amplifying the crisis internationally, using the framework of Eichengreen's *Golden Fetters*.
4. Compare the Friedman-Schwartz monetary explanation, the Keynesian aggregate demand explanation, and the Bernanke credit channel explanation of the Depression, and assess the strengths and limitations of each.
5. Evaluate the policy responses to the Depression, including the abandonment of the gold standard, monetary expansion, fiscal stimulus, and the institutional reforms of the New Deal.
6. Explain the institutional legacy of the Depression, including deposit insurance, the separation of commercial and investment banking, the Securities and Exchange Commission, and the Bretton Woods system.

## Session Outline

This session is divided into the following subsections:

**5.1 The Roaring Twenties and the Interwar Gold Standard.** The economic boom of the 1920s, the return to the gold standard at prewar parities, the structural imbalances between the United States and Europe, and the stock market and credit expansion that preceded the crash.

**5.2 The Crash of 1929 and the Banking Crises of 1930-1933.** The stock market crash of October 1929, the three waves of bank failures in the United States, the international spread of banking crises, and the deepening of the initial recession into a depression.

**5.3 The Gold Standard as Transmission Mechanism.** How the interwar gold standard transmitted the American contraction to the rest of the world. Eichengreen's "golden fetters" thesis, the role of France's gold accumulation, and the evidence that early abandonment of the gold standard was associated with earlier recovery.

**5.4 Competing Explanations.** The three main academic interpretations of the Great Depression: Friedman and Schwartz's monetary contraction thesis, the Keynesian collapse of aggregate demand, and Bernanke's credit channel. What each explanation gets right and where each falls short.

**5.5 Policy Responses and Recovery.** The failure of the initial policy response, Roosevelt's banking holiday and gold policy, the role of fiscal stimulus, and the uneven recovery across countries. Why the Depression ended and whether policy deserves the credit.

**5.6 Institutional Legacy: The New Deal Financial Architecture.** The regulatory innovations born from the Depression: deposit insurance (FDIC), Glass-Steagall separation, the SEC, the Bretton Woods system. How these institutions shaped the postwar financial landscape and why many were eventually dismantled.
